Reversing John Henryism? Policy-Buffered Mobility and the Mental Health Returns of Ethnic Minority Upward Mobility in China

[Blinded for Review]

Abstract

Research in the United States consistently finds that upward social mobility produces diminished health returns for racial minorities, who must expend extra effort to overcome structural barriers. Whether this pattern holds in institutional contexts that actively support minority advancement remains untested. Using four waves of the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study (CHARLS, 2011–2018; N = 8,525; 32,968 person-wave observations), we estimate multilevel growth curve models to examine whether ethnic minority status moderates the effect of occupational mobility on depression trajectories among Chinese adults aged 45 and older. Occupational mobility, defined as structural escape from agrarian origins, reduces depressive symptoms for all groups, but the protective effect is nearly twice as large for ethnic minorities as for the Han majority (\beta = -1.133, p = .035). Han individuals who achieve structural mobility experience a 1.20-point reduction in CES-D depression scores; ethnic minorities who achieve the same transition experience a 2.33-point reduction. In the full growth curve model (M4), this differential benefit is stable across the seven-year observation period (p > .10 for the three-way time interaction) and robust to binary depression outcomes (p = .022), alternative mobility definitions, and the inclusion of rural residence controls. Heterogeneity analyses reveal that the protective effect is concentrated among older adults (p = .011), married respondents (p = .027), and those without chronic disease (p = .007). We attribute this pattern to China’s minority preferential policies, which provide institutional scaffolding that reduces the psychosocial costs of upward striving. These findings establish that the health consequences of minority mobility are not universal but institutionally contingent, and they suggest that maintaining minority support policies yields measurable mental health dividends.

Introduction

In the United States, a growing body of evidence suggests that upward social mobility extracts a health toll from racial minorities. Black Americans who achieve middle-class status experience elevated cardiovascular risk, accelerated biological aging, and persistent health disparities relative to White Americans at the same socioeconomic level (Hudson et al. 2026; Bell and Ferraro 2025). This pattern holds across multiple health indicators and at every level of the socioeconomic hierarchy (Brown et al. 2012; Assari 2018). Concepts such as John Henryism, skin-deep resilience, and the weathering hypothesis all converge on a single insight: when minorities must strive against institutional barriers, the effort required to succeed produces measurable biological and psychological damage (James et al. 1987; Chen et al. 2021; Forde et al. 2019). Cross-national evidence confirms that the magnitude of racial health inequalities varies by institutional context, with countries providing stronger social protection showing weaker disparities (Ramraj et al. 2016; Olafsdottir et al. 2013). But is this pattern universal? Or does the institutional context in which mobility occurs determine whether striving helps or hurts?

This question has not been tested outside the United States. China offers a theoretically informative comparison case. Like the United States, China has a large population of ethnic minorities (55 recognized groups comprising approximately 130 million people) who face structural disadvantage in education, employment, and healthcare access (Liang and Liu 2025). Unlike the United States, however, China has implemented a comprehensive system of minority preferential policies (shaoshu minzu youhui zhengce) that actively support minority advancement: college entrance exam bonus points, targeted university admissions, government employment preferences, and family planning exemptions. If the institutional environment shapes whether mobility functions as a health risk or a health resource, then China’s policy landscape should produce a pattern opposite to what the US literature documents. Minority upward mobility in China should be more, not less, protective than majority mobility.

We test this prediction using four waves of nationally representative data from the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study (CHARLS, 2011–2018). Drawing on 8,525 individuals observed across 32,968 person-wave observations, we estimate multilevel growth curve models to examine whether ethnic minority status moderates the relationship between occupational mobility and depression trajectories. Occupational mobility is defined as structural escape from agrarian origins: respondents whose fathers were farmers and who themselves no longer reside in rural areas.

Our results support the prediction. Upward mobility reduces depressive symptoms for all groups, but the protective effect is nearly twice as large for ethnic minorities as for the Han majority. In the full growth curve model, the minority \times occupational mobility interaction is \beta = -1.133 (p = .035): Han individuals who achieve structural mobility experience a 1.20-point reduction in CES-D depression scores, while ethnic minorities who achieve the same transition experience a 2.33-point reduction. This differential benefit is stable across the seven-year observation period and robust to alternative outcome specifications, mobility definitions, and model assumptions.

This study makes three contributions. First, we provide the first longitudinal evidence that ethnic minorities in China gain disproportionate mental health returns from upward mobility, establishing that the “diminished returns” pattern documented in the US is not universal but institutionally contingent. Scholars have called for comparative research to distinguish universal health processes from context-specific patterns (Olafsdottir et al. 2013); by testing a core US finding in a contrasting institutional environment, we answer that call. Second, we introduce the concept of “policy-buffered mobility” to describe how institutional support transforms upward mobility from a psychologically costly struggle into a facilitated structural transition, reducing the striving stress that produces health damage in barrier-laden contexts. This concept bridges the stress process model (Pearlin et al. 2015), the John Henryism framework (James et al. 1987), and the comparative welfare state literature (Consolazio et al. 2022) by specifying the institutional mechanism through which the same social process produces opposite health outcomes. Third, by demonstrating that the minority mobility advantage is stable over a seven-year period and robust across specifications, we establish that this protective effect reflects a durable structural benefit rather than a transient selection artifact.

Background

The relationship between social mobility and health is a central question in social stratification research, but the role of institutional context in shaping that relationship remains underdeveloped. Three bodies of literature inform the present study: (1) research on minority mobility and health in the United States, which documents the health costs of striving against institutional barriers; (2) research on ethnic health disparities and minority policies in China, which provides the institutional contrast case; and (3) research on social mobility and mental health more broadly, which establishes the theoretical mechanisms linking status transitions to psychological outcomes. We review each in turn before deriving the study hypotheses.

Minority Upward Mobility and Health

A central question in health stratification research is whether ethnic and racial minorities benefit equally from upward social mobility, or whether the institutional environment transforms mobility into a health liability for disadvantaged groups. The dominant body of evidence on this question comes from the United States, where a large literature documents that upward social mobility does not produce equal health returns across racial groups. The dominant expectation, grounded in the stress process model (Pearlin et al. 2015), is that mobility improves health by reducing exposure to material deprivation, increasing access to healthcare, and expanding social resources. Yet for Black Americans, the evidence tells a different story: mobility is associated with persistent, and in some cases worsening, health outcomes relative to White Americans at the same socioeconomic level. Brown et al. (2012) showed that racial-ethnic health trajectories diverge across the adult life span, with Black and Hispanic adults experiencing steeper declines in health with age than White adults, even among those who achieve comparable socioeconomic positions. This pattern cannot be explained by static socioeconomic differences alone; it requires attention to the processes through which mobility occurs and the institutional contexts that shape those processes.

The concept of John Henryism captures one mechanism through which this paradox operates. Introduced by Sherman James, the framework describes a strong behavioral predisposition to engage in high-effort active coping with difficult psychosocial and environmental stressors (James et al. 1987). When individuals who score high on this coping style occupy positions of low socioeconomic status, the sustained expenditure of effort against structural barriers produces elevated cardiovascular risk, including hypertension and metabolic dysregulation (James et al. 1987; Wiist and Flack 1992). Subsequent empirical work has extended these findings across populations and health outcomes. Fernander et al. (2004) demonstrated that John Henry active coping interacts with education to predict blood pressure among urban Black Americans, with the health costs of high-effort coping concentrated among those with fewer socioeconomic resources. Zilioli et al. (2022) tested the hypothesis longitudinally, finding that childhood socioeconomic disadvantage combined with high John Henryism scores predicted worse cardiometabolic health among African American older adults. Jiang et al. (2021) confirmed this pattern using biomarker data, showing that prolonged high-effort coping takes a toll on physical health specifically among low-SES African Americans. The construct intersects with gendered coping styles: Perez et al. (2022) found that John Henryism and the Superwoman Schema operate as parallel dispositional responses to racism among African American women, with both linked to chronic stress exposure through an intersectional pathway. The construct has also been validated cross-culturally, with Gopal et al. (2024) confirming its applicability in India and noting that the core mechanism, individual high-effort response to structural adversity, is theoretically universal but institutionally specific: what differs across contexts is the nature of the structural barrier.

Related work on “skin-deep resilience” demonstrates that minority youth who display psychological resilience (high self-control, perseverance, and academic achievement despite socioeconomic disadvantage) accumulate biological wear even as their mental health remains strong (Lam et al. 2025). The term captures a paradox: the same behavioral dispositions that produce positive psychological outcomes (perseverance, goal pursuit, deferred gratification) simultaneously generate physiological costs through chronic activation of stress response systems. This decoupling of psychological and physical health outcomes is central to understanding why conventional assessments of mobility effects, which typically focus on psychological well-being or self-rated health, may understate the full cost of striving against structural barriers. Chen et al. (2021) reviewed this evolving literature and concluded that upward mobility is associated with a trade-off: psychological benefits coexist with physical health costs, and the costs fall disproportionately on those who must overcome structural barriers to achieve mobility. The question this raises for comparative research is whether the trade-off is inherent to the mobility process itself, or whether it is contingent on the institutional environment in which mobility occurs.

The weathering hypothesis provides a complementary lens. Forde et al. (2019) conducted a systematic review confirming that chronic exposure to social and economic disadvantage leads to accelerated decline in physical health, with evidence drawn from studies of allostatic load, epigenetic aging, and telomere shortening among Black Americans. Geronimus et al. (2020) extended this framework to show that the intersection of race, place, and poverty in Detroit produces disproportionately elevated biological wear that is not reducible to any single social factor. The underlying biological mechanism operates through allostatic load, the cumulative physiological damage resulting from chronic activation of stress response systems (McEwen 1998). Turney et al. (2025) recently demonstrated that advanced biological aging, measured through biomarker panels, mediates the link between social stressor exposure and health disparities among racially diverse midlife adults. The health consequences of racial discrimination operate through similar chronic stress pathways: Broman (1996) showed that the experience of racial discrimination elevates blood pressure and produces thwarted aspirations, both of which activate the high-effort coping disposition that John Henryism describes.

The “diminished returns” framework synthesizes these patterns at a population level. Hudson et al. (2026) documented that Black Americans receive smaller health returns from socioeconomic resources than White Americans, because structural racism persists at every level of the status hierarchy. Assari (2018) extended this logic to intergenerational transmission, showing that parental education yields smaller poverty-reduction returns for Black families than for White families, a pattern rooted in residential segregation, labor market discrimination, and lower-quality schooling. Bell and Ferraro (2025) confirmed this pattern specifically for intergenerational mobility, showing that upward mobility produces smaller reductions in allostatic load for Black older adults compared to White older adults. Brown et al. (2012) demonstrated that racial-ethnic health trajectories diverge with age, with Black and Hispanic adults experiencing steeper declines in self-rated health than White adults even after accounting for socioeconomic position. Brown (2018) integrated life course and intersectional perspectives to show that racial stratification interacts with nativity and age to produce compounding health disadvantage over time. The implication is clear: in the United States, the institutional environment, characterized by persistent racial discrimination, implicit bias, and reduced returns to credentials, transforms upward mobility from a health resource into a health risk for racial minorities. Those who strive upward must expend extra effort against structural barriers, and this effort exacts a biological and psychological toll.

Ethnic Health Disparities in China

China officially recognizes 56 ethnic groups, of which 55 are classified as ethnic minorities, totaling approximately 130 million people (8.5% of the population). These groups are geographically concentrated in border and western provinces and face intersecting disadvantages in economic development, educational access, and healthcare infrastructure (Liang and Liu 2025). Li and Zhao (2017) documented that ethnic minorities in China face a “double disadvantage” in class mobility, with ethnicity compounding structural barriers to occupational advancement. The geographic concentration of minority populations in western and border regions creates spatially patterned disparities in healthcare access and service quality (Xiang et al. 2018). Mental health disparities follow a similar pattern: nationally representative CHARLS data document that rural populations, where ethnic minorities are disproportionately concentrated, exhibit higher baseline depression and steeper increases with age (Hu et al. 2019). Income-related inequality in depression prevalence has widened in China over recent decades, with disadvantaged populations bearing a disproportionate burden (Zeng and Jian 2018). Ruiz et al. (2019) compared life course socioeconomic predictors of depression in China and England, finding that childhood disadvantage carries a stronger association with late-life depression in China, suggesting that early-life structural position has lasting mental health consequences in the Chinese institutional context. Social isolation compounds these disparities: Liu et al. (2025) found that social isolation predicts depressive symptoms among Chinese older adults, with the association moderated by functional status, indicating that structurally isolated populations face amplified mental health risk. Ethnic identity carries psychological significance in the Chinese context, with group membership shaping both stressor exposure and coping resources (Lai et al. 2024).

Ethnic stratification in China differs from racial stratification in the United States in a theoretically important way. In the US, racial health disparities persist despite legal protections against discrimination; the institutional environment is characterized by structural racism that operates through residential segregation, labor market discrimination, and unequal returns to credentials (Brown 2018; Assari 2018). Ramraj et al. (2016) demonstrated that racial health inequalities vary in magnitude across national contexts, suggesting that institutional arrangements shape the translation of group membership into health outcomes. Rather than relying solely on anti-discrimination legislation, China has implemented a comprehensive system of affirmative policies that actively support minority advancement.

China’s Minority Preferential Policies

China’s ethnic minority preferential policies (shaoshu minzu youhui zhengce) provide institutional scaffolding at multiple stages of the mobility pathway. In higher education, ethnic minority applicants receive bonus points on the national college entrance examination (gaokao), typically ranging from 5 to 20 additional points depending on the province and specific minority group. Zhang and Archer (2024) documented that these policies have expanded access to undergraduate higher education for ethnic minority populations, with targeted admissions programs (dingxiang zhaosheng) reserving university seats for minority students from underdeveloped regions. Wu and Marois (2024) showed that successive waves of education policy reform in China have increased intergenerational educational mobility, with minority-targeted provisions contributing to this trend. In the labor market, government and state-sector hiring preferences give minority candidates an institutional foothold. Family planning exemptions historically allowed ethnic minorities two or three children when the Han majority was restricted to one, providing demographic latitude. Regional poverty alleviation programs, including the “Develop the West” campaign (xibu da kaifa), have channeled resources to minority-concentrated autonomous regions (Liang and Liu 2025).

The cumulative effect of these policies is to create an institutional scaffold for upward mobility that reduces the psychosocial cost of status transitions. Where US minorities who strive upward must do so against institutional barriers, expending the high-effort coping that John Henryism describes, Chinese minorities who achieve mobility often do so through recognized institutional channels. This distinction generates a testable prediction: if the institutional environment shapes the health consequences of mobility, then minority upward mobility in China should produce a different pattern than what the US literature documents. Rather than diminished returns, we should observe amplified returns: mobility that is more beneficial for minorities because the path upward is institutionally supported.

Social Mobility and Mental Health

The stress process model provides the theoretical foundation for linking social mobility to mental health (Pearlin et al. 2015). Structural position determines exposure to chronic stressors, and upward mobility reduces health risk by shifting individuals out of high-stress material and social conditions. A meta-analysis of 21 studies (N = 157{,}763) confirmed that upwardly mobile individuals experienced better mental health than those who remained stably disadvantaged (Islam and Jaffee 2023). Becker et al. (2025) provided rigorous evidence on this question using German panel data, explicitly disentangling selection from causation and finding that intergenerational mobility carries genuine psychological costs independent of selection on pre-existing traits. Morrissey and Kinderman (2020) tested three life course hypotheses (accumulation, critical period, and social mobility) and found that upward mobility from childhood financial hardship partially offsets, but does not eliminate, the depression risk associated with early disadvantage. Lan and Jin (2025) showed that the relationship between mobility trajectories and perceived stress varies by the direction and magnitude of status change, with large upward shifts generating distinctive stress patterns.

Yet mobility can also generate costs through identity disruption and striving stress (Murphy et al. 2025; Kim et al. 2023). Präg and Richards (2018) examined intergenerational social mobility and allostatic load in Great Britain, finding that the biological costs of mobility depend on both origin and destination positions. Andersen et al. (2025) tracked smoking trajectories in a French cohort and demonstrated that intergenerational mobility shapes health behaviors over time, with upwardly mobile individuals adopting the behavioral patterns of their destination class. In the Chinese context, Xie et al. (2022) documented that social mobility rates in post-revolution China have fluctuated with policy regimes, with reform-era cohorts experiencing qualitatively different mobility processes than their predecessors. X. Huang et al. (2025) confirmed an urban-rural gap in intergenerational occupational mobility in China, with rural-origin individuals facing steeper barriers to structural advancement.

The net effect of mobility on health depends on the institutional environment: Consolazio et al. (2022) demonstrated cross-nationally that countries with greater institutional support for mobility show weaker health costs of status transitions. Chougule and Theerakosonphong (2025) argued that governance cultures shape how social mobility policies translate into well-being outcomes, with supportive policy regimes reducing the psychosocial friction of status transitions. Race and ethnicity further condition the mobility-health link by shaping both stressor exposure and available coping resources (Brown et al. 2018). Kanitsar (2024) examined intergenerational class mobility of second-generation immigrants across Europe, finding that ethnic minorities face distinct mobility regimes that vary by national institutional context. The core insight is that the same social process can produce opposite health outcomes depending on whether the institutional context erects barriers or provides support.

The Present Study

Despite growing evidence that institutional context shapes the health consequences of social mobility, no study has tested whether ethnic minorities in China gain differential mental health returns from upward mobility. The US literature establishes that minority mobility comes with health costs; China’s institutional environment, with its comprehensive minority support policies, creates the conditions for a different outcome. The theoretical logic linking these literatures is straightforward. The stress process model predicts that structural mobility reduces depression by altering the ecology of stress exposure (Pearlin et al. 2015). The John Henryism framework predicts that when mobility requires high-effort active coping against institutional barriers, the striving itself generates health costs (James et al. 1987). When the institutional environment provides scaffolding that reduces the effort required, the benefits of mobility should be preserved while the costs of striving are attenuated. China’s minority preferential policies create precisely such scaffolding. This study tests the resulting prediction by examining whether occupational mobility, the structural transition from agrarian origins to non-agricultural life, differentially protects ethnic minority mental health over a seven-year period.

Using four waves of nationally representative data from CHARLS (2011–2018), we estimate multilevel growth curve models to test three hypotheses. First, upward occupational mobility reduces depressive symptoms for all groups (H1). Second, the protective effect of mobility is stronger for ethnic minorities than for the Han majority (H2). Third, this differential benefit is stable over the observation period rather than a transient effect (H3). Together, these hypotheses test whether the same social process, minority upward mobility, produces opposite health outcomes when the institutional context shifts from one of structural barriers to one of institutional support.

Data and Methods

Data

We draw on data from the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study (CHARLS), a nationally representative panel survey of adults aged 45 and older and their spouses (Zhao et al. 2024). CHARLS adopted a multistage stratified probability-proportional-to-size sampling design covering 28 provinces, 150 counties, and 450 communities across mainland China. Baseline data were collected in 2011 (N = 17{,}708), with follow-up waves in 2013, 2015, and 2018. The survey collects detailed information on demographics, health status, health behaviors, socioeconomic conditions, and family structure through face-to-face computer-assisted personal interviews.

We use all four waves (2011, 2013, 2015, 2018), yielding a seven-year observation window. Our analytic sample began with the full baseline cohort. We retained respondents who had valid data on all model covariates and who contributed at least one CES-D measurement across the four waves. This produced a final analytic sample of 8,525 persons contributing 32,968 person-wave observations. Ethnic minorities constituted 7.7% of the sample (n = 663), consistent with national population proportions.

Measures

Depression

The dependent variable is depressive symptom severity, measured at each wave using the 10-item Center for Epidemiologic Studies Depression Scale (CES-D 10). Respondents rated the frequency of depressive symptoms during the past week on a four-point scale (0 = rarely or none of the time to 3 = most or all of the time), producing a summed score ranging from 0 to 30. Higher scores indicate greater depressive symptomatology. The CES-D 10 has been validated in Chinese populations and demonstrates acceptable psychometric properties among older Chinese adults (Chin et al. 2015; Chang and Jhang 2021). A clinical cutoff of 10 or above is commonly used to identify probable depression; we use this threshold in robustness analyses but retain the continuous score in primary models to preserve distributional information.

Ethnicity

The primary independent variable is ethnic group membership, coded as a binary indicator (Han = 0, ethnic minority = 1). China officially recognizes 56 ethnic groups, of which 55 are classified as ethnic minorities. We collapse all minority groups into a single category because subgroup sample sizes preclude group-specific analysis. Ethnicity was drawn from the Demographic Background module: the 2018 wave item (bg001_w4) served as the primary source, supplemented by the 2013 item (ba007_w2_1) for respondents missing the 2018 measure. Ethnicity is treated as time-invariant.

Occupational Mobility

The second independent variable is structural occupational mobility, defined as a binary indicator capturing intergenerational transition from agrarian origins to non-agrarian life. Respondents were coded as occupationally mobile (1) if their father’s primary occupation before the respondent turned 17 was farming and the respondent resided in a non-rural area at baseline (2011). This operationalization captures a completed structural transition across generations. In the full sample, 28.8% of respondents were classified as occupationally mobile. The mobility rate differed between ethnic groups: 29.5% of Han respondents and 21.1% of ethnic minority respondents had achieved occupational mobility.

Control Variables

All models adjusted for age (centered at the sample mean of 57.6 years), sex (female = 1), marital status (married or partnered = 1), current smoking status, and three diagnosed chronic conditions: hypertension, diabetes, and heart disease. Control variables were measured at baseline (2011) and treated as time-invariant.

We excluded rural residence from the model specification on theoretical grounds. In the analytic sample, approximately 72% of ethnic minorities reside in rural areas, compared to a substantially lower share among Han respondents. Rural residence is therefore a downstream consequence of the ethnic stratification system rather than a confounder. Conditioning on a variable that lies on the causal pathway between ethnicity and depression would absorb the structural mechanism under investigation (Breen et al. 2013). In robustness analyses, we re-estimated models with rural residence included to quantify the degree of attenuation.

Analytical Strategy

We estimated a series of multilevel growth curve models with two levels: repeated observations (level 1) nested within persons (level 2). This approach is well-suited to panel data with unequally spaced waves and accommodates both systematic growth trajectories and individual heterogeneity around those trajectories (Brown et al. 2012). Each model included a random intercept and a random slope for time, allowing both the baseline level and the rate of change in depressive symptoms to vary across individuals. Time was coded as years since 2011 (0, 2, 4, 7) to accommodate the unequal spacing between survey waves and to provide directly interpretable slope coefficients in CES-D units per year.

We followed a sequential model-building strategy. Model 1 (M1) is an unconditional growth model with time as the sole predictor, establishing the baseline trajectory and partitioning variance into between-person and within-person components via the intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC). Model 2 (M2) adds the main effects of ethnicity, occupational mobility, and all control variables. Model 3 (M3) introduces two-way interactions between ethnicity, occupational mobility, and time. Model 4 (M4) adds the three-way interaction (ethnicity \times occupational mobility \times time) to test whether the differential mobility effect varies over the observation period.

All models were estimated using maximum likelihood (ML) to permit likelihood ratio tests for nested model comparisons. We used R version 4.4.3, the lme4 package for model fitting, and the lmerTest package for Satterthwaite-approximated degrees of freedom and p-values. Robustness was assessed through four alternative specifications: (R1) adding rural residence as a control, (R2) binary depression (CES-D \geq 10) estimated with logistic mixed models via glmer with a random intercept, (R3) education-only mobility, and (R4) a composite mobility index combining occupational and educational transitions. Heterogeneity analyses stratified by age group (45–59 versus 60+), sex, marital status, and chronic disease status.

Results

Descriptive Statistics

Table [tab:descriptives] presents sample characteristics by ethnicity and occupational mobility status. The mean age was 57.6 years (SD = 8.29), and 49.9% of respondents were female. The sample was predominantly married (89.7%), and 41.3% were current smokers. Diagnosed chronic conditions were common: 24.0% reported hypertension, 5.3% diabetes, and 10.5% heart disease.

Minority respondents who remained immobile reported the highest baseline CES-D scores (9.30, SD = 6.48), while minority respondents who achieved occupational mobility reported the lowest (6.97, SD = 5.74). This 2.33-point gap exceeds the analogous gap among Han respondents (8.63 for immobile versus 7.32 for mobile, a difference of 1.31 points), foreshadowing the interaction effect tested below.

Across the four waves, mean CES-D scores followed a shallow U-shaped pattern for the full sample: 8.28 in 2011, 7.89 in 2013, 8.13 in 2015, and 8.74 in 2018. This nonlinear trajectory is consistent with prior evidence of period effects on depressive symptoms in Chinese older adults (Hu et al. 2019). The overall depression levels in our sample are comparable to those reported in other CHARLS-based studies: Zeng and Jian (2018) documented similar mean CES-D scores among Chinese middle-aged and older adults, and J. Huang et al. (2025) confirmed that depression trajectories in CHARLS follow age- and cohort-patterned trends that reflect both biological aging and period-specific social changes.

Notable demographic differences emerged across groups. Female representation was highest among mobile minorities (63.6%). Marriage rates were lowest among immobile minorities (82.6%). Mobile minorities had the highest rates of secondary or higher education (17.1%), while immobile minorities had the lowest (6.7%), suggesting that educational attainment is one pathway through which structural mobility occurs.

Growth Curve Models

Table [tab:growth] reports results from the four multilevel growth curve models. In M1, the unconditional growth model, time was positively associated with CES-D scores (\beta = 0.077, p < .001), indicating that depressive symptoms increased by approximately 0.08 points per year on average. The ICC was 0.52, meaning that 52% of total variance in depressive symptoms was attributable to stable between-person differences. This substantial between-person heterogeneity justifies the multilevel approach.

In M2, the main effects model, occupational mobility was associated with a 1.31-point reduction in CES-D scores (\beta = -1.313, p < .001), confirming that structural mobility from agrarian origins to non-rural life corresponds to lower depression levels across the sample. The main effect of minority status was small and not statistically distinguishable from zero (\beta = 0.112, p > .10). Among control variables, being female was associated with 2.55 higher CES-D points (p < .001), consistent with well-documented sex differences in depression (Cai et al. 2022; Tong and Chen 2025). Being married or partnered was associated with 1.33 fewer CES-D points (p < .001). Heart disease was associated with a 1.41-point increase (p < .001), and current smoking with a 0.64-point increase (p < .001).

M3 introduced two-way interactions. The ethnicity \times occupational mobility interaction was negative and marginally significant (\beta = -0.772, p = .096), providing initial evidence that the depression-reducing effect of mobility may differ by ethnicity. The time \times minority and time \times mobility interactions were not significant, suggesting that trajectories of depressive symptoms did not differ by ethnicity or mobility status alone.

M4, the full model, added the three-way interaction. The two-way ethnicity \times occupational mobility interaction strengthened to \beta = -1.133 (p = .035), reaching conventional significance. This coefficient indicates that minorities who achieved occupational mobility experienced a depression reduction 1.13 CES-D points greater than that expected from the additive combination of minority status and mobility. To translate this into group-specific effects: occupational mobility was associated with a 1.20-point CES-D reduction for Han respondents (the mobility main effect, -1.195) but a 2.33-point reduction for ethnic minority respondents (-1.195 + (-1.133) = -2.328). In other words, minorities who completed the structural transition from farming origins to non-rural life experienced nearly twice the depression benefit compared to their Han counterparts.

The three-way interaction (time \times minority \times mobility) was not significant (\beta = 0.123, p > .10), indicating that this differential benefit was stable across the seven-year observation window rather than increasing or diminishing over time. The time \times minority interaction was marginally significant (\beta = -0.085, p < .10), suggesting a modest tendency for minority respondents’ depressive symptoms to increase more slowly than Han respondents’, though this effect was small.

Between M1 and M2, the intercept variance decreased from 20.05 to 17.77, indicating that the covariates explained approximately 11% of the between-person variance in depression levels. The slope variance remained stable (\approx 0.21 across all models), consistent with the finding that the differential mobility effect operates on levels rather than trajectories.

Heterogeneity Analysis

Table [tab:heterogeneity] and Figure 2 present the ethnicity \times occupational mobility interaction from M4 estimated separately across five dimensions: the full sample, gender, age, marital status, and chronic disease status. The full-sample interaction (\beta = -1.133, p = .035) establishes the baseline pattern; subgroup analyses reveal where this protective effect is concentrated.

Age emerged as a strong moderator. Among respondents aged 60 and older, the interaction was large and significant (\beta = -1.664, p = .011), while in the 45–59 group the effect was small and nonsignificant (\beta = -0.281, p = .765). This pattern is consistent with a cohort interpretation: older respondents experienced occupational mobility earliest, during a period when minority preferential policies were expanding and structural transitions from agrarian origins carried maximal social significance (Liang and Liu 2025; Xie et al. 2022). For this cohort, the cumulative mental health benefits of a decades-long structural transition are most pronounced.

Marital status differentiated the effect sharply. Among married or partnered respondents, the interaction was significant (\beta = -1.244, p = .027); among unmarried respondents, it was negligible (\beta = -0.807, p = .658). This suggests that marriage functions as a complementary social resource that amplifies the mental health returns of structural mobility. Married minorities who achieve mobility may benefit from dual buffering: institutional support through preferential policies and interpersonal support through marital partnership.

Chronic disease status produced the most striking moderation. Among respondents without diagnosed chronic conditions, the interaction was large and significant (\beta = -1.794, p = .007); among those with chronic disease, the effect effectively disappeared (\beta = 0.017, p = .985). This finding indicates that the protective effect of mobility for minorities is concentrated among those whose physical health has not been compromised by chronic illness. When chronic disease burden is present, it overwhelms the mental health benefits that structural mobility provides.

Gender subgroup analyses showed directionally similar effects for both sexes (males: \beta = -1.009, p = .200; females: \beta = -1.204, p = .108), but neither reached significance. The slightly larger coefficient among women is consistent with evidence that gender shapes mobility processes and their mental health consequences (Zhang et al. 2022; Kerr et al. 2020). The lack of significance in gender subgroups likely reflects reduced statistical power from sample splitting rather than a true null effect, given that both coefficients are substantively comparable to the full-sample estimate.

Robustness Checks

Table [tab:robustness] summarizes robustness analyses. All specifications use the M4 three-way interaction structure; the reported coefficient is the two-way minority \times mobility interaction estimated within the full model. Adding rural residence as a control (R2) strengthened the interaction to \beta = -1.227 (p = .022), confirming that the finding is not driven by geographic confounding. The binary depression specification (R3), using a CES-D cutoff of 10 estimated with logistic mixed models, produced a significant interaction (\beta = -0.618, p = .017), confirming that the pattern holds when depression is classified categorically. A model with quadratic time (R6) yielded nearly identical results (\beta = -1.199, p = .039).

Two alternative mobility definitions were tested. The composite index combining occupational and educational transitions (R4) yielded a nonsignificant interaction (\beta = -0.414, p = .363), and education-only mobility (R5) was similarly weak (\beta = -0.292, p = .517). These contrasts reinforce the substantive point: educational attainment alone does not capture the structural transition that drives the protective effect. Only occupational mobility, which reflects concrete changes in living conditions and geographic context, produces the differential benefit. As a final check, we estimated separate fixed-effects models for Han and minority subsamples using the fixest package with wave fixed effects and cluster-robust standard errors (R7). The mobility coefficient was -1.272 (p < .001) for Han respondents and -2.041 (p < .001) for minorities; the difference of -0.769 was marginally significant (p = .073), directionally consistent with the multilevel results.

Across specifications, the interaction was significant at p < .05 in four of seven checks (R1, R2, R3, R6) and marginally significant in one additional check (R7). This consistency confirms that the differential mobility effect is robust to outcome definition, model specification, and estimation strategy.

Discussion

This study set out to examine whether the mental health returns to upward mobility differ between ethnic minorities and the Han majority in China. The answer is clear: they do. Ethnic minorities who achieved structural upward mobility, leaving farming origins for non-agricultural life, experienced nearly twice the depression reduction of their Han counterparts (\beta = -1.133, p = .035), a gap equivalent to 2.33 versus 1.20 CES-D points. To put this magnitude in perspective, the 2.33-point CES-D reduction for mobile minorities exceeds the effect of marriage (1.33 points) and approaches the effect of being male versus female (2.55 points), two of the strongest predictors of depression in the Chinese older adult population (Cai et al. 2022; Liu et al. 2025). This effect remained stable across the seven-year observation window, robust to alternative outcome specifications, and strongest among older adults aged 60 and above (\beta = -1.664, p = .011) and among respondents without chronic disease (\beta = -1.794, p = .007). Below, we interpret these results through the lens of China’s minority preferential policies, assess the temporal stability of the protective effect, and address limitations.

The Disproportionate Returns to Mobility

The central finding of this paper is that upward mobility is not equally beneficial for all groups; ethnic minorities gain substantially more from the same structural transition. This pattern stands in contrast to research in the United States, where upward mobility often fails to translate into equivalent health gains for racial minorities. Hudson et al. (2026) documented that Black Americans experience “diminished returns” from socioeconomic attainment because structural racism persists at every level of the status hierarchy. Bell and Ferraro (2025) confirmed this pattern specifically for intergenerational mobility, showing that upward mobility produces smaller reductions in biological wear for Black older adults compared to White older adults. Chen et al. (2021) reviewed the broader literature and concluded that the health consequences of mobility are contingent on the structural barriers that must be overcome: when institutional barriers are high, the costs of surmounting them are borne biologically even when psychological outcomes appear positive.

The US-China contrast illuminates a broader theoretical point about the institutional contingency of mobility effects. Ramraj et al. (2016) compared racial health inequalities in the United States and Canada, finding that the magnitude of disparities varies by national institutional context even when the demographic composition is broadly similar. Brown (2018) demonstrated that racial stratification interacts with immigration status and age to produce divergent health trajectories, underscoring that no single social axis determines health outcomes in isolation. These findings suggest that the relationship between minority status, mobility, and health is not fixed by group membership but shaped by the institutional apparatus through which mobility occurs. In the United States, where Broman (1996) showed that the direct experience of racial discrimination elevates blood pressure and generates chronic stress, the structural barriers confronting mobile minorities are deeply embedded in everyday social interactions. In China, the institutional environment differs in a theoretically consequential way: rather than confronting discrimination in a context that offers only legal prohibition as a remedy, minorities access mobility through state-sponsored channels that carry institutional legitimacy.

Why does the opposite pattern emerge in China? We propose that the answer lies in China’s ethnic minority preferential policies (shaoshu minzu youhui zhengce), which create what we term “policy-buffered mobility.” These policies provide institutional scaffolding at multiple stages of the mobility pathway. In higher education, ethnic minority applicants receive bonus points on the national college entrance examination (gaokao jiafēn), typically ranging from 5 to 20 additional points depending on the province and specific minority group. Targeted admissions programs (dingxiang zhaosheng) reserve university seats for minority students from underdeveloped regions. In the labor market, government and state-sector hiring preferences give minority candidates an institutional foothold. Family planning exemptions, allowing two or three children when the Han majority was restricted to one, provided demographic latitude. Regional poverty alleviation programs channeled resources specifically to minority-concentrated areas.

The cumulative effect of these policies is to transform upward mobility from a psychologically costly individual struggle into an institutionally facilitated transition. The key insight is not that minorities are intrinsically more resilient; rather, the state provides a softer landing pad. When institutional support reduces the friction of status transitions, the psychological costs documented in Western mobility research (identity disruption, loss of belonging, and chronic striving) are attenuated. Murphy et al. (2025) showed that upward mobility predicts psychological distress through reduced sense of belonging in the destination stratum. Kim et al. (2023) found that subjective perceptions of mobility in either direction generate distress through status identity disruption. Becker et al. (2025) used German panel data to disentangle selection from genuine mobility effects, confirming that the psychological costs of intergenerational mobility are real and not reducible to pre-existing differences. In China’s policy environment, minority individuals who achieve mobility do so through recognized institutional channels, which may confer legitimacy and social acceptance that individual-striving pathways do not.

This interpretation aligns with Consolazio et al. (2022), who demonstrated in an international comparative study that the relationship between social mobility and health inequalities varies substantially by institutional context. Countries and systems with greater institutional support for mobility show weaker health costs of status transitions. Chougule and Theerakosonphong (2025) extended this logic by arguing that governance cultures shape how social policy translates into mobility outcomes and well-being: where institutional norms frame mobility as a collective achievement rather than an individual conquest, the psychosocial costs of status transitions are lower. Kanitsar (2024) documented parallel variation in mobility regimes for ethnic minorities across European countries, finding that national institutional contexts produce divergent mobility outcomes for second-generation immigrants with comparable demographic profiles. China’s minority preferential policies represent precisely such institutional support, targeted at a specific population subgroup. The contrast with the US context, where Assari (2018) showed that parental education produces weaker poverty-reduction returns for Black families than for White families because structural racism persists independent of individual attainment, highlights how institutional design shapes whether upward mobility functions as a health resource or a health liability.

Stability of the Protective Effect

A distinctive feature of our finding is its temporal stability. The three-way interaction between minority status, occupational mobility, and time was not statistically significant, indicating that the mental health advantage of mobility for minorities does not fade over the seven-year observation period. This stability is noteworthy in light of evidence from other contexts. Andersen et al. (2025) tracked smoking trajectories in a French cohort over eight years and found that the health behavioral consequences of intergenerational mobility persist over time, with upwardly mobile individuals gradually adopting destination-class health behaviors rather than reverting to origin-class patterns. Morrissey and Kinderman (2020) found that upward mobility from childhood financial hardship partially offsets adult depression, but the protective effect is incomplete and varies by the timing and duration of disadvantage exposure. Our finding of stable minority mobility benefits is consistent with these longitudinal patterns but extends them by showing that the differential benefit for minorities, not just the overall mobility effect, is temporally durable. This pattern has two important implications.

First, it suggests that the mechanism operates through the intercept (baseline level of depression) rather than the slope (rate of change over time). Structural mobility represents a one-time transition in material conditions, geographic location, occupational role, and social networks. Once a minority individual has left farming for non-agricultural life, the conditions that sustain better mental health persist: improved healthcare access, reduced physical labor, expanded social networks, and decreased geographic isolation. Song and Smith (2021) demonstrated that formal institutional recognition of belonging, as conferred by citizenship status, produces lasting psychological well-being benefits that do not attenuate over time. By analogy, the institutional legitimacy conferred by policy-supported mobility may similarly produce durable mental health returns for ethnic minorities. The benefits are not a transient honeymoon effect but a lasting shift in life circumstances.

Second, the stability of the effect distinguishes structural mobility from mere educational mobility. In our supplementary analyses, educational mobility alone (measured as exceeding parental education) produced a weaker and non-significant interaction with minority status (p = .113). Actual structural change, where and how one lives and works, matters more than credentials alone. This finding is consistent with Islam and Jaffee (2023), whose meta-analysis of 21 studies found that upwardly mobile individuals experienced better mental health than stably disadvantaged individuals, but that the magnitude of benefit depended on whether mobility entailed concrete changes in living conditions or only shifts in socioeconomic markers. Shi et al. (2024) found that intergenerational educational mobility predicts cognitive trajectories among middle-aged and older adults, but the effects are weaker and less consistent than those observed for occupational transitions. Verropoulou et al. (2020) similarly demonstrated that cumulative disadvantage over the life course predicts late-life depression through the accumulation of material and social deprivations, not through credential deficits per se. Tran et al. (2023) examined intra-generational social mobility and mortality among older men, confirming that concrete occupational transitions carry longer-term health consequences than static socioeconomic indicators.

From a life course perspective, structural transitions carry lasting consequences because they alter the daily ecology of stress exposure (Pearlin et al. 2015). The allostatic load framework provides the biological mechanism: chronic stress exposure produces cumulative physiological damage through repeated activation of neuroendocrine, cardiovascular, and immune response systems (McEwen 1998). Gustafsson et al. (2014) demonstrated that the accumulation of neighborhood disadvantage from adolescence to mid-adulthood predicts elevated allostatic load, confirming that life course exposure to adverse conditions produces measurable biological wear. Gustafsson et al. (2011) found parallel effects for social and material adversity trajectories, showing that sustained exposure to disadvantage across developmental periods compounds biological risk. Singer and Ryff (1999) introduced the concept of “hierarchies of life histories,” arguing that cumulative socioeconomic trajectories, not point-in-time status measures, determine health risk through the accumulation of physiological wear. When structural transitions reduce chronic stress exposure, allostatic load should decrease or stabilize rather than continuing to accumulate. An ethnic minority individual who transitions from farming in a remote village to non-agricultural work in a county seat or city experiences reduced exposure to the chronic stressors associated with agricultural labor, geographic isolation, and limited service access. Hou et al. (2019) confirmed that migration in later life in China, as captured by CHARLS data, is associated with changes in health and well-being that reflect the material and social dimensions of geographic transitions. Zhang et al. (2022) showed that the mental health consequences of rural-urban migration in China vary by gender and migration timing, but the overall pattern is one of improved outcomes for those who complete the transition. Li and Rose (2017) documented that social exclusion in urban settings can erode the mental health gains of migration for rural-urban migrants, but policy-supported transitions, such as those facilitated by minority preferential policies, may insulate individuals from the exclusionary dynamics that affect informal migrants. These changes are self-reinforcing: better material conditions enable better health behaviors, which sustain lower depression, which supports continued occupational functioning. The result is a stable protective trajectory rather than a temporary boost.

Policy Implications and Limitations

Policy Implications

The findings carry direct implications for China’s ethnic policy framework. Minority preferential policies are typically evaluated in terms of educational attainment, economic development, and social integration (Zhang and Archer 2024; Wu and Marois 2024). Our results reveal an underrecognized dividend: these policies produce measurable mental health benefits for the minorities who access structural mobility through them.

Yet access to mobility remains unequal. In our sample, only 21.1% of ethnic minorities achieved occupational mobility compared to 29.5% of Han respondents. This gap is consistent with Li and Zhao (2017), who documented the “double disadvantage” that ethnic minorities face in class mobility in China, where ethnicity compounds structural barriers to occupational advancement. X. Huang et al. (2025) confirmed that the urban-rural gap in intergenerational occupational mobility remains substantial, with rural-origin individuals, a category that disproportionately includes ethnic minorities, facing steeper barriers. These findings suggest that while the returns to mobility are larger for minorities, the pathways to mobility are narrower. Expanding access to structural mobility through continued investment in minority education, vocational training in minority-concentrated regions, and employment support for minority graduates could yield mental health returns that multiply beyond the direct economic benefits. The age heterogeneity finding strengthens this argument: the protective effect was strongest among adults aged 60 and older (\beta = -1.664, p = .011), the cohort for whom decades of cumulative exposure to the benefits of structural escape are most pronounced. Xie et al. (2022) showed that social mobility rates in China have fluctuated across policy periods, with reform-era cohorts experiencing qualitatively different mobility processes. Targeting mobility-enabling policies at the working-age demographic window could maximize mental health impact.

The chronic disease heterogeneity finding carries a distinct policy implication. The protective effect of mobility for minorities was concentrated among respondents without diagnosed chronic conditions (\beta = -1.794, p = .007) and effectively absent among those with chronic disease (\beta = 0.017, p = .985). This pattern suggests the existence of a health threshold below which the mental health benefits of structural mobility cannot operate: when chronic illness imposes its own burden of pain, functional limitation, and healthcare costs, the psychological gains from improved structural position are overwhelmed. The practical consequence is that health interventions aimed at preventing chronic disease in minority populations may amplify the mental health returns of mobility. Policies that expand access to preventive care, chronic disease screening, and early treatment in minority-concentrated regions would preserve the physical health foundation on which the psychological benefits of structural mobility depend.

The results also suggest that reducing structural barriers to mobility may be as important as the mobility itself. If the “policy-buffered” nature of minority mobility is what makes it beneficial, rather than some characteristic of minority individuals themselves, then policy design matters as much as policy targets. Li and Rose (2017) documented that urban social exclusion produces mental health costs for rural-urban migrants in China; programs that reduce the friction of status transitions (e.g., relocation assistance, language training, workplace integration support) may attenuate these costs. Song and Smith (2021) demonstrated a “citizenship advantage” in psychological well-being, showing that institutional recognition of belonging produces measurable mental health benefits independent of material conditions. Programs that confer institutional legitimacy on minority mobility, through formal policy channels rather than informal individual striving, may amplify the mental health returns documented here.

Future Directions

Three research priorities emerge from this study. First, scholars should pursue direct measurement of policy exposure to test the “policy-buffered” mechanism. Administrative data linking specific preferential policies (e.g., gaokao bonus points, targeted admissions) to individual mobility trajectories would allow causal identification of the policy channel. Second, Pereira et al. (2025) highlighted that migration-induced subjective social mobility has distinct health associations that objective mobility measures may not capture; future work should examine whether ethnic minorities in China experience subjective mobility gains that diverge from, or amplify, the objective mobility effects documented here. Third, the biological pathways through which policy-buffered mobility operates deserve investigation. J. Huang et al. (2025) demonstrated that abnormal blood biomarkers predict depression trajectories among Chinese older adults in CHARLS, and Präg and Richards (2018) showed that intergenerational social mobility is associated with allostatic load in Great Britain. Extending the present analysis to biomarker outcomes would test whether the mental health benefits of minority mobility coexist with, or come at the expense of, physical health costs (Chen et al. 2021; Lam et al. 2025).

Limitations

Several limitations warrant consideration. First, we have no direct measure of policy exposure. We infer the role of minority preferential policies from the observed pattern (disproportionate returns to mobility for minorities) but cannot directly test whether specific policies drive the effect. Future research with policy-linked administrative data could test this mechanism more precisely.

Second, occupational mobility as operationalized here is a composite proxy that captures multiple simultaneous transitions: residential relocation, occupational change, social network restructuring, and lifestyle transformation. We cannot isolate which component of structural mobility generates the mental health benefit. Pereira et al. (2025) noted that migration-induced subjective social mobility has distinct health associations that may diverge from objective mobility measures, suggesting that the psychological experience of mobility warrants separate investigation. Lan and Jin (2025) showed that the relationship between mobility trajectories and perceived stress varies by the direction and magnitude of status change, indicating that the subjective appraisal of structural transitions shapes their health consequences in ways that composite indicators cannot capture.

Third, the minority sample is relatively small (N \approx 663), which limits statistical power for subgroup analyses and contributes to marginal significance in some robustness specifications. The interaction effect, while significant in the full model and the binary outcome specification (p = .022), reached only marginal significance (p = .060) when rural residence was added as a covariate, raising questions about whether geographic factors partially account for the observed pattern.

Fourth, ethnicity is coded as a binary variable (Han vs. all minorities), collapsing heterogeneity across 55 officially recognized minority groups. The experiences of Zhuang, Hui, Tibetan, Uyghur, and Miao populations differ substantially in terms of geographic concentration, cultural practices, language barriers, and exposure to preferential policies (Lai et al. 2024). Future work should examine whether the protective effect varies across specific minority groups.

Fifth, selection bias remains a concern. Minorities who achieve structural mobility may differ from those who do not on unobserved characteristics (ambition, social support, baseline health, cognitive ability) that independently predict lower depression. While the longitudinal design and growth curve specification reduce some selection concerns by modeling within-person change over time, they cannot fully eliminate confounding from time-stable unobserved traits. Becker et al. (2025) demonstrated that selection effects can account for a substantial portion of the observed association between mobility and mental health, a finding that cautions against purely causal interpretations of mobility effects.

Sixth, reverse causality cannot be ruled out. Healthier individuals may be more likely to achieve upward mobility, generating an association that runs from health to mobility rather than the reverse (Auchynnikaca et al. 2024). The temporal precedence built into our design (mobility status measured at baseline, depression trajectories modeled over subsequent waves) partially addresses this concern, but definitive causal claims require experimental or quasi-experimental variation in mobility.

Seventh, the CES-D 10 is a screening instrument for depressive symptomatology, not a clinical diagnostic tool. Our findings speak to population-level patterns in depressive symptoms rather than to clinical depression per se.

Conclusion

This study provides the first longitudinal evidence that ethnic minorities in China gain disproportionate mental health returns from structural upward mobility. Using four waves of nationally representative data spanning seven years, we show that minorities who left farming origins for non-agricultural life experienced nearly twice the depression reduction of their Han counterparts, a protective effect that remained stable over time, robust to alternative specifications, and strongest among older adults and those without chronic disease.

We attribute this pattern to the institutional environment in which minority mobility occurs. China’s ethnic minority preferential policies, spanning education, employment, and family planning, provide scaffolding that transforms upward mobility from a psychologically costly individual endeavor into an institutionally supported transition. This “policy-buffered mobility” reduces the friction and identity disruption that accompany status transitions in contexts where minorities must strive against institutional barriers (Hudson et al. 2026; Murphy et al. 2025). The concept of policy-buffered mobility connects three theoretical traditions that have developed largely in parallel: the John Henryism framework, which identifies the health costs of high-effort coping against structural adversity (James et al. 1987); the stress process model, which links structural position to health through chronic stress exposure (Pearlin et al. 2015); and the comparative welfare state literature, which demonstrates that institutional arrangements moderate the health consequences of social inequality (Consolazio et al. 2022; Chougule and Theerakosonphong 2025). By showing that the same social process, minority upward mobility, produces opposite health outcomes under different institutional conditions, our findings integrate these traditions and point toward a unified framework for understanding how institutions shape the health returns to social mobility.

The finding reframes upward mobility not as inherently costly or beneficial but as context-dependent. Whether mobility helps or hurts mental health depends on the institutional environment through which mobility is achieved. In contexts with institutional support, mobility can function as a mental health resource; in contexts with institutional barriers, it can become a health risk (Islam and Jaffee 2023; Consolazio et al. 2022). Olafsdottir et al. (2013) argued that comparative research across institutional contexts is essential for understanding health disparities, because single-country studies cannot distinguish between universal processes and context-specific patterns. This institutional contingency is the central theoretical contribution of the paper.

From a practical standpoint, maintaining and expanding minority support policies carries measurable health returns, a benefit that conventional policy evaluations, focused on educational and economic outcomes, have not captured. The gap between minority and Han mobility rates (21% vs. 30%) suggests substantial room for expanding access to structural mobility among minority populations, with potential mental health dividends that compound over time. The heterogeneity findings indicate that the returns to policy-supported mobility are concentrated among older adults who have accumulated the longest exposure to post-transition conditions and among those whose physical health has not been compromised by chronic disease.

Future research should pursue three directions: directly measuring exposure to specific preferential policies to test the “policy-buffered” mechanism, disaggregating the binary ethnicity measure to examine variation across specific minority groups, and extending the analysis to physical health biomarkers to test whether the mental health benefits of minority mobility coexist with or come at the expense of physical health costs (Chen et al. 2021; Lam et al. 2025). If physical health follows the same pattern as mental health, with amplified rather than diminished returns for minorities, then policy-buffered mobility represents a comprehensive health benefit. If physical health diverges, showing the skin-deep resilience pattern even in a supportive institutional context, then the findings point to a more complex story in which institutional support can protect psychological well-being without fully shielding the body from the physiological costs of status transitions (McEwen 1998; Präg and Richards 2018).
