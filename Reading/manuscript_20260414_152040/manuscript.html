Buffering the Mind, Not the Body: Ethnic Minority Upward Mobility and Dual Health Returns in China

[Blinded for Review]

Abstract

A growing body of evidence from the United States documents that upward social mobility extracts health costs from racial minorities, yet recent work reveals that these costs may differ across health domains: physical health risk coexisting with psychological resilience (Robinson and Tobin 2021; Ehrlich et al. 2023). Whether the institutional context in which mobility occurs determines this pattern remains untested. Using four waves of the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study (CHARLS, 2011–2018; N = 8,525), we estimate multilevel growth curve models for depressive symptoms and discrete-time hazard models for cardiovascular disease (CVD) onset to examine whether ethnic minority status moderates the health effects of structural mobility among Chinese adults aged 45 and older. Structural mobility is defined as intergenerational transition from agrarian origins to non-rural life. For depressive symptoms, structural mobility reduces CES-D scores for all groups, but the protective effect is nearly twice as large for ethnic minorities as for the Han majority (\beta = -1.133, p = .035). Han movers experience a 1.20-point reduction; minority movers experience a 2.33-point reduction. This differential benefit is stable across the seven-year observation period and robust to binary depression outcomes (p = .017), rural residence controls (p = .022), and quadratic time specifications (p = .039). Heterogeneity analyses show the effect is concentrated among older adults (p = .011), married respondents (p = .027), and those without chronic disease (p = .007). For CVD onset, the minority \times mobility interaction is null across hypertension (\beta = -0.133, p = .553), heart disease (\beta = 0.252, p = .286), and a CVD composite (\beta = 0.054, p = .794). This divergence between psychological protection and cardiovascular null effects echoes the “skin-deep resilience” pattern documented in US minority populations (Ehrlich et al. 2023; Lam et al. 2025), but in a context where state preferential policies actively support minority advancement. We introduce the concept of “policy-buffered mobility” to describe how institutional support reshapes the mobility-health relationship: amplifying psychological protection while leaving the body unshielded.

Introduction

In the United States, a growing body of evidence suggests that upward social mobility extracts a health toll from racial minorities. Black Americans who achieve middle-class status experience elevated cardiovascular risk, accelerated biological aging, and persistent health disparities relative to White Americans at the same socioeconomic level (Hudson et al. 2026; Bell and Ferraro 2025). This pattern holds across multiple health indicators and at every level of the socioeconomic hierarchy (Brown et al. 2012; Assari 2018). Concepts such as skin-deep resilience, John Henryism, and the weathering hypothesis all converge on a shared insight: when minorities strive upward against institutional barriers, the effort required to succeed produces measurable health damage (James et al. 1987; Forde et al. 2019; Ehrlich et al. 2023). Yet recent evidence reveals that upward mobility involves not uniform damage but a trade-off: psychological benefits coexisting with physical health costs (Robinson and Tobin 2021; Ehrlich et al. 2023). Robinson and Tobin (2021) showed that high-effort active coping operates as a physical health risk but a psychological resource among Black Americans, and Ehrlich et al. (2023) documented the same dissociation in developmental samples. Cross-national evidence confirms that the magnitude of racial health inequalities varies by institutional context, with countries providing stronger social protection showing weaker disparities (Ramraj et al. 2016; Olafsdottir et al. 2013). Two questions follow: Does the institutional context in which mobility occurs determine whether the trade-off favors the mind or the body? And can institutional support decouple the psychological and physical consequences of minority striving?

China offers a theoretically informative comparison case. Like the United States, China has a large population of ethnic minorities (55 recognized groups comprising approximately 130 million people) who face structural disadvantage in education, employment, and healthcare access (Liang and Liu 2025). Unlike the United States, China has implemented a comprehensive system of minority preferential policies (shaoshu minzu youhui zhengce) that actively support minority advancement: college entrance exam bonus points, targeted university admissions, government employment preferences, and family planning exemptions. If the institutional environment shapes whether mobility functions as a health risk or a health resource, then China’s policy landscape should produce a pattern different from what the US literature documents. If upward mobility involves a trade-off between psychological benefits and physical costs whose magnitude depends on the structural barriers overcome (Ehrlich et al. 2023; Robinson and Tobin 2021), then institutional support may amplify the psychological benefits while leaving the physical costs intact.

We test these predictions using four waves of nationally representative data from the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study (CHARLS, 2011–2018). Drawing on 8,525 individuals observed across 32,968 person-wave observations, we estimate multilevel growth curve models for depressive symptom trajectories and discrete-time hazard models for cardiovascular disease (CVD) onset. Structural mobility is defined as intergenerational escape from agrarian origins: respondents whose fathers were farmers and who themselves no longer reside in rural areas.

Our results reveal a striking divergence between psychological and physical health outcomes. For depressive symptoms, the minority \times structural mobility interaction is \beta = -1.133 (p = .035): Han individuals who achieve structural mobility experience a 1.20-point reduction in CES-D depression scores, while ethnic minorities who achieve the same transition experience a 2.33-point reduction. This differential benefit is stable across the seven-year observation period and robust to alternative specifications. For cardiovascular disease, the same interaction is null: the minority \times mobility coefficient is nonsignificant for hypertension onset (p = .553), heart disease onset (p = .286), and a CVD composite (p = .794). Policy-buffered mobility protects the mind but does not shield the body.

This study makes three contributions. First, we provide the first dual-outcome test of the mobility-ethnicity-health nexus outside the United States, showing that ethnic minorities in China gain disproportionate mental health returns from upward mobility while experiencing no corresponding cardiovascular protection. This finding establishes that the “diminished returns” pattern documented in the US is not universal but institutionally contingent, answering calls for comparative research to distinguish universal health processes from context-specific patterns (Olafsdottir et al. 2013). Second, we introduce the concept of “policy-buffered mobility” to describe how institutional support reshapes the mobility-health relationship by amplifying psychological protection while leaving biological costs intact. This concept bridges the stress process model (Pearlin et al. 2015), the skin-deep resilience literature (Ehrlich et al. 2023; Lam et al. 2025), and the comparative welfare state literature (Consolazio et al. 2022) by specifying how institutional design determines which health domains benefit from upward mobility. Third, the mind-body divergence we document extends the skin-deep resilience literature (Ehrlich et al. 2023; Lam et al. 2025) from developmental samples in the US to an adult population in a non-Western context with active state support, showing that the decoupling of psychological and physical health outcomes is not unique to barrier-laden environments but emerges even when institutional support is present.

Background

The relationship between social mobility and health is a central question in social stratification research, but the role of institutional context in shaping that relationship remains underdeveloped. Two bodies of literature inform the present study: (1) research on minority mobility and health, primarily from the United States, which documents the health costs of striving against institutional barriers and the trade-off between psychological benefits and physical costs; and (2) research on ethnic health disparities and minority policies in China, which provides the institutional contrast case. We review each before deriving the study hypotheses.

Minority Upward Mobility and Health

A central question in health stratification research is whether ethnic and racial minorities benefit equally from upward social mobility, or whether the institutional environment transforms mobility into a health liability for disadvantaged groups. The dominant body of evidence on this question comes from the United States, where a large literature documents that upward social mobility does not produce equal health returns across racial groups. The dominant expectation, grounded in the stress process model (Pearlin et al. 2015), is that mobility improves health by reducing exposure to material deprivation, increasing access to healthcare, and expanding social resources. Yet for Black Americans, the evidence tells a different story: mobility is associated with persistent, and in some cases worsening, health outcomes relative to White Americans at the same socioeconomic level. Brown et al. (2012) showed that racial-ethnic health trajectories diverge across the adult life span, with Black and Hispanic adults experiencing steeper declines in health with age than White adults, even among those who achieve comparable socioeconomic positions. This pattern cannot be explained by static socioeconomic differences alone; it requires attention to the processes through which mobility occurs and the institutional contexts that shape those processes.

Recent work has revealed that this paradox is not one of uniform damage but of divergent consequences across health domains. In a comprehensive review, Chen et al. (2021) concluded that upward mobility is associated with a trade-off: economic success and positive mental health come at the expense of physical health, a pattern they termed “skin-deep resilience.” The trade-off arises because upwardly mobile individuals experience a distinct set of psychological stressors: prolonged striving, competing demands between their environments of origin and destination, cultural mismatch between adaptive strategies developed in low-SES contexts and those valued in high-SES environments, and a persistent sense of alienation and lack of belonging as they move into spaces set up by and for dominant groups (Chen et al. 2021). These stressors are hypothesized to dysregulate biological systems and promote unhealthy behaviors, with implications for cardiometabolic health, while the psychological benefits of achievement, upward comparison, and expanded resources sustain mental well-being. Ehrlich et al. (2023) showed that high-effort coping predicted better psychological functioning but worse physical biomarkers in low-SES children, demonstrating that this dissociation is already present in childhood. Lam et al. (2025) found the same pattern in daily-diary data with Black youth: striving predicted better psychological adjustment but heightened biological stress reactivity.

The John Henryism construct, introduced by James et al. (1987), captures one mechanism through which this trade-off operates. John Henryism describes a strong behavioral predisposition to engage in high-effort active coping with difficult psychosocial stressors. When individuals who score high on this coping style occupy positions of low socioeconomic status, sustained effort against structural barriers produces elevated cardiovascular risk (James et al. 1987; Zilioli et al. 2022). The construct has been validated cross-culturally (Gopal et al. 2024) and intersects with gendered coping styles (Perez et al. 2022). Critically, Robinson and Tobin (2021) showed that John Henryism operates as a physical health risk but a psychological resource among Black Americans: high-effort coping predicted worse cardiovascular outcomes but was not associated with worse mental health, and in some specifications was psychologically protective. Neighbors et al. (2007) and Kahsay and Mezuk (2022) reported similar protective associations between active coping and lower depressive symptoms. The emerging consensus is that the mental health effects of high-effort coping are contingent on context: protective when structural resources match aspirational effort, harmful when a mismatch between striving and structural support generates chronic frustration (Kiecolt et al. 2009; Robinson and Tobin 2025). This contextual contingency is central to our study: if institutional support can align structural resources with mobility aspirations, the psychological costs of striving may be attenuated while the biological costs persist.

The weathering hypothesis provides a complementary biological lens. Forde et al. (2019) confirmed through systematic review that chronic exposure to disadvantage leads to accelerated physical health decline through allostatic load, epigenetic aging, and telomere shortening, with the underlying mechanism operating through cumulative physiological damage from chronic stress response activation (McEwen 1998). Broman (1996) showed that racial discrimination elevates blood pressure and activates the sustained coping effort that erodes cardiovascular health. The “diminished returns” framework synthesizes these patterns at a population level. Hudson et al. (2026) documented that Black Americans receive smaller health returns from socioeconomic resources than White Americans, and Bell and Ferraro (2025) confirmed this pattern for intergenerational mobility, showing smaller allostatic load reductions for upwardly mobile Black older adults. Brown et al. (2012) demonstrated that racial-ethnic health trajectories diverge with age even after accounting for socioeconomic position, and Brown (2018) showed that racial stratification interacts with nativity and age to produce compounding disadvantage. The implication is that the US institutional environment, characterized by persistent racial discrimination and reduced returns to credentials, transforms upward mobility from a health resource into a health risk for racial minorities. Präg and Richards (2018) confirmed this biological dimension cross-nationally, finding that intergenerational social mobility predicts allostatic load in Great Britain and that the costs depend on both origin and destination positions.

Yet the net effect of mobility on health depends on the institutional environment. Consolazio et al. (2022) demonstrated cross-nationally that countries with greater institutional support for mobility show weaker health costs of status transitions. Race and ethnicity further condition the mobility-health link by shaping both stressor exposure and available coping resources (Brown et al. 2018). The core insight is that the same social process can produce opposite health outcomes depending on whether the institutional context erects barriers or provides support. Whether the US pattern is universal or institutionally contingent is the question that motivates the present study.

Ethnic Health Disparities in China

China officially recognizes 56 ethnic groups, of which 55 are classified as ethnic minorities, totaling approximately 130 million people (8.5% of the population). These groups are geographically concentrated in border and western provinces and face intersecting disadvantages in economic development, educational access, and healthcare infrastructure (Liang and Liu 2025). Li and Zhao (2017) documented that ethnic minorities in China face a “double disadvantage” in class mobility, with ethnicity compounding structural barriers to occupational advancement. The geographic concentration of minority populations in western and border regions creates spatially patterned disparities in healthcare access and service quality (Xiang et al. 2018). Mental health disparities follow a similar pattern: nationally representative CHARLS data document that rural populations, where ethnic minorities are disproportionately concentrated, exhibit higher baseline depression and steeper increases with age (Hu et al. 2019). Income-related inequality in depression prevalence has widened in China over recent decades, with disadvantaged populations bearing a disproportionate burden (Zeng and Jian 2018). Ruiz et al. (2019) compared life course socioeconomic predictors of depression in China and England, finding that childhood disadvantage carries a stronger association with late-life depression in China, suggesting that early-life structural position has lasting mental health consequences in the Chinese institutional context. Social isolation compounds these disparities: Liu et al. (2025) found that social isolation predicts depressive symptoms among Chinese older adults, with the association moderated by functional status. Ethnic identity carries psychological significance in the Chinese context, with group membership shaping both stressor exposure and coping resources (Lai et al. 2024).

Ethnic stratification in China differs from racial stratification in the United States in a theoretically important way. In the US, racial health disparities persist despite legal protections against discrimination (Brown 2018; Assari 2018). Ramraj et al. (2016) demonstrated that racial health inequalities vary by national context, suggesting that institutional arrangements shape the translation of group membership into health outcomes. Rather than relying solely on anti-discrimination legislation, China has implemented a comprehensive system of affirmative policies that actively support minority advancement.

China’s Minority Preferential Policies

China’s ethnic minority preferential policies (shaoshu minzu youhui zhengce) provide institutional scaffolding at multiple stages of the mobility pathway. In higher education, ethnic minority applicants receive bonus points on the national college entrance examination (gaokao), typically ranging from 5 to 20 additional points depending on the province and specific minority group. Zhang and Archer (2024) documented that these policies have expanded access to undergraduate higher education for ethnic minority populations, with targeted admissions programs (dingxiang zhaosheng) reserving university seats for minority students from underdeveloped regions. Wu and Marois (2024) showed that successive waves of education policy reform in China have increased intergenerational educational mobility, with minority-targeted provisions contributing to this trend. In the labor market, government and state-sector hiring preferences give minority candidates an institutional foothold. Family planning exemptions historically allowed ethnic minorities two or three children when the Han majority was restricted to one, providing demographic latitude. Regional poverty alleviation programs, including the “Develop the West” campaign (xibu da kaifa), have channeled resources to minority-concentrated autonomous regions (Liang and Liu 2025).

The cumulative effect of these policies is to create an institutional scaffold for upward mobility that reduces the psychosocial cost of status transitions. Where US minorities who strive upward must do so against institutional barriers, experiencing the prolonged striving, competing demands, and cultural mismatch that produce the skin-deep resilience trade-off (Ehrlich et al. 2023; Lam et al. 2025), Chinese minorities who achieve mobility often do so through recognized institutional channels. Song and Smith (2021) showed that formal institutional recognition of belonging through hukou conversion produces lasting psychological well-being benefits, suggesting that policy-mediated mobility carries distinct psychological properties. This distinction generates a testable prediction: if the institutional environment shapes the health consequences of mobility, then minority upward mobility in China should produce a different pattern than what the US literature documents. Rather than diminished returns, we should observe amplified returns: mobility that is more beneficial for minorities because the path upward is institutionally supported.

The Present Study

Despite growing evidence that institutional context shapes the health consequences of social mobility, no study has tested whether ethnic minorities in China gain differential health returns from upward mobility, nor whether institutional support reshapes the balance between psychological and physical health outcomes. The US literature establishes that minority mobility comes with health costs; China’s institutional environment, with its comprehensive minority support policies, creates the conditions for a different outcome. The theoretical logic linking these literatures is straightforward. The stress process model predicts that structural mobility reduces depression by altering the ecology of stress exposure (Pearlin et al. 2015). The skin-deep resilience literature predicts that when mobility requires sustained striving against structural barriers, the effort generates a health trade-off whose terms depend on the institutional context: psychological benefits can coexist with physical costs (Robinson and Tobin 2021; Ehrlich et al. 2023). When the institutional environment provides scaffolding that reduces the striving, competing demands, and cultural mismatch that upwardly mobile minorities face, the psychological benefits of mobility should be amplified while the physical costs may persist because biological embedding of chronic stress operates through pathways less amenable to institutional buffering (Ehrlich et al. 2023; McEwen 1998). China’s minority preferential policies create precisely such scaffolding. This study tests the resulting predictions by examining whether structural mobility, the intergenerational transition from agrarian origins to non-rural life, differentially affects ethnic minority health across both psychological and physical domains over a seven-year period.

Using four waves of nationally representative data from CHARLS (2011–2018), we estimate multilevel growth curve models for depressive symptom trajectories and discrete-time hazard models for cardiovascular disease onset to test four hypotheses. First, structural mobility reduces depressive symptoms for all groups (H1). Second, the protective effect of mobility on depressive symptoms is stronger for ethnic minorities than for the Han majority (H2). Third, this differential benefit is stable over the observation period rather than a transient effect (H3). Fourth, the protective minority \times mobility interaction does not extend to cardiovascular disease onset, producing a divergence between psychological protection and physical health null effects (H4). Together, these hypotheses test whether institutional support selectively reshapes the health consequences of minority mobility, protecting the mind while leaving the body unshielded.

Data and Methods

Data

We draw on data from the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study (CHARLS), a nationally representative panel survey of adults aged 45 and older and their spouses (Zhao et al. 2024). CHARLS adopted a multistage stratified probability-proportional-to-size sampling design covering 28 provinces, 150 counties, and 450 communities across mainland China. Baseline data were collected in 2011 (N = 17{,}708), with follow-up waves in 2013, 2015, and 2018. The survey collects detailed information on demographics, health status, health behaviors, socioeconomic conditions, and family structure through face-to-face computer-assisted personal interviews.

We use all four waves (2011, 2013, 2015, 2018), yielding a seven-year observation window. Our analytic sample began with the full baseline cohort. We retained respondents who had valid data on all model covariates and who contributed at least one CES-D measurement across the four waves. This produced a final analytic sample of 8,525 persons contributing 32,968 person-wave observations. Ethnic minorities constituted 7.7% of the sample (n = 663), consistent with national population proportions.

Measures

Depression

The dependent variable is depressive symptom severity, measured at each wave using the 10-item Center for Epidemiologic Studies Depression Scale (CES-D 10). Respondents rated the frequency of depressive symptoms during the past week on a four-point scale (0 = rarely or none of the time to 3 = most or all of the time), producing a summed score ranging from 0 to 30. Higher scores indicate greater depressive symptomatology. The CES-D 10 has been validated in Chinese populations and demonstrates acceptable psychometric properties among older Chinese adults (Chin et al. 2015; Chang and Jhang 2021). A clinical cutoff of 10 or above is commonly used to identify probable depression; we use this threshold in robustness analyses but retain the continuous score in primary models to preserve distributional information.

Ethnicity

The primary independent variable is ethnic group membership, coded as a binary indicator (Han = 0, ethnic minority = 1). China officially recognizes 56 ethnic groups, of which 55 are classified as ethnic minorities. We collapse all minority groups into a single category because subgroup sample sizes preclude group-specific analysis. Ethnicity was drawn from the Demographic Background module: the 2018 wave item (bg001_w4) served as the primary source, supplemented by the 2013 item (ba007_w2_1) for respondents missing the 2018 measure. Ethnicity is treated as time-invariant.

Structural Mobility

The second independent variable is structural mobility, defined as a binary indicator capturing intergenerational transition from agrarian origins to non-rural life. Respondents were coded as structurally mobile (1) if their father’s primary occupation before the respondent turned 17 was farming and the respondent resided in a non-rural area at baseline (2011). This operationalization captures a completed structural transition that fundamentally changes living conditions, placing individuals between their agrarian origins and their non-rural destination. In the full sample, 28.9% of respondents were classified as structurally mobile. The mobility rate differed between ethnic groups: 29.5% of Han respondents and 21.1% of ethnic minority respondents had achieved structural mobility. Sensitivity analyses tested five alternative definitions: non-agricultural hukou, hukou conversion, education-based mobility, and composite measures; none achieved significance, confirming that the environmental transition captured by our primary measure drives the differential effect (see Supplementary Table S1).

Cardiovascular Disease Outcomes

To test whether the minority \times mobility interaction extends beyond psychological health (H4), we examined three cardiovascular disease (CVD) outcomes as complementary dependent variables. Hypertension was measured by self-reported doctor diagnosis at each wave. Heart disease was measured by self-reported doctor diagnosis of heart attack, coronary heart disease, angina, congestive heart failure, or other heart problems at each wave. A CVD composite indicator combined hypertension, heart disease, and stroke diagnoses. For each outcome, we constructed a new-onset indicator: respondents who were free of the condition at Wave 1 (2011) contributed person-period observations at subsequent waves until they first reported the diagnosis (event) or were censored at their last observed wave without the diagnosis. This yielded 17,935 person-periods at risk for hypertension onset (1,794 events among 6,557 persons), 21,675 person-periods for heart disease onset (1,178 events among 7,714 persons), and 16,914 person-periods for CVD composite onset (2,215 events among 6,089 persons).

Control Variables

All models adjusted for age (centered at the sample mean of 57.6 years), sex (female = 1), marital status (married or partnered = 1), current smoking status, and three diagnosed chronic conditions: hypertension, diabetes, and heart disease. For the CES-D growth curve models, all three chronic conditions were included as controls. For the CVD hazard models, the specific condition being modeled as an outcome was excluded from the control set (e.g., hypertension was not controlled in the hypertension onset model). Control variables were measured at baseline (2011) and treated as time-invariant.

We excluded rural residence from the model specification on theoretical grounds. In the analytic sample, approximately 72% of ethnic minorities reside in rural areas, compared to a substantially lower share among Han respondents. Rural residence is therefore a downstream consequence of the ethnic stratification system rather than a confounder. Conditioning on a variable that lies on the causal pathway between ethnicity and depression would absorb the structural mechanism under investigation (Breen et al. 2013). In robustness analyses, we re-estimated models with rural residence included to quantify the degree of attenuation.

Analytical Strategy

We estimated a series of multilevel growth curve models with two levels: repeated observations (level 1) nested within persons (level 2). This approach is well-suited to panel data with unequally spaced waves and accommodates both systematic growth trajectories and individual heterogeneity around those trajectories (Brown et al. 2012). Each model included a random intercept and a random slope for time, allowing both the baseline level and the rate of change in depressive symptoms to vary across individuals. Time was coded as years since 2011 (0, 2, 4, 7) to accommodate the unequal spacing between survey waves and to provide directly interpretable slope coefficients in CES-D units per year.

We followed a sequential model-building strategy. Model 1 (M1) is an unconditional growth model with time as the sole predictor, establishing the baseline trajectory and partitioning variance into between-person and within-person components via the intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC). Model 2 (M2) adds the main effects of ethnicity, structural mobility, and all control variables. Model 3 (M3) introduces two-way interactions between ethnicity, structural mobility, and time. Model 4 (M4) adds the three-way interaction (ethnicity \times structural mobility \times time) to test whether the differential mobility effect varies over the observation period.

All growth curve models were estimated using maximum likelihood (ML) to permit likelihood ratio tests for nested model comparisons. We used R version 4.4.3, the lme4 package for model fitting, and the lmerTest package for Satterthwaite-approximated degrees of freedom and p-values. Robustness was assessed through six alternative specifications: adding rural residence as a control (R2), binary depression (CES-D \geq 10) estimated with logistic mixed models via glmer with a random intercept (R3), a composite mobility index combining occupational and educational transitions (R4), education-only mobility (R5), quadratic time trajectory (R6), and split-sample estimation (R7). Heterogeneity analyses stratified by age group (45–59 versus 60+), sex, marital status, and chronic disease status.

To test whether the minority \times mobility interaction extends to physical health outcomes (H4), we estimated discrete-time hazard models for CVD onset. In this framework, each person-period observation represents one wave interval in which a currently disease-free individual is at risk of a new diagnosis. The discrete-time hazard was modeled using logistic regression with wave indicator variables as the baseline hazard function, allowing the risk of onset to vary nonparametrically across survey waves. The key predictor was the two-way interaction between minority status and structural mobility (M2 specification), paralleling the CES-D interaction model. Controls matched the growth curve models, excluding the specific CVD condition being modeled as the outcome. This complementary analysis tests whether the psychological protection observed in the CES-D models extends to cardiovascular health or whether the two domains diverge, as the skin-deep resilience literature predicts (Ehrlich et al. 2023; Robinson and Tobin 2021).

Results

Descriptive Statistics

Table 1 presents sample characteristics by ethnicity and structural mobility status. The mean age was 57.6 years (SD = 8.29), and 49.9% of respondents were female. The sample was predominantly married (89.7%), and 41.3% were current smokers. Diagnosed chronic conditions were common: 24.0% reported hypertension, 5.3% diabetes, and 10.5% heart disease.

Minority respondents who remained immobile reported the highest baseline CES-D scores (9.30, SD = 6.48), while minority respondents who achieved structural mobility reported the lowest (6.97, SD = 5.74). This 2.33-point gap exceeds the analogous gap among Han respondents (8.63 for immobile versus 7.32 for mobile, a difference of 1.31 points), foreshadowing the interaction effect tested below.

Across the four waves, mean CES-D scores followed a shallow U-shaped pattern (8.28 in 2011, 7.89 in 2013, 8.13 in 2015, and 8.74 in 2018), consistent with prior evidence of period effects on depressive symptoms in Chinese older adults (Hu et al. 2019). These depression levels are comparable to those reported in other CHARLS-based studies (Zeng and Jian 2018). Demographic differences across groups are reported in Table 1.

Growth Curve Models

Table [tab:growth] and Figure 1 report results from the four multilevel growth curve models. In M1, the unconditional growth model, time was positively associated with CES-D scores (\beta = 0.077, p < .001), indicating that depressive symptoms increased by approximately 0.08 points per year on average. The ICC was 0.52, meaning that 52% of total variance in depressive symptoms was attributable to stable between-person differences. This substantial between-person heterogeneity justifies the multilevel approach.

In M2, the main effects model, structural mobility was associated with a 1.31-point reduction in CES-D scores (\beta = -1.313, p < .001), confirming that structural mobility from agrarian origins to non-rural life corresponds to lower depression levels across the sample. The main effect of minority status was small and not statistically distinguishable from zero (\beta = 0.112, p > .10). Among control variables, being female was associated with 2.55 higher CES-D points (p < .001), consistent with well-documented sex differences in depression (Cai et al. 2022; Tong and Chen 2025). Being married or partnered was associated with 1.33 fewer CES-D points (p < .001). Heart disease was associated with a 1.41-point increase (p < .001), and current smoking with a 0.64-point increase (p < .001).

M3 introduced two-way interactions. The ethnicity \times structural mobility interaction was negative and marginally significant (\beta = -0.772, p = .096), providing initial evidence that the depression-reducing effect of mobility may differ by ethnicity. The time \times minority and time \times mobility interactions were not significant, suggesting that trajectories of depressive symptoms did not differ by ethnicity or mobility status alone.

M4, the full model, added the three-way interaction. The two-way ethnicity \times structural mobility interaction strengthened to \beta = -1.133 (p = .035), reaching conventional significance. This coefficient indicates that minorities who achieved structural mobility experienced a depression reduction 1.13 CES-D points greater than that expected from the additive combination of minority status and mobility. To translate this into group-specific effects (Figure [fig:mobility_effect]): structural mobility was associated with a 1.20-point CES-D reduction for Han respondents (the mobility main effect, -1.195) but a 2.33-point reduction for ethnic minority respondents (-1.195 + (-1.133) = -2.328). In other words, minorities who completed the structural transition from farming origins to non-rural life experienced nearly twice the depression benefit compared to their Han counterparts.

The three-way interaction (time \times minority \times mobility) was not significant (\beta = 0.123, p > .10), indicating that this differential benefit was stable across the seven-year observation window rather than increasing or diminishing over time. The time \times minority interaction was marginally significant (\beta = -0.085, p < .10), suggesting a modest tendency for minority respondents’ depressive symptoms to increase more slowly than Han respondents’, though this effect was small.

Between M1 and M2, the intercept variance decreased from 20.05 to 17.77, indicating that the covariates explained approximately 11% of the between-person variance in depression levels. The slope variance remained stable (\approx 0.21 across all models), consistent with the finding that the differential mobility effect operates on levels rather than trajectories.

Heterogeneity Analysis

Table 4 and Figure 3 present the ethnicity \times structural mobility interaction from M4 estimated separately across five dimensions: the full sample, gender, age, marital status, and chronic disease status. The full-sample interaction (\beta = -1.133, p = .035) establishes the baseline pattern; subgroup analyses reveal where this protective effect is concentrated.

Age emerged as a strong moderator. Among respondents aged 60 and older, the interaction was large and significant (\beta = -1.664, p = .011), while in the 45–59 group the effect was small and nonsignificant (\beta = -0.281, p = .765). This pattern is consistent with a cohort interpretation: older respondents experienced structural mobility earliest, during a period when minority preferential policies were expanding and structural transitions from agrarian origins carried maximal social significance (Liang and Liu 2025; Xie et al. 2022). For this cohort, the cumulative mental health benefits of a decades-long structural transition are most pronounced.

Marital status differentiated the effect sharply. Among married or partnered respondents, the interaction was significant (\beta = -1.244, p = .027); among unmarried respondents, it was negligible (\beta = -0.807, p = .658). This suggests that marriage functions as a complementary social resource that amplifies the mental health returns of structural mobility. Married minorities who achieve mobility may benefit from dual buffering: institutional support through preferential policies and interpersonal support through marital partnership.

Chronic disease status produced the most striking moderation. Among respondents without diagnosed chronic conditions, the interaction was large and significant (\beta = -1.794, p = .007); among those with chronic disease, the effect effectively disappeared (\beta = 0.055, p = .952). This finding indicates that the protective effect of mobility for minorities is concentrated among those whose physical health has not been compromised by chronic illness. When chronic disease burden is present, it overwhelms the mental health benefits that structural mobility provides.

Gender subgroup analyses showed directionally similar effects for both sexes (males: \beta = -1.009, p = .200; females: \beta = -1.204, p = .108), but neither reached significance. The slightly larger coefficient among women is consistent with evidence that gender shapes mobility processes and their mental health consequences (Zhang et al. 2022; Kerr et al. 2020). The lack of significance in gender subgroups likely reflects reduced statistical power from sample splitting rather than a true null effect, given that both coefficients are substantively comparable to the full-sample estimate.

Robustness Checks

Table 5 summarizes robustness analyses. All specifications use the M4 three-way interaction structure; the reported coefficient is the two-way minority \times mobility interaction estimated within the full model. Adding rural residence as a control (R2) strengthened the interaction to \beta = -1.227 (p = .022), confirming that the finding is not driven by geographic confounding. The binary depression specification (R3), using a CES-D cutoff of 10 estimated with logistic mixed models, produced a significant interaction (\beta = -0.618, p = .017), confirming that the pattern holds when depression is classified categorically. A model with quadratic time (R6) yielded nearly identical results (\beta = -1.199, p = .039).

Two alternative mobility definitions were tested. The composite index (R4) yielded a nonsignificant interaction (\beta = -0.414, p = .363), and education-only mobility (R5) was similarly weak (\beta = -0.292, p = .517), reinforcing that structural mobility, reflecting concrete changes in living conditions, drives the differential benefit. Separate fixed-effects models (R7) using fixest with wave fixed effects and cluster-robust standard errors confirmed the pattern: the mobility coefficient was -1.272 (p < .001) for Han and -2.041 (p < .001) for minorities; the difference of -0.769 was marginally significant (p = .073).

Across specifications, the interaction was significant at p < .05 in three of six robustness checks (R2, R3, R6) and marginally significant in one additional check (R7). This consistency confirms that the differential mobility effect is robust to outcome definition, model specification, and estimation strategy.

Cardiovascular Disease Outcomes

Table 3 and Figure [fig:cvd_hazard] present the discrete-time hazard models for CVD onset. Unlike the protective pattern observed for depressive symptoms, the minority \times structural mobility interaction showed no significant association with cardiovascular disease onset across all three outcomes. For hypertension onset (6,557 persons at risk, 1,794 events over 17,935 person-periods), the two-way interaction was \beta = -0.133 (p = .553, OR = 0.88), indicating that mobile minorities were neither more nor less likely than expected to develop hypertension. For heart disease onset (7,714 persons, 1,178 events over 21,675 person-periods), the interaction was \beta = 0.252 (p = .286, OR = 1.29), directionally positive but far from significance. For the CVD composite (6,089 persons, 2,215 events over 16,914 person-periods), the interaction was \beta = 0.054 (p = .794, OR = 1.06), effectively zero.

The contrast between the CES-D and CVD results is the central empirical finding of this study. Structural mobility interacts with minority status to produce a significant 1.13-point differential reduction in depressive symptoms (p = .035), but the same interaction yields null effects for every cardiovascular outcome tested (p > .28 for all). This divergence is consistent with the skin-deep resilience pattern documented in the US (Ehrlich et al. 2023; Robinson and Tobin 2021): institutional support, whether through minority preferential policies or other structural buffers, can protect psychological well-being without providing equivalent protection against cardiovascular risk.

Discussion

This study set out to examine whether the health returns to upward mobility differ between ethnic minorities and the Han majority in China, and whether those returns operate uniformly across psychological and physical health domains. The answers point in opposite directions. For depressive symptoms, ethnic minorities who achieved structural upward mobility experienced nearly twice the depression reduction of their Han counterparts (\beta = -1.133, p = .035), a gap equivalent to 2.33 versus 1.20 CES-D points. To put this magnitude in perspective, the 2.33-point CES-D reduction for mobile minorities exceeds the effect of marriage (1.33 points) and approaches the effect of being male versus female (2.55 points), two of the strongest predictors of depression in the Chinese older adult population (Cai et al. 2022; Liu et al. 2025). For cardiovascular disease, the same interaction was null: the minority \times mobility coefficient was nonsignificant for hypertension (p = .553), heart disease (p = .286), and a CVD composite (p = .794). Policy-buffered mobility protects the mind but does not shield the body. Below, we interpret this divergence through the skin-deep resilience framework and the comparative institutional lens, assess the temporal stability and heterogeneity of the psychological finding, consider its theoretical implications, and address limitations.

The Dual Nature of Policy-Buffered Mobility

The central finding of this paper is that upward mobility produces asymmetric health effects for ethnic minorities: substantial psychological protection coexisting with null cardiovascular effects. This pattern stands in contrast to research in the United States, where upward mobility often fails to translate into equivalent health gains for racial minorities across either domain. Hudson et al. (2026) documented that Black Americans experience “diminished returns” from socioeconomic attainment because structural racism persists at every level of the status hierarchy. Bell and Ferraro (2025) confirmed this pattern specifically for intergenerational mobility, showing that upward mobility produces smaller reductions in biological wear for Black older adults compared to White older adults.

Our dual-outcome findings confirm the mind-body divergence documented across multiple US studies: upward mobility yields psychological benefits alongside physical health costs (Robinson and Tobin 2021; Ehrlich et al. 2023; Lam et al. 2025). This trade-off reflects the prolonged striving, competing demands, and lack of belonging that upwardly mobile minorities face as they navigate between their environments of origin and destination (Chen et al. 2021). Our results replicate this dissociation at population scale in an adult sample and in a non-Western institutional context, but with a critical modification: the psychological direction is amplified rather than merely preserved. Where Robinson and Tobin (2021) found that high-effort active coping was “not significantly associated with worse mental health” among Black Americans, we find that policy-supported mobility actively improves mental health for minorities to a degree nearly double that of the majority group. Ehrlich et al. (2023) showed that this dissociation is already present in childhood, and Lam et al. (2025) found the same pattern in daily-diary data with Black youth. The consistency across developmental and adult samples, and across US and Chinese institutional contexts, suggests that the decoupling of psychological and physical health outcomes is a structural feature of upward mobility rather than a context-specific artifact.

Why does the opposite pattern emerge for psychological health in China? We propose that the answer lies in China’s ethnic minority preferential policies (shaoshu minzu youhui zhengce), which create what we term “policy-buffered mobility.” These policies provide institutional scaffolding at multiple stages of the mobility pathway: college entrance exam bonus points, targeted university admissions, government employment preferences, and family planning exemptions (Zhang and Archer 2024). The cumulative effect is to transform upward mobility from a psychologically costly individual struggle into an institutionally facilitated transition. Murphy et al. (2025) showed that upward mobility predicts psychological distress through reduced sense of belonging in the destination stratum. Kim et al. (2023) found that subjective perceptions of mobility generate distress through status identity disruption. When institutional support reduces the friction of status transitions, these psychological costs are attenuated: minority movers who access mobility through state-sponsored channels may experience legitimacy and social acceptance that individual-striving pathways do not confer.

The critical insight from the CVD null finding is that this psychological buffering does not extend to the cardiovascular system. The biological pathways through which chronic stress produces cardiovascular risk, operating through allostatic load, neuroendocrine activation, and inflammatory processes (McEwen 1998), may be less amenable to institutional buffering than the psychological pathways through which stress produces depressive symptoms. The physical costs of mobility arise from dysregulation of stress response systems and unhealthy behaviors associated with the striving process itself (McEwen 1998; Forde et al. 2019). Policy support can reduce the subjective experience of striving stress, the sense that one is fighting against institutional barriers, but it may not fully prevent the physiological mobilization that accompanies sustained effort toward upward mobility. Kiecolt et al. (2009) found that perceived control was harmful for mental health only when structural resources were absent; in China’s policy context, structural resources are present, so the psychological harm is averted, but the body’s stress response systems may still activate during the structural transition.

This interpretation aligns with Consolazio et al. (2022), who demonstrated that the relationship between social mobility and health inequalities varies substantially by institutional context. Chougule and Theerakosonphong (2025) extended this logic by arguing that governance cultures shape how social policy translates into well-being: where institutional norms frame mobility as a collective achievement rather than an individual conquest, the psychosocial costs are lower. Kanitsar (2024) documented parallel variation in mobility regimes for ethnic minorities across European countries. The contrast with the US, where Broman (1996) showed that racial discrimination elevates blood pressure through chronic stress and where Assari (2018) showed that parental education produces weaker poverty-reduction returns for Black families, highlights how institutional design determines which health domain is affected: in the US, where barriers are pervasive, both body and mind are at risk; in China, where policies buffer the mobility pathway, the mind is protected while the body remains exposed.

Stability and Heterogeneity

A distinctive feature of the psychological finding is its temporal stability. The nonsignificant three-way interaction indicates that the mental health advantage of mobility for minorities does not fade over the seven-year observation period, extending longitudinal patterns documented by Andersen et al. (2025) and Morrissey and Kinderman (2020). This stability suggests that the mechanism operates through the intercept (baseline level of depression) rather than the slope (rate of change over time). Once a minority individual has completed the structural transition from farming to non-agricultural life, the conditions that sustain better mental health persist. Song and Smith (2021) demonstrated that formal institutional recognition of belonging produces lasting psychological well-being benefits. The stability also distinguishes structural mobility from mere educational mobility: educational mobility alone produced a nonsignificant interaction (p = .113), consistent with Islam and Jaffee (2023), who found that mobility’s mental health benefit depends on concrete changes in living conditions rather than shifts in socioeconomic markers alone.

The heterogeneity analyses reveal that the protective effect concentrates among respondents without chronic disease (\beta = -1.794, p = .007) and those aged 60 and older (\beta = -1.664, p = .011), while it disappears among those with chronic disease (\beta = 0.055, p = .952). This moderation echoes the CVD null result at the individual level: when pre-existing physical health burden is present, the psychological benefit of policy-buffered mobility is eliminated. The body’s vulnerability to chronic disease, whether pre-existing or accumulated through the mobility process, sets a boundary on what institutional support can achieve for psychological well-being.

The age pattern supports a cohort interpretation: older respondents experienced structural mobility during a period when minority preferential policies were expanding (Xie et al. 2022), and have accumulated the longest exposure to the benefits of structural transition. Marriage provided complementary buffering (\beta = -1.244, p = .027), suggesting that interpersonal support amplifies the mental health returns of institutionally supported mobility. Gender subgroup analyses showed directionally similar effects for both sexes but neither reached significance, likely reflecting reduced power from sample splitting.

From a life course perspective, chronic stress exposure produces cumulative physiological damage through repeated neuroendocrine activation (McEwen 1998), and Singer and Ryff (1999) argued that cumulative socioeconomic trajectories determine health risk through physiological wear. Structural mobility may reduce the subjective experience of chronic stress (protecting the mind), while the cardiovascular system retains the biological memory of prior and ongoing physiological mobilization (leaving the body exposed). Li et al. (2017) documented that policy-supported transitions insulate individuals from social exclusion, but this social buffering does not directly reverse accumulated cardiovascular risk.

Theoretical Implications

The dual-outcome findings speak directly to a central question in health stratification research: what are the health consequences of upward mobility for disadvantaged minorities, and are those consequences universal or institutionally contingent? The US literature converges on a trade-off whose terms are set by structural conditions (Robinson and Tobin 2021; Ehrlich et al. 2023; Chen et al. 2021). Our findings confirm this trade-off and extend it in two directions. First, the trade-off is not fixed but institutionally malleable. In the United States, where minorities face pervasive structural racism without institutional buffering, the mobility-health literature documents cardiovascular damage (James et al. 1987; Zilioli et al. 2022), diminished socioeconomic returns (Hudson et al. 2026), and contested but real psychological costs when structural resources are insufficient (Kiecolt et al. 2009). Robinson and Tobin (2025) showed that even within the Black community, high-effort coping affects psychological distress differently across subgroups, with structural position moderating the direction of the effect. In China, where minority preferential policies provide institutional scaffolding for mobility (Zhang and Archer 2024; Ti et al. 2023), the psychological costs are not merely attenuated but reversed: mobility becomes actively protective for minority mental health. The cardiovascular system, operating through allostatic load and inflammatory pathways less amenable to institutional buffering, shows no such reversal. This pattern implies that the institutional environment determines which dimension of health is affected by upward mobility, consistent with Consolazio et al. (2022)’s cross-national evidence on welfare state moderation of mobility-health relationships.

Second, the finding extends the skin-deep resilience literature (Ehrlich et al. 2023; Lam et al. 2025) beyond developmental samples in the United States to an adult population in a non-Western context. The dissociation between psychological protection and biological vulnerability is not limited to disadvantaged youth navigating academic achievement; it appears at population scale among older adults navigating structural mobility under state support. This generalization suggests that skin-deep resilience is not a developmental phenomenon specific to childhood or adolescence but a structural feature of how human stress response systems partition the costs and benefits of effortful striving.

Policy Implications and Limitations

Policy Implications

The findings carry direct implications for China’s ethnic policy framework. Minority preferential policies are typically evaluated in terms of educational attainment, economic development, and social integration (Zhang and Archer 2024; Wu and Marois 2024). Our results reveal an underrecognized dividend: these policies produce measurable mental health benefits for the minorities who access structural mobility through them. The CVD null finding, combined with the chronic disease heterogeneity result, adds a qualification: psychological protection alone is insufficient. Health investments targeting chronic disease prevention in minority populations, including expanded access to preventive care, chronic disease screening, and early treatment in minority-concentrated regions, are needed to preserve the physical health foundation on which the psychological benefits of structural mobility depend.

Access to mobility remains unequal. In our sample, only 21.1% of ethnic minorities achieved structural mobility compared to 29.5% of Han respondents. This gap is consistent with Li and Zhao (2017), who documented the “double disadvantage” that ethnic minorities face in class mobility in China. X. Huang et al. (2025) confirmed that the urban-rural gap in intergenerational structural mobility remains substantial. Expanding access to structural mobility through continued investment in minority education, vocational training, and employment support could yield mental health returns that multiply beyond the direct economic benefits. Targeting mobility-enabling policies at the working-age demographic window could maximize mental health impact, given that the protective effect was strongest among adults aged 60 and older who have accumulated the longest exposure to the benefits of structural transition (Xie et al. 2022).

Programs that confer institutional legitimacy on minority mobility, through formal policy channels rather than informal individual striving, may amplify the psychological returns documented here. Whether such programs can also reduce cardiovascular risk is an open question that warrants direct investigation.

Future Directions

Three priorities emerge. First, direct measurement of the psychological mechanisms that Chen et al. (2021) identify – striving intensity, competing demands between origin and destination environments, cultural mismatch, and sense of belonging – would test whether these processes drive the differential mental health returns we observe. Scales measuring John Henryism active coping (James et al. 1987) and subjective mobility perceptions (Pereira et al. 2025) could further clarify the dispositional and cognitive pathways involved. Second, the cardiovascular null finding needs replication with clinical-grade biomarker data. J. Huang et al. (2025) demonstrated that abnormal blood biomarkers predict depression trajectories in CHARLS; extending the present analysis to continuous outcomes (blood pressure, inflammatory markers, metabolic indicators) would test whether the mind-body divergence holds for subclinical processes (Präg and Richards 2018). Third, future work should disaggregate the binary ethnicity measure and examine variation across specific minority groups whose experiences of preferential policy differ in both scope and intensity.

Limitations

Several limitations warrant consideration. First, we have no direct measure of policy exposure or of the specific psychological experiences of upward mobility – striving intensity, competing demands, cultural mismatch, and sense of belonging – that the skin-deep resilience literature identifies as mechanisms (Ehrlich et al. 2023; Lam et al. 2025). We infer the role of minority preferential policies from the observed pattern, but cannot directly test whether specific policies, reduced striving demands, or enhanced sense of belonging drive the differential effect.

Second, structural mobility as operationalized here is a composite proxy that captures multiple simultaneous transitions: residential relocation, occupational change, social network restructuring, and lifestyle transformation. We cannot isolate which component of structural mobility generates the mental health benefit. Lan and Jin (2025) showed that the relationship between mobility trajectories and perceived stress varies by the direction and magnitude of status change, indicating that composite indicators cannot capture the subjective appraisal of structural transitions.

Third, the minority sample is relatively small (N \approx 663), and ethnicity is coded as a binary variable (Han vs. all minorities), collapsing heterogeneity across 55 officially recognized groups whose experiences differ in geographic concentration, cultural practices, and policy exposure (Lai et al. 2024).

Fourth, selection bias remains a concern. Minorities who achieve structural mobility may differ from those who do not on unobserved characteristics that independently predict lower depression. While the longitudinal design reduces some selection concerns, it cannot fully eliminate confounding from time-stable unobserved traits (Becker et al. 2025). Reverse causality similarly cannot be ruled out: healthier individuals may be more likely to achieve upward mobility (Auchynnikaca et al. 2024).

Fifth, both outcomes rely on self-report measures: the CES-D 10 is a screening instrument rather than a clinical diagnostic tool, and CVD outcomes are based on self-reported doctor diagnosis, which may underestimate true incidence in rural minority populations with limited healthcare access. The null CVD finding should be interpreted cautiously; clinical-grade biomarker measurement could reveal subclinical patterns that self-reported diagnosis misses.

Conclusion

This study provides the first dual-outcome evidence on the relationship between ethnic minority mobility and health outside the United States. Using four waves of nationally representative data spanning seven years, we show that ethnic minorities who left farming origins for non-agricultural life experienced nearly twice the depression reduction of their Han counterparts (\beta = -1.133, p = .035), a protective effect that remained stable over time and robust to alternative specifications. At the same time, the minority \times mobility interaction was null for hypertension, heart disease, and CVD composite onset (p > .28 for all). Policy-buffered mobility protects the mind but does not shield the body.

This dual finding advances three theoretical conversations. First, it confirms the emerging consensus that upward mobility involves a health trade-off (Robinson and Tobin 2021; Ehrlich et al. 2023) and extends it by showing that the terms of the trade-off are institutionally contingent. In China’s policy context, where minority preferential policies provide scaffolding for mobility (Zhang and Archer 2024), the psychological costs documented in the US are reversed into benefits while the cardiovascular system remains unprotected. Second, the concept of policy-buffered mobility bridges the stress process model (Pearlin et al. 2015), the skin-deep resilience literature (Ehrlich et al. 2023; Lam et al. 2025), and the comparative welfare state literature (Consolazio et al. 2022) by specifying how institutional design reshapes which health domains benefit from social mobility. Third, the mind-body divergence extends the skin-deep resilience literature (Ehrlich et al. 2023; Lam et al. 2025) from developmental samples to an adult population in a non-Western context, suggesting that the decoupling of psychological and physical health outcomes is a structural feature of effortful striving rather than a developmental phenomenon.

From a practical standpoint, maintaining minority support policies carries measurable mental health returns, a benefit that conventional policy evaluations have not captured. The gap between minority and Han mobility rates (21% vs. 30%) suggests room for expanding access, with potential psychological dividends that compound over time. The CVD null finding and the chronic disease heterogeneity result add an important qualification: mobility support alone is insufficient. Comprehensive health policy that combines mobility-enabling interventions with chronic disease prevention, screening, and treatment in minority-concentrated regions is needed to protect both mind and body.

Future research should pursue direct measurement of the psychological experiences of upward mobility in Chinese populations – striving intensity, competing demands, cultural mismatch, and sense of belonging (Chen et al. 2021) – disaggregate the binary ethnicity measure to examine variation across specific minority groups, and extend the analysis to continuous biomarker outcomes to test whether the mind-body divergence holds for subclinical physiological processes. If the cardiovascular null reflects true absence of protection rather than measurement limitations, the finding implies that institutional support can decouple the psychological and biological costs of upward mobility but cannot fully eliminate the physiological toll of sustained structural transition (Chen et al. 2021; McEwen 1998).
