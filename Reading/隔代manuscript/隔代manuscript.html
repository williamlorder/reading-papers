Intergenerational Transmission of Caregiver Maltreatment and Joint Developmental Trajectories of Internalizing and Externalizing Behavior: Prospective Links to Young Adult Psychopathology

[Blinded for Review]

Abstract

Caregivers who experienced childhood maltreatment raise children at elevated risk for behavioral problems, yet the developmental pathways connecting intergenerational maltreatment exposure to offspring psychopathology in early adulthood remain underspecified. Using data from the Longitudinal Studies of Child Abuse and Neglect (LONGSCAN; N = 1{,}197), this study modeled joint internalizing and externalizing behavioral trajectories from ages 4 to 16 via dual group-based trajectory modeling (GBTM) and tested whether these trajectories mediate the link between caregiver childhood victimization history and five domains of young adult psychopathology at age 18 (depression, generalized anxiety, PTSD, panic, and substance use). Parallel-process latent growth models using CBCL raw scores indicated that caregiver victimization predicted elevated intercepts for both internalizing (B = 0.34, p < .001) and externalizing (B = 0.73, p < .001) problems but did not predict externalizing slopes (p = .851), indicating that intergenerational risk operates through initial levels established before school entry. Separate three-class GBTMs were estimated for each domain using Stata’s traj command, and the resulting group assignments were cross-tabulated to form five joint trajectory categories: Low-Low (48.0%), Moderate (10.6%), Elevated Both (28.2%), High Unilateral (8.7%), and Comorbid High (4.5%). Multinomial logistic regression showed that each additional type of caregiver victimization increased the relative risk of membership in the Elevated Both (\text{RRR} = 1.25, p < .001), High Unilateral (\text{RRR} = 1.26, p < .001), and Comorbid High (\text{RRR} = 1.18, p < .05) groups. All four elevated trajectory categories exhibited significantly higher symptom counts across multiple psychiatric domains at age 18, with the Comorbid High group showing the broadest elevations. OLS-based Karlson–Holm–Breen decomposition confirmed significant indirect effects of caregiver victimization on all five psychiatric outcomes through trajectory group membership (MDD p < .001; GAD p = .019; PTSD p = .002; panic p = .001; substance use p = .039), with the Elevated Both group contributing the largest share for MDD (41.8%) and PTSD (53.1%) and the High Unilateral group contributing most for substance use (48.0%). The direct effect of caregiver victimization on most outcomes was not significant after accounting for trajectory membership, suggesting that joint behavioral trajectories constitute the primary developmental conduit for intergenerational psychopathological risk. These findings support early transdiagnostic screening and intervention for children of maltreated caregivers before behavioral patterns consolidate.

Keywords: intergenerational transmission; child maltreatment; internalizing behavior; externalizing behavior; developmental trajectories; group-based trajectory modeling; CBCL; LONGSCAN; young adult psychopathology

Introduction

Adults who were maltreated during childhood are two to three times more likely than non-maltreated adults to raise children with persistent behavioral and emotional problems (Widom 1989; Kaufman and Zigler 1987; IJzendoorn et al. 2019). In the United States, approximately 3.5 million children are investigated by child protective services each year (Fluke et al. 2011; Duffee et al. 2021), and a substantial proportion of these children live with caregivers whose own maltreatment histories place the family at intergenerational risk (Cicchetti and Toth 2016; Norman et al. 2012). This pattern of cross-generational transmission has been replicated across diverse samples and cultural contexts (Langevin et al. 2025; Zhang et al. 2022), yet the developmental pathways through which a caregiver’s victimization history translates into offspring psychopathology in early adulthood remain poorly understood (Thornberry et al. 2012; Ertem et al. 2000).

One reason for this gap is that most intergenerational research examines either concurrent behavioral outcomes in childhood or static diagnostic endpoints in adulthood. These designs bypass the developmental trajectory of behavioral problems across childhood and adolescence, a span of 12 or more years during which behavioral patterns consolidate, diversify, or remit (Duprey et al. 2020; Binks et al. 2025). Group-based trajectory modeling (GBTM) has shown that children do not follow a single normative behavioral course; instead, distinct subgroups, including low-stable, chronic-high, and late-onset patterns, characterize both internalizing and externalizing domains (D. S. Nagin and Odgers 2010; Moffitt 1993; Nagin et al. 2024). A minority of children (typically 5–15%) follow chronically elevated or escalating paths that are strongly predictive of later maladjustment. Whether a caregiver’s own maltreatment history differentially sorts offspring into these trajectory subgroups, and whether those subgroups in turn forecast specific domains of adult psychiatric illness, has received limited attention.

A second limitation is that trajectory studies typically model internalizing and externalizing problems as separate processes. Yet these domains are moderately to strongly correlated across development, and joint or dual-trajectory approaches that model both domains simultaneously can reveal comorbidity patterns that domain-specific models obscure (Nagin 2005; Jones and Nagin 2007; Nivard et al. 2016). Joint trajectory categories capture the breadth of behavioral maladjustment, distinguishing, for example, children with co-occurring elevation on both domains from those with high levels on only one, and this distinction may carry prognostic significance for the range of adult psychiatric outcomes a child is likely to develop.

The present study addresses these gaps using data from the Longitudinal Studies of Child Abuse and Neglect (LONGSCAN), a five-site prospective cohort that followed N = 1{,}354 children from age 4 through 18 (Runyan et al. 1998). We pursue three objectives: (1) model joint internalizing and externalizing trajectories from ages 4 to 16 using dual GBTM with raw CBCL scores, (2) test whether caregiver childhood victimization history predicts membership in elevated trajectory groups, and (3) examine whether trajectory group membership mediates the association between caregiver victimization and five domains of young adult psychopathology at age 18, including depression, generalized anxiety, PTSD, panic, and substance use disorders. By linking intergenerational maltreatment exposure to developmental behavioral trajectories and from trajectories to disorder-specific adult outcomes, we aim to specify a complete developmental pathway connecting distal family-level risk to proximal psychiatric outcomes in early adulthood.

Background and Theory

Intergenerational Transmission of Maltreatment

Meta-analytic estimates place the rate of intergenerational maltreatment transmission at roughly 30%, far exceeding base rates in the general population (Kaufman and Zigler 1987; IJzendoorn et al. 2019). Adults who were maltreated as children are at elevated risk of perpetrating maltreatment against their own children or raising children with poor developmental outcomes (Widom 1989; Ertem et al. 2000; Marshall et al. 2022), and these effects persist after accounting for socioeconomic confounds (Thornberry et al. 2012). Both childhood-limited and adolescent-onset victimization predict adverse parenting outcomes, suggesting that the timing and chronicity of the caregiver’s own experiences shape the scope of intergenerational effects.

Three complementary mechanisms have been proposed to account for this link, each pointing to how early the intergenerational signal may register in offspring behavior. Social learning theory posits that maltreated individuals internalize coercive interaction patterns and replicate them with their children (Corso et al. 2025; Jiang et al. 2025). Through repeated exposure to harsh, unpredictable caregiving, offspring learn that aggression, withdrawal, and emotional suppression are normative interpersonal responses, and these patterns become entrenched in the child’s behavioral repertoire from an early age (Younas and Gutman 2022). Attachment theory emphasizes a complementary route: disrupted internal working models impair parental sensitivity and emotional availability (Cicchetti and Toth 2016; Buchheim et al. 2022), and caregivers with unresolved maltreatment histories may struggle to read and respond to their children’s emotional cues, fostering insecure attachment that undermines emotion regulation capacity from the first years of life (Beeghly 2024; Condon et al. 2024). Neurobiological accounts point to stress-system dysregulation as a third pathway. Childhood maltreatment has been linked to altered hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis reactivity, reduced prefrontal cortical volume, and heightened amygdala responsivity, changes that compromise the caregiver’s capacity for co-regulation with the child (Teicher and Samson 2016; McLaughlin et al. 2014; Kurbatfinski et al. 2024). These neurobiological alterations may also be transmitted epigenetically, though human evidence remains preliminary (Scorza et al. 2023). The three mechanisms are not mutually exclusive; they likely operate in concert to shape the offspring’s behavioral risk profile before school entry (Howland and Glynn 2024; Meinck et al. 2025).

A shared implication of these mechanisms is that the intergenerational effect should be detectable in the child’s initial level of behavioral problems rather than in the subsequent rate of change. If social learning, attachment disruption, and HPA-axis dysregulation jointly program the caregiving environment from birth, their combined influence should be reflected in elevated behavioral baselines by the time standardized assessment begins, with less impact on the slope of behavioral change across later childhood. An important conceptual distinction sharpens this prediction: the caregiver’s own victimization history is separable from documented maltreatment of the index child. The present study focuses on the former, capturing a distal intergenerational exposure that shapes the caregiving environment without necessarily crossing the threshold for CPS involvement. By isolating caregiver history from the child’s own abuse experiences, we can test whether intergenerational risk registers through early behavioral levels. On this basis, we hypothesize:

H1: Caregiver childhood victimization history predicts membership in elevated (vs. Low-Low) joint trajectory categories, after controlling for child sex and race/ethnicity.

H2: This intergenerational effect operates primarily through elevated initial levels of behavioral problems (intercepts) rather than through altered rates of developmental change (slopes), consistent with an early programming mechanism.

Developmental Trajectories of Behavioral Problems

If caregiver victimization elevates offspring behavioral levels from an early age, the next question is whether these elevated levels unfold along distinguishable developmental paths. Longitudinal studies using GBTM, growth mixture modeling, and latent class growth analysis have repeatedly shown that children do not follow a single normative course of behavioral development (Cicchetti and Toth 2016; Nagin 2005; D. Nagin and Odgers 2010). For externalizing behavior, the most replicated distinction is between a life-course-persistent group (approximately 5–10% of the population) characterized by chronic antisocial behavior from childhood through adulthood and an adolescence-limited group that desists after the teenage years (Moffitt 1993). For internalizing problems, the evidence is less consistent, but chronic-high, increasing, decreasing, and low-stable subgroups have been identified across childhood and adolescence (Miller et al. 2020; Wang et al. 2016). Across both domains, a minority of children follow chronically elevated or escalating trajectories that are strongly predictive of later psychopathology, criminal behavior, substance use disorders, and poor physical health (Vergunst et al. 2022; Latham et al. 2020).

These trajectory subgroups gain additional explanatory power when internalizing and externalizing problems are modeled jointly rather than separately. Children with high externalizing problems frequently show elevated internalizing symptoms as well, and vice versa. Dual-trajectory or joint-trajectory GBTM addresses this co-occurrence by estimating latent classes that simultaneously capture patterns in both domains (D. S. Nagin and Odgers 2010; Jones and Nagin 2007; Dugré et al. 2019). This approach can reveal subgroups, such as children with co-occurring escalation across both internalizing and externalizing problems, that would be invisible in domain-specific models. Joint trajectory groups may carry greater predictive validity for adult outcomes than single-domain groups because they capture the breadth of behavioral maladjustment rather than its domain-specific manifestation (Nivard et al. 2016).

Early adversity, including maltreatment, poverty, and household instability, has been identified as a distal predictor of membership in high-risk trajectory subgroups (Wong et al. 2020; Pei 2024; Evangelist et al. 2023). Yet few studies have tested whether caregiver victimization history, as distinct from the child’s own maltreatment experience, predicts joint trajectory membership. This gap is consequential because caregiver history represents a pre-birth risk factor that could inform preventive screening before behavioral problems emerge. If caregiver victimization sorts children into elevated joint trajectory categories, these categories would constitute a developmental pathway through which intergenerational risk is transmitted to adult psychiatric outcomes. Trajectory membership would then function as a mediator: the mechanism by which a distal family-level exposure is carried forward across 14 years of development and deposited in disorder-specific symptomatology at age 18. We therefore hypothesize:

H3: Joint trajectory category membership mediates the association between caregiver victimization and age-18 psychiatric symptom counts, such that the indirect effect through trajectory membership is statistically significant.

From Behavioral Trajectories to Adult Psychiatric Outcomes

The mediation hypothesis tested above implies that joint trajectory categories not only receive intergenerational risk but also transmit it forward to adult psychiatric outcomes. A key empirical question is whether this transmission is domain-specific or domain-general. If children in chronic-high or escalating trajectory groups develop symptoms across multiple psychiatric domains, including both internalizing disorders (depression, anxiety, PTSD) and externalizing problems (substance use), the trajectory itself functions as a developmental vehicle for broad psychiatric vulnerability. If, by contrast, trajectory groups predict only domain-congruent outcomes (e.g., high externalizing trajectories predicting substance use but not depression), a specificity model would be supported.

Existing evidence favors the domain-general account. Chronic externalizing trajectories have been linked not only to antisocial outcomes but also to depression and anxiety in adulthood (Moffitt 1993; Duprey et al. 2020). Chronic internalizing trajectories predict substance use in some studies, not only mood and anxiety disorders (Fitzgerald 2021; Mason et al. 2022). This cross-domain prediction is consistent with a developmental cascade framework (Masten 2005; Masten and Barnes 2018) in which behavioral problems in one domain set off secondary consequences, including peer rejection, academic failure, and family conflict, that amplify risk across domains over time. Within this framework, joint trajectory categories that capture co-occurring elevation on both internalizing and externalizing domains should exhibit the broadest pattern of adult psychiatric risk, because co-occurrence signals the operation of cascading processes across both behavioral domains simultaneously. We hypothesize:

H4: Elevated joint trajectory categories are associated with higher symptom counts across multiple psychiatric domains at age 18, with the Comorbid High category (co-occurring elevation on both domains) exhibiting the broadest pattern of risk.

The Present Study

Drawing on the literatures reviewed above, we propose a developmental cascade model in which caregiver childhood victimization history shapes the child’s joint internalizing–externalizing trajectory from ages 4 to 16, which in turn predicts disorder-specific symptom counts at age 18 (see Figure 1). The four hypotheses derived from each strand of the literature (H1–H4) are summarized above and tested sequentially through latent growth modeling (H2), dual GBTM with multinomial regression (H1), Poisson outcome models (H4), and KHB mediation decomposition (H3).

The LONGSCAN consortium offers several strengths for this investigation: prospective multi-site design with geographic and demographic diversity, deliberate oversampling of children at risk for maltreatment, repeated CBCL assessments spanning 12 years (ages 4 through 16), and structured DISC-IV-based diagnostic interviews at age 18 (Runyan et al. 1998). By using raw CBCL scores rather than age-normed T-scores, we preserve actual developmental variation across age, which is essential for accurate trajectory estimation. An important methodological consideration guides this choice: T-scores are standardized against age-specific norms, which means they deliberately remove normative developmental trends from the data. While T-scores are appropriate for identifying clinical caseness at a given age, they are inappropriate for trajectory modeling because the very age trends that trajectories are designed to capture have already been statistically removed by the norming process. Raw scores, by contrast, retain the full developmental signal, allowing the GBTM to detect genuine increases, decreases, or stability in behavioral problem levels across age.

This study extends prior work in three ways. First, whereas most intergenerational transmission studies examine single-domain behavioral outcomes at one or two time points, we model the joint developmental course of internalizing and externalizing problems across six assessment waves using dual GBTM. Second, we connect caregiver-level risk (victimization history) to child-level developmental processes (trajectory groups) to individual-level adult outcomes (psychiatric symptoms) within a single analytic framework, testing formal mediation through KHB decomposition. Third, by using Stata’s khb command for the mediation analysis rather than ad hoc bootstrapping approaches, we ensure that the decomposition properly corrects for the confounding-rescaling bias that can arise in comparisons across nested models.

Data and Methods

Data

Data come from the Longitudinal Studies of Child Abuse and Neglect (LONGSCAN), a consortium of five prospective cohort studies conducted across geographically diverse U.S. sites: Eastern (EA; North Carolina), Midwestern (MW; Illinois/Iowa), Northwestern (NW; Washington State), Southern (SO; Alabama), and Southwestern (SW; California) (Runyan et al. 1998). Children were enrolled between birth and age 4 and followed through age 18, with structured assessments at ages 4, 6, 8, 12, 14, 16, and 18. The sampling design deliberately overrepresented children at risk for maltreatment: the Eastern site recruited children reported to CPS by age 4, the Midwestern and Southern sites recruited from low-income families, the Southwestern site enrolled children in foster care, and the Northwestern site recruited a mixed community/CPS sample. The pooled dataset therefore captures substantial heterogeneity in both risk exposure and demographic composition, strengthening generalizability within the population of high-risk children while limiting generalizability to the broader population.

The initial pooled sample comprised N = 1{,}354 children with at least one CBCL assessment across the six included waves (ages 4, 6, 8, 12, 14, and 16; wave 10 was excluded due to sparse coverage at several sites). To ensure sufficient data for trajectory estimation, we excluded cases with fewer than three available CBCL assessment waves on both the internalizing and externalizing subscales, yielding an analytic sample of N = 1{,}197 (88.5% retention). Excluded cases (n = 157) did not differ significantly from retained cases on child sex (\chi^2 < 1) but were more likely to be from the Southwestern site, which experienced higher overall attrition.

Measures

Caregiver Victimization History

The primary caregiver (typically the biological mother) reported on her own childhood and adolescent victimization experiences at the baseline assessment using a structured interview (LONGSCAN form VICA0404). Eight binary items (yes/no) assessed whether the caregiver had experienced: (1) physical abuse as a child, (2) excessive physical punishment as a child, (3) sexual fondling before age 13, (4) forced fondling before age 13, (5) sexual intercourse before age 13, (6) sexual fondling during adolescence, (7) forced fondling during adolescence, and (8) sexual intercourse during adolescence. Items assessing adult victimization experiences (physical assault, sexual assault as an adult) were excluded to maintain focus on developmental-period exposures that are theoretically most relevant to attachment disruption and social learning mechanisms of intergenerational transmission. A cumulative victimization count (range 0–8) was computed by summing endorsed items. Higher scores reflect exposure to a greater number of victimization types during the caregiver’s own childhood and adolescence.

Child Behavior Checklist (CBCL)

The CBCL/4-18 (Achenbach 1991) was administered to the primary caregiver at ages 4, 6, 8, 12, 14, and 16 (wave 10, corresponding to approximately age 10, was excluded due to sparse coverage at several sites). We used raw scores for the Internalizing broadband scale (sum of Anxious/Depressed, Withdrawn, and Somatic Complaints syndrome scales) and Externalizing broadband scale (sum of Aggressive Behavior and Rule-Breaking/Delinquent Behavior syndrome scales). Raw scores were chosen over age-normed T-scores because T-scores are standardized against age-specific norms and thereby remove normative developmental trends (Achenbach et al. 2024). When the goal is to model within-person change across age, as in trajectory modeling, this age-norming creates a ceiling on observable developmental variation and can mask genuine increases or decreases in problem behavior (Achenbach 1991; Deighton et al. 2014). Raw scores preserve the actual developmental signal.

Age-18 Psychopathology Outcomes

At the age-18 assessment, participants completed the Diagnostic Interview Schedule for Children, Version IV (DISC-IV), a highly structured respondent-based interview designed for use by trained lay interviewers. The DISC-IV assesses DSM-IV diagnostic criteria for a range of childhood and adolescent psychiatric disorders and has demonstrated acceptable test-retest reliability and criterion validity in community and clinical samples (Newbury et al. 2018). Five modules were administered: major depressive disorder (MDD; 20 symptom items), generalized anxiety disorder (GAD; 30 items), posttraumatic stress disorder (PTSD; 42 items), panic disorder (19 items), and substance use disorders (69 items covering alcohol, marijuana, and other substances). For each disorder, symptom counts were computed by summing endorsed screening and diagnostic items. These continuous symptom counts were used as outcome variables rather than binary diagnostic categories for two reasons: first, continuous measures preserve statistical power in samples where diagnostic prevalence may be low; second, symptom counts capture subthreshold symptomatology that is clinically meaningful but falls below formal diagnostic thresholds, a consideration that is particularly relevant in a young adult sample where many disorders are still emerging.

Covariates

Covariates included child sex (coded 1 = male, 0 = female), child race/ethnicity (dummy-coded as Black, Hispanic, and Other, with White as reference category), and study site. These covariates were selected based on prior LONGSCAN research documenting sex and racial/ethnic differences in both CBCL trajectories and age-18 psychiatric outcomes (Stewart et al. 2024; Fluke et al. 2011). Boys tend to exhibit higher externalizing and lower internalizing problems than girls across childhood, while racial/ethnic differences in CBCL scores have been documented in LONGSCAN and other high-risk samples, partly reflecting measurement bias and partly reflecting differential exposure to neighborhood-level stressors. Study site was included to account for systematic differences in sampling strategy and regional context across the five LONGSCAN sites. Family socioeconomic indicators (caregiver education, family income) were considered but not included in final models due to collinearity with site and race/ethnicity in this sample, as sites differed substantially in their socioeconomic composition.

Missing Data

Missing data on CBCL waves and covariates were handled by predictive mean matching (PMM) imputation using the missRanger package in R with k = 5 nearest-neighbor donors and 100 trees per variable. Imputation was performed on the wide-format dataset (one row per child) prior to analysis, following the recommended sequence of first filtering the sample (retaining only cases with \geq 3 waves) and then imputing remaining missing values (Nagin 2005). This approach preserves the joint distribution of variables more effectively than casewise deletion and reduces bias from non-random missingness patterns associated with site-level differences in assessment completion rates. Age-18 outcome variables were not imputed; analyses involving these outcomes used available cases.

Analytic Strategy

The analytic strategy proceeded in four stages, following the sequential procedure recommended by Nagin (2005) for dual-trajectory GBTM.

Stage 1: Latent Growth Model

Unconditional and conditional parallel-process latent growth models (LGM) were fit in lavaan (Rosseel 2012) using full-information maximum likelihood (FIML) estimation. Linear and quadratic specifications were compared via BIC to determine the optimal polynomial order for each domain. Time was coded so that age 4 = 0 and age 16 = 12. The conditional model regressed intercept and slope factors on caregiver victimization count and child sex to test whether intergenerational risk operates through initial levels (intercept), rates of change (slope), or both. Correlations between internalizing and externalizing growth factors were freely estimated.

Stage 2: Separate GBTM for Each Domain

To determine the optimal number of latent classes for each domain, separate GBTMs were fit for internalizing and externalizing raw scores with 1 to 5 groups using Stata’s traj command with censored normal (CNORM) distributions. Model selection criteria included BIC (lower is better), average posterior probability of correct classification (> .70), and minimum group size (\geq 5% of the sample). The polynomial order determined in Stage 1 was used for the mixture components.

Stage 3: Dual-Trajectory GBTM

Separate three-class GBTMs were estimated for internalizing and externalizing raw scores using Stata’s traj command, which fits censored normal (CNORM) mixture models with polynomial time specifications (Jones and Nagin 2007; Nagin et al. 2024). Each child received two independent group assignments (one per domain). The resulting 3 \times 3 cross-tabulation of domain-specific assignments was then collapsed into five substantively meaningful joint trajectory categories based on the empirical trajectory profiles: Low-Low, Moderate, Elevated Both, High Unilateral, and Comorbid High. This dual independent procedure avoids imposing a single latent class structure on two correlated but distinct behavioral domains and allows the joint distribution of internalizing and externalizing trajectories to emerge from the data.

Stage 4: Outcome and Mediation Models

To test H1, multinomial logistic regression predicted joint trajectory category membership from caregiver victimization count, controlling for child sex and race/ethnicity, with the Low-Low group as the reference category. To test H4, Poisson regression models with robust standard errors predicted each age-18 symptom count from joint trajectory category membership and covariates (caregiver victimization, child sex, race/ethnicity), yielding incidence rate ratios (IRR) for each elevated group relative to the Low-Low reference. To test H3, Karlson–Holm–Breen (KHB) decomposition using OLS models separated the total effect of caregiver victimization on each outcome into direct and indirect (through joint trajectory category) components. The KHB method corrects for the rescaling bias that can arise in decompositions involving categorical mediators (Imai et al. 2010). The disentangle option was used to quantify the contribution of each trajectory category to the total indirect effect.

Results

Descriptive Statistics

Table [tab:descriptives] presents sample characteristics. The analytic sample (N = 1{,}197) was 48.5% male and racially/ethnically diverse: 56.7% Black, 26.2% White, 4.3% Hispanic, and 12.8% other race/ethnicity. This composition reflects the LONGSCAN sampling strategy, which overrepresented minority children in high-risk communities. Approximately two-thirds of caregivers (65.9%) reported at least one childhood or adolescent victimization experience, with a mean of 1.33 types of victimization endorsed (SD = 1.72, range 0–8). Physical abuse and excessive punishment were the most commonly endorsed items, followed by sexual victimization experiences before age 13 and during adolescence. Approximately 15% of caregivers endorsed three or more types of victimization, indicating a subgroup with poly-victimization histories.

Mean raw internalizing scores were relatively stable across assessment waves, ranging from 5.1 (SD = 5.0) at age 4 to 6.9 (SD = 6.4) at age 8, before leveling off through age 16. Mean raw externalizing scores showed a normative decline from 13.1 (SD = 8.7) at age 4 to 10.9 (SD = 10.4) at age 16, consistent with population-level trends toward lower externalizing behavior across childhood and adolescence. At age 18, mean symptom counts were 4.24 (SD = 4.38) for MDD, 1.42 (SD = 2.35) for GAD, 1.72 (SD = 3.89) for PTSD, 0.44 (SD = 0.98) for panic disorder, and 0.24 (SD = 0.98) for substance use disorders. These distributions were right-skewed, reflecting the typical pattern of symptom endorsement in community samples.

Latent Growth Model

Quadratic specifications fit substantially better than linear models for both internalizing (BIC = 44{,}576 vs. 44{,}726; CFI = .982 vs. .915) and externalizing (BIC = 49{,}426 vs. 49{,}464; CFI = .989 vs. .972) raw scores. The quadratic terms captured the non-linear developmental course of both domains: internalizing scores rose slightly from ages 4 to 8 before stabilizing, while externalizing scores declined across the full age range but at a decelerating rate.

In the conditional parallel-process LGM (S1), caregiver victimization count significantly predicted the internalizing intercept (B = 0.34, SE = 0.06, p < .001) and the externalizing intercept (B = 0.73, SE = 0.11, p < .001). Each additional type of caregiver victimization was associated with 0.34 additional raw internalizing points and 0.73 additional raw externalizing points at the age-4 baseline. The internalizing slope showed a marginal association with caregiver victimization (B = 0.018, p = .026), suggesting a slight tendency for children of more severely victimized caregivers to show steeper internalizing increases over time. The externalizing slope was not associated with caregiver victimization (B = -0.002, p = .851). These results indicate that intergenerational risk primarily operates through elevated starting levels of behavioral problems, established before the first assessment at age 4, rather than through accelerated growth or declining desistance over time, supporting H2.

Dual Group-Based Trajectory Model

Following the LGM results, quadratic polynomial specifications were used for both domains. Separate single-domain GBTMs were first fit for internalizing and externalizing raw scores with 1 to 5 groups each (S2). For both domains, BIC decreased monotonically across solutions. The elbow in BIC improvement occurred at k = 3 for internalizing (\Delta\text{BIC}_{3 \text{ vs } 2} = 271; \Delta\text{BIC}_{4 \text{ vs } 3} = 107) and at k = 3 for externalizing (\Delta\text{BIC}_{3 \text{ vs } 2} = 347; \Delta\text{BIC}_{4 \text{ vs } 3} = 94). Three-group solutions were selected for both domains, and a dual-trajectory GBTM was then estimated using Stata’s traj command with k_{\text{INT}} = 3 \times k_{\text{EXT}} = 3 (Nagin 2005; Jones and Nagin 2007).

The three internalizing trajectory groups (Figure 2, left panel) were: Low-Stable (n = 668, 55.8%; M = 3.1–4.0 across waves), Moderate-Rising (n = 456, 38.1%; M = 7.0 \rightarrow 11.7), and High-Chronic (n = 73, 6.1%; M = 11.1 \rightarrow 24.6). The three externalizing groups (right panel) were: Low-Declining (n = 608, 50.8%; M = 8.6 \rightarrow 5.1), Moderate-Stable (n = 450, 37.6%; M = 14.4–16.3), and High-Chronic (n = 139, 11.6%; M = 23.1 \rightarrow 31.8). All groups exceeded the 5% minimum size threshold.

Cross-tabulating the 3 \times 3 domain-specific assignments yielded nine joint trajectory cells, which were collapsed into five substantively meaningful categories: Low-Low (n = 574, 48.0%; low on both domains), Moderate (n = 127, 10.6%; elevated on one domain only at moderate levels), Elevated Both (n = 338, 28.2%; moderate-level elevation on both domains), High Unilateral (n = 104, 8.7%; high on one domain but not both), and Comorbid High (n = 54, 4.5%; high on both domains simultaneously).

Caregiver Victimization and Trajectory Group Membership

Multinomial logistic regression (Table [tab:multinomial]) showed that caregiver childhood victimization count significantly predicted membership in three of four elevated joint trajectory groups relative to the Low-Low reference, providing strong support for H1. Each additional type of victimization increased the relative risk of membership in the Elevated Both group (\text{RRR} = 1.25, p < .001), the High Unilateral group (\text{RRR} = 1.26, p < .001), and the Comorbid High group (\text{RRR} = 1.18, p < .05). The Moderate group was not significantly predicted by victimization count. The dose-response pattern across groups is consistent with a cumulative risk model: higher levels of caregiver victimization are associated with progressively more severe joint trajectory profiles.

Trajectory Groups and Young Adult Psychopathology

Poisson regression with robust standard errors (Table [tab:outcomes]; Figure 3) revealed a clear gradient of psychiatric risk across joint trajectory groups. Relative to the Low-Low reference, the Comorbid High group showed the largest and broadest elevations: MDD (\text{IRR} = 1.72, p < .001), GAD (\text{IRR} = 1.69, p = .005), panic (\text{IRR} = 2.58, p < .001), and substance use (\text{IRR} = 2.95, p = .003). The Elevated Both group showed significant elevations on MDD (\text{IRR} = 1.33, p < .001), PTSD (\text{IRR} = 1.54, p = .002), panic (\text{IRR} = 1.67, p < .001), and substance use (\text{IRR} = 1.77, p = .034). The High Unilateral group predicted elevated MDD (\text{IRR} = 1.35, p < .001), panic (\text{IRR} = 1.85, p = .004), and substance use (\text{IRR} = 2.56, p = .003). The Moderate group showed circumscribed elevations on panic (\text{IRR} = 1.56, p = .021) and substance use (\text{IRR} = 2.81, p = .001) only.

These results support H4: the Comorbid High group, characterized by chronic elevation across both internalizing and externalizing domains, was associated with the broadest pattern of elevated psychopathology at age 18. The gradient from Low-Low through Moderate, Elevated Both, High Unilateral, to Comorbid High reflects increasing severity and breadth of adult psychiatric outcomes as a function of the joint behavioral trajectory profile.

Mediation Analysis

KHB decomposition (Table [tab:mediation]) decomposed the total effect of caregiver victimization on each outcome into direct and indirect (through joint trajectory group membership) components. The indirect effect was statistically significant for all five psychiatric outcomes: MDD (indirect = 0.069, p < .001), GAD (indirect = 0.020, p = .019), PTSD (indirect = 0.045, p = .002), panic (indirect = 0.013, p = .001), and substance use (indirect = 0.008, p = .039). These results provide strong support for H3.

The direct effect of caregiver victimization was not significant for MDD, GAD, panic, or substance use after accounting for trajectory group membership, indicating that the intergenerational effect operates primarily through the behavioral trajectory pathway. For PTSD, a significant negative direct effect remained (B = -0.136, p < .05), suggesting that trajectory membership mediates more than the total effect (a suppression pattern in which the trajectory pathway and the direct pathway operate in opposite directions).

The disentangle component revealed that no single joint group dominated the indirect effect uniformly. The Elevated Both group contributed the largest share for MDD (41.8%) and PTSD (53.1%). The High Unilateral group contributed the most for substance use (48.0%). For GAD and panic, the contributions were more evenly distributed across the Elevated Both, High Unilateral, and Comorbid High groups (27–34% each). This pattern indicates that different joint trajectory configurations carry intergenerational risk for different psychiatric domains, consistent with the multifinality principle in developmental psychopathology.

Sensitivity Analyses

Three sets of sensitivity analyses confirmed the robustness of the main findings (S3–S5). First, negative binomial models were estimated to evaluate sensitivity to overdispersion (S3). IRR point estimates were similar across Poisson and negative binomial specifications; substance use became significant under the negative binomial model (\hat\alpha = 8.42), supporting the use of robust Poisson estimation in the main analyses. Second, replacing the victimization count with a binary indicator (any vs. none; 54.6% endorsed) yielded significant multinomial associations with three of the four non-reference trajectory groups and significant KHB indirect effects for all five outcomes (S4). Third, sex-stratified Poisson models (S5) showed that among boys, the elevated trajectory groups predicted symptoms across multiple outcomes, while among girls the pattern was narrower, consistent with sex differences in the psychiatric expression of behavioral trajectory risk.

Discussion

This study used dual group-based trajectory models with raw CBCL scores to trace the developmental course of internalizing and externalizing behavior problems from ages 4 to 16, cross-tabulated the resulting domain-specific group assignments into five joint trajectory categories, and tested whether these categories mediate the intergenerational transmission of maltreatment to young adult psychopathology. Three principal findings warrant discussion.

Intergenerational Risk Operates Through Initial Levels

The LGM results revealed that caregiver victimization history predicted elevated intercepts for both internalizing and externalizing raw scores but did not meaningfully predict externalizing slopes. This pattern indicates that intergenerational risk is largely established before the first CBCL assessment at age 4 rather than accumulating progressively across childhood. Children of maltreated caregivers enter the study period with higher behavioral problem levels, and these initial differences persist without widening further over the subsequent 12 years of observation. This finding aligns with stress sensitization models positing that early caregiving environments shape neurobiological stress-response systems during sensitive periods of brain development (McLaughlin et al. 2014; Teicher and Samson 2016; Kurbatfinski et al. 2024). Disrupted attachment, inconsistent discipline, and impaired co-regulation in the first years of life may “program” elevated behavioral baselines that are difficult to reverse once established (Beeghly 2024; Javakhishvili and Widom 2021).

The practical implication is that the window for interrupting intergenerational transmission may close early. Interventions targeting parenting quality, co-regulation skills, and caregiver mental health should begin before school entry, and ideally before age 4, if they are to prevent the consolidation of elevated behavioral baselines. Programs such as Child-Parent Psychotherapy and the Nurse-Family Partnership, which intervene during pregnancy or infancy, may be best positioned to interrupt this early transmission pathway (Mejdoubi et al. 2015; Ahmed and Shaban 2025). The marginal significance of the internalizing slope effect (p = .026) raises the possibility that internalizing problems may continue to accumulate beyond age 4 in children of maltreated caregivers, warranting further investigation with larger samples and more frequent early assessments.

Joint Behavioral Trajectories as a Developmental Conduit

The cross-tabulation of domain-specific trajectory assignments yielded five joint categories: Low-Low (48.0%), Moderate (10.6%), Elevated Both (28.2%), High Unilateral (8.7%), and Comorbid High (4.5%). This approach extends prior single-domain trajectory research by capturing co-occurring patterns across internalizing and externalizing domains without forcing both domains into a single latent class structure. The Comorbid High group is particularly noteworthy: comprising only 4.5% of the sample, these children showed chronic elevation on both internalizing and externalizing domains and exhibited the broadest pattern of psychiatric risk at age 18. The Elevated Both group (28.2%) showed moderate-level elevation across both domains and contributed the largest share of the mediated effect for MDD and PTSD. The High Unilateral group (8.7%), characterized by high levels on one domain but not the other, showed a distinct risk profile concentrated in panic and substance use disorders. This differentiation across joint categories may reflect a self-reinforcing developmental cascade (Masten 2005; Tein et al. 2023) in which co-occurring problems across both domains generate secondary consequences, including peer rejection, academic failure, family conflict, and reduced access to protective resources, that amplify risk for a broader range of adult psychiatric outcomes than domain-specific elevation alone.

The OLS-based KHB decomposition provided a formal test of the mediation hypothesis. The indirect effect through joint trajectory category membership was statistically significant for all five psychiatric outcomes (MDD p < .001; GAD p = .019; PTSD p = .002; panic p = .001; substance use p = .039), indicating that joint behavioral trajectories mediate the intergenerational transmission of risk across the full spectrum of assessed disorders. This broad mediation pattern is consistent with a developmental cascade model in which co-occurring behavioral problems generate secondary consequences, including peer rejection, academic failure, and cumulative stress, that elevate risk for multiple forms of adult psychopathology rather than a single disorder domain (Masten 2005).

The direct effect of caregiver victimization was not significant for MDD, GAD, panic, or substance use after accounting for trajectory category membership, indicating that, in this sample, behavioral trajectories represent the primary developmental conduit through which intergenerational risk reaches adult psychiatric symptomatology. For PTSD, a significant negative direct effect remained, suggesting a suppression pattern in which the trajectory pathway and the direct pathway operate in opposite directions. This near-full-mediation pattern implies that caregiver victimization does not exert a substantial residual influence on offspring psychiatric outcomes beyond its role in shaping the trajectory of behavioral development. However, this finding should be interpreted cautiously for several reasons. First, non-significance of the direct effect may partly reflect limited statistical power given the modest effect sizes involved. Second, unmeasured mediators such as ongoing maltreatment of the index child, parenting quality, household chaos, peer victimization, and neighborhood context may operate through channels not captured by CBCL trajectory categories. Third, the CBCL mediator and the victimization predictor share the same informant (the primary caregiver), which could inflate the apparent mediation effect. Future studies incorporating multiple mediators simultaneously, multi-informant measurement, and genetically informed designs could clarify the relative contribution of behavioral trajectories versus alternative mediating pathways.

The disentangle component of the KHB decomposition revealed that no single joint category dominated the indirect effect uniformly. The Elevated Both group contributed the largest share for MDD (41.8%) and PTSD (53.1%), while the High Unilateral group contributed the most for substance use (48.0%). For GAD and panic, the contributions were more evenly distributed across the Elevated Both, High Unilateral, and Comorbid High groups (27–34% each). This pattern indicates that different joint trajectory configurations carry intergenerational risk for different psychiatric domains, consistent with the multifinality principle in developmental psychopathology. Children with co-occurring moderate elevation across both domains may be at particular risk for mood-related outcomes, whereas those with pronounced elevation on a single domain may be more vulnerable to substance-related disorders.

Differential Associations with Psychiatric Domains

The four elevated joint trajectory categories showed differentiated patterns of association with age-18 psychiatric outcomes, carrying implications for understanding how developmental trajectory profiles translate into adult risk. The Comorbid High group predicted elevated symptoms across the broadest range of outcomes (MDD, GAD, panic, and substance use), consistent with a transdiagnostic vulnerability model (Choate et al. 2022) in which chronic co-occurring elevation across both internalizing and externalizing domains reflects deep-seated emotion dysregulation that predisposes to multiple forms of psychopathology. The Elevated Both group showed a partially overlapping but distinct risk profile (MDD, PTSD, panic, and substance use), suggesting that moderate-level co-occurrence carries prognostic significance even when behavioral levels do not reach the highest severity. The High Unilateral group, elevated on one domain but not both, predicted MDD, panic, and substance use, with particularly strong associations for substance use (\text{IRR} = 2.56). This pattern may reflect the role of domain-specific behavioral elevation, whether predominantly externalizing or predominantly internalizing, in channeling risk toward substance-related outcomes through pathways involving impulsivity, peer deviance, or self-medication.

The Moderate group showed the most circumscribed risk profile, with significant elevations limited to panic and substance use. This pattern suggests that mild elevation on a single domain carries less prognostic weight for internalizing disorders such as depression and anxiety but may nonetheless confer vulnerability to externalizing-spectrum outcomes. The finding that all four elevated groups predicted substance use is noteworthy given that substance use disorders at age 18 showed the lowest base rate of any outcome (M = 0.24). This broad association may reflect shared underlying vulnerabilities, including impulsivity, sensation-seeking, and poor self-regulation, that are captured by both internalizing and externalizing trajectory elevations. The DISC-IV assessment at age 18 may still be too early to capture the full emergence of substance use disorders, which often onset in the early-to-mid 20s. Longitudinal follow-up through the mid-20s could clarify whether the differentiated risk profiles observed here persist or converge as these disorders become more prevalent (Thurston et al. 2023).

Comparison with Prior LONGSCAN Research

The present findings extend and complement prior trajectory research using the LONGSCAN dataset. Stargel et al. (2022) examined trajectories of emotion dysregulation in LONGSCAN and found that caregiver maltreatment history predicted children’s emotional functioning, though that study did not extend the chain to adult psychiatric outcomes. Our study builds on this work by demonstrating that the intergenerational effect operates through specific joint trajectory categories and terminates in measurable psychiatric symptomatology at age 18. The use of dual GBTM with cross-tabulated joint categories represents a methodological advance over single-domain approaches, as it captures the joint distribution of internalizing and externalizing trajectories and reveals clinically meaningful subgroups, such as the Elevated Both and Comorbid High categories, that would not be identifiable in domain-specific analyses.

Our finding that caregiver victimization predicts elevated intercepts but not slopes is consistent with the broader developmental literature on early adversity and behavioral trajectories. Studies using the National Institute of Child Health and Human Development (NICHD) Study of Early Child Care have reported similar patterns for maternal sensitivity and child externalizing behavior: early caregiving quality sets the initial level of behavioral problems, with subsequent developmental change driven more by peer, school, and neighborhood factors than by continued family-of-origin effects (Pei 2024; Sager et al. 2024). This “early programming” pattern has practical implications: it suggests that the most effective point of intervention is before school entry, when behavioral baselines are being established, rather than during middle childhood or adolescence when the trajectory has already been set in motion.

Limitations and Future Directions

Several limitations warrant acknowledgment. First, caregiver victimization history was assessed retrospectively by self-report, introducing the possibility of recall bias, social desirability effects, and systematic underreporting of sensitive experiences. The binary item format (yes/no for each abuse type) does not capture severity, frequency, chronicity, or developmental timing of the caregiver’s experiences (Baldwin et al. 2019). More nuanced measurement of caregiver history, including validated instruments such as the Childhood Trauma Questionnaire, could improve predictive precision.

Second, the LONGSCAN sample was deliberately enriched for maltreatment risk, and the distribution of joint trajectory categories (48.0% Low-Low, 28.2% Elevated Both, 10.6% Moderate, 8.7% High Unilateral, 4.5% Comorbid High) may differ from what would be observed in population-representative cohorts. The elevated base rates of behavioral problems and victimization exposure in this sample may attenuate effect sizes relative to lower-risk populations. Replication in population-representative cohorts is needed to establish the generalizability of these trajectory patterns and their associations with adult outcomes (Thurston et al. 2025; Blackwell et al. 2024).

Third, we did not model concurrent maltreatment of the index child, genetic confounds, or other plausible mediators such as parenting quality, peer relationships, and school engagement. The causal interpretation of the mediation analysis is therefore provisional. The KHB decomposition estimates associations consistent with mediation but cannot establish temporal precedence between the mediator (trajectory group membership, determined across ages 4–16) and the outcome (age-18 symptoms). Future studies employing genetically informed designs, experimental parenting interventions, or more intensive longitudinal measurement could strengthen causal inference (Scardera et al. 2024).

Fourth, the CBCL was completed by the primary caregiver at each wave, introducing shared-method variance between the victimization predictor (caregiver self-report) and the trajectory mediator (caregiver-reported child behavior). Multi-informant designs incorporating teacher reports, self-reports, and observational measures could address this limitation (Dovgan et al. 2019; Watts et al. 2021).

Fifth, although the five joint trajectory categories were derived from well-separated three-class solutions in each domain, the Comorbid High category (n = 54, 4.5%) was small. This limited statistical power for detecting associations with the most severely affected group, as reflected in the wider confidence intervals for the Comorbid High coefficients in the Poisson and multinomial models. Larger samples or pooled analyses across multiple cohorts could provide greater statistical leverage for characterizing the antecedents and consequences of small but clinically important trajectory subgroups.

Future research should pursue several directions. First, protective factors that buffer the intergenerational transmission pathway deserve attention. Caregiver mental health treatment, social support networks, stable co-parenting relationships, and access to high-quality early childhood education may moderate the link between caregiver victimization and child behavioral trajectories (Panisch et al. 2020; Radey et al. 2022; Chang et al. 2025). Identifying such protective factors is essential for designing interventions that interrupt the intergenerational cycle without requiring reversal of the caregiver’s own trauma history.

Second, the identification of an Elevated Both group (28.2%), whose members show moderate-level elevation across both internalizing and externalizing domains, raises the question of what distinguishes these children from the Low-Low group. Potential explanations include cumulative exposure to new adversities (e.g., household moves, parental separation, community violence), the absence of age-appropriate coping resources, and neurobiological differences in stress reactivity that become more consequential as academic and social demands increase across middle childhood. The High Unilateral group (8.7%), elevated on one domain but not the other, presents a related puzzle: what factors channel risk into domain-specific rather than cross-domain elevation? Understanding the determinants of different joint trajectory configurations could inform targeted prevention strategies that address both the breadth and the domain-specificity of behavioral risk.

Third, replication with population-representative samples and multi-informant behavioral assessment (combining parent, teacher, and child self-report) would strengthen confidence in the trajectory categories identified here. The LONGSCAN sample’s enrichment for maltreatment risk means that the joint trajectory distribution observed here (48.0% Low-Low, 28.2% Elevated Both, 10.6% Moderate, 8.7% High Unilateral, 4.5% Comorbid High) may differ from what would be found in lower-risk populations, where the proportion in the Low-Low category would likely be larger. Multi-informant approaches would also address the shared-method variance concern inherent in using caregiver-reported victimization and caregiver-reported child behavior within the same analytic model.

Conclusion

This study makes three contributions to the literature on intergenerational maltreatment transmission and developmental psychopathology. First, using dual GBTM with raw CBCL scores spanning ages 4 through 16, we identified three trajectory groups in each domain and cross-tabulated them into five joint categories in a high-risk prospective cohort, including an Elevated Both category (28.2%) with moderate co-occurring elevation across both domains, a High Unilateral category (8.7%) with high levels on one domain, and a Comorbid High category (4.5%) with chronic elevation on both internalizing and externalizing domains. Second, we demonstrated that caregiver childhood victimization history prospectively predicts membership in the Elevated Both, High Unilateral, and Comorbid High trajectory categories, extending the intergenerational transmission literature from static outcomes to developmental processes. Third, KHB decomposition confirmed that trajectory category membership mediates the association between caregiver victimization and age-18 symptoms across all five psychiatric outcomes, with the Elevated Both group contributing the largest share for MDD and PTSD and the High Unilateral group contributing most for substance use, establishing joint behavioral trajectories as a primary developmental conduit for intergenerational psychiatric risk.

These findings carry direct implications for prevention and early intervention at multiple levels. At the screening level, the observation that intergenerational risk operates primarily through initial behavioral levels, established before the first assessment at age 4, underscores the importance of identifying at-risk families during the perinatal period or early infancy. Screening caregiver victimization history at the point of contact with child welfare, pediatric, or obstetric services could identify families at elevated intergenerational risk before the child’s behavioral trajectory has begun to consolidate. Such screening is low-cost, non-invasive, and could be integrated into existing intake protocols at maternity wards, well-child visits, and CPS case openings.

At the intervention level, the finding that all four elevated trajectory categories predicted symptoms across multiple psychiatric domains, rather than a single disorder, argues for transdiagnostic rather than disorder-specific approaches. Evidence-based programs that target shared risk mechanisms, such as emotion dysregulation, parent-child interaction quality, and stress reactivity, may be better positioned to interrupt the developmental cascade than programs focused narrowly on externalizing behavior management or anxiety reduction alone. Programs such as the Incredible Years, Parent-Child Interaction Therapy (PCIT), and Child-Parent Psychotherapy (CPP) that address both parental functioning and child behavioral adjustment within a relational framework are particularly well-suited to this population (Lee et al. 2018; Featherston et al. 2024). The differentiation among joint trajectory categories suggests that monitoring systems capable of detecting early signs of co-occurring behavioral elevation, such as routine CBCL screening at pediatric visits, could trigger stepped-care interventions calibrated to both the severity and the cross-domain breadth of the child’s behavioral profile.

Understanding when and how behavioral trajectories diverge into distinct joint configurations, and what factors protect against co-occurring elevation in the presence of intergenerational risk, remains a priority for research aimed at breaking the cycle of maltreatment across generations.
