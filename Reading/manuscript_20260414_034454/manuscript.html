Selection on Trends or Social Causation? Living Arrangements and Health Among Older Adults Using Fixed Effects with Individual Slopes

[Authors]

Abstract

Objectives: Living arrangements are associated with health among older adults, yet observational estimates confound the effects of residential context with the health trajectories that drive residential selection. We applied Fixed Effects with Individual Slopes (FE-IS) models to disentangle social causation from trajectory-driven selection across three health domains.

Methods: We analyzed 28,770 respondents contributing 191,822 person-wave observations from the Health and Retirement Study (2000–2020). Living arrangements were classified into six categories: living alone (reference), with spouse only, with spouse and children, with children only, with others, and institutionalized. Outcomes included depressive symptoms (CES-D 8-item), self-rated health, and activities of daily living (ADL) limitations. We compared standard fixed effects and FE-IS estimates, with heterogeneity analyses by gender, race/ethnicity, and age group.

Results: After controlling for individual-specific health trajectories, all forms of coresidence reduced depressive symptoms relative to living alone, with spousal coresidence showing the largest effect (b = -0.433, p < .001). The apparent protective effects on self-rated health and ADL limitations disappeared under FE-IS: the spousal SRH coefficient reversed from +0.121 (p < .001) in standard FE to -0.031 (p < .05) in FE-IS, and the spousal ADL coefficient became nonsignificant. The depression-reducing effect was three to four times larger for men than for women, intensified with advancing age, and varied by race/ethnicity.

Discussion: Standard fixed effects estimates overstate the physical health benefits of coresidence by failing to account for differential health trajectories. The mental health benefits of shared living represent genuine social causation operating through emotional support and stress buffering, with the strongest effects among men and the oldest-old.

Introduction

One in four Americans aged 65 and older lives alone, and that share has risen steadily over the past three decades. Whether solo living harms health or simply reflects the trajectories of those who end up alone remains one of the most consequential unanswered questions in social gerontology. The distinction matters: if living arrangements causally affect health, then housing and social policy can intervene; if the association is driven by selection, then policy efforts directed at living arrangements will miss their target.

A large body of observational research links living alone to elevated depression, worse self-rated health, and greater functional limitations among older adults (Nakou et al. 2025; Safieddine et al. 2025; Hughes and Waite 2002; Ng et al. 2020). Coresidence with a spouse appears consistently protective, while living with adult children produces more mixed results that vary by cultural context and the circumstances prompting the arrangement (Caputo and Cagney 2023; Yahirun et al. 2018). These findings, however, rest on methods that cannot fully separate cause from selection. Cross-sectional designs conflate the effect of an arrangement with the characteristics of people who occupy it. Panel models with individual fixed effects improve on this by removing time-invariant confounders, but they fail when the confounders are time-varying in a specific way: when individuals differ not only in their baseline health levels but in their rates of health change. An older adult on a steeper decline is simultaneously more likely to transition from living alone to living with children and more likely to report worse health at the next observation, producing a spurious association that within-person differencing cannot remove (Rüttenauer and Ludwig 2023).

The Fixed Effects with Individual Slopes (FE-IS) estimator, developed by Wodtke (2018) and formalized by Rüttenauer and Ludwig (2023), addresses this problem by allowing each person their own linear time trend in the outcome. By absorbing individual-specific health trajectories, FE-IS separates the causal effect of a residential transition from the trajectory that may have precipitated it. This estimator has been applied to questions of marital wage premiums and neighborhood effects, but it has not been used to study living arrangements and health among older adults.

We address this gap using data from the Health and Retirement Study (HRS), a nationally representative panel of Americans aged 50 and older followed biennially from 2000 to 2020. We classify respondents into six living arrangement categories at each wave (living alone, with a spouse only, with a spouse and children, with children only, with others, and in institutional care) and examine three health outcomes spanning psychological, subjective, and functional domains: depressive symptoms (CES-D), self-rated health, and activities of daily living (ADL) limitations. We estimate both standard fixed effects and FE-IS models, allowing direct comparison of the bias introduced by ignoring individual health trajectories. We further test whether the living arrangement-health relationship varies by gender, race and ethnicity, and age group.

Three findings stand out. First, all forms of coresidence significantly reduce depressive symptoms relative to living alone in the FE-IS models, with spousal coresidence showing the largest effect (-0.433 on the 8-point CES-D scale, p < .001). This effect survives trajectory correction, supporting a genuine social causation interpretation for mental health. Second, the apparent protective effects of coresidence on self-rated health and functional limitations largely disappear or reverse under FE-IS – the spousal SRH coefficient flips from +0.121 (p < .001) in standard FE to -0.031 (p < .05) in FE-IS – exposing selection on health trends as the dominant force. Third, the depression-reducing benefit of coresidence is three to four times larger for men than for women, increases with age, and varies by race and ethnicity. These results provide the first application of FE-IS to the living arrangement-health literature, demonstrate that the causal effect of living arrangements on health is domain-specific, and document systematic heterogeneity that challenges one-size-fits-all policy prescriptions.

Background and Theoretical Framework

The Selection Problem in Living Arrangement Research

A large body of research documents associations between living arrangements and health among older adults. Persons who live alone report higher rates of depression, worse self-rated health, and greater functional limitations than those who share a household with a spouse or family members (Hughes and Waite 2002; Nakou et al. 2025; Safieddine et al. 2025). Social relations are among the most robust predictors of health and well-being across the life course (Antonucci et al. 2017), and living arrangements determine the proximity and density of daily social ties available to older adults (Berkman et al. 2000). These associations, however, are causally ambiguous. The central threat to inference is bidirectional: health shapes living arrangements just as living arrangements may shape health. An older adult whose functional capacity is declining may move in with adult children or enter institutional care precisely because of that decline, producing an association between coresidence and poor health that reflects reverse causality rather than a protective or harmful effect of the arrangement itself (Zimmer 2005; Zhang et al. 2023). A life course epidemiological perspective underscores that health in later life reflects cumulative exposures across the entire biography, and living arrangement transitions are embedded in these longer trajectories (Ben‐Shlomo 2002). Zhang et al. (2023) tested the social causation and social selection hypotheses simultaneously and found that both operate on older adults’ health outcomes, with selection forces dominating for self-rated health and causation forces approaching parity only for depression. Standard fixed effects models, which control for time-invariant individual characteristics, remain vulnerable to this problem when health trajectories differ across individuals: a person on a steeper decline is both more likely to change living arrangements and more likely to report worse health at any given time point, producing time-varying confounding that within-person differencing cannot remove (Rüttenauer and Ludwig 2023; Leszczensky and Wolbring 2019; Bell et al. 2018). Distinguishing genuine causal effects from trajectory-driven selection requires analytic strategies that account for heterogeneous individual health trends.

Theoretical Perspectives

The stress process model (Pearlin 1989) provides the foundational mechanism linking living arrangements to health. In this framework, social structural positions shape exposure to stressors and access to coping resources. Living arrangements constitute one such position: an older adult living alone faces isolation, sole responsibility for household maintenance, and the absence of immediate emotional support during health crises – stressors that co-residents help to manage. The model’s distinction between primary stressors (arising from structural conditions) and secondary stressors (proliferating through role strains) helps explain why coresidence with adult children may generate role conflict even while providing instrumental support (Caputo 2019). The framework predicts that living alone will elevate depression by increasing stressor exposure and depleting buffering resources.

What the stress process model leaves underspecified is the structural organization of the social ties through which support flows. The social convoy model (Antonucci et al. 2019) fills this gap by arranging an individual’s social ties in concentric layers ordered by emotional closeness. Co-resident household members occupy the innermost circle, where daily physical presence enables continuous exchanges of emotional support, companionship, and instrumental assistance (Berkman et al. 2000; Fuller et al. 2020). Berkman et al. (2000) identified four behavioral pathways through which these ties influence health: social support, social influence on health behaviors, social engagement, and access to material resources. Living alone compromises all four pathways, but psychological health, which responds directly to emotional engagement, should be more sensitive to residential isolation than physical health, which is driven by biological processes that social arrangements can influence only indirectly. The convoy model thus predicts that losing a spouse (innermost layer) should produce larger health effects than losing coresidence with adult children (a more peripheral layer).

Both frameworks, however, treat living arrangements as static positions rather than transitions embedded in longer biographical trajectories. The life course perspective adds this temporal dimension. Webster et al. (2022) integrated the convoy framework with the principle of linked lives: an older adult’s health trajectory is interconnected with the transitions of their spouse, children, and other household members, and living arrangement changes reflect these linked biographical sequences. Widowhood follows decades of marriage, coresidence with adult children may follow a parent’s health crisis, and institutionalization typically caps prolonged functional decline (Zimmer 2005; Ben‐Shlomo 2002). Individuals age along substantially different social network trajectories, with wide variation in the timing and magnitude of losses in personal network size (Tilburg 1998). This heterogeneity carries a direct methodological implication: if living arrangement transitions are embedded in individual health trajectories, then any method that fails to model those trajectories will conflate the effect of the transition with the pre-existing trend. The Fixed Effects with Individual Slopes (FE-IS) model operationalizes this insight by estimating each person’s idiosyncratic health trajectory, isolating the effect of living arrangement changes from the trajectory that may have precipitated them (Rüttenauer and Ludwig 2023).

Empirical Evidence on Living Arrangements and Health

A consistent gradient emerges across the literature: spousal coresidence is the most health-protective arrangement, living alone carries the greatest risk, and coresidence with adult children falls between the two with effects that depend heavily on context. Hughes and Waite (2002), analyzing HRS data, found that married couples living together were the most advantaged across self-rated health, mobility limitation, and depressive symptoms, while single women living with children appeared disadvantaged on all outcomes. A meta-analysis of 86 studies confirmed that living alone was associated with a 21% increase in all-cause mortality risk (HR = 1.21, 95% CI 1.13–1.30), with effects persisting after adjustment for loneliness and social isolation (Nakou et al. 2025). Ng et al. (2020) replicated this pattern in Swedish register data, with gender moderating the magnitude of risk. Living alone in later life is increasingly prevalent globally, particularly among older women due to higher widowhood rates and greater longevity (Reher and Requena 2018). The health risks of solo living operate partly through subjective loneliness: Cacioppo and Hawkley (2003) proposed that perceived social isolation activates neurobiological stress responses that accelerate health decline, while O’Súilleabháin et al. (2019) demonstrated that emotional loneliness predicted mortality over 19 years among the oldest-old. These risks, however, are not universal – Dykstra (2009) showed that compensatory social connections outside the household can offset the costs of solo living.

The complexity of multigenerational coresidence complicates the protective gradient. Caputo and Cagney (2023), using HRS data, found that coresidence with adult children was associated with higher depressive symptoms among White parents, while the predictors and meaning of coresidence varied across racial and ethnic groups. Caputo (2019) showed that transitions into coresidence increased parental depressive symptoms, particularly when the coresiding child was not employed. Both positive and negative social exchanges within households predict health outcomes, meaning that coresidence introduces potential conflict and loss of autonomy alongside support (Ingersoll‐Dayton et al. 1997). Yahirun et al. (2018) showed that adult children’s socioeconomic resources shape the health returns to coresidence, with higher-resourced children providing arrangements that reduce parental depressive symptoms.

The loss of spousal coresidence through widowhood represents one of the most health-damaging residential transitions in later life, and a substantial share of older adults who live alone do so following spousal death, meaning that health risks attributed to solo living partly reflect bereavement and support loss (Berkman et al. 2000). Hawes et al. (2022) used HRS panel data to show that social resources moderate the magnitude and duration of the depressive response to spousal loss. At the other end of the residential trajectory, nursing home admission is heavily confounded by the steep functional decline that precedes it. Preston (1985) documented that institutionalized elderly were overwhelmingly characterized by advanced functional decline and cognitive impairment, and Jordan et al. (2024) confirmed that caregiving arrangement type predicts both institutionalization and mortality among ADL-limited older adults. Any estimate that does not account for pre-existing health trajectories will be inflated by this selection into institutional care.

Heterogeneity by Gender, Race/Ethnicity, and Age

The health consequences of living arrangements are not uniform across demographic groups. Pavlidis et al. (2022) documented that exclusion from social relations generates differential depressive burdens by gender, with men facing higher mortality risks from social isolation. Men tend to rely more heavily on co-resident partners for emotional support and social regulation, making them more vulnerable when spousal coresidence is lost (Berkman et al. 2000). Regarding race and ethnicity, Jester et al. (2023) used HRS data to show that social determinants of health explain racial disparities in psychological health among Black and Hispanic adults relative to White adults, while Cook et al. (2024) documented greater ADL-disability prevalence among Hispanic and non-Hispanic Black older adults. The cultural normativity of multigenerational coresidence among Black and Hispanic families means that the same living arrangement carries different relational meanings and health implications across groups (Caputo and Cagney 2023; Liu 2024). Kong et al. (2024) used HRS growth curve models to document that health trajectories for depression and ADL limitations diverge substantially by race/ethnicity, with Black and Hispanic older adults showing steeper adverse trajectories – precisely the individual-level heterogeneity that motivates individual-slope modeling. With respect to age, the oldest-old face the greatest vulnerability: their social networks have contracted through bereavement and health-related withdrawal from community activities, making co-resident household members the last remaining daily social contacts (Chen et al. 2023; O’Súilleabháin et al. 2019). Cross-national evidence from Europe shows that living arrangements moderate chronic disease accumulation differently for native-born and immigrant older adults, with family coresidence providing a buffer against disease progression whose magnitude varies by the cultural context shaping expectations about household composition (Jang et al. 2024).

Research Gaps and Hypotheses

Prior fixed-effects studies of coresidence and health (Hughes and Waite 2002; Caputo and Cagney 2023) control for time-invariant individual characteristics but cannot separate the causal effect of a living arrangement transition from the pre-existing health trend that may have precipitated it. No study has applied FE-IS (Rüttenauer and Ludwig 2023) to this relationship or examined whether trajectory-driven selection bias operates differently across health domains. We address these gaps using 20 years of HRS panel data (2000–2020), three health outcomes (CES-D, self-rated health, ADL limitations), and heterogeneity tests by gender, race/ethnicity, and age.

We test three hypotheses:

H1:

Coresidence reduces depressive symptoms compared to living alone, reflecting genuine social causation through stress buffering and emotional support.

H2:

The FE-IS estimator will attenuate or eliminate the apparent protective effects of coresidence on self-rated health and ADL limitations observed in standard fixed effects models, revealing that these associations are driven by trajectory-based selection rather than causal effects of living arrangements.

H3:

The protective effect of coresidence on depressive symptoms is larger for men (who depend more on co-residential support), for the oldest-old (whose alternative social ties have thinned), and varies by race/ethnicity (reflecting the differential cultural normativity of multigenerational living).

Data and Methods

Data

We used data from the Health and Retirement Study (HRS), a nationally representative longitudinal survey of U.S. adults aged 50 and older, conducted biennially since 1992 (Börsch‐Supan et al. 2013). We combined two harmonized sources: the RAND HRS Longitudinal File (1992–2020, Version 2), which provides cleaned and consistently coded measures across waves, and the Gateway Harmonized HRS, which supplies detailed household composition variables not available in the RAND file. Our analytic window spans Waves 5 through 15 (2000–2020), covering 11 waves over 20 years. We restricted the sample to respondents aged 50 or older at each observation with at least three non-missing living arrangement observations across waves, a minimum required for estimation of individual-specific slopes. The resulting analytic sample included 28,770 persons contributing 191,822 person-wave observations (Section S1 presents the sample selection flowchart). Because the 8-item Center for Epidemiologic Studies Depression Scale (CES-D) was not administered to proxy respondents, analyses of depressive symptoms used a reduced sample of 27,084 persons (177,772 person-waves).

Measures

Living Arrangement

The primary independent variable was living arrangement, constructed from the Gateway Harmonized HRS household roster variable (h*lvwith) and nursing home residence indicator (r*nhmliv). We classified respondents into six mutually exclusive categories at each wave: (1) living alone (reference category), (2) living with spouse or partner only, (3) living with spouse/partner and adult children, (4) living with adult children only (no spouse), (5) living with others or in mixed households, and (6) institutionalized (nursing home or long-term care facility). This classification captures the primary dimensions of household composition relevant to older adults’ daily social environment. We did not include marital status as a separate control variable because it is largely absorbed by the living arrangement categories; all spousal coresidence categories implicitly condition on being married or partnered.

Health Outcomes

We examined three health outcomes spanning psychological, subjective, and functional domains. Depressive symptoms were measured with the 8-item CES-D scale (range 0–8), where higher scores indicate more depressive symptoms (Steffick 2000). Self-rated health was measured on a 5-point scale recoded so that higher values indicate better health (1 = poor, 5 = excellent), a validated global health indicator that independently predicts mortality (Idler and Benyamini 1997). Activities of daily living (ADL) limitations were measured as the sum of six items (bathing, eating, dressing, walking across a room, getting in and out of bed, and toileting; range 0–6), where higher scores indicate greater functional impairment.

Control Variables

All models controlled for age (centered at 65) and age-squared to capture nonlinear aging effects, inverse hyperbolic sine (IHS)-transformed household income and household wealth, current employment status, government health insurance coverage, current smoking status, any alcohol consumption, body mass index (BMI), a count of eight physician-diagnosed chronic conditions (hypertension, diabetes, cancer, lung disease, heart disease, stroke, psychiatric problems, and arthritis), hospitalization in the past two years, and census region.

Analytic Strategy

The central methodological challenge is that individuals who experience different residential transitions may differ systematically in their underlying health trajectories (Zimmer 2005; Zhang et al. 2023). Standard fixed effects (FE) models address time-invariant confounding by comparing each individual to themselves over time:

\begin{equation} Y_{it} = \alpha_i + \mathbf{X}_{it}'\boldsymbol{\gamma} + \epsilon_{it} \label{eq:fe} \end{equation}

where Y_{it} is the health outcome for person i at wave t, \alpha_i is an individual fixed effect absorbing all time-invariant characteristics, \mathbf{X}_{it} is a vector of time-varying covariates including living arrangement indicators, and \epsilon_{it} is an idiosyncratic error term.

Standard FE models, however, assume that all individuals follow the same average health trajectory conditional on covariates (Bell and Jones 2014; Allison 2019). If individuals on steeper health decline are disproportionately selected into certain living arrangements, then individual health trends become a time-varying confounder that standard FE cannot remove (Rüttenauer and Ludwig 2023; Leszczensky and Wolbring 2019). The Fixed Effects with Individual Slopes (FE-IS) model extends the standard FE framework by allowing each person their own health trajectory:

\begin{equation} Y_{it} = \alpha_i + \beta_i \cdot \text{age}_{it} + \mathbf{X}_{it}'\boldsymbol{\gamma} + \epsilon_{it} \label{eq:feis} \end{equation}

where \beta_i is a person-specific slope on age (centered at 65). The coefficient vector \boldsymbol{\gamma} now estimates the effect of within-person changes in living arrangement on health, net of both time-invariant characteristics (\alpha_i) and individual-specific health trajectories (\beta_i \cdot \text{age}_{it}). Identification comes from deviations in health that coincide with living arrangement transitions but cannot be explained by the individual’s pre-existing trajectory (Rüttenauer and Ludwig 2023).

We estimated standard FE models using the fixest R package with standard errors clustered at the individual level, and FE-IS models using the feisr R package with heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors (Rüttenauer and Ludwig 2023). Correlated random effects (Mundlak) models provide an alternative approach for handling time-invariant heterogeneity in unbalanced panels (Schunck 2013; Wooldridge 2019); we used the Hausman-type test comparing FE and FE-IS to evaluate whether individual slope terms are necessary. For each outcome, we report four specifications: basic FE (living arrangement only), full FE (with all controls), basic FE-IS, and full FE-IS (a detailed comparison of the two estimators is provided in Section S3).

To examine whether the association between living arrangements and health varied across population subgroups, we estimated stratified FE-IS models by gender (male, female), race/ethnicity (non-Hispanic White, non-Hispanic Black, Hispanic), and age group (50–64, 65–79, 80+). We tested for significant subgroup differences using Wald tests on interaction terms in pooled FE models with living arrangement \times subgroup product terms.

Missing data were handled using the missRanger algorithm with predictive mean matching (k = 5) applied in wide format, which combines the accuracy of random forest imputation with the distributional fidelity of donor-based matching (Young and Johnson 2015; Junaid et al. 2025). As robustness checks, we re-estimated main models using (1) binary living arrangement coding (alone vs. any coresidence), (2) one-wave lagged living arrangement, (3) exclusion of the COVID-disrupted Wave 15 (2020), (4) complete cases only, and (5) inverse probability weighting (IPW) to adjust for differential attrition. These sensitivity analyses are reported in Section S5 of the Supplementary Materials.

Results

Descriptive Characteristics

Table [tab:descriptives] presents descriptive statistics by living arrangement category. The full sample had a mean age of 68.1 years (SD = 10.6), was 58.5% female, and was 67.9% non-Hispanic White. Living arrangement categories differed markedly in demographic composition. Persons living alone were older (mean age 71.7), predominantly female (71.3%), and had lower income (IHS mean = 10.54) and wealth (IHS mean = 9.75) than those living with a spouse only (mean age 68.0, 49.9% female, IHS income = 11.56, IHS wealth = 12.40). Institutionalized respondents were the oldest (mean age 83.4), had the highest chronic disease burden (mean = 3.46), and reported the most depressive symptoms (CES-D mean = 3.20) and functional limitations (ADL mean = 3.71). Those living with adult children only were disproportionately female (79.1%) and had elevated depressive symptoms (CES-D mean = 2.02) relative to the sample average (1.55).

The transition matrix (Table [tab:transitions]) revealed substantial stability in living arrangements: 87.1% of those living with a spouse and 85.0% of those living alone remained in the same category between consecutive waves. Spouse-and-children households were less stable (65.8% retention), with 21.1% transitioning to spouse-only arrangements as children left the household. The most common transitions from living alone were to living with adult children (4.1%) and others/mixed households (5.4%), while 2.7% of those living alone transitioned to institutional care.

Main Results: FE versus FE-IS Estimates

Table [tab:main_results] presents the main estimates from standard FE and FE-IS models for all three health outcomes. The central finding is domain specificity: FE-IS estimation preserves the protective effects of coresidence on depressive symptoms but eliminates them for self-rated health and functional limitations, revealing that the physical health associations are driven by selection on individual health trends.

For depressive symptoms, all forms of coresidence reduced CES-D scores relative to living alone in the full FE-IS model, and the direction and significance of these coefficients were consistent across FE and FE-IS specifications. Spousal coresidence showed the largest reduction (b = -0.433, p < .001), followed by spouse and children (b = -0.390, p < .001), others/mixed (b = -0.205, p < .001), and children only (b = -0.145, p < .001). Institutionalization was null in the FE-IS model (b = -0.010, p > .05), in contrast to the significant positive association in the basic FE model (b = 0.252, p < .001). The survival of coresidence effects under trajectory correction supports a genuine social causation interpretation for mental health.

The picture reversed for self-rated health and ADL limitations. Standard FE estimated that spousal coresidence improved SRH by 0.121 points (p < .001) and reduced ADL limitations by 0.160 (p < .001). Once individual health trajectories were accounted for, however, both effects disappeared: the FE-IS estimate for spousal coresidence on SRH reversed to -0.031 (p < .05), and the ADL estimate collapsed to -0.012 (not significant). Spouse-and-children coresidence showed parallel reversals – SRH shifted to -0.055 (p < .01) and ADL to -0.009 (not significant). These sign flips expose the selection mechanism: individuals on more favorable health trajectories are more likely to remain in spousal households, inflating the estimated benefit in standard FE models. Living with children only remained associated with more ADL limitations in the FE-IS model (b = 0.045, p < .05), consistent with health-driven selection into this arrangement (Zimmer 2005). The institutionalization coefficients were attenuated but remained significant: SRH dropped from -0.513 to -0.153 (70% reduction), and ADL from 2.693 to 1.577 (41% reduction), demonstrating that pre-existing decline trajectories account for a large share of the apparent institutionalization effect.

Heterogeneity in Depressive Symptom Effects

Given that coresidence effects on depressive symptoms were robust to trajectory correction, we examined heterogeneity in these associations by gender, race/ethnicity, and age group using stratified FE-IS models with full controls (Section S5). The overarching pattern is that the depression-reducing effect of coresidence is concentrated among those with the fewest alternative sources of social support.

The gender gap was the most pronounced dimension of heterogeneity (interaction p < .001). Men showed reductions in depressive symptoms from spousal coresidence that were three to four times larger than women’s: living with a spouse reduced men’s CES-D scores by 0.548 points versus 0.403 for women, and the gap widened for non-spousal arrangements – living with children only was associated with a 0.313-point reduction for men but only 0.085 for women. These patterns are consistent with men’s greater reliance on co-resident partners for emotional support and social regulation (Pavlidis et al. 2022). The racial and ethnic gradient in spousal coresidence effects paralleled this logic of alternative support availability. Non-Hispanic White respondents showed the largest spousal effect (b = -0.494), followed by Hispanic (b = -0.373) and non-Hispanic Black (b = -0.237) respondents (interaction p < .001), while the children-only effect was more comparable across groups (-0.128, -0.151, and -0.145, respectively). Spousal coresidence may carry stronger normative meaning for White older adults, while multigenerational coresidence traditions among Black and Hispanic families provide alternative emotional support that attenuates the contrast between coresidence and living alone (Caputo and Cagney 2023; Liu 2024). The protective effect also increased with age (interaction p < .001): spousal coresidence was associated with a 0.354-point CES-D reduction among those aged 50–64, 0.500 among those aged 65–79, and 0.576 among the oldest-old (80+). The oldest-old appear most vulnerable to the depressive consequences of living alone, likely because their social networks have contracted through bereavement and health-related withdrawal, leaving co-resident household members as the last remaining daily social contacts.

Robustness Checks

Sensitivity analyses confirmed the main patterns across alternative specifications. Binary living arrangement coding (alone vs. any coresidence) replicated the protective CES-D effect and the null FE-IS results for SRH and ADL. One-wave lagged living arrangement produced similar point estimates, reducing concerns about contemporaneous reverse causality. Excluding the 2020 wave (COVID-disrupted data collection) did not substantively alter coefficients. Complete-case analysis and inverse probability-weighted models both yielded results consistent with the imputed-data models, suggesting that attrition and missing data patterns did not drive the main findings. Full results for all robustness checks are reported in Section S5.

Discussion

This study applied Fixed Effects with Individual Slopes (FE-IS) to twenty years of HRS panel data, separating the causal effects of living arrangements on health from the individual health trajectories that drive residential selection. The central finding is one of domain specificity: coresidence genuinely reduces depressive symptoms, but its apparent benefits for self-rated health and functional limitations are largely artifacts of selection on health trends. This pattern held across all coresidence types and was robust to alternative specifications, lagged treatments, and exclusion of the COVID-affected 2020 wave.

Selection on Trends and Domain Specificity

The comparison between standard FE and FE-IS estimates reveals substantial bias in prior work. For self-rated health, the FE model estimated that spousal coresidence improved SRH by 0.121 points (p < .001), but the FE-IS model reversed this to -0.031 (p < .05), a sign flip that exposes the selection mechanism. Individuals on favorable health trajectories are more likely to remain in spousal households; standard FE conflates these pre-existing trends with a treatment effect. The pattern was even starker for functional limitations: the FE estimate of -0.160 (p < .001) for spousal coresidence collapsed to -0.012 (not significant) under FE-IS. For institutionalization, the ADL coefficient dropped from 2.693 to 1.577, a 41% reduction that quantifies how much prior estimates were inflated by the steep functional decline that precedes nursing home admission (Preston 1985).

These results align with the growing recognition that conventional panel models understate selection bias when individual outcome trajectories correlate with treatment timing (Rüttenauer and Ludwig 2023; Bell et al. 2018). Prior studies of coresidence and health using standard FE or random-effects models (Hughes and Waite 2002; Caputo and Cagney 2023) likely overestimated the physical health returns to shared living. The FE-IS framework, by absorbing each person’s idiosyncratic aging slope, isolates the residual effect of residential transitions from the trajectory-driven selection into different arrangements (Rüttenauer and Ludwig 2023).

Depression, by contrast, showed a different pattern. All forms of coresidence reduced CES-D scores relative to living alone, and these effects survived the FE-IS correction. The spousal coresidence coefficient of -0.433 (p < .001) represents roughly half a standard deviation on the CES-D 8-item scale, a clinically meaningful effect size. Living with a spouse and children (-0.390), with others (-0.205), and with children only (-0.145) all showed significant reductions, while institutionalization was null (-0.010, not significant). This domain specificity – robust mental health effects paired with null physical health effects after trajectory correction – is the paper’s central empirical contribution.

Mechanisms: Why Depression But Not Physical Health?

The stress process model (Pearlin 1989) offers a parsimonious account. Co-resident household members provide daily emotional regulation, companionship, and practical assistance that directly buffer psychological distress. The social convoy model (Antonucci et al. 2019) reinforces this interpretation: household co-residents constitute the innermost layer of the social convoy, and when this layer is absent – as when living alone – the buffering capacity against psychological stressors is sharply reduced. Jin and Hu (2025) demonstrated that social support fully mediates the association between living alone and depression among older adults.

Physical health, by contrast, follows biological aging trajectories that social arrangements cannot easily redirect. Self-rated health captures accumulated chronic conditions and functional capacity (Idler and Benyamini 1997); ADL limitations reflect progressive musculoskeletal and neurological decline. While co-resident others may encourage exercise or monitor medication, these behavioral channels are weaker than the direct emotional buffering that protects mental health (Berkman et al. 2000). The null institutionalization effect on depression (-0.010, not significant) fits this account: the depressive trajectories of nursing home residents are already captured by the individual slope, and the institutional environment provides structured social contact that may partially offset the isolation of losing one’s household.

The graded pattern across coresidence types is informative. The spouse effect (-0.433) was three times larger than the children-only effect (-0.145), consistent with the emotional primacy of the spousal relationship (Hughes and Waite 2002). Living with children offers companionship but can also generate role conflict and intergenerational tension that partially offset support benefits (Caputo 2019; Caputo and Cagney 2023). The “others/mixed” category (-0.205) fell between spouse and children, suggesting that diverse household configurations still provide some social buffering relative to living alone.

Heterogeneity: Gender, Race, and Age

The protective effect of coresidence on depression is unevenly distributed, with the largest benefits accruing to those with the fewest alternative sources of social support. Men benefited three to four times more than women (-0.548 vs. -0.403 for spousal coresidence; -0.313 vs. -0.085 for children only), consistent with men’s tendency to concentrate emotional support in the spousal relationship (Berkman et al. 2000) while women maintain more diversified networks that partially compensate for the absence of a co-resident partner (Pavlidis et al. 2022). Men living alone thus face disproportionate depression risk reflecting a structural dependence on co-residential support.

A parallel gradient emerged across racial and ethnic groups: non-Hispanic White older adults showed the largest spousal effect (-0.494), followed by Hispanic (-0.373) and non-Hispanic Black (-0.237) respondents. This pattern likely reflects the cultural normativity of different living arrangements – for White families, where solo living is more common, the transition to living alone carries greater psychological weight (Liu 2024), while extended family networks among Black and Hispanic families provide alternative emotional support (Caputo and Cagney 2023; Wang et al. 2026). The children-only effect was more comparable across groups, consistent with the gradient operating primarily through the relative normativity of spousal versus extended-family arrangements. The protective effect also increased with age, from -0.354 (ages 50–64) to -0.576 (80+), as the oldest-old face the greatest isolation risk with social networks contracted through bereavement and health-related withdrawal (Chen et al. 2023).

Limitations

Several limitations warrant consideration. First, the FE-IS model assumes linear individual health trajectories. Acute health events (stroke, hip fracture, onset of dementia) create non-linear trajectory shifts that the linear slope cannot fully absorb. To the extent that such events both alter health and trigger living arrangement changes, residual confounding may persist. Second, the HRS does not distinguish voluntary from involuntary living arrangement transitions. An older adult who chooses to move in with children differs in unmeasured ways from one whose health decline necessitates the move, yet both appear identically in the data. Third, the biennial measurement interval means that transitions occurring between waves are unobserved; short-duration arrangements (e.g., temporary coresidence during recovery) are missed. Fourth, Wave 15 (2020) was conducted during the COVID-19 pandemic with modified interview protocols. Although robustness checks excluding this wave confirmed the main results, the pandemic altered both living arrangements and mental health in ways that may not generalize. Fifth, the “others/mixed” living arrangement category groups diverse configurations (living with siblings, friends, or non-relatives) that may carry distinct health implications. Sixth, the data do not capture relationship quality within households. Two spousal households with identical compositions but different relationship dynamics will produce different health effects, and this within-category heterogeneity is unobserved (Caputo 2019).

Implications

Three implications follow from these findings. First, the large body of evidence linking living arrangements to physical health outcomes in older adults should be interpreted with caution. Standard fixed-effects estimates do not eliminate the bias from differential health trends, and the apparent physical health “benefits” of coresidence may reflect the health trajectories of those who maintain shared living rather than a causal effect of the arrangement itself. Second, the genuine depression-reducing effect of coresidence, especially spousal coresidence, points to a clear policy target. Mental health screening and social support interventions should prioritize older adults living alone, with particular attention to men and the oldest-old. Housing policies that facilitate social integration, such as shared housing programs and community-based social services, may yield greater returns for mental health than for physical health (Zai 2024; Henning-Smith 2024). Evidence from long-term care insurance pilots in China suggests that formal care policy can reshape living arrangement patterns with downstream effects on well-being (Zhu and Bai 2025). Third, the heterogeneity by gender, race/ethnicity, and age cautions against one-size-fits-all approaches: the psychological meaning and health consequences of a given living arrangement depend on who occupies it and what alternatives are available. Future research should examine specific transition pathways – from spousal coresidence to living alone following widowhood, from living alone to coresidence following a health crisis – and cross-national applications of FE-IS would test the generalizability of the selection-on-trends pattern identified here.

Conclusion

This study demonstrates that the relationship between living arrangements and health among older Americans is domain-specific. After accounting for individual health trajectories using FE-IS models, coresidence genuinely reduces depressive symptoms – with spousal coresidence showing the largest effect – but its apparent benefits for self-rated health and functional limitations are largely artifacts of selection on health trends. The domain specificity documented here offers a new lens for understanding how social arrangements shape health in later life: social causation operates through mental health pathways, while physical health associations reflect the trajectories of those who select into different residential contexts.
