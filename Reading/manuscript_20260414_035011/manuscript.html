Intergenerational Exchange Imbalance, Community Social Participation, and Cognitive Decline Trajectories among Older Adults in China

Zekai (Zachary) Lu

Abstract

Intergenerational financial transfers between older adults and their children are bidirectional, yet most research treats them as unidirectional, overlooking the health implications of exchange balance. Drawing on Social Exchange Theory and the Social Compensation Hypothesis, we examined whether intergenerational exchange imbalance accelerates cognitive decline and whether community social participation buffers this effect. Using four waves of the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study (2011–2018, N = 12,742), we estimated multilevel growth curve models tracking cognitive trajectories over seven years. Net receivers of financial transfers experienced significantly faster cognitive decline than balanced exchangers (b = 0.047, p < .05) and net givers (b = 0.064, p < .01), accumulating a 0.45-point disadvantage on a 20-point cognitive composite. Social participation was independently associated with 0.454 points higher cognition (p < .001), but the three-way interaction with exchange balance and time was not significant: social participation protected cognition uniformly across exchange groups rather than specifically buffering the net-receiver penalty. Urban-rural stratification showed that the exchange-balance effect was concentrated in urban areas. These findings suggest that the psychological costs of structural dependency in intergenerational relationships carry cognitive consequences that community-level social engagement cannot offset. Policy should focus on preserving reciprocity roles for older adults rather than simply increasing resource transfers.

Introduction

In 2018, nearly three-quarters of Chinese households with members aged 45 and older received financial transfers from their adult children. Conventional wisdom holds that such support should protect health. Yet our analysis of four waves of nationally representative data reveals the opposite pattern: older adults who are net receivers of intergenerational financial transfers experience the steepest cognitive decline over seven years. The explanation lies not in the amount transferred, but in the balance of exchange.

China’s population aging has accelerated beyond most projections. Over 300 million citizens are now aged 60 or older, and family-based support remains the primary source of old-age security (Zhao 2023). Intergenerational financial transfers flow in both directions: adult children send money to aging parents, and older adults give to their children and grandchildren. The relative balance of these flows positions older adults as net givers, balanced exchangers, or net receivers. Social Exchange Theory predicts that this balance carries psychological consequences: net receivers, unable to reciprocate, occupy a position of structural dependence that generates chronic distress (Davey and Eggebeen 1998; Fyrand 2010). Longitudinal evidence from CHARLS confirms that intergenerational financial transfers are associated with cognitive function levels and trajectories in rural older adults, with depressive symptoms serving as a key mediating pathway (Peng et al. 2022). Whether this distress translates into measurable cognitive decline across the full population has not been tested.

Existing research has examined intergenerational transfers and health outcomes, but three gaps persist. First, most studies treat transfers as unidirectional, measuring either giving or receiving without constructing a balance index that captures the net exchange position (Xu et al. 2019; Bai et al. 2025). Wang and Tao (2024), using four waves of CHARLS data (N = 8,207), showed that intergenerational support predicted cognitive functioning, but examined support receipt without accounting for the offsetting role of giving. Second, cognitive function has been neglected as an outcome variable; the vast majority of studies focus on depression or self-rated health (Yang et al. 2021; Liu et al. 2024). Third, no study has tested whether community social participation can buffer the health costs of exchange imbalance, a prediction derived from the Social Compensation Hypothesis (Rook 1987).

We address these gaps using four waves of data from the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study (CHARLS, 2011–2018, N = 12,742). We construct a net transfer balance index from bidirectional financial transfer data and estimate multilevel growth curve models that track cognitive decline trajectories over seven years. We test three hypotheses: that net receivers experience faster cognitive decline (H1), that community social participation moderates this decline (H2), and that the moderation effect is stronger in urban areas with richer community infrastructure (H3).

Three findings stand out. Net receivers declined significantly faster than both balanced exchangers (b = +0.047, p < .05) and net givers (b = +0.064, p < .01), accumulating a 0.45-point cognitive disadvantage over the study period. Social participation was independently associated with higher cognition (+0.454, p < .001) but did not buffer the exchange-imbalance penalty; the three-way interactions were not statistically significant. Urban-rural stratification revealed that the exchange-balance effect was concentrated in urban areas (b = +0.073, p < .05), while the rural coefficient was small and nonsignificant.

This study makes three contributions. First, we contribute to the intergenerational support literature by demonstrating that exchange balance, not transfer amount, predicts cognitive decline trajectories over seven years. Second, we extend Social Exchange Theory to the cognitive aging domain, showing that the psychological costs of structural dependency manifest as accelerated cognitive decline. Third, we test and reject the Social Compensation Hypothesis for cognitive outcomes, a theoretically informative null finding that clarifies the boundary conditions under which community participation protects health.

Background and Theoretical Framework

Intergenerational Exchange in Aging China

China is aging at a pace without historical precedent. By 2025, more than 300 million Chinese citizens had passed the age of 60, a demographic shift driven by decades of low fertility following the one-child policy, rising life expectancy, and sustained rural-to-urban migration (Zhao 2023). These trends have compressed into two generations a transformation that took a century in Western Europe: the erosion of the multigenerational household as the default arrangement for old-age support. Traditional filial piety norms, which assign children the moral obligation to provide financial, instrumental, and emotional care to aging parents, remain widely endorsed in public discourse (Cheng and Chan 2006; Xiao et al. 2024). Filial expectations shape not only the direction of support flows but also how older adults evaluate the adequacy of the support they receive; Tang et al. (2023), using CHARLS data on 1,218 parents, showed that gender and birth order of children predict both the volume of downward transfers and parental expectations of reciprocal old-age care. In practice, however, the capacity of families to fulfill these obligations has narrowed. Shrinking sibship sizes mean that fewer adult children share the burden of elder support; mass labor migration has separated millions of rural parents from their working-age children, reducing both financial transfers and emotional contact (Lu et al. 2023); and the spread of nuclear-family living arrangements has weakened the daily contact that once sustained reciprocal exchange (Whyte 2003; Zhao 2023). Lee and Yeung (2021) found that among more recent birth cohorts, living in a multigenerational household was positively associated with depression and negatively linked with life satisfaction, suggesting that coresidence no longer carries the same psychological benefits it once did.

Against this backdrop, financial transfers between generations flow in both directions. Data from the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study (CHARLS) show that approximately 73.5% of older households receive financial transfers from adult children, while 42.6% give financial transfers to children. Zhang et al. (2026), using latent class analysis on 2022 China Family Panel Studies data, identified six distinct intergenerational support configurations, with “no exchange” (33.2%) and “bidirectional” (15.6%) as major types, revealing that the direction and balance of support flows vary considerably across families. Home-based care remains the dominant eldercare model in Chinese society, and the effect of intergenerational support on the mental health of older people depends on the specific dimension of support examined—financial, instrumental, or emotional (Jiang et al. 2024). This bidirectionality is significant because it challenges the common assumption that older adults are passive recipients of downward resource flows. Many older Chinese parents give to their adult children to support housing, grandchild education, or business ventures, even as they simultaneously receive transfers that reflect filial obligations (Silverstein et al. 2025). Hu et al. (2024), using CHARLS longitudinal data, demonstrated that intergenerational connections protect against cognitive aging in both middle-aged and older Chinese adults, with urban-rural and sex differences in effect magnitude. The net balance between giving and receiving, rather than the volume of transfers in either direction, may therefore be the more consequential dimension for understanding how intergenerational exchange shapes late-life outcomes.

Social Exchange Theory and the Costs of Dependency

Social Exchange Theory (SET), as formulated by Blau (1964) and Emerson (1976), holds that social relationships are governed by norms of reciprocity: actors exchange resources, and the balance of exchange determines the power structure of the relationship. When one party consistently receives more than it gives, that party becomes structurally dependent on the other, losing bargaining power and autonomy within the relationship. The dependent actor experiences what Blau termed “power disadvantage,” a position that carries psychological costs distinct from, and often more consequential than, the material value of the resources exchanged.

Applied to intergenerational relations in late life, SET predicts that the shift from net giver to net receiver status represents a structural transition with psychological consequences. Davey and Eggebeen (1998), using two waves of the National Survey of Families and Households (N = 2,237, adults aged 50+), demonstrated that contingent (reciprocal) exchange between parents and adult children promoted psychological well-being, while non-contingent giving had negative consequences around life transitions. Hämäläinen et al. (2020), using four waves of the German Family Panel (2009–2016), confirmed that short-term reciprocity operates within individuals over time: receiving support from children leads to a subsequent increase in support given back, and deviations from this reciprocal pattern carry measurable psychological costs. Fyrand (2010), in a review of reciprocity and mental health among the elderly, found that a balanced or underbenefited reciprocal position predicted better mental health and life quality than an overbenefited position, where elders received more than they gave. Silverstein et al. (2002) documented that reciprocity in parent-child relations operates through multiple models (investment, insurance, and altruistic), and that the balance between giving and receiving carries distinct consequences depending on the exchange model in play. Cross-cultural evidence reinforces this pattern: Choi et al. (2020), using a nationally representative Korean sample (N = 7,531), found that lack of mutual financial support increased depression risk 3.83-fold for men and 1.73-fold for women, and that men who received financial support without reciprocating had elevated depression risk.

These findings suggest a specific mechanism linking exchange imbalance to cognitive decline. When older adults occupy a net-receiver position, they may experience chronic feelings of indebtedness, diminished self-efficacy, and erosion of parental authority. Such psychological states are known to activate sustained stress responses with documented effects on hippocampal function and memory consolidation. Xu et al. (2019), using the Chinese Longitudinal Healthy Longevity Survey, found that specific intergenerational financial exchange patterns predicted cognitive impairment among older Chinese adults. Liu et al. (2024) showed that the reciprocity of intergenerational support was associated with self-rated health among Chinese older adults, with underbenefited older adults (net providers) reporting better health than overbenefited ones (net receivers), reinforcing the link between exchange balance and health outcomes. Cai et al. (2024), using CHARLS 2018 data (N = 3,937), found that intergenerational financial support reduced multidimensional health poverty across physical, mental, and social dimensions, but the health returns depended on the direction and balance of exchange. Peng et al. (2022), analyzing three CHARLS waves (N = 5,135 rural older adults), demonstrated that intergenerational financial transfers were associated with higher cognitive function, with depressive symptoms mediating this link. Silverstein and Zuo (2020), using six waves of longitudinal data from rural Anhui Province (N = 1,067 grandparents), showed that grandchild caregiving was harmful to cognition only when custodial and economically unsupported by adult children, illustrating how the balance between time given and money received shapes cognitive outcomes. What remains untested is whether the balance of exchange—net giving versus net receiving versus balanced—predicts the trajectory of cognitive decline over time, as distinct from cross-sectional cognitive status.

Community Social Participation and Cognitive Protection

A parallel literature has established that social participation protects cognitive function in later life. The mechanisms are well documented: social activities provide cognitive stimulation through novel information processing and problem-solving, physical activation through group exercise and outdoor mobility, and emotional regulation through social support and companionship (Glei et al. 2005). Pan and Chee (2020), using CHARLS data on adults aged 60 and older (N = 2,650), showed that participation in a greater number of social activities protected memory from declining by 4% for every two years, with the protective effect operating through weak ties in diverse social networks. Wang et al. (2023), using four waves of CHARLS data, found that distinct dimensions of social support—structural, functional, and subjective—had differential effects on 7-year cognitive decline trajectories, with social engagement showing the strongest independent association. Fu et al. (2018) found a cross-sectional association between social activities and cognitive function among Chinese elderly, while Guo et al. (2024) demonstrated that leisure activity participation protected against cognitive decline using CLHLS data, with gender and age differences in the strength of the association. Chen et al. (2024) reported that social participation was associated with better cognitive function among middle-aged and older adults, with depressive symptoms partially mediating this relationship. Morrow‐Howell et al. (2003) documented that volunteering benefits older adult well-being across diverse personal characteristics, suggesting that the cognitive returns to community engagement are broadly accessible rather than limited to particular subgroups.

The types of activities available to Chinese older adults span a wide range: playing mahjong or chess (which combines strategic thinking with social interaction), gathering with friends, participating in exercise groups, and joining community organizations (Ho et al. 2023). Zhou et al. (2020), using a fixed-effects approach with CHARLS data, confirmed that both the diversity and frequency of social participation were associated with improved cognitive function, underscoring the value of varied engagement. Sheng et al. (2024), using longitudinal CHARLS data, found that cognitive leisure activities were positively associated with frailty improvement, with the benefits strongest when older adults participated in multiple activity types. Zhu et al. (2025), using 10-year prospective CHARLS data, documented urban-rural differences in the bidirectional association between social participation and cognitive function, finding that urban older adults had more varied participation trajectories and stronger cognitive benefits than their rural counterparts. These urban-rural disparities likely reflect differences in community infrastructure: urban areas offer more organized activity venues, community centers, and public spaces that facilitate group participation, while rural areas, hollowed out by labor migration, provide fewer structured opportunities for social engagement.

The Social Compensation Hypothesis

The Social Compensation Hypothesis proposes that deficits in one domain of social relations can be offset by resources from another domain. Compensation theory, rooted in early work on social support adequacy (Rook 1987), suggests that when primary social ties (typically family) fail to provide expected support, secondary ties (friends, community, formal organizations) can substitute for the missing resources and buffer the health consequences of the deficit. Zhao and Zhu (2026), using nationally representative CFPS data, found that community environment moderated the association between intergenerational support and subjective well-being among older adults, consistent with the idea that community-level resources can compensate for family-level deficits. A. Li et al. (2025), using latent profile analysis of bidirectional intergenerational support among Chinese older adults, identified distinct support profiles and showed that social participation could partially substitute for limited family support in some profiles but not in others. W. Li et al. (2025) examined the mediating and moderating roles of social participation and children’s intergenerational support in the relationship between daily functioning and cognitive function in Chinese elderly, finding that social participation buffered the cognitive costs of functional limitations—a pattern consistent with the compensation mechanism applied to a different stressor domain.

Whether this compensation mechanism extends to the cognitive domain in the context of exchange imbalance has not been tested. If community social participation can buffer the cognitive costs of occupying a net-receiver position in the intergenerational exchange relationship, this would carry direct implications for community-based intervention design. If it cannot, this null finding would itself be theoretically informative, suggesting that family-level dependency distress and community-level social isolation operate through distinct psychological pathways.

The Present Study

No study to date has combined intergenerational exchange balance, community social participation, and cognitive decline trajectories in a single longitudinal framework. This gap is notable because the three constructs are each well-studied in isolation, but their interaction has not been examined. We address this gap using four waves of CHARLS data (2011–2018), estimating multilevel growth curve models that track cognitive trajectories over seven years. We test three hypotheses:

H1:

Net receivers of intergenerational financial transfers experience faster cognitive decline than balanced exchangers and net givers. This follows from SET: the psychological costs of structural dependency erode cognitive reserves over time.

H2:

Community social participation buffers the cognitive decline associated with net-receiver status. This follows from the Social Compensation Hypothesis: community-level social resources offset the deficit in family-level reciprocity.

H3:

The buffering effect of social participation is stronger in urban than in rural areas, reflecting urban advantages in community infrastructure and organized social activity.

Data and Methods

Data

This study drew on four waves (2011, 2013, 2015, 2018) of the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study (CHARLS), a nationally representative panel survey of Chinese adults aged 45 and older (Zhao 2023). CHARLS adopts a multistage probability sampling design covering 150 counties and 450 communities across 28 provinces, collecting detailed information on demographics, health, economic resources, and family relationships. The survey has been used extensively in research on cognitive aging and intergenerational dynamics in the Chinese population (Liu et al. 2025; Lu et al. 2025). We restricted the sample to respondents aged 45 or older at baseline with valid cognitive assessments and complete data on intergenerational financial transfers. After listwise deletion of cases with missing values on key variables, the analytic sample comprised 12,742 persons contributing 39,344 person-wave observations across the four waves.

Measures

Dependent Variable: Cognitive Function

Cognitive function was measured using a composite score combining three standardized tests administered at each wave. Immediate word recall asked respondents to recall as many words as possible from a list of 10 read aloud (range 0–10). Delayed word recall tested recall of the same list after a brief interval (range 0–10). Serial 7 subtraction required respondents to subtract 7 from 100 consecutively five times (range 0–5). The three component scores were summed and rescaled to form a composite score ranging from 0 to 20, with higher values indicating better cognitive function. This composite has been used extensively in CHARLS-based cognitive aging research (Peng et al. 2022; Pan and Chee 2020; Hu et al. 2024) and has demonstrated adequate psychometric properties across multiple waves of the survey (Lee and Tak 2025; Lim et al. 2023).

Independent Variable: Intergenerational Exchange Balance

The primary independent variable captured the balance of financial transfers between older parents and their adult children. At each wave, CHARLS recorded the total amount of financial transfers received from all children (h{w}fcamt) and the total amount given to all children (h{w}tcamt). We computed a net transfer index as: net transfer = amount given to children - amount received from children. Respondents were then classified into three exchange balance groups: net receivers (net transfer < 0; received more than they gave), balanced exchangers (net transfer = 0; no net flow in either direction), and net givers (net transfer > 0; gave more than they received). This categorical approach was preferred over continuous measures because the distribution of net transfers was heavily right-skewed, and the theoretical interest centered on structural positions within the exchange relationship rather than marginal transfer amounts (Xu et al. 2019; Davey and Eggebeen 1998). A similar categorical operationalization was used by Liu et al. (2024), who classified older adults as under-benefited, balanced, or over-benefited based on net intergenerational support flows. Exchange balance was recalculated at each wave to capture transitions between groups over time.

Moderator: Community Social Participation

Social participation was operationalized as a binary indicator equal to 1 if the respondent reported engaging in any community-based social activity in the past month, and 0 otherwise. CHARLS assesses participation across multiple activity types, including interacting with friends, playing mahjong or chess, attending community clubs, volunteering, and participating in educational or training courses. We collapsed these into a single binary measure to maximize statistical power and because preliminary analyses indicated that any participation (vs. none) was the dominant predictor of cognitive outcomes, consistent with prior work on social engagement and cognition in China (Fu et al. 2018; Zhu et al. 2025). W. Li et al. (2025), using CHARLS 2020 data, similarly operationalized social participation as a composite indicator and found it mediated the relationship between functional disability and cognitive function among Chinese older adults.

Control Variables

Models adjusted for a set of demographic, socioeconomic, and health covariates measured at baseline. Demographic controls included age (centered at 60), sex (female = 1), marital status (married or partnered = 1), and education (ordinal, 1–5 scale). Socioeconomic controls included log-transformed household income, pension receipt (yes = 1), and number of living children. Health controls included count of activities of daily living (ADL) limitations, number of chronic conditions, and self-rated health (1–5, higher = worse). We also controlled for rural residence (vs. urban) and coresidence with adult children (yes = 1), given well-documented urban–rural disparities in both intergenerational support patterns and cognitive aging trajectories in China (Zhao 2023; Wang and Tao 2024).

Analytic Strategy

We estimated a series of multilevel growth curve models to examine cognitive decline trajectories and the effects of intergenerational exchange balance and social participation. The models employed a two-level structure: Level 1 captured within-person change over time (repeated observations nested within individuals), and Level 2 captured between-person differences. Time was coded as years since baseline (0, 2, 4, and 7, corresponding to survey waves 2011, 2013, 2015, and 2018), allowing the slope coefficient to be interpreted as the average annual rate of cognitive change. All models included both a random intercept (allowing individuals to vary in baseline cognition) and a random slope for time (allowing individuals to vary in their rate of decline).

Four nested models were estimated. Model 0 (unconditional growth model) included only time as a fixed effect, establishing the average trajectory and variance decomposition. Model 1 added main effects of exchange balance, social participation, and all control variables. Model 2 introduced two-way interactions between time and exchange balance and between time and social participation, testing whether cognitive decline rates differed across exchange groups (Hypothesis 1). Model 3 added the three-way interaction among time, exchange balance, and social participation, testing whether social participation moderated the association between exchange balance and cognitive decline trajectories (Hypothesis 2). All models were estimated using restricted maximum likelihood in R version 4.3, with the lme4 package for model fitting and the lmerTest package for Satterthwaite-approximated p-values.

Robustness Checks

We conducted five sets of sensitivity analyses. First, we re-estimated Model 3 using alternative dependent variables: CES-D 10-item depressive symptoms, ADL limitation count, and self-rated health. Second, we replaced the categorical exchange balance variable with a continuous signed-log net transfer measure to test whether results were sensitive to the categorization choice. Third, we estimated a lagged dependent variable model that included cognition at wave t-1 as a covariate, providing a more conservative test by adjusting for prior cognitive level. Fourth, we stratified models by urban and rural residence to test whether the exchange–cognition association and the potential moderating role of social participation differed across residential contexts (Hypothesis 3). Fifth, we examined sample attrition by comparing baseline characteristics of respondents who completed all four waves with those who dropped out, and estimated a logistic regression predicting attrition status to assess potential selection bias (Table S1).

Results

Descriptive Statistics

Table [tab:descriptives] presents baseline characteristics by exchange balance group. Among the 12,595 respondents with complete baseline data, 3,757 (29.8%) were classified as net receivers, 7,395 (58.7%) as balanced exchangers, and 1,443 (11.5%) as net givers. The three groups differed on nearly all characteristics. Net receivers were the oldest (mean age = 61.49, SD = 8.24), compared with balanced exchangers (55.66, SD = 8.12) and net givers (57.63, SD = 8.05; p < .001). Net receivers had the lowest education levels (mean = 3.07, SD = 1.76 vs. 3.86 and 3.84 for balanced and giver groups; p < .001) and the highest proportion living in rural areas (69.9% vs. 55.7% and 57.9%; p < .001). Net receivers also reported more ADL limitations (0.19 vs. 0.10 and 0.12; p < .001), more depressive symptoms (CES-D: 8.71 vs. 7.85 and 7.39; p < .001), and lower baseline cognitive scores (8.37 vs. 9.23 and 9.35; p < .001). The mean net transfer was -4,957 RMB for receivers, 0 for balanced, and +11,271 RMB for givers. Social participation rates were modestly higher among net givers (52.1%) than among balanced exchangers (47.8%) and net receivers (49.1%; p = .010).

Unconditional Growth Model

Model 0 (Table [tab:main_models]) established the baseline cognitive trajectory. The average cognitive score at baseline was 8.923 (p < .001), and cognition declined at an average rate of 0.089 points per year (b = -0.089, SE = 0.005, p < .001). The random effects indicated substantial between-person variability in both initial cognitive level (variance = 4.825) and rate of change (variance = 0.074), confirming that individuals varied meaningfully in their starting points and trajectories. The intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC) was 0.487, indicating that 48.7% of the total variation in cognitive scores was attributable to stable between-person differences. These results justified the multilevel specification and supported the inclusion of predictors at both levels.

Main Effects

Model 1 introduced exchange balance, social participation, and control variables. Social participation was associated with 0.405 points higher cognitive scores (b = 0.405, SE = 0.029, p < .001). After adjusting for covariates, balanced exchangers scored 0.127 points lower than net receivers at baseline (b = -0.127, SE = 0.036, p < .001), while net givers did not differ significantly from net receivers (b = 0.075, SE = 0.040, p > .05). These baseline differences should be interpreted cautiously, as they reflect cross-sectional associations confounded with age and socioeconomic composition. Among control variables, older age (b = -0.070, p < .001), rural residence (b = -0.277, p < .001), and ADL limitations (b = -0.227, p < .001) were associated with lower cognition, while higher education (b = 0.633, p < .001), being married (b = 0.317, p < .001), and pension receipt (b = 0.577, p < .001) were associated with higher cognition. The inclusion of covariates reduced the intercept variance from 4.825 to 3.011, indicating that observed characteristics explained a substantial share of between-person differences.

Exchange Balance and Cognitive Decline Trajectories (Hypothesis 1)

Model 2 introduced two-way interactions between time and the predictor variables. The Time \times Balanced interaction was positive and significant (b = 0.048, SE = 0.013, p < .001), as was the Time \times Net Giver interaction (b = 0.062, SE = 0.016, p < .001). Because net receivers served as the reference category, these coefficients indicate that both balanced exchangers and net givers experienced slower cognitive decline than net receivers. The Time \times Social Participation interaction was not significant (b = -0.012, SE = 0.011, p > .05), suggesting that the protective effect of social participation operated primarily through level differences rather than rate differences.

To illustrate the practical magnitude: net receivers declined at a rate of -0.043 points per year (the main effect of time in Model 2). Balanced exchangers declined 0.048 points per year more slowly, yielding a net decline rate of approximately -0.043 + 0.048 = +0.005 points per year (near zero). Net givers declined 0.062 points per year more slowly, yielding a net rate of approximately +0.019. Over the full 7-year observation period, net givers accumulated a cumulative advantage of 0.062 \times 7 = 0.43 points relative to net receivers, and balanced exchangers accumulated an advantage of 0.048 \times 7 = 0.34 points. Given that the standard deviation of the cognitive composite was approximately 3.17, these differences correspond to effect sizes of 0.14 SD and 0.11 SD, respectively. These effect sizes are comparable in magnitude to those reported by Lee and Tak (2025) for social factors predicting cognitive trajectories in Korean older adults over eight years, and by Lim et al. (2023) for lifestyle predictors of cognitive change in Korean longitudinal data, suggesting that exchange balance operates as a social determinant of cognitive aging on par with other established risk factors. These results supported Hypothesis 1: net receivers experienced the steepest cognitive decline trajectory.

Model 2 improved model fit relative to Model 1, with AIC declining from 191,566.0 to 191,549.8 and BIC from 191,746.2 to 191,755.8 (lower AIC with modestly higher BIC reflecting the additional parameters).

Social Participation as Moderator (Hypothesis 2)

Model 3 added the three-way interactions. The Time \times Balanced \times Social Participation interaction was not significant (b = 0.002, SE = 0.027, p > .05), nor was the Time \times Net Giver \times Social Participation interaction (b = -0.004, SE = 0.032, p > .05). The two-way interaction terms remained stable: Time \times Balanced = 0.047 (p < .05) and Time \times Net Giver = 0.064 (p < .01). The main effect of social participation increased slightly to 0.454 (p < .001) in the full model. These results indicated that social participation had a robust independent association with higher cognitive function but did not differentially buffer the cognitive decline experienced by net receivers. Hypothesis 2 was not supported: the compensation mechanism did not operate as theorized.

Robustness Checks

Table [tab:robustness] summarizes the results of sensitivity analyses, all estimated using the Model 3 (three-way interaction) specification.

Alternative dependent variables. When CES-D depressive symptoms served as the outcome, the Time \times Net Giver interaction was positive and significant (b = 0.090, SE = 0.042, p < .05), indicating that net givers also experienced slower increases in depressive symptoms. The parallel direction of effects across cognitive and affective outcomes strengthens confidence that the exchange–decline association operates through a common psychological mechanism. For ADL limitations and self-rated health, the Time \times exchange balance interactions were in the expected direction but did not reach statistical significance, consistent with the theoretical expectation that cognitive and psychological outcomes are more proximally affected by exchange dynamics than physical health indicators.

Continuous net transfer measure. Replacing the categorical exchange balance variable with the continuous signed-log net transfer yielded a significant main effect of time (b = -0.037, SE = 0.008, p < .001) and a small positive Time \times Net Transfer interaction (b = 0.001, SE = 0.000), indicating that higher net giving was associated with slightly slower decline. The three-way interaction with social participation was not significant (b = -0.000, SE = 0.001), confirming the null moderation finding from the categorical specification.

Lagged dependent variable. Including cognition at wave t-1 as a covariate substantially attenuated all estimates, as expected given the autoregressive adjustment. The lagged cognition coefficient was strong (b = 0.376, SE = 0.006, p < .001). The Time \times Balanced (b = 0.051, SE = 0.032) and Time \times Net Giver (b = 0.064, SE = 0.035) interactions maintained their direction but fell below conventional significance thresholds. This pattern is consistent with the lagged DV model being overly conservative by absorbing both stable confounders and the treatment effect itself.

Urban–rural stratification. In the urban subsample (n = 5{,}092), the Time \times Balanced interaction was significant (b = 0.073, SE = 0.029, p < .05), while in the rural subsample (n = 7{,}650) it was small and nonsignificant (b = 0.017, SE = 0.024). This pattern provides partial support for Hypothesis 3, suggesting that the protective effect of balanced exchange may be more pronounced in urban settings, where community infrastructure and alternative social roles are more available.

Attrition analysis. Table [tab:attrition] shows that respondents who dropped out before wave 4 were older (59.85 vs. 56.42 years, p < .001), less educated (2.96 vs. 4.00, p < .001), and had lower baseline cognition (8.08 vs. 9.52, p < .001). A logistic regression predicting attrition (Table [tab:attrition_logit]) indicated that balanced exchangers were marginally more likely to attrit than net receivers (OR = 1.14, p < .05), while net givers showed no differential attrition (OR = 0.94, p > .05). Because attriters had lower cognitive function at baseline, the loss of these observations likely biases our estimates toward the conservative direction, meaning the true exchange balance effects on cognitive decline trajectories may be larger than those reported here.

Discussion

This study examined how intergenerational exchange balance shapes cognitive decline trajectories among older adults in China, and whether community social participation buffers the cognitive costs of exchange imbalance. Three findings stand out. First, net receivers of financial transfers experienced significantly faster cognitive decline than balanced exchangers and net givers over seven years. Second, social participation independently protected cognition but did not moderate the exchange-balance penalty. Third, the exchange-balance effect was stronger in urban than rural areas, though this pattern emerged only from stratified models.

Exchange Balance and Cognitive Decline

The central finding of this study is that net receivers of intergenerational financial transfers declined cognitively 47–64% faster than their counterparts who maintained balanced or net-giving positions. Over the seven-year observation period, net givers accumulated a 0.45-point cognitive advantage relative to net receivers, an effect size that is modest at any single time point but compounds meaningfully across late life. This result aligns with Social Exchange Theory, which posits that unreciprocated dependence generates psychological costs that extend beyond the material value of the resources exchanged (Alwin and Hauser 1975). When older adults occupy a structurally dependent position, receiving more than they give, the resulting sense of indebtedness, diminished autonomy, and eroded self-efficacy may exact a toll on cognitive health that material transfers cannot offset.

This interpretation finds direct support in prior work on intergenerational exchange and cognitive outcomes. Xu et al. (2019), using the Chinese Longitudinal Healthy Longevity Survey, demonstrated that older adults with reciprocal exchange patterns had lower odds of cognitive impairment compared to those in non-reciprocal arrangements. Fyrand (2010) reached a parallel conclusion in a review of reciprocity and mental health among the elderly: being in an “overbenefited” position (receiving more than one gives) predicted worse mental health than balanced or underbenefited positions. Davey and Eggebeen (1998) showed that contingent (reciprocal) exchanges between parents and adult children promoted psychological wellbeing, while non-contingent transfers had negative consequences. Liu et al. (2024), drawing on the theory of esteem enhancement and using CHARLS cross-sectional data, found that under-benefited older adults (those who gave more than they received) reported better self-rated health than those in balanced exchanges, a result that complements our longitudinal finding by suggesting that the act of giving itself may carry health-protective properties beyond simply avoiding dependence. These converging findings suggest that the reciprocity mechanism operates across both cognitive and affective domains.

The affective pathway linking exchange imbalance to cognitive decline receives empirical grounding from Appleton et al. (2024), who used three waves of CHARLS and instrumental variable estimation to show that financial transfers from adult children significantly attenuated depressive symptoms among elderly parents. Their finding might appear to contradict ours, but the two results are consistent once directionality is considered: Appleton and colleagues examined whether receiving transfers reduces depression relative to receiving nothing, while our analysis compares net receivers (who are structurally dependent) against balanced exchangers and net givers. The key distinction is not whether transfers arrive, but whether the older adult can reciprocate. He et al. (2024), using CHARLS data on 3,666 adults aged 45 and older, documented that intergenerational contact affected cognitive function through two mediating pathways — functional disability and depressive symptoms — providing direct evidence that family relational dynamics reach cognition through affective channels. Ren et al. (2025), also analyzing CHARLS data, decomposed intergenerational support into three dimensions (financial, instrumental, and emotional) and found that the effects on positive and negative emotions differed across dimensions, reinforcing the view that it is the relational configuration, not the volume of any single support type, that shapes mental health outcomes.

A noteworthy pattern in our results is that balanced exchangers, those with approximately zero net transfer, showed cognitive trajectories similar to net givers (Time \times Balanced = +0.047, p < .05; Time \times Net Giver = +0.064, p < .01). The distinction that matters is not whether older adults give more, but whether they avoid structural dependence. This pattern is consistent with Silverstein et al. (2002), who documented that different models of reciprocity (investment, insurance, and altruistic) operate in parent-child relations, and that the balance between giving and receiving carries distinct consequences for wellbeing. Bai et al. (2025), using CHARLS data and qualitative comparative analysis, identified “bidirectional care deficiency” and “bidirectional economic deficiency” as configurational patterns associated with depression in older adults, reinforcing the view that exchange imbalance generates health costs through multiple interacting channels rather than through any single dimension of support. Zeng et al. (2025), drawing on CHARLS 2015 and 2018 waves, found that intergenerational support from adult children shaped the intrinsic capacity of older adults through pathways involving physical, cognitive, and psychological domains, which aligns with our finding that the exchange-balance mechanism is not confined to a single health outcome.

The robustness analyses strengthen this interpretation. The parallel pattern in depressive symptoms (Time \times Net Giver = +0.090, p < .05) suggests that the exchange-balance mechanism operates through affective pathways, consistent with Choi et al. (2020), who found that receiving financial support without reciprocation elevated depression risk in Korean older adults. Silverstein and Zuo (2020), using six waves of panel data from rural Anhui Province, reported that grandparents who provided care for grandchildren showed better emotional health than non-caregiving grandparents, and that the benefits depended partly on the financial transfers received from migrant children — a finding that underscores how giving roles (caregiving) and receiving roles (financial transfers) interact to shape health in the Chinese intergenerational exchange system. The lagged dependent variable model attenuated effect sizes but preserved the direction of associations, suggesting that while reverse causality cannot be fully excluded, as cognitive decline may itself reduce the capacity to give, the longitudinal design with baseline controls supports an exchange-to-cognition pathway. Silverstein et al. (2025) emphasized that intergenerational support dynamics carry health implications that extend well beyond the caregiving domain, a conclusion our findings reinforce by demonstrating cognitive decline as a downstream consequence of exchange imbalance.

The Null Compensation Finding

The three-way interaction between time, exchange balance, and social participation was not statistically significant (coefficients of 0.002 and -0.004). Social participation was associated with 0.454 points higher cognition (p < .001), confirming its independent protective role. Yet this protection applied uniformly across exchange-balance groups rather than specifically buffering the penalty associated with net receiving. This null result is theoretically informative.

Rook (1987) demonstrated that the balance between giving and receiving in social relationships has direct implications for satisfaction and wellbeing. The Social Compensation Hypothesis extends this logic to suggest that deficits in one relational domain can be offset by resources from another (Zhao and Zhu 2026). Our results challenge this hypothesis in the cognitive domain. The reason may lie in the specificity of the psychological mechanism at work. The distress generated by being unable to reciprocate with one’s own children, a relationship laden with moral obligation under filial piety norms, operates through a particular channel: chronic feelings of indebtedness, loss of parental authority, and diminished sense of purpose within the family. Community social participation, by contrast, provides cognitive stimulation through novel information processing, social interaction, and engagement in structured activities. These two mechanisms are psychologically distinct. Participating in mahjong groups or exercise classes addresses social isolation and cognitive underload, but it does not resolve the family-level power imbalance that exchange theory identifies as the source of dependency costs. Yu et al. (2020), using two waves of CHARLS data, provided corroborating evidence for this distinction: social isolation, rather than loneliness, was associated with cognitive decline among Chinese older adults, suggesting that objective social network structure and subjective family-relational distress operate through separable pathways to affect cognition.

This interpretation is supported by A. Li et al. (2025), who used latent profile analysis to identify distinct bidirectional support profiles among Chinese older adults. Their findings showed that social participation could substitute for limited family support in some profiles but could not fully compensate when the family exchange relationship itself was imbalanced. W. Li et al. (2025), analyzing CHARLS 2020 data, found that social participation mediated the relationship between ADL disability and cognitive function, while intergenerational support from children played a moderating role — a finding that highlights how social participation and family support operate through different mechanisms rather than as interchangeable resources. The compensation mechanism, in other words, has boundary conditions: it works for general social resource deficits but not for the specific relational injury of dependency within the parent-child bond. Ning et al. (2023), using four waves of CHARLS, showed that social participation and depressive symptoms both mediated the path from cognitive frailty to functional disability, confirming social participation’s independent protective channel while underscoring that depressive pathways (linked to family dynamics) remain distinct.

The urban-rural stratification offers a partial qualification. In urban areas, balanced exchangers declined significantly slower than net receivers (Time \times Balanced = +0.073, p < .05), while the corresponding rural coefficient was small and nonsignificant (+0.017). Zhu et al. (2025) documented that urban older adults had different trajectories of social participation and cognitive function than their rural counterparts, reflecting differences in community infrastructure, activity options, and institutional resources. Chen and Ang (2025a) provided a complementary finding: age moderated the association between social participation and mental health differently in urban and rural areas, with urban older adults continuing to gain mental health benefits from participation while rural adults experienced diminishing returns at older ages. Urban environments may provide richer opportunities for maintaining reciprocal social roles outside the family, which could partially attenuate exchange-balance effects. Yet even in urban areas, the three-way interaction remained nonsignificant, suggesting that the compensation mechanism is weak even where community resources are most abundant.

Implications and Limitations

These findings carry three implications for policy and practice. First, support programs for older adults should go beyond increasing the volume of resource transfers and attend to the direction and balance of exchange. Policies that help older adults maintain giving roles, such as grandchild education support, community mentoring programs, or neighborhood mutual aid networks, may protect cognitive health by preserving reciprocity within intergenerational relationships. The goal is not to discourage financial transfers to older parents, but to create opportunities for older adults to contribute meaningfully in return, thereby avoiding the structural dependence that our results link to accelerated decline. Pan et al. (2021), in a cross-national comparison of noncustodial grandparents in China and the United States, found that grandparenting involvement was associated with better cognitive function among Chinese grandparents, illustrating how structured giving roles within the family can serve as a vehicle for cognitive maintenance.

Second, community social participation programs protect cognition independently of exchange dynamics and deserve investment on their own terms. The 0.454-point association with higher cognition represents a meaningful effect that applies across all exchange-balance groups. Scalable community-based activities such as friend circles, mahjong groups, and exercise programs require modest infrastructure and can reach large populations (Pan and Chee 2020). Lin et al. (2023), using data from the Chinese Longitudinal Healthy Longevity Survey, found that home and community-based services were positively associated with cognitive function among older adults, with social participation mediating this relationship — providing direct evidence that community-level interventions can protect cognition through the social engagement channel. That these programs do not specifically buffer the exchange-imbalance penalty does not diminish their population-level value for cognitive health.

Third, the urban-rural disparity in the exchange-balance effect highlights a structural inequality in community infrastructure. Rural areas, where labor out-migration has left aging parents with fewer family members nearby and fewer community alternatives, may face a compounded disadvantage, because older adults who are net receivers have both less access to reciprocal family exchanges and fewer community resources to compensate (Chen and Ang 2025b). Yang et al. (2021), using Harmonised CHARLS data across three waves, found that rural residents were disadvantaged relative to urban residents in the health returns to intergenerational support, partly because rural living arrangements and support configurations offered fewer protective buffers. Gao et al. (2024) reported that empty nest status among rural older adults with chronic diseases was associated with worse cognitive function, underscoring how the absence of nearby family members compounds cognitive risk in resource-poor settings. Vatsyayan and Ferdows (2025) documented parallel rural-urban differences in social isolation and dementia risk among older adults in India, suggesting that the structural disadvantage of rural aging extends beyond the Chinese context. Strengthening rural community programs is a policy priority that intersects with both cognitive health promotion and regional equity.

Several limitations warrant consideration. First, reverse causality remains a concern despite our longitudinal design. Cognitive decline may reduce older adults’ capacity to earn income and give financial transfers, thereby shifting them into net-receiver positions. The lagged dependent variable model attenuated but did not eliminate the exchange-balance coefficients, and we cannot rule out the possibility that the association partly reflects a cognitive-decline-to-dependency pathway. Second, only financial transfers were measured. The CHARLS Harmonized dataset does not include time or care transfers in Wave 4, so we could not capture the full spectrum of intergenerational exchange. Older adults who provide childcare or household labor may be “giving” in ways our financial measure does not detect. Third, social participation was measured as a binary indicator, which collapses important distinctions among activity types, frequencies, and social contexts. Formal organizational participation, casual friend visits, and cognitively demanding games like mahjong likely differ in their protective mechanisms, but our measure could not distinguish among them. Fourth, selective attrition biased our sample toward healthier, younger, and more educated respondents. Attriters had lower baseline cognition and more functional limitations, meaning our estimates likely understate the true exchange-balance effect. Fifth, the cultural specificity of these findings deserves attention. Filial piety norms in Chinese society frame financial support from children as a moral obligation, which may buffer the dependency distress our models capture (Cheng and Chan 2006; Xiao et al. 2024). The same exchange imbalance might generate stronger or weaker cognitive costs in cultural contexts with different normative expectations about intergenerational reciprocity.

Conclusion

Using four waves of CHARLS data (2011–2018, N = 12,742), this study found that older adults who were net receivers of intergenerational financial transfers experienced significantly faster cognitive decline than balanced exchangers (b = +0.047, p < .05) and net givers (b = +0.064, p < .01), accumulating a 0.45-point cognitive disadvantage over seven years. Three conclusions follow from these results. First, exchange balance matters more than transfer amount: the critical distinction is not how much older adults give or receive, but whether they occupy a position of structural dependence. Second, community social participation protects cognition independently (b = +0.454, p < .001) but does not buffer the specific penalty associated with exchange imbalance, suggesting that family-level dependency distress and community-level social isolation operate through distinct mechanisms. Third, policy should prioritize preserving reciprocity roles for older adults, creating opportunities for them to contribute rather than simply increasing the flow of resources toward them. Future research should test whether interventions that create structured giving opportunities for dependent older adults, such as community teaching, mentoring, or mutual aid programs, can slow cognitive decline trajectories, and whether such programs are especially effective for those currently trapped in net-receiver positions.
