Extended Methodological Details

Health and Retirement Study Design

The Health and Retirement Study (HRS) is a nationally representative longitudinal panel survey sponsored by the National Institute on Aging and conducted by the University of Michigan. Initiated in 1992, the HRS follows Americans aged 50 and older through biennial in-person and telephone interviews, refreshing its sample every six years to maintain representativeness of the 50+ population. Waves 9–14 (2008–2018) used in the present study were conducted in survey years 2008, 2010, 2012, 2014, 2016, and 2018.

The HRS employs a complex probability sampling design that oversamples Black and Hispanic Americans and residents of Florida. We use the HRS-provided individual weights in descriptive statistics but rely on unweighted regression models for RSA, following standard practice in longitudinal panel analysis with fixed-effects-adjacent controls. The HRS is approved by the University of Michigan’s Institutional Review Board.

Variable Construction and Coding

Objective Health (X). The ADL scale in HRS measures self-reported difficulty or inability to perform six tasks: bathing or showering, dressing, eating, getting in and out of bed, walking across a room, and using the toilet. The IADL scale measures difficulty with five tasks: using a map to find locations, managing medications, handling money and bills, making phone calls, and grocery shopping. For each task, respondents indicated whether they had any difficulty. We summed across all 11 items (range 0–11), reverse-coded so that higher scores indicate fewer limitations (better function), and standardized across the full person-wave sample. The resulting variable X has mean \approx 0 and SD \approx 1, with positive values indicating better-than-average functional health.

Self-Rated Health (Y). HRS asks respondents: “Would you say your health is excellent, very good, good, fair, or poor?” Responses were coded 1 (poor) to 5 (excellent). We standardized this variable across the full person-wave sample to yield Y.

Life Satisfaction (Z). HRS includes the item “All things considered, how satisfied are you with your life these days?” with responses from 1 (not at all satisfied) to 5 (completely satisfied). We standardized to yield Z.

Subjective Social Status. We use the Cantril Self-Anchoring Ladder (1–10) as a measure of subjective social standing, consistent with prior HRS-based research (Singh-Manoux et al. 2005).

Wealth. Total non-housing wealth (from RAND HRS data) was log-transformed as \log(\max(\text{wealth}, 0) + 1) to accommodate zero or negative wealth values (negative wealth set to 0 before transformation).

Sample Construction and Attrition

Table S1 presents the step-by-step sample selection process. We began with all HRS respondents who appeared in any of Waves 9–15 (n = 24,891 unique individuals). We then applied the following sequential exclusions:

  1. Wave 9 age \geq 60: Restricted to respondents aged 60 or older at their Wave 9 interview, establishing a cohort with homogeneous aging trajectory exposure (n = 16{,}842 retained).

  2. \leq 2 missing on life satisfaction across Waves 9–14: Excluded respondents with more than 2 missing waves on the standardized life satisfaction item (n = 11{,}207 retained).

  3. \leq 2 missing on ADL count: Applied the same criterion to the ADL limitation measure (n = 8{,}932 retained).

  4. \leq 2 missing on IADL count: Applied to the IADL limitation measure (n = 7{,}812 retained).

  5. \leq 2 missing on SRH: Applied to the self-rated health item (n = 6{,}708 retained; final analytic sample).

The requirement that respondents have observed (not necessarily non-missing) data across Waves 9–14 means that our sample is balanced in the sense that each person has up to six wave-observations, with residual missing values within this window imputed. Respondents excluded primarily due to mortality between waves (approximately 3,200) and sustained non-response, both of which are likely non-random. The excluded sample is somewhat older, in poorer health, and disproportionately male and non-White at Wave 9, consistent with known HRS attrition patterns. We address this limitation through missRanger imputation within the retained sample and through the stability of our findings across waves—including later waves where survivorship is more selective.

Missing Data Imputation

Within the final analytic sample (n = 6{,}708), residual missing values on time-varying covariates (marital status, region, insurance, employment, wealth) were imputed using the missRanger algorithm (Mayer 2023), which extends random forest imputation with predictive mean matching. Imputation was conducted on the wide-format dataset (with wave-specific variable columns), then reshaped to long format for analysis. The imputation model included all wave-specific observations of focal variables, demographics, and covariates.

Imputation rates for the key variables in the retained sample were low: less than 3% of person-wave observations had missing values on any single covariate after restricting to the balanced panel. Post-imputation checks confirmed that univariate distributions and bivariate correlations were preserved relative to the complete-case subsample.

Response Surface Analysis: Technical Details

RSA models were estimated using lavaan-based structural equation modeling as implemented in the RSA R package (version 0.10.6; Schönbrodt (2016)). The full polynomial model specified in Equation (1) of the main text was compared against a series of nested and alternative models using chi-square difference tests and AICc; full model comparison statistics are presented in Supplementary Table S3 (Section S4). The full model provided significantly better fit than the interaction-only (IA) model (\chi^2_{\text{diff}}(2) = 69.9, p < .001) and the additive model (\chi^2_{\text{diff}}(3) = 133.2, p < .001), confirming that the quadratic and interaction terms carry meaningful information. The full model was not significantly worse than the unconstrained cubic model (\chi^2_{\text{diff}}(4) = 6.3, p = .177), supporting the adequacy of the polynomial specification.

Surface parameters (a_1–a_5) are derived from polynomial coefficients (b_1–b_5) using the delta method for standard error estimation as described in the main text. The location of the stationary point (the point on the surface where both partial derivatives are zero) was estimated at X = -1.183, Y = 4.469—well outside the observed data range, indicating that the surface does not “peak” within the realistic health distribution but continues rising toward the high-health region.

For the wave-by-wave analyses, each wave’s RSA model was estimated separately with identical control specifications. The wave-9 model used n = 6{,}708 persons; each subsequent wave’s model drew on the same set of persons (balanced panel), ensuring comparability across waves.

Panel C Control Variable Estimates. The RSA package’s lavaan implementation treats all continuous and factor control variables as single numeric predictors. Because categorical variables (marital status, race/ethnicity) are stored as R factors with integer codes, the lavaan SEM produces a single slope per variable rather than separate dummy variable contrasts. To present more interpretable and methodologically transparent control variable estimates in Table 2 Panel C, we re-estimated an equivalent OLS polynomial model with properly dummy-coded categorical predictors (reference categories: Married, Non-Hispanic White, South census region). The RSA surface parameters (a_1–a_5) reported in Panels A and B are identical across both specifications; only the presentation of categorical control variables differs. Panel C OLS estimates are based on ordinary least squares with robust (HC3) standard errors; 95% CIs use OLS normal approximation.

Bootstrap Tests of Parameter Stability

The 5,000-replication person-cluster bootstrap resampled individuals (with replacement) rather than person-wave observations, preserving the within-person longitudinal structure. At each bootstrap replication, all six wave-specific RSA models were estimated; surface parameters were extracted for each wave; and all 15 pairwise differences for each of the five RSA parameters were computed. This yielded a 75-element vector of parameter differences for each replication, from which empirical 95% confidence intervals were constructed.

Table S2 presents all bootstrap-estimated wave-pair differences for each RSA parameter, with 95% confidence intervals. Among the 75 tests, exactly two reached statistical significance: the change in a_2 (LOC curvature) from Wave 11 to Wave 12 (\Delta a_2 = -0.055, 95% CI [-0.098, -0.011]) and from Wave 11 to Wave 14 (\Delta a_2 = -0.058, 95% CI [-0.100, -0.016]). No significant changes were detected in a_1, a_3, a_4, or a_5 across any wave pair, supporting the conclusion of structural stability in the congruence and asymmetry effects.

Sensitivity Analyses

Complete-Case Analysis

To assess whether missRanger imputation influenced our estimates, we re-estimated the pooled RSA model using only person-wave observations with complete data on all focal variables and covariates (no imputation). The complete-case subsample comprised approximately 36,800 person-wave observations (\sim 91\% of the full analytic sample). Surface parameter estimates were: a_1 = 0.371 (95% CI [0.345, 0.397]), a_2 = -0.035 (95% CI [-0.050, -0.020]), a_3 = -0.141 (95% CI [-0.172, -0.109]), a_4 = -0.001 (95% CI [-0.021, 0.020]), a_5 = 0.043 (95% CI [0.032, 0.054]). These are within 0.005 of the main model estimates for all parameters, confirming that imputation did not materially alter our findings.

Gender-Stratified Models

We estimated separate RSA models for women (n = 4{,}090 individuals; 24,540 person-waves) and men (n = 2{,}618 individuals; 15,708 person-waves). For women: a_1 = 0.381 (p < .001), a_2 = -0.041 (p < .001), a_3 = -0.158 (p < .001), a_4 = 0.002 (p = .883), a_5 = 0.048 (p < .001). For men: a_1 = 0.362 (p < .001), a_2 = -0.031 (p = .001), a_3 = -0.127 (p < .001), a_4 = -0.002 (p = .911), a_5 = 0.039 (p < .001). Both the congruence benefit (a_1) and the optimism advantage (a_3) are significant in both subgroups and directionally identical. The magnitude of a_3 is modestly larger among women, consistent with the larger literature suggesting that health self-perceptions are more behaviorally and psychologically consequential for women’s well-being (Benyamini et al. 2000). Bootstrap tests for gender differences in a_3 yield 95% CI for the gender gap of [-0.063, -0.009], significant at p < .05.

ADL-Only Objective Health Measure

As an alternative operationalization of objective health, we replaced the ADL+IADL composite with ADL limitations only (range 0–6, reverse-coded and standardized). Results were substantively identical: a_1 = 0.358 (95% CI [0.334, 0.382]), a_3 = -0.142 (95% CI [-0.173, -0.111]). The IADL component does not drive our findings, and the pattern is robust to this alternative operationalization.

Race/Ethnicity-Stratified Models

We estimated models separately for Non-Hispanic White (n = 5{,}128), Non-Hispanic Black (n = 883), and Hispanic (n = 561) respondents (the “Other” category was too small for reliable separate estimation). For Non-Hispanic White respondents: a_3 = -0.148 (p < .001). For Non-Hispanic Black respondents: a_3 = -0.134 (p = .003). For Hispanic respondents: a_3 = -0.097 (p = .094, marginally significant). The congruence benefit (a_1) was significant and positive for all three groups. The weaker a_3 among Hispanic respondents may reflect culturally specific patterns in health self-assessment, though the smaller sample size limits interpretation.

Wave-by-Wave Response Surface Plots

Figures S1 and S2 display the three-dimensional response surfaces and contour plots, respectively, for each of the six waves (9–14) analyzed separately. The stability of the surface geometry—particularly the consistent tilt toward the optimistic misalignment region—is visually apparent across all waves, supporting the quantitative conclusion that the RSA structure is temporally stable.

Nested RSA Model Comparisons

To verify the choice of the full second-order polynomial specification, we compared five nested RSA models using RSA::compare() (Schönbrodt 2016). Models are ordered from most constrained (null, controls only) to least constrained (cubic, unrestricted third-order). The chi-square difference statistic (\Delta\chi^2) for each row compares the focal model against the next more complex model in the sequence.
