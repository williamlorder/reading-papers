Generational Imprinting and the Persistence of Low Fertility Ideals:
An Age-Period-Cohort Analysis of Ideal Number of Children in China, 2010–2023

[Blinded for Review]

Abstract

China reversed its one-child policy through three successive liberalizations between 2013 and 2021, yet stated fertility ideals have not increased. Using Hierarchical Age-Period-Cohort (HAPC) models on eight waves of the Chinese General Social Survey (2010–2023; N = 79{,}991), we decompose the ideal number of children into age, period, and cohort components. Cohort effects are an order of magnitude larger than period effects (cohort \sigma^2 = 0.019 versus period \sigma^2 \approx 0.000), indicating that generational socialization under the one-child policy, not contemporary policy changes, drives variation in fertility ideals. Women show three times the cohort variance of men (\sigma^2 = 0.041 versus 0.014), and the gender gap in ideals is twice as large in urban areas (\beta = -0.108) as in rural areas (\beta = -0.060). Age effects follow a U-shaped pattern, with the youngest and oldest groups expressing higher ideals than middle-aged respondents. Actual number of children is the strongest individual-level predictor (\beta = 0.319), while marriage is associated with lower, not higher, fertility ideals (\beta = -0.086). An Age-Period-Cohort-Interaction (APCI) model corroborates these patterns under alternative identifying assumptions. These results suggest that pronatalist policy reversal cannot undo generational norm change, and that gender-specific interventions targeting the opportunity costs of childbearing may be more effective than universal birth-permit relaxation.

Introduction

China’s three-child policy, announced in May 2021, was designed to reverse four decades of state-imposed fertility restriction. Two years later, the average ideal number of children among Chinese adults fell to 1.87, the lowest value recorded in fifteen years of the Chinese General Social Survey (CGSS). This paper explains why pronatalist policy reversal has failed to raise fertility ideals: the one-child policy did not merely constrain behavior; it reshaped the norms of an entire generation.

The stakes are considerable. China’s total fertility rate dropped below 1.1 in 2023, among the lowest in recorded demographic history (Zhai and Jin 2023; Yang et al. 2022). Policy responses have focused on removing administrative restrictions, from the selective two-child relaxation in 2013 to the universal two-child policy in 2016 and the three-child policy in 2021. The implicit assumption underlying these interventions is that pent-up demand for children exists and will be released once restrictions are lifted. Our findings challenge this assumption. If stated fertility ideals have declined not because of contemporary policy constraints but because of generational norm change, removing restrictions addresses the wrong problem.

Prior research has documented the persistent two-child norm in Chinese fertility preferences (Chen and Gietel‐Basten 2023; Zheng et al. 2009) and the limited behavioral response to policy liberalization (Zhai and Jin 2023; Wang et al. 2025). What existing studies cannot explain is whether the observed stability in aggregate fertility ideals conceals meaningful variation across birth cohorts, survey periods, and stages of the life course. Standard regression analyses confound these three temporal dimensions. Without a formal Age-Period-Cohort (APC) decomposition, it is impossible to determine whether changes in fertility ideals reflect life-course maturation (age effects), macro-level shocks that affect all respondents simultaneously (period effects), or lasting differences between generations socialized under different policy regimes (cohort effects).

We address this gap by estimating Hierarchical Age-Period-Cohort (HAPC) models (Yang and Land 2006, 2008) on eight waves of CGSS data spanning 2010 to 2023 (N = 79{,}991). This design exploits the temporal structure of China’s policy transitions: the eight survey waves bracket three major policy changes, while the birth cohorts in our sample range from those born in the 1930s (who came of age before the one-child policy) to those born after 2000 (who never experienced reproductive restrictions). We supplement the HAPC estimates with the Age-Period-Cohort-Interaction (APCI) model (Xu and Luo 2022) as a robustness check that does not rely on the same identifying assumptions.

Three principal findings emerge. First, cohort effects on ideal number of children are an order of magnitude larger than period effects (cohort \sigma^2 = 0.019 versus period \sigma^2 \approx 0.000), indicating that generational socialization, not contemporary policy signals, drives variation in fertility ideals. Second, women exhibit dramatically larger cohort differentiation than men (cohort \sigma^2 = 0.041 versus 0.014), with the gender gap in fertility ideals twice as large among urban residents (-0.108) as among their rural counterparts (-0.060). Third, actual number of children is the strongest individual-level predictor of fertility ideals (\beta = 0.319), while married respondents report lower ideals than the never-married (\beta = -0.086), suggesting that lived experience constrains rather than inflates stated preferences.

This study makes three contributions to the literature on fertility and population policy. First, we provide the first systematic HAPC decomposition of fertility ideals in China, demonstrating that the apparent stability of the two-child norm masks substantial cohort-level variation. Second, we identify women as the primary demographic agents of declining fertility ideals, a finding with direct implications for the design of pronatalist policy. Third, we offer methodological triangulation by applying the APCI model to Chinese fertility preference data, addressing longstanding concerns about HAPC identification (Bell and Jones 2014; Fosse and Winship 2019).

Background and Theoretical Framework

Fertility Preferences in Demographic Theory

The ideal number of children occupies a distinctive position in the architecture of fertility research. Unlike fertility intentions, which reflect plans contingent on current circumstances, and unlike actual fertility, which incorporates involuntary constraints, the stated ideal captures a normative orientation toward family size that functions as a leading indicator of reproductive culture (Hagewen and Morgan 2005). When ideal family size shifts downward across a population, actual fertility tends to follow, though the two may diverge for years or decades due to institutional barriers, economic constraints, and timing adjustments. Bongaarts (1998) demonstrated that in post-transitional societies, desired family size converges near two children while actual fertility frequently falls below replacement, a gap attributable to postponement, union instability, and rising opportunity costs rather than to any desire for childlessness. The distinction between the tempo and quantum of fertility is central here: Bongaarts and Feeney (1998) showed that tempo distortions—shifts in the timing of births—can depress period fertility rates well below the level implied by completed family size, meaning that observed TFR declines may overstate the fall in lifetime fertility desires. Globally, Bongaarts (2020) documented that while fertility rates have converged toward replacement in most regions, desired family size varies substantially across societies and has continued to decline in East Asia, suggesting that the quantum itself is shifting downward rather than merely the tempo. The implication is that when ideals themselves fall below two, the usual mechanisms for closing the ideal-behavior gap cease to operate as a floor on fertility.

The Second Demographic Transition (SDT) framework locates the engine of preference change in value transformation. As societies industrialize and secularize, individual autonomy, self-expression, and gender egalitarianism displace collective obligations and familistic norms (Surkyn and Lesthaeghe 2004; Sobotka 2008). In this account, below-replacement fertility preferences emerge not from policy coercion but from a shift in what individuals want from life. Inglehart and Baker (2000) showed, using World Values Survey data from 65 societies, that economic development drives systematic value change toward rationality, tolerance, and self-expression, yet cultural heritage leaves an enduring imprint—Confucian, Protestant, and Catholic societies retain distinctive value profiles even at similar income levels. The wealth flows theory offers a complementary economic logic: as the direction of intergenerational resource transfers reverses from children-to-parents toward parents-to-children, the economic rationale for large families collapses (Caldwell 2005). In China, this reversal has been particularly sharp, with parental investment in a single child’s education and housing consuming a growing share of household resources.

Whether SDT dynamics apply to China remains contested. Yu and Xie (2022) found that key behavioral indicators of the SDT—delayed marriage, rising cohabitation, tolerance of nonmarital childbearing—are present in China, but that ideational changes precede behavioral ones, and that China’s pattern departs from Western trajectories in important respects. The central question for our study is whether Chinese fertility ideals reflect the gradual value change predicted by SDT theory or a more abrupt normative shift induced by the one-child policy. An age-period-cohort framework can distinguish these possibilities: SDT-driven preference change should appear as smooth cohort trends tracking modernization, while policy-induced change should produce discontinuities aligned with the birth cohorts directly exposed to the one-child regime.

China’s Fertility Policy Regime and Its Temporal Structure

China’s birth planning policies have passed through four distinct phases, each creating a different normative environment for the cohorts socialized under it. The first phase, from the early 1970s through 1979, centered on the “later, longer, fewer” campaign, which encouraged delayed marriage, longer birth intervals, and smaller families. This campaign alone reduced the total fertility rate from approximately 5.7 in 1969 to 2.7 by 1978 (Chen and Huang 2020), initiating a decline that would push China below replacement fertility by the early 1990s (S. et al. 2009).

The second phase, the one-child policy proper (1979–2015), imposed strict birth quotas enforced through a combination of economic penalties, administrative sanctions, and social pressure (Scharping 2013). Enforcement, however, was not uniform. Urban residents faced the strictest constraints: fines for unauthorized births, loss of employment in state enterprises, and denial of household registration for second children. Rural households received partial exemptions—couples whose first child was a daughter could apply for a second birth in most provinces—and ethnic minorities were permitted two or three children depending on region (Zhang 2017; Yang and Wu 2018). Even before the one-child policy, planned fertility decline was already under way in rural areas; Yang (1990) documented how collective-era mobilization campaigns in Anhui province reduced birth rates through a combination of ideological persuasion and material incentives. The enforcement of birth quotas intersected with the hukou system, which Chan and Zhang (1999) characterized as a key institutional mechanism structuring rural-urban inequality in China, creating sharply different reproductive environments for urban and rural populations. This differential enforcement is analytically consequential. Urban cohorts born between 1980 and the late 1990s grew up in families where the single-child household was not merely common but legally mandated. Rural cohorts of the same era experienced a more permissive regime, with many observing two-child or even three-child families among neighbors and kin. Cai and Wang (2021) documented how the one-child policy altered China’s social fabric across nearly four decades, reshaping gender relations, family structures, and intergenerational expectations in fundamental ways.

The third and fourth phases represent policy reversal. In November 2013, the selective two-child policy allowed couples in which one spouse was an only child to have a second birth. In January 2016, the universal two-child policy extended this permission to all couples. In May 2021, a three-child policy was announced, accompanied by rhetoric about “fertility-supportive” social infrastructure (Zhai and Jin 2023; H. Yang et al. 2023). Early assessments suggest that the three-child policy has had limited impact on fertility intentions, with structural constraints outweighing the symbolic effect of permit relaxation (Xu 2023). The response to these liberalizations was modest. Jiang and Liu (2016) showed that China’s low fertility was sustained by factors well beyond the birth quota itself, and the selective two-child policy produced fewer applicants than projected. The universal two-child policy generated a temporary rebound in births in 2016–2017, but the effect dissipated by 2018, and births have declined each year since (Chen 2023). City-level studies confirmed the pattern: Qiu et al. (2019) found that even in Dalian, where economic conditions were favorable, fertility intentions remained low after the two-child relaxation, with most respondents citing economic burden and childcare costs as primary deterrents. Chen and Gietel‐Basten (2023) exploited the 2015 policy shift in a difference-in-differences design and found that removing the one-child restriction increased ideal family size by approximately 0.2 children among married individuals aged 20–39, but sub-replacement ideals in urban China remained genuine even after the policy constraint was lifted.

For APC analysis, this policy chronology generates a testable prediction. If pronatalist policy reversal raises fertility ideals, the effect should appear as a positive period shift after 2013 or 2016. If, instead, fertility ideals are governed by formative experiences during adolescence, the policy reversals should produce negligible period effects while cohort effects corresponding to the one-child generation remain dominant.

Generational Socialization and Cohort Imprinting

The sociology of generations holds that attitudes and values formed during late adolescence and early adulthood tend to persist across the life course, even when the structural conditions that produced them change. Individuals do not continuously update their normative orientations in response to new information; rather, the worldview absorbed during formative years becomes a durable cognitive frame through which later experiences are interpreted. Applied to fertility, this perspective predicts that cohorts who came of age under the one-child policy—born roughly between 1980 and 1999—should carry the small-family norm they absorbed during childhood into their adult reproductive years, regardless of subsequent policy liberalization.

Chen and Gietel‐Basten (2025) provided direct causal evidence for this mechanism. Using a fuzzy regression discontinuity design around the 1980 introduction of the one-child policy, they showed that being an only child significantly reduces ideal family size among urban Chinese. The effect operates through what they term a “low-fertility trap” channel: individuals raised without siblings treat the one-child family as the default, and this default persists into adulthood as a normative anchor. The finding is consistent with socialization theory but goes further by identifying a specific transmission pathway—sibship size during childhood—rather than relying on period-of-birth as a proxy for exposure.

The erosion of son preference across cohorts provides additional evidence of generational norm change. Ni (2023) found through qualitative interviews with one-child-generation cohorts in urban Jiangsu that son preference has given way to a more balanced preference composition, with two-daughter families gaining greater acceptance among younger couples. At the population level, Tang and Hou (2024) documented a corresponding decline in the sex ratio at birth, tracing the weakening of son preference across successive cohorts of parents. In rural areas, Tilt et al. (2019) found that fertility trends and son preference differ between Han and ethnic minority households, with minority communities retaining higher fertility norms even as sex ratio imbalances moderate. This shift matters because son preference historically sustained demand for additional births: couples who produced a daughter first would attempt a second or third birth to obtain a son. As son preference weakens among one-child-generation cohorts, one traditional motivation for larger families weakens with it.

The gendered dimension of cohort change deserves particular attention. Urban women in the one-child generation experienced a confluence of forces that depressed fertility ideals more sharply than for men of the same cohorts. They were raised in single-child families, obtained higher levels of education than any previous female cohort in Chinese history (Chen 2023), and delayed marriage to ages that would have been unusual a generation earlier (Song et al. 2023). They entered a labor market that penalizes maternal career interruptions and confronted housing costs that make the economic burden of children especially visible. In rural areas, internet access has exposed women to urban lifestyle models and expanded information about the costs of childrearing, further depressing fertility preferences (Li and Wei 2023). Cheng (2020) reviewed the institutional context of ultra-low fertility across East Asia and emphasized that Confucian patriarchal expectations—which assign women primary responsibility for childcare while demanding continued labor force participation—create compounding opportunity costs that rational actors seek to minimize by limiting family size. In urban China, these opportunity costs fall disproportionately on women, a pattern that should manifest as larger cohort effects among women than among men if generational socialization is the operative mechanism.

The Age-Period-Cohort Framework for Fertility Ideals

Trend data on fertility ideals confound three temporal processes. Age effects capture life-course dynamics: as individuals marry, bear children, and accumulate resources, their stated ideals may shift. Period effects capture macro-level shocks that affect all respondents in a given survey year: a new policy announcement, an economic crisis, or a media campaign. Cohort effects capture generational socialization: the durable imprint of formative experiences shared by individuals born in the same historical window. Without a framework for separating these processes, one cannot determine whether a decline in fertility ideals reflects population aging, contemporaneous economic conditions, or a genuine generational shift in reproductive norms.

The hierarchical age-period-cohort cross-classified random effects model (HAPC-CCREM), developed by Yang and Land (2006) and elaborated in Yang and Land (2008) and Yang and Land (2013), addresses this challenge by treating period and cohort as crossed random grouping factors at the second level of a multilevel model. Individual responses are modeled as a function of age (fixed effects) plus random intercepts for the period in which the survey was conducted and the birth cohort to which the respondent belongs. The variance components \sigma^2_{\text{period}} and \sigma^2_{\text{cohort}} provide a direct comparison of the relative importance of period versus cohort variation, net of age and individual-level controls.

The APC identification problem—the exact linear dependency \text{Cohort} = \text{Period} - \text{Age}—has been recognized as a fundamental challenge since Fienberg and Mason (1979), and the HAPC model has been subject to sustained methodological scrutiny in this light. Bell and Jones (2014) demonstrated through simulations that the model can misattribute linear trends when both period and cohort effects are present, and Bell and Jones (2015) and Bell and Jones (2017) argued that HAPC estimates may reflect data structure rather than substantive temporal processes. Fosse and Winship (2019) provided a comprehensive review of the identification problem inherent in all APC methods and outlined conditions under which different approaches yield reliable estimates. In response, Reither et al. (2015) demonstrated through extensive simulations that HAPC models perform well when basic assumptions are met, particularly when one temporal dimension exhibits minimal linear trend. These critiques and defenses do not invalidate the HAPC framework but impose conditions on its appropriate use, reducing the risk of confounding.

As a robustness strategy, we supplement the HAPC with the age-period-cohort-interaction (APC-I) model (Xu and Luo 2022), which decomposes temporal variation without relying on random-effects assumptions. The APC-I model identifies age and period main effects through sum-to-zero constraints, estimates cohort average deviations from the age-by-period surface, and recovers cohort-specific life-course slopes. Convergence between HAPC and APC-I estimates provides stronger evidence that the temporal decomposition is not an artifact of either model’s identifying assumptions. The broader literature on mixed models for APC analysis (O’Brien 2017) provides additional guidance on when random-effects specifications are appropriate for temporal decomposition. Recent applications of HAPC models to Chinese social attitudes—including gender attitudes (Su and Ottervik 2023) and social values (Gao et al. 2024)—have demonstrated the framework’s utility for studying generational change in the Chinese context, and APC methods have been applied to other outcomes in China such as cognitive aging (Yang and Yu 2024), though no prior study has applied this framework to fertility ideals.

Research Gap and Hypotheses

No study has used hierarchical APC methods to decompose age, period, and cohort effects on fertility ideals in China. Existing research documents the persistence of a two-child norm and the limited impact of policy liberalization, but treats these as period-level phenomena rather than testing whether the primary source of variation is generational. By applying both HAPC and APC-I models to eight waves of nationally representative survey data, we can adjudicate between two competing accounts: a period-driven narrative in which pronatalist policy shapes contemporaneous fertility ideals, and a cohort-driven narrative in which generational socialization under the one-child regime produces durable preference change that policy reversal cannot undo.

We test four hypotheses, each mapped to a specific model parameter:

H1:

Cohort effects on ideal fertility are larger than period effects. If the one-child policy created lasting generational norms, the cohort variance component (\sigma^2_{\text{cohort}}) should substantially exceed the period variance component (\sigma^2_{\text{period}}).

H2:

Cohorts socialized under the one-child policy exhibit lower fertility ideals. Cohort random effects for the 1980–1999 birth cohorts should be negative relative to the grand mean, reflecting the normative imprint of growing up in single-child families.

H3:

The urban–rural gap in fertility ideals reflects differential cohort socialization. Because urban enforcement of the one-child policy was stricter than rural enforcement, the cohort gradient in fertility ideals should be steeper for urban residents, manifesting as a significant random slope of hukou status across cohorts.

H4:

Women drive cohort-level fertility norm change. Given the compounding opportunity costs of childbearing for urban women and their concentrated exposure to single-child socialization, the cohort variance among women (\sigma^2_{\text{cohort, women}}) should exceed that among men (\sigma^2_{\text{cohort, men}}).

Data and Methods

Data

We use data from the Chinese General Social Survey (CGSS), China’s longest-running nationally representative repeated cross-sectional household survey, conducted annually or biennially since 2003 by the National Survey Research Center at Renmin University of China. The CGSS has been widely used to study social attitudes and wellbeing in China (Xu et al. 2023). The CGSS employs a multi-stage stratified probability sampling design covering all provinces, autonomous regions, and municipalities in mainland China, with respondents drawn from both urban and rural households. We pool eight waves containing our dependent variable: 2010, 2012, 2013, 2015, 2017, 2018, 2021, and 2023. The 2023 wave adopted a mixed-mode design incorporating telephone interviews alongside traditional face-to-face administration. We restrict the analytic sample to respondents aged 18–70, exclude cases with missing data on the dependent variable, and arrive at a final pooled sample of N = 79{,}991 individual-level observations.

Dependent Variable

The dependent variable is the ideal number of children, measured by the item: “If there were no policy restrictions, how many children would you want?” This question appears in all eight waves, though the variable name differs across years (a371, a37a, A37_1). We recode special response codes (-3, -2, -1, 96, 97, 98, 99, and 100) as missing, and treat values exceeding 10 as probable entry errors, also coded as missing. The resulting variable is treated as continuous in all models. In the pooled sample, the mean ideal number of children is 1.94 (SD = 0.88), with the distribution concentrated at 2, reflecting a persistent two-child norm. Values of 0 and 1 together account for roughly one-fifth of responses, while values of 3 or higher account for approximately 15%.

Independent Variables

Age-period-cohort variables.

We construct a standard APC framework. Age enters the model as a set of fixed effects for eleven 5-year age groups (18–22 through 68–72), with 18–22 as the reference category, allowing the model to capture non-linear age patterns without imposing a functional form. Period is defined by the eight survey years (2010, 2012, 2013, 2015, 2017, 2018, 2021, 2023). Birth cohort is constructed as five-year groups derived from each respondent’s year of birth (survey year minus age), yielding 18 cohort categories spanning pre-1945 to post-2000.

Individual-level controls.

We include sex (female = 1), education in four categories (junior high school or below [reference], senior high school or vocational, associate degree, bachelor’s degree or above), rural hukou status (1 = agricultural household registration), marital status in three categories (never married [reference], currently married, formerly married including divorced and widowed), logged personal annual income, religious affiliation (1 = any religious identification), ethnic minority status (1 = non-Han ethnicity), and actual number of children (continuous, computed as total sons plus total daughters). Variable names and coding differ across CGSS waves; we harmonize all variables to a common coding scheme (details in the online appendix).

Missing Data

We handle item-level missingness using the missRanger algorithm, a random-forest-based chained imputation method with predictive mean matching (k = 5) to preserve distributional properties. Pre-imputation missing rates are as follows: actual number of children is highest at 24.7%, driven primarily by questionnaire skip logic in the 2023 wave; personal income is missing at 8.4%; ideal number of children at 2.5%; and all remaining variables fall below 3%. We generate a single imputed dataset and conduct all analyses on the completed data, treating the dependent variable’s missing cases as listwise deletions prior to imputation.

Analytical Strategy

Hierarchical age-period-cohort models.

Our primary analytical framework is the hierarchical age-period-cohort cross-classified random effects model (HAPC-CCREM), which treats period and cohort as crossed random grouping factors at level 2, with individuals nested non-hierarchically within both (Yang and Land 2006, 2008, 2013). At level 1, individual i observed in period j and cohort k has ideal number of children modeled as: \begin{equation} Y_{ijk} = \beta_0 + \sum_{g=2}^{11} \beta_g \text{AgeGroup}_{g,ijk} + \boldsymbol{\gamma} \mathbf{X}_{ijk} + u_{0j} + v_{0k} + e_{ijk} \end{equation} where \text{AgeGroup}_{g} are dummy indicators for the eleven 5-year age groups (reference: 18–22), u_{0j} \sim N(0, \sigma^2_u) is the period random intercept, v_{0k} \sim N(0, \sigma^2_v) is the cohort random intercept, and e_{ijk} \sim N(0, \sigma^2_e) is the individual residual. All models are estimated via restricted maximum likelihood (REML) using the lme4 package in R. We estimate three nested models: Model 1 includes only the APC structure (age group fixed effects, period and cohort random intercepts); Model 2 adds the full set of individual-level controls; Model 3 extends Model 2 by allowing a random slope on rural hukou across cohorts, testing whether the urban–rural gap in fertility ideals varies by birth cohort.

Heterogeneity analyses.

To examine how APC patterns differ across social groups, we re-estimate the Model 2 specification on six subsamples: urban hukou holders, rural hukou holders, men, women, respondents with at least one child, and childless respondents.

Robustness check: APC-I model.

As a robustness check, we implement the age-period-cohort-interaction (APC-I) model proposed by Xu and Luo (2022), which decomposes temporal variation into age and period main effects (identified via sum-to-zero constraints), cohort average deviations from the age-by-period surface, and cohort-specific life-course slopes. The APC-I model does not require the random-effects assumptions of the HAPC framework and thus provides a model-free benchmark for our estimates. We implement this model using the APCI R package.

Addressing identification concerns.

The APC identification problem — the exact linear dependency among age, period, and cohort — has generated extensive debate (Bell and Jones 2014; Bell and Jones 2015; Fosse and Winship 2019), and recent work on causal inference in observational designs underscores the importance of transparent identifying assumptions when decomposing temporal effects (Rohrer 2025). Bell and Jones (2017) caution that HAPC models may conflate linear trends when period and cohort effects are both substantial. In our application, the near-zero period variance (\sigma^2_u < 0.001 across all specifications) alleviates this concern: because there is essentially no period-level variation to misattribute, the risk of confounding between period and cohort trends is minimal. We report both HAPC and APC-I results to demonstrate that the dominance of cohort over period effects is robust to alternative decomposition strategies.

Results

Descriptive Patterns

Table [tab:descriptives] presents sample characteristics across all eight survey waves. The mean ideal number of children is remarkably stable at approximately 1.95 from 2010 through 2021, before declining to 1.87 in 2023 — the lowest value in the series. The standard deviation increases in 2023 (1.05 vs. 0.82–0.90 in earlier waves), indicating growing heterogeneity in fertility preferences. The two-child ideal remains dominant but shows signs of erosion.

The sample reflects ongoing structural transformations in Chinese society. The proportion holding rural hukou ranges from 50.6% to 68.9% across waves, averaging 56.4%. Women constitute 51.2% of the pooled sample. Educational expansion is striking: the share holding a bachelor’s degree or above rose from 7.9% in 2010 to 29.6% in 2023. The proportion never married increased from 10.5% to 22.0% over the same period, consistent with delayed marriage trends. The mean actual number of children declined from 1.59 in 2010 to 1.24 in 2023.

HAPC Main Results

Table [tab:hapc_main] reports estimates from three nested HAPC models. In Model 1 (baseline), age effects on ideal fertility follow a U-shaped pattern (Figure 2, Panel A). Relative to the youngest respondents (aged 18–22), middle-aged groups report lower fertility ideals in the unadjusted model, while older age groups report substantially higher ideals, with the 68–72 group at \hat{\beta} = 0.524 (p < 0.001). The key finding at this stage is the relative magnitude of the random effects: cohort variance is \sigma^2_v = 0.030, while period variance is \sigma^2_u = 0.0002 — a ratio exceeding 100:1. Birth cohort accounts for the vast majority of contextual variation in ideal fertility.

Model 2 introduces the full set of individual-level controls. With covariates included, the U-shaped age pattern sharpens: relative to the 18–22 reference group, the nadir falls at ages 33–37 (\hat{\beta} = -0.251, p < 0.001), and fertility ideals then rise among older respondents, with the 68–72 group showing \hat{\beta} = 0.129 (p < 0.01) above the reference. The actual number of children emerges as the strongest predictor (\hat{\beta} = 0.319, p < 0.001): each additional child is associated with an increase of 0.319 in the ideal number. Rural hukou holders express higher fertility ideals than urban residents (\hat{\beta} = 0.105, p < 0.001). Women report lower ideals than men (\hat{\beta} = -0.085, p < 0.001). Religious respondents (\hat{\beta} = 0.142, p < 0.001) and ethnic minorities (\hat{\beta} = 0.101, p < 0.001) both report higher ideals. Among education categories, only the bachelor’s-or-above group differs significantly from the reference (\hat{\beta} = 0.061, p < 0.001), indicating that the most educated respondents express higher fertility ideals after controlling for actual fertility and other characteristics. Currently married respondents report lower ideals than the never-married (\hat{\beta} = -0.086, p < 0.001), and formerly married respondents show an even larger negative coefficient (\hat{\beta} = -0.112, p < 0.001). Log income is not significantly associated with ideal fertility (\hat{\beta} = -0.001, p > 0.05).

The random-effects structure in Model 2 reveals a pattern of central importance: after adjusting for individual characteristics, cohort variance is \sigma^2_v = 0.0194, while period variance is \sigma^2_u = 0.0001. The cohort-to-period variance ratio exceeds 190:1. This result indicates that the individual-level controls absorb period-related variation (compositional shifts across survey years) but leave cohort-level variation intact — consistent with genuine generational differences in fertility ideals that cannot be reduced to observed characteristics.

Model 3 allows rural hukou to have a random slope across cohorts. This specification improves model fit (AIC = 190,775 vs. 190,885 in Model 2), and cohort variance increases further to \sigma^2_v = 0.027. The urban–rural gap in fertility ideals is not constant across generations; it varies systematically by birth cohort.

Heterogeneity Analyses

Table [tab:heterogeneity] presents the Model 2 specification estimated on six subsamples, revealing three notable patterns.

Urban–rural divergence.

The intercept gap between urban (1.292) and rural (1.554) subsamples is 0.26 children — the baseline urban–rural difference in fertility ideals after controlling for all covariates. The gender gap is twice as large in urban areas (\hat{\beta}_{\text{female}} = -0.108) as in rural areas (\hat{\beta}_{\text{female}} = -0.060). Urban cohort variance (\sigma^2_v = 0.030) exceeds rural cohort variance (\sigma^2_v = 0.013) by a factor of 2.3, suggesting that generational differentiation in fertility ideals is more pronounced in urban settings where modernization pressures are strongest.

Gendered cohort dynamics.

The most striking finding across all subgroup models is the gender difference in cohort variance: women’s cohort variance (\sigma^2_v = 0.041) is nearly three times men’s (\sigma^2_v = 0.014). Generational shifts in fertility ideals are driven disproportionately by women. The income coefficient diverges by sex: positive for men (\hat{\beta} = 0.004, p < 0.01) and negative for women (\hat{\beta} = -0.004, p < 0.001), suggesting that higher personal income is associated with slightly higher fertility ideals among men but lower ideals among women — a pattern consistent with gendered opportunity costs of childbearing.

Parenthood status.

Among respondents with children, cohort variance is small (\sigma^2_v = 0.002), and period variance is near zero (\sigma^2_u = 0.001). Among childless respondents, both cohort variance (\sigma^2_v = 0.021) and period variance (\sigma^2_u = 0.011) are substantially larger, indicating that the childless are more responsive to both generational and contemporaneous influences on fertility ideals. The gender gap among childless respondents (\hat{\beta}_{\text{female}} = -0.182) is triple that among parents (\hat{\beta}_{\text{female}} = -0.061). The positive education effect (bachelor’s or above) is significant only among parents (\hat{\beta} = 0.075, p < 0.001) and vanishes among the childless (\hat{\beta} = -0.002, n.s.), suggesting that the positive association between higher education and fertility ideals reflects a selection process: highly educated individuals who have already become parents may be those who actively desired children.

APC-I Robustness Check

Figure 4 presents results from the APC-I model, which decomposes temporal variation without relying on the random-effects assumptions of the HAPC framework. Panel A displays the age main effects: deviations follow a U-shaped pattern consistent with the HAPC age group fixed effects, with middle-aged groups (33–47) below the grand mean and both younger (18–22) and older (58–72) groups above it. The largest age effect is for the 68–72 group (\hat{\delta} = 0.141), and the most negative is for the 33–37 group (\hat{\delta} = -0.080).

Panel B displays period deviations, which are uniformly small: the largest positive deviation is 2015 (\hat{\delta} = 0.046), and the largest negative is 2023 (\hat{\delta} = -0.030). No period deviation exceeds 0.05 in absolute value, confirming the HAPC finding that survey-year variation is minimal. Panel C shows cohort average deviations, which reveal meaningful generational variation. Cohorts born 1975–1999 show positive deviations, with the 1990–94 cohort at the peak. The 2000+ cohort shows a sharply negative deviation, consistent with the HAPC finding that younger generations have internalized lower fertility ideals.

Panel D presents the signature output of the APC-I framework: cohort-specific life-course slopes. These slopes capture how each birth cohort’s fertility ideals change across survey periods, net of age and period main effects. Cohorts born 1965–1989 display significantly positive slopes, meaning their ideals have risen over the observation window; the 1965–69 cohort shows the steepest positive trajectory. In contrast, the 2000+ cohort shows a significantly negative slope, indicating that its fertility ideals are declining as members enter adulthood. This divergence in life-course trajectories between older and younger cohorts provides direct evidence of generational imprinting that cannot be captured by the HAPC’s constant cohort intercepts alone.

The APC-I results corroborate the HAPC estimates in three respects: (1) age effects on ideal fertility exhibit a U-shaped pattern, with the youngest and oldest groups above the middle-aged; (2) period effects are small and substantively negligible; (3) cohort effects exhibit meaningful variation that dominates the temporal structure of the data. The consistency of these findings across two models with different identifying assumptions strengthens confidence that the dominance of cohort over period effects is not an artifact of the HAPC specification (Bell and Jones 2014; Fosse and Winship 2019).

Discussion

This study set out to decompose age, period, and cohort effects on fertility ideals in China using eight waves of CGSS data spanning 2010 to 2023. The results point to a clear conclusion: cohort socialization, not period-specific policy change, is the dominant force shaping how many children Chinese adults say they want. We organize the discussion around three sets of findings that carry distinct implications for demographic theory and population policy.

The Persistence of One-Child Norms

The most striking result is the near-complete absence of period effects on fertility ideals. In the full-controls model (Model 2), the period variance component is \sigma^2 = 0.0001, roughly 190 times smaller than the cohort variance (\sigma^2 = 0.019). This disparity persists across all model specifications and subgroup analyses. Three major pronatalist policy shifts occurred during the observation window—the selective two-child policy in 2013, universal two-child in 2016, and three-child in 2021—yet none produced detectable variation in stated fertility ideals at the period level. The mean ideal number of children held steady near 1.95 from 2010 to 2021 and dropped to 1.87 in 2023.

What drives the cohort pattern? We propose a generational socialization mechanism rooted in Mannheim’s theory of generational imprinting. Individuals born between 1980 and 1999 grew up in families shaped—and in urban areas, strictly constrained—by the one-child policy. Many were themselves only children who had no siblings, no cousins of similar age, and limited exposure to multi-child family life. Chen and Gietel‐Basten (2025) provided causal evidence for this channel: using a regression discontinuity design around the 1980 policy introduction, they showed that being an only child significantly reduces ideal family size among urban Chinese, a mechanism they link to the “low-fertility trap” hypothesis. Our HAPC estimates are consistent with their finding. After controlling for individual-level characteristics in Model 2, the cohort variance shifts from 0.030 to 0.019, indicating that observed characteristics account for some cohort-level variation, yet a substantial generational component persists that cannot be reduced to compositional shifts in education, income, or urbanization.

The social consequences of this generational shift extend beyond individual preferences. Cai and Wang (2021) documented how the one-child policy altered China’s demographic and social fabric in fundamental ways, reshaping gender relations, family structures, and intergenerational expectations across nearly four decades of enforcement. Our results suggest that these transformations left a durable imprint on reproductive preferences: the cohorts socialized under the policy carry its normative legacy even after the policy itself has been dismantled. Chen and Gietel‐Basten (2023) reached a related conclusion through a different design. Using difference-in-differences around the 2015 policy relaxation, they found that removing the one-child restriction increased ideal family size by about 0.2 children among married individuals aged 20–39—but sub-replacement ideals in urban China remained genuine even after the policy change. Their estimate of the policy constraint effect (0.2 children) is modest relative to the generational gap we observe across cohorts, which reinforces the interpretation that norm internalization, not policy restriction alone, sustains low fertility ideals.

The implication for population policy is sobering. Pronatalist measures that focus on lifting birth restrictions address only the constraint component of low fertility. If fertility ideals themselves have declined through generational socialization—as the dominance of cohort over period effects suggests—then the policy challenge is not removing barriers to childbearing but reversing preferences that were formed during adolescence and early adulthood. Economic factors compound this generational norm shift. Yang et al. (2020) showed that perceived upward social mobility is associated with lower demand for children in China, as parents substitute child quantity for quality in pursuit of status. Cai and Xie (2025) found that intergenerational mobility expectations shape fertility desires, with parents who aspire to upward mobility for their children preferring smaller families to concentrate resources. Xu and Wu (2025) documented that rising income inequality widens the gap between desired and realized fertility, as households in the lower tail of the income distribution face binding constraints on achieving their stated ideals. The experience of other East Asian societies facing ultra-low fertility indicates that the reversal of generational norm change has not been achieved even with substantial financial incentives (Cheng 2020).

Gender, Urbanization, and the Agents of Change

The heterogeneity analyses reveal that the cohort-driven decline in fertility ideals is not uniform across the population. Two dimensions of stratification stand out: gender and hukou status. Women exhibit a cohort variance (\sigma^2 = 0.041) roughly three times that of men (\sigma^2 = 0.014), and the gender gap in fertility ideals is twice as large in urban areas (-0.108, p < 0.001) as in rural areas (-0.060, p < 0.001). Urban cohort effects (\sigma^2 = 0.030) also exceed rural ones (\sigma^2 = 0.013) by a factor of more than two.

These patterns suggest that urban women are the primary demographic agents of declining fertility ideals in China. Several reinforcing mechanisms are at work. First, one-child policy enforcement was considerably stricter in cities than in rural areas, where exceptions for agricultural households and ethnic minorities were common (Zhang 2017). Urban cohorts therefore experienced more uniform exposure to the single-child family norm during their formative years. Second, urban women face compounding opportunity costs of childbearing that rural women experience less acutely: longer educational trajectories, greater labor-market attachment, higher housing costs, and stronger normative expectations of career continuity. Zhang et al. (2025) reported that higher educational attainment and urbanization are associated with lower fertility intention among married adults under the three-child policy, with the desire for three or more children lowest in eastern (most urbanized) regions at 8.2%. Third, patriarchal norms that historically sustained high fertility preferences have weakened more rapidly in urban settings. Ni (2023) found that among one-child generation cohorts in urban Jiangsu, son preference has given way to a more balanced—though not reversed—preference composition, with two-daughter families gaining greater acceptance. This erosion of son preference removes one traditional motivation for wanting larger families.

The gender gap in cohort variance (0.041 vs. 0.014) deserves theoretical attention. It implies that the generational transformation of fertility norms has proceeded at a faster pace among women than among men. One interpretation is that women bear a disproportionate share of the costs associated with childbearing and childrearing—a pattern well documented in the literature on the “second shift” in domestic labor—and are therefore more responsive to structural changes that raise those costs. X. Yang et al. (2023) found that fertility policy liberalization has not been accompanied by commensurate advances in gender equality, leaving women to absorb the costs of additional children without institutional support. Among urban women specifically, Yu et al. (2023) reported that second-child intentions remain low, constrained by career concerns and the unequal distribution of domestic labor. The Model 3 random slope specification, which allows cohort effects to vary by hukou status, improves model fit (AIC = 190,775 vs. 190,885 in Model 2), confirming that the rural-urban divide in fertility ideals is not merely a level difference but a difference in the pace of generational change.

For policy, these findings indicate that universal birth-permit relaxation—the approach China has pursued since 2013—treats the symptom rather than the cause. Gender-specific interventions that reduce the opportunity costs of childbearing for women, such as subsidized childcare, flexible and non-penalizing parental leave, and workplace protections against maternal discrimination, may hold greater promise. Yang (2025) noted that expectations of old-age support from children continue to influence fertility desires among some subgroups, but this traditional motivation is weakening as pension coverage expands, further eroding the instrumental rationale for larger families. The fact that the steepest cohort declines concentrate among urban women points to a target population where such policies could have the largest marginal effect.

Experience, Constraints, and the Education Paradox

The individual-level predictors in Model 2 reveal a pattern in which lived experience—rather than abstract attitudes—anchors fertility ideals. Actual number of children is the strongest predictor in the model (\beta = 0.319, p < 0.001): each additional child a respondent already has is associated with an increase of 0.319 in their stated ideal. Married individuals report lower ideals than the never-married (\beta = -0.086, p < 0.001), and formerly married respondents show an even larger negative association (\beta = -0.112, p < 0.001).

The positive parity effect reflects two processes that cannot be fully separated in cross-sectional data. The first is rationalization: parents adjust their stated ideals upward to match their lived reality, reducing cognitive dissonance between preference and behavior. The second is selection: individuals who have children are disproportionately those who wanted them in the first place. Both processes produce the same empirical signature, and disentangling them requires panel data that track ideals before and after births. What the cross-sectional association does establish is that parenthood experience is far more strongly associated with fertility ideals than any policy-period effect—a result that aligns with Chen et al. (2023), who found that among families under the three-child policy, the ideal number of children averaged 1.85, with fertility intentions strongly conditioned by gender, age, residence, and existing parity. Zhang et al. (2021) reached a complementary conclusion: intentions to have a second or third child depend heavily on family support availability, and the gap between intended and actual fertility widens when institutional childcare is absent. Zhu et al. (2022) similarly found that willingness to have a second or third child varies sharply by parity, income, and housing conditions, underscoring that structural constraints mediate the translation of ideals into behavior.

The negative marriage coefficient is consistent with a “reality check” mechanism. The experience of marriage—and its associated financial pressures, domestic labor negotiations, and childcare demands—may constrain the upward revision of fertility ideals that characterizes the unmarried population. The heterogeneity analysis sharpens this interpretation: among childless respondents, being married is associated with higher ideals (\beta = 0.099, p < 0.001), while among those with children, the marriage coefficient is negative and non-significant (\beta = -0.034). This suggests that marriage itself does not depress fertility ideals; rather, the combination of marriage and parenthood triggers the constraining effect.

The education results present what we call an “education paradox.” After controlling for actual children and other covariates, holding a bachelor’s degree or above is associated with higher fertility ideals than junior high education or below (\beta = 0.061, p < 0.001). This positive coefficient contradicts the well-established negative education-fertility gradient observed in behavioral data. The heterogeneity analysis resolves this apparent contradiction: the positive education effect appears only among respondents who already have children (\beta = 0.075, p < 0.001) and vanishes among the childless (\beta = -0.002, n.s.). The pattern is consistent with positive selection into parenthood among the highly educated: in a context where educated urban women face the highest opportunity costs, those who do become parents are likely those with the strongest pronatalist preferences. The positive coefficient thus reflects a compositional artifact of selection, not a causal effect of education on fertility ideals.

The practical implication is that the gap between ideal and actual fertility—not the level of ideals themselves—may be the domain where policy can intervene most effectively. Wang et al. (2025) found that among college students in Guangzhou, only 43.9% planned to have children, while 26.3% had no intention of doing so. If a substantial share of the population already holds fertility ideals at or below replacement, the most productive policy approach may be to help those who do want children achieve their stated ideals, by reducing the structural barriers—cost, time, institutional support—that prevent desired births from being realized.

Limitations

Several limitations warrant acknowledgment. First, the CGSS is a repeated cross-sectional survey, not a panel. We cannot observe within-person change in fertility ideals over time, which limits our ability to distinguish rationalization from selection in the parity-ideal association. Second, stated ideals are not the same as fertility intentions, and intentions do not perfectly predict behavior; the distance from ideal to actual childbearing involves intervening decisions, constraints, and contingencies that our models do not capture. Third, while the HAPC framework provides a principled decomposition of age, period, and cohort effects, the fundamental linear dependency among these three temporal dimensions cannot be fully resolved by any statistical method. Our APCI robustness check yields a U-shaped age profile consistent with the HAPC age group estimates, which is reassuring but not definitive. Fourth, the 2023 wave of CGSS included a phone survey component that may affect sample composition relative to earlier face-to-face waves. Fifth, the 41.2% missing rate on actual number of children in the 2023 wave—imputed via the missRanger algorithm—introduces additional uncertainty, though sensitivity analyses excluding 2023 produce substantively identical cohort and period variance estimates.

Conclusion

Using hierarchical age-period-cohort models applied to eight waves of the Chinese General Social Survey (2010–2023, N = 79{,}991), this study demonstrates that fertility ideals in China are shaped primarily by cohort socialization rather than period-specific policy changes. The one-child policy generation—cohorts born between 1980 and 1999 who grew up in single-child families—has internalized small-family norms that persist well after the policy’s dismantling. Period effects on ideal number of children are negligible (\sigma^2 = 0.0001) relative to cohort effects (\sigma^2 = 0.019), despite three successive pronatalist policy liberalizations during the observation window.

These findings carry three implications. For demographic theory, they demonstrate that generational norm formation under coercive fertility policy exhibits path dependence: preferences shaped during formative years do not simply revert when the policy environment changes. The “stickiness” of cohort-level fertility ideals challenges models that treat fertility preferences as responsive to contemporaneous incentives, and supports instead a socialization framework in which reproductive norms are transmitted and solidified within generational cohorts.

For population policy, our results suggest that pronatalist incentives face a ceiling imposed by generational norm change. Removing birth restrictions—the primary strategy China has pursued—addresses a constraint that is less binding than the underlying shift in preferences. The concentration of declining fertility ideals among urban women points toward gender-targeted policies—subsidized childcare, flexible parental leave, workplace protections—as potentially more effective than universal permit relaxation. The gap between ideal and actual fertility offers a second, more tractable policy lever: helping those who want children overcome the structural barriers that prevent them from realizing their preferences.

For methodology, the HAPC and APCI decomposition reveals dynamics in fertility ideals that are invisible to standard regression approaches. Without separating cohort from period variation, one might conclude that fertility ideals in China are stable—the mean has hovered near 1.95 for over a decade. The age-period-cohort lens shows that this aggregate stability masks a generational transformation: each successive cohort wants fewer children, offset in the aggregate by the aging of older, higher-ideal cohorts out of the sample.

As China’s post-2000 cohorts—who grew up after the one-child policy’s strictest enforcement but who never experienced large-family norms firsthand—enter their childbearing years with the lowest recorded fertility ideals, the tension between population policy ambitions and individual reproductive preferences will only deepen.
