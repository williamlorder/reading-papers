The Uneven Sexual Revolution: Age, Period, and Cohort Dynamics in the Divergent Liberalization of American Sexual Attitudes, 1973–2024

[Blinded for Review]

Abstract

American sexual attitudes have changed unevenly over five decades. Using General Social Survey data spanning 1973 to 2024 (30 waves, N = 69{,}517), we apply Hierarchical Age-Period-Cohort cross-classified random effects models (HAPC-CCREM) to three sexual attitude domains measured on identical scales: premarital sex, homosexual sex, and extramarital sex. The analysis decomposes attitude variation into age, period, and cohort components, enabling the first formal cross-domain comparison of APC dynamics in U.S. sexual attitudes. We find three qualitatively distinct signatures. Premarital sex attitudes are cohort-dominant: birth cohort explains 4.4% of variance versus period’s 0.5%, consistent with generational replacement as the primary engine of liberalization. Homosexuality acceptance is period-dominant: survey period accounts for 5.3% of variance versus cohort’s 0.3%, reflecting society-wide attitude shifts driven by legal milestones, media visibility, and interpersonal contact that reached citizens across all birth cohorts. Extramarital sex condemnation shows near-zero variance on both dimensions (cohort 0.3%, period 0.9%), confirming a durable cross-generational moral consensus. Gender-stratified models reveal that women exhibit the strongest cohort effects for premarital sex and the strongest period effects for homosexuality acceptance. These patterns are robust to alternative specifications including the APC Intrinsic Estimator, binary logit models, and exclusion of post-2021 web-survey data. We propose a consent-autonomy boundary framework: sexual liberalization extends to behavior between consenting adults but halts at behavior involving non-consensual harm to third parties. The divergence documented here – replicated in recent Chinese data – suggests this boundary reflects a general feature of how societies moralize sexual conduct.

Introduction

Over the past half century, Americans underwent not one sexual revolution but three, each propelled by fundamentally different social forces. Between 1973 and 2024, the proportion of General Social Survey (GSS) respondents who considered premarital sex “not wrong at all” doubled from 33% to 67%, while acceptance of homosexual sex quadrupled from 14% to 60%. Over the same period, condemnation of extramarital sex held firm near 75% for four straight decades before dropping to 65% only in the 2020s. That three attitudes measured on the same scale, in the same survey, by the same respondents could follow such divergent trajectories raises a question that prior research has documented but not resolved: what distinct social processes drive liberalization in some sexual domains while leaving others untouched?

The answer matters for scholarship, policy, and public life. Sexual attitudes shape legislative outcomes on sex education, same-sex marriage, family law, and reproductive rights. If attitude change is driven by generational replacement, it is slow but nearly irreversible; if driven by period-level institutional shocks, it can be rapid but potentially reversible. Knowing which engine operates in which domain is not merely an academic exercise. It determines whether legal victories like Obergefell v. Hodges consolidate over time or remain vulnerable to backlash, and whether the apparent stability of extramarital sex norms reflects a durable moral consensus or a boundary poised to shift.

Prior work has established the divergence descriptively. Harding and Jencks (2003) decomposed premarital sex attitude change through 2002 and found cohort replacement to be the dominant mechanism. Loftus (2001) documented a two-track liberalization of homosexuality attitudes through 1998. Pampel (2016) examined how the social distribution of sexual tolerance changed across cohorts. Yet no study has applied formal Hierarchical Age-Period-Cohort (HAPC) models to all three domains simultaneously, and no study has extended the analysis beyond 2018 to capture the striking developments of the 2020s. Gao and Wang (2025) and Liu and Yang (2024) recently applied HAPC to Chinese GSS data and found parallel domain-specific divergence, but no comparable analysis exists for the United States, despite its far longer time series.

We address these gaps by fitting Yang and Land’s ((2006)) cross-classified random effects model (HAPC-CCREM) to three GSS sexual attitude items across 30 survey waves spanning 1973 to 2024 (N = 69{,}517). The model decomposes attitude variation into age (fixed), period (random), and cohort (random) components, enabling direct comparison of APC structures across the three domains. We supplement the main analysis with four dimensions of heterogeneity (gender, religious attendance, education, political views) and four robustness specifications (APC Intrinsic Estimator, alternative cohort groupings, binary logit, and exclusion of post-2021 web-mode data).

The results reveal a striking pattern. Premarital sex attitudes are cohort-dominant: birth cohort accounts for 4.4% of total variance, dwarfing period’s 0.5%. Homosexuality acceptance is period-dominant: survey period explains 5.3% of variance versus cohort’s 0.3%. Extramarital sex condemnation shows near-zero variance on both dimensions, confirming its status as a cross-generational moral consensus. These signatures are robust across all specifications and hold in gender-, religion-, education-, and politics-stratified models, though with instructive variation: women exhibit the most extreme cohort effect for premarital sex and the most extreme period effect for homosexuality acceptance.

This study makes three contributions. First, we provide the first application of formal HAPC-CCREM to U.S. sexual attitudes, extending prior decomposition approaches (Harding and Jencks 2003; Elias et al. 2013; Ekstam 2021) with a multilevel random-effects framework that treats periods and cohorts as cross-classified grouping structures. Second, we offer the first cross-domain comparative APC analysis of sexual attitudes with more than 50 years of data, revealing that “the sexual revolution” comprises three distinct processes with qualitatively different APC engines. Third, we propose a consent-autonomy boundary framework that accounts for the divergence: the moral logic of individual autonomy extended to consensual sexual behavior (premarital, homosexual) through cohort replacement and period-level social movements, but did not extend to behavior involving non-consensual third-party harm (extramarital), where condemnation reflects fairness intuitions that transcend generational and historical boundaries.

Background and Theoretical Framework

The Divergence in American Sexual Attitudes

Over the past half century, Americans became more accepting of premarital sex and homosexuality while continuing to condemn extramarital sex. These three trajectories present a puzzle. Loftus (2001), using GSS data from 1973 to 1998, showed that attitudes toward homosexuality followed a two-track pattern: Americans grew steadily more supportive of gay civil liberties, yet remained or became more negative about the morality of homosexual sex through 1990 before reversing course. Harding and Jencks (2003) decomposed premarital sex attitudes over a similar period and found that growing permissiveness was driven almost entirely by cohort replacement rather than within-cohort change. Pampel (2016), extending the analysis through 2014 with multilevel APC models, confirmed the cohort gradient for premarital sex tolerance and documented a parallel but weaker pattern for same-gender sex, while finding that extramarital sex tolerance showed little cohort variation at all. Elias et al. (2013) added nuance by showing that premarital sex attitudes reflected both between-cohort and within-cohort changes, with permissiveness peaking among baby-boomer cohorts.

If sexual liberalization were a single cultural process, all three domains – measured on the same 4-point scale, asked of the same respondents, in the same surveys – should exhibit similar temporal patterns. They do not. Premarital sex permissiveness rose gradually and monotonically; homosexuality acceptance stagnated for decades before accelerating sharply after 2000; extramarital sex condemnation held firm across all five decades. This domain-specific divergence demands domain-specific explanations. The theoretical perspectives below address, in turn, why each trajectory takes the shape it does.

Cohort Replacement as the Engine of Attitudinal Change

A large literature on political socialization holds that core attitudes form during a “critical period” in late adolescence and early adulthood and then persist, with limited change, across the life course (Davis 2004). On this account, aggregate attitude shifts occur not because individuals change their minds but because more conservative older cohorts die and are replaced by more liberal younger ones. Harding and Jencks (2003) applied this framework to GSS premarital sex data through 2002 and concluded that cohort replacement was the primary engine of liberalization. Each cohort born after the 1940s entered adulthood with progressively more permissive premarital sex norms, and those norms proved durable as each cohort aged. The rise of cohabitation as an alternative to marriage further normalized premarital sexual unions across successive cohorts (Esteve and Lesthaeghe 2016; Kuperberg and Padgett 2015).

Two recent studies reinforce the mechanism from the opposite direction. Ekstam (2022), using panel data representative of the U.S. adult population, demonstrated that individual-level attitudes toward homosexuality are remarkably stable once formed: attitude stability increases with age, consistent with an aging-stability hypothesis in which susceptibility to change declines across the adult lifespan. If individual attitudes rarely change, then aggregate-level liberalization must be compositional. Jeannet and Dražanová (2023), analyzing ten rounds of the European Social Survey across 13 countries, found that the political climate during individuals’ formative years predicted their later attitudes toward sexual orientation: cohorts that came of age when equality values were politically dominant held more tolerant views as adults, net of period and age effects. Together, these findings predict that sexual attitudes formed through early socialization – such as those toward premarital sex – should display a strong cohort signature in APC models.

Period Effects and Social Movements

Not all attitude change follows the slow rhythm of generational turnover. Some shifts occur within cohorts, sweeping across age groups in response to institutional, legal, or cultural events that define a historical period. Andersen and Fetner (2008), comparing Canada and the United States from 1981 to 2000, documented “remarkable” within-cohort change in tolerance of homosexuality – a finding that challenged the aging-stability hypothesis for this particular domain. The authors attributed the pattern to differing political climates across country and time, suggesting that external forces can override the inertia of early socialization.

What forces? Ofosu et al. (2019) provided quasi-experimental evidence: state-by-state same-sex marriage legalization was associated with reduced implicit and explicit anti-gay bias, with sharper declines following legalization in states that passed their own legislation. Legal recognition operated as a period-level attitude shifter that reached citizens regardless of birth cohort. Witeck (2014) traced the broader cultural arc: Supreme Court rulings, growing media visibility of LGBT individuals, and the framing of marriage equality as a civil rights issue combined to produce a society-wide shift in acceptance. Cross-national evidence supports the generalizability of these institutional forces: Manning and Gustafson (2025) documented significant increases in support for same-sex parents between 2012 and 2022, while Hadler and Symons (2018) showed that world opinion on homosexuality has become increasingly polarized as states diverged in their legal approaches, and Paradela-López et al. (2023) found that attitude change in Chile toward homosexuality was driven largely by structural societal shifts beyond individual demographics. Kranjac and Wagmiller (2021) decomposed GSS homosexuality attitudes from 1973 to 2018 and found that intracohort (period) change was more important than cohort replacement during the post-2002 period of legislative and judicial liberalization. These findings predict that homosexuality acceptance should display a strong period signature in HAPC models, reflecting the institutional cascade that accelerated after 2000.

Moral Individualism and the Consent Boundary

If cohort replacement explains premarital sex liberalization and period effects explain homosexuality acceptance, what explains the persistence of extramarital sex condemnation? The answer, we argue, lies in the moral logic that distinguishes these domains.

Fitouchi et al. (2022) developed a framework for “puritanical morality” rooted in cooperative cognition. Their account holds that societies moralize apparently harmless pleasures (lust, gluttony, intoxication) because such indulgences are perceived as undermining the self-control required for cooperative behavior. On this view, the liberalization of premarital sex and homosexuality reflects a declining perception that these behaviors threaten cooperative norms: as they came to be understood as expressions of personal autonomy between consenting adults, the puritanical rationale for condemning them weakened. Extramarital sex, by contrast, involves a direct betrayal of a partner’s trust – a harm that activates fairness-based moral condemnation, not merely purity-based disgust.

Andersson (2022) provided qualitative evidence for this boundary. In interviews with 22 practitioners of consensual non-monogamy (CNM) in Sweden, participants drew a bright moral line between CNM – which rests on mutual consent, honesty, and negotiation – and infidelity, which they condemned on grounds of deception and consent violation. The moral objection to extramarital sex, then, is not that it involves sex outside marriage per se, but that it involves sex that violates a partner’s autonomy. This consent-autonomy boundary predicts that condemnation of extramarital sex should persist even as consensual sexual behaviors are liberalized, producing a near-zero APC signature in HAPC models.

Recent evidence suggests this boundary may not be immovable. Gupta et al. (2023), reviewing 209 studies on polyamory and CNM, documented a surge of research interest and growing public visibility of consensual alternatives to monogamy. Masterson et al. (2025) found that 23% of a national U.S. sample reported decreased importance of monogamy following the COVID-19 pandemic, and 11% expressed greater interest in CNM relationships. RALL (2025) illustrated how individuals actively negotiate between competing religious and secular moral regimes governing sexual ethics, suggesting the boundaries around permissible sexual conduct are subject to ongoing renegotiation. If the consent-autonomy boundary is the operative principle, then explicit reframing of extra-dyadic sexual activity as consensual could, over time, erode what has been the most stable sexual taboo.

Mediating Mechanisms: Secularization, Education, and Political Sorting

Three cross-cutting forces operate alongside – and partly through – cohort and period dynamics to shape sexual attitudes.

First, secularization. Religious attendance is the strongest single predictor of conservative sexual morality cross-nationally (Scheepers et al. 2002; Peri-Rotem and Skirbekk 2022; Huynh-Tran et al. 2024). Molteni and Biolcati (2023) demonstrated that religious decline in Europe proceeds primarily through generational replacement: each successive cohort attends religious services less frequently than the last, and this compositional shift drives aggregate secularization. In the United States, GSS data show a parallel decline from a mean attendance score of 4.0 in the 1970s to 2.8 in the 2020s. Reimer and Watts (2025), using World Values Survey data from five Western countries, documented a weakening link between religiosity and moral restrictiveness over time, though the connection remained strongest for sexual morality. Declining religiosity thus amplifies both cohort and period liberalization by removing a key anchor of conservative sexual attitudes (Woodell and Schwadel 2020; Evli 2024).

Second, education. Pampel (2016) proposed a diffusion-of-innovations model: highly educated groups adopt tolerant sexual attitudes first, and the association between education and tolerance strengthens before eventually weakening as the attitudes diffuse to less educated groups. This pattern was strongest for premarital sex tolerance across GSS cohorts from 1900 to 1985.

Third, political sorting. Baldassarri and Park (2020) showed that the growing gap between Democrats and Republicans on moral issues reflects not a culture war but a partisan secular trend: both parties adopted more progressive views over time, but Democrats did so faster. This asymmetric pace of change creates the appearance of polarization and predicts that APC patterns will differ by political orientation, with liberals showing sharper temporal signatures.

A broader macro-level account embeds these mechanisms within postmaterialist value change. Inglehart and Appel (1989) argued that rising existential security across generations in industrial societies produced a shift from materialist concerns (economic and physical security) toward postmaterialist priorities (self-expression, quality of life). This intergenerational value shift was closely linked to declining traditional orientations, including restrictive sexual norms (LAFFERTY and KNUTSEN 1985; Inglehart and Abramson 1999; Inglehart 2016).

The Present Study

Despite extensive research on individual sexual attitude domains, no study has applied formal HAPC-CCREM to all three GSS sexual attitude items simultaneously, compared their APC signatures side by side, or extended the analysis beyond 2018. Gao and Wang (2025) and Liu and Yang (2024) conducted APC analyses of sexual attitudes using Chinese General Social Survey data, finding domain-specific divergence that parallels the U.S. pattern: premarital sex and homosexuality attitudes liberalized through period and cohort effects while extramarital sex attitudes remained stable. No comparable formal decomposition exists for the United States with its far longer time series (1973–2024).

We test five hypotheses that follow from the theoretical perspectives above:

H1 (Cohort dominance for premarital sex): The cohort variance component will substantially exceed the period variance component for PREMARSX, reflecting generational socialization as the primary engine of liberalization.

H2 (Period dominance for homosexuality): The period variance component will substantially exceed the cohort variance component for HOMOSEX, reflecting the society-wide impact of legal milestones, media visibility, and social movements.

H3 (Temporal stability for extramarital sex): Both cohort and period variance components for XMARSEX will be near zero through 2018, reflecting the cross-generational moral consensus anchored in consent-based reasoning.

H4 (Possible 2020s period shift in extramarital sex): The post-2018 XMARSEX data may show a modest period-level uptick in permissiveness, consistent with growing CNM discourse, though this may also reflect survey mode effects.

H5 (Gender-differentiated APC patterns): Women will show a stronger cohort signature for PREMARSX (reflecting generational erosion of the sexual double standard) and a stronger period signature for HOMOSEX (reflecting greater responsiveness to rights-based social movements) than men.

Each hypothesis maps onto a specific variance component or variance ratio in the HAPC-CCREM: H1 and H2 predict the relative magnitudes of \sigma^2_{\text{cohort}} and \sigma^2_{\text{period}}; H3 and H4 predict the absolute magnitudes of both components for XMARSEX; and H5 predicts gender differences in the period-to-cohort ratio (P/C).

Data and Methods

Data

We draw on 30 waves of the General Social Survey (GSS), 1973–2024 (Release 3), a nationally representative repeated cross-sectional survey of non-institutionalized English- and Spanish-speaking adults aged 18 and older in the United States. The core items used in this study demonstrate strong reliability across the GSS panel waves (Hout and Hastings 2016). The GSS uses a multi-stage area probability sampling design, and has been conducted annually or biennially since 1972. After multiple imputation and restriction to respondents with at least one non-missing dependent variable, our analytic sample comprises N = 70{,}326 individual observations spanning five decades of American public opinion on sexual morality. Table [tab:desc] presents full descriptive statistics by decade.

The sample composition reflects gradual demographic shifts in the U.S. adult population over this period. Mean age rose from 44.5 years in the 1970s to 50.5 in the 2020s, consistent with population aging. Women constituted approximately 55% of respondents across all decades. Mean educational attainment increased from 11.7 to 14.4 years. Religious service attendance declined from 4.0 to 2.8 on the 0–8 scale, and the married share fell from 67.8% to 44.6%. The racial composition diversified: the proportion identifying as White decreased from 87.8% to 73.9%, while the “Other” category grew from 0.7% to 11.3%.

Beginning in 2021, the GSS transitioned from in-person interviewing to a mixed-mode design incorporating web-based surveys (Phoo et al. 2022). We address this mode change explicitly in our robustness checks by estimating models that exclude 2021–2024 data.

Measures

Dependent Variables

We examine three GSS items measuring attitudes toward distinct sexual domains, each rated on a 4-point scale recoded so that higher values indicate greater permissiveness (1 = “always wrong,” 4 = “not wrong at all”):

Premarital sex attitudes (PREMARSX): “If a man and woman have sex relations before marriage, do you think it is always wrong, almost always wrong, wrong only sometimes, or not wrong at all?” Full-sample mean = 2.90 (SD = 1.24), rising from 2.57 in the 1970s to 3.32 in the 2020s.

Homosexual sex attitudes (HOMOSEX): “What about sexual relations between two adults of the same sex—do you think it is always wrong, almost always wrong, wrong only sometimes, or not wrong at all?” Full-sample mean = 2.12 (SD = 1.37), rising from 1.63 in the 1970s to 2.96 in the 2020s.

Extramarital sex attitudes (XMARSEX): “What is your opinion about a married person having sexual relations with someone other than the marriage partner—is it always wrong, almost always wrong, wrong only sometimes, or not wrong at all?” Full-sample mean = 1.39 (SD = 0.75), declining from 1.47 in the 1970s to a nadir of 1.30 in the 2000s before rising to 1.54 in the 2020s.

Independent Variables

Age is centered at 40 and entered as both a linear and quadratic term to capture non-linear life-course effects. Female is a binary indicator. Education is measured as years of completed schooling (mean = 13.08, SD = 3.18). Religious attendance uses the GSS 9-point scale (0 = never, 8 = more than once per week; mean = 3.62, SD = 2.75). Race is coded as White (reference), Black, and Other. Real income is measured in $10,000 units (2020 dollars; mean = 3.24, SD = 3.01). Married is a binary indicator of current marital status. Political views use the GSS 7-point scale (1 = extremely liberal, 7 = extremely conservative; mean = 4.09, SD = 1.41).

Analytical Strategy

HAPC Cross-Classified Random Effects Model

To decompose temporal change in sexual attitudes into age, period, and cohort components, we estimate Hierarchical Age-Period-Cohort Cross-Classified Random Effects Models (HAPC-CCREM; Yang and Land 2006, 2008; Yang 2006). This framework has been applied in substantive research across a range of attitudinal domains, including electoral change (Rekker 2024; Steiner 2023; Stoker 2020), moral attitudes (Tormos et al. 2023), and public health (Hayes et al. 2024). This framework treats individual respondents as Level-1 units cross-classified within period (survey year) and cohort (5-year birth groups) at Level 2. The specification is:

Level 1 (individuals): \begin{equation} Y_{ijk} = \beta_{0jk} + \beta_1 \text{Age}_{ijk} + \beta_2 \text{Age}^2_{ijk} + \boldsymbol{\beta X}_{ijk} + e_{ijk} \end{equation}

Level 2 (period and cohort): \begin{equation} \beta_{0jk} = \gamma_{00} + u_{0j(\text{period})} + v_{0k(\text{cohort})} \end{equation}

where Y_{ijk} is the attitude score for individual i in period j and cohort k; \boldsymbol{X} is the vector of individual-level covariates (female, education, religious attendance, race, income, married, political views); u_{0j} \sim N(0, \sigma^2_{\text{period}}) and v_{0k} \sim N(0, \sigma^2_{\text{cohort}}) are random intercepts for period and cohort, respectively; and e_{ijk} \sim N(0, \sigma^2_{\text{residual}}) is the individual-level error.

The variance components \sigma^2_{\text{period}} and \sigma^2_{\text{cohort}} provide direct estimates of the relative importance of period and cohort effects net of age and covariates. We compute intraclass correlation coefficients as ICC_{\text{cohort}} = \sigma^2_{\text{cohort}} / (\sigma^2_{\text{cohort}} + \sigma^2_{\text{period}} + \sigma^2_{\text{residual}}) and ICC_{\text{period}} analogously.

Cohorts are defined as 5-year birth groups (e.g., 1900–1904, 1905–1909, ..., 2000–2004), yielding 26 groups. Periods correspond to the 35 distinct survey years between 1973 and 2024. We estimate the model separately for each dependent variable to enable cross-domain comparison of APC signatures. All models are estimated in R using the lme4 package with restricted maximum likelihood (REML).

Multiple Imputation

To address item non-response, we employ multiple imputation via the missRanger package in R, using predictive mean matching (PMM) with k = 5 nearest neighbors across m = 5 imputed datasets. Point estimates and standard errors are pooled using Rubin’s rules. The total post-imputation analytic sample is N = 69{,}517 for the HAPC models.

Heterogeneity Analysis

To examine whether APC dynamics vary across social groups, we re-estimate HAPC-CCREM models on subsamples stratified by: gender (male vs. female), religious attendance (high \geq 5 vs. low < 5), education (college degree or higher vs. no college), and political views (liberal, codes 1–3 vs. conservative, codes 5–7). For each subgroup model, we report the period-to-cohort variance ratio (P/C = \sigma^2_{\text{period}} / \sigma^2_{\text{cohort}}) as a summary index of APC structure.

Robustness Checks

We conduct four alternative specifications to assess the stability of our main findings. First, we estimate the APC-Intrinsic Estimator (APC-IE; Yang et al. 2008), a constrained OLS approach with sum-to-zero constraints on cohort effects. Second, we redefine cohorts using 10-year birth groups to test sensitivity to cohort bandwidth. Third, we estimate binary logit HAPC models that dichotomize responses into “permissive” (codes 3–4) versus “restrictive” (codes 1–2), with the logistic residual variance set to \pi^2/3. Fourth, we exclude the 2021–2024 waves to assess whether the post-pandemic web-mode transition (Phoo et al. 2022; Dahlhamer et al. 2019; Hewitt 2002) altered our conclusions.

We acknowledge the identification critique raised by Bell and Jones (2014) and Bell and Jones (2017), who demonstrate via simulation that HAPC models can, under certain data-generating processes, misattribute linear trends between periods and cohorts. Our response to this critique rests on multi-method triangulation: we compare HAPC results with APC-IE estimates, vary cohort bandwidth, test alternative functional forms, and examine whether substantive conclusions change across specifications (E. Fosse and Winship 2019; Ethan Fosse and Winship 2019). The convergent pattern across these approaches strengthens confidence in our decomposition, even if no single method can fully resolve the APC identification problem.

Results

Descriptive Trends

Table [tab:desc] and Figure 1 document five decades of sexual attitude change in the United States. The three domains followed strikingly different trajectories. Premarital sex attitudes liberalized steadily from a mean of 2.57 in the 1970s to 3.32 in the 2020s, a shift of 0.61 standard deviations. Homosexual sex attitudes underwent a more dramatic transformation—from 1.63 to 2.96, a full standard-deviation change—but the trajectory was non-linear: attitudes remained stable and conservative through the 1980s (M = 1.59), then accelerated sharply after 2000. Extramarital sex attitudes, by contrast, remained anchored near the floor of the scale across all five decades, fluctuating narrowly between 1.30 and 1.54. Even the modest uptick in the 2020s (M = 1.54) left extramarital sex as the most condemned of the three domains by a wide margin.

These raw trends suggest that the three domains are governed by different temporal dynamics. The gradual, monotonic rise in premarital sex permissiveness is consistent with cohort replacement—each successive generation entering the survey more permissive than the last. The nonlinear, accelerating trajectory of homosexuality acceptance points instead to period-level forces that swept across age groups simultaneously. The near-invariance of extramarital sex condemnation implies minimal influence from either cohort succession or period-level change. The HAPC models below test these interpretations formally.

Variance Components

Panel B of Table [tab:hapc_main] reports the random-effect variance components and intraclass correlation coefficients. For premarital sex, the cohort variance (\sigma^2_{\text{cohort}} = 0.0503) exceeds the period variance (\sigma^2_{\text{period}} = 0.0061) by a factor of 8.2, yielding ICC_{\text{cohort}} = 0.0437 (4.4%) and ICC_{\text{period}} = 0.0053 (0.5%). Premarital sex liberalization is cohort-driven: generational replacement accounts for the bulk of between-context variation net of individual characteristics. This finding is consistent with and extends earlier decomposition work on GSS premarital sex data (Harding and Jencks 2003; Elias et al. 2013).

For homosexual sex, the pattern reverses. Period variance (\sigma^2_{\text{period}} = 0.0728) dominates cohort variance (\sigma^2_{\text{cohort}} = 0.0036) by a factor of 20.2, producing ICC_{\text{period}} = 0.0526 (5.3%) and ICC_{\text{cohort}} = 0.0026 (0.3%). Homosexuality acceptance is period-driven: the liberalizing shift occurred across all birth cohorts simultaneously, consistent with society-wide forces such as legal milestones, media visibility, and interpersonal contact (Ofosu et al. 2019; Andersen and Fetner 2008). The period ICC of 5.3% is the largest contextual effect observed for any domain, indicating that the historical moment of the survey matters more for homosexuality attitudes than it does for any other sexual question. Figure 2 illustrates the sharp post-2000 acceleration in period random effects for HOMOSEX.

For extramarital sex, both variance components are small: \sigma^2_{\text{cohort}} = 0.0014 and \sigma^2_{\text{period}} = 0.0049, yielding ICC_{\text{cohort}} = 0.0028 (0.3%) and ICC_{\text{period}} = 0.0094 (0.9%). Neither generational replacement nor period-level change has meaningfully shifted attitudes toward extramarital sex. This near-zero APC signature confirms extramarital sex condemnation as a cross-generational moral consensus that has persisted despite the liberalization of other sexual domains.

Taken together, these variance decompositions establish that American sexual attitudes cannot be described by a single “sexual revolution” narrative. Premarital sex, homosexual sex, and extramarital sex follow three distinct APC logics—cohort-driven, period-driven, and temporally stable, respectively.

Fixed Effects

Panel A of Table [tab:hapc_main] reports the fixed effects, which reveal domain-specific patterns in the covariates.

Gender. Women hold significantly more conservative attitudes toward premarital sex (b = -0.128, p < .001) and extramarital sex (b = -0.081, p < .001), but significantly more liberal attitudes toward homosexual sex (b = 0.192, p < .001). This gender reversal—women more restrictive on heterosexual permissiveness but more accepting of homosexuality—is consistent with the sexual double standard (England and Bearak 2014; Hyde 2013; Wu et al. 2019; Kruk and Matsick 2021) and with patterns of gendered moral reasoning documented cross-nationally.

Education. Each additional year of schooling is associated with more permissive attitudes across all three domains, with the strongest effect for homosexual sex (b = 0.081, p < .001), followed by premarital sex (b = 0.032, p < .001) and extramarital sex (b = 0.021, p < .001). The education gradient for homosexuality is 2.5 times larger than for premarital sex, indicating that formal education operates as a stronger liberalizing force for attitudes toward sexual minorities than for heterosexual norms (Scheepers et al. 2002).

Religious attendance. Frequency of religious service attendance is the strongest single predictor of conservative sexual attitudes across all three domains: b = -0.165 for premarital sex, b = -0.124 for homosexual sex, and b = -0.046 for extramarital sex (all p < .001). The monotonically declining religious attendance documented in Table [tab:desc]—from 4.0 in the 1970s to 2.8 in the 2020s—suggests that secularization operates as a cross-cutting force amplifying both cohort and period liberalization (Molteni and Biolcati 2023).

Political conservatism. Conservative political identification is associated with more restrictive attitudes in all three domains, with the largest effect for homosexual sex (b = -0.183, p < .001), followed by premarital sex (b = -0.129, p < .001) and extramarital sex (b = -0.063, p < .001). The political gradient for homosexuality is nearly three times the extramarital sex gradient, indicating that homosexuality attitudes are the most politically polarized of the three domains (Baldassarri and Park 2020).

Race. Black respondents hold significantly more conservative attitudes toward homosexual sex (b = -0.257, p < .001) and slightly more permissive attitudes toward extramarital sex (b = 0.069, p < .001), but do not differ from White respondents on premarital sex (b = 0.023, p = .10).

Age, marital status, and income. Net of cohort and period, the linear age effect is negative but small across all domains (b \approx -0.005), indicating modest conservatizing over the life course. Married respondents are more conservative on all three items (premarital: b = -0.173; homosexual: b = -0.239; extramarital: b = -0.134; all p < .001). Higher income is associated with greater permissiveness across all domains.

Gender

The gender stratification reveals pronounced asymmetries. For premarital sex, women show an extreme cohort signature (\sigma^2_C = 0.0887, P/C = 0.08), while men show a more balanced pattern (P/C = 0.90). Women’s premarital sex attitudes are almost entirely structured by birth cohort: younger cohorts of women have converged with men in permissiveness, while older cohorts remain distinctly conservative. For homosexual sex, the pattern inverts: women show an extreme period signature (\sigma^2_P = 0.0863, P/C = 213), far exceeding the already period-dominant pattern among men (P/C = 5.24). Women responded to the society-wide homosexuality acceptance shift more uniformly across cohorts than men did. These results suggest that women were both the primary beneficiaries of generational premarital sex norm change and the most responsive audience for period-level homosexuality acceptance movements.

Religious Attendance

Low-attendance respondents show amplified APC effects in both directions. For premarital sex, the low-attendance group has the most extreme cohort dominance of any subgroup (\sigma^2_C = 0.1254, P/C = 0.05), while the high-attendance group shows a more muted and balanced pattern (P/C = 0.81). For homosexual sex, the low-attendance group shows stronger period dominance (\sigma^2_P = 0.1119, P/C = 81.7) than the high-attendance group (\sigma^2_P = 0.0363, P/C = 191.5), though both are overwhelmingly period-driven. The high-attendance group’s extremely large P/C ratio reflects its near-zero cohort variance (\sigma^2_C = 0.0002), indicating that highly religious Americans’ attitudes toward homosexuality are almost entirely invariant across cohorts—any change that did occur was a period-level phenomenon. Religious participation constrains intergenerational attitude divergence, particularly on homosexuality (Reimer and Watts 2025).

Education

College-educated respondents show slightly stronger cohort dominance for premarital sex (P/C = 0.09) compared to those without college (P/C = 0.27). For homosexuality, both groups are strongly period-dominant, with the college group showing a P/C of 20.4 and the non-college group 15.6. The educational gradient is modest relative to gender and religion as a moderator of APC structure.

Political Views

Self-identified liberals exhibit the most extreme APC signatures. For premarital sex, liberals show P/C = 0.04 (extreme cohort dominance with \sigma^2_C = 0.1008), while conservatives show P/C = 0.77. For homosexual sex, liberals show P/C = 92.2 (extreme period dominance), while conservatives also show period dominance (\sigma^2_C \approx 0, P/C undefined due to near-zero cohort variance). These patterns indicate that political liberals underwent the sharpest generational divide on premarital sex and the most uniform period-level shift on homosexuality.

Robustness Checks

Table [tab:robustness] reports variance components across four alternative specifications. The core finding—premarital sex as cohort-dominant, homosexual sex as period-dominant, and extramarital sex as stable—holds across all specifications.

APC-Intrinsic Estimator

The APC-IE produces cohort variance (\sigma^2_C = 0.1078) that is substantially larger than period variance (\sigma^2_P = 0.0042) for premarital sex, confirming cohort dominance. For homosexual sex, the IE yields larger absolute magnitudes for both components (\sigma^2_C = 0.1146, \sigma^2_P = 0.0157), but the period ICC (0.011) remains positive and the cohort estimate is likely inflated by the IE’s tendency to absorb linear trends into cohort coefficients under sum-to-zero constraints (Yang et al. 2008). The qualitative ranking of domains is preserved: premarital sex has the highest cohort-to-total ratio, and extramarital sex has the smallest variance components overall.

Alternative Cohort Bandwidth

Using 10-year instead of 5-year cohort groups produces nearly identical variance components for premarital sex (\sigma^2_C = 0.0511, \sigma^2_P = 0.0061) and extramarital sex (\sigma^2_C = 0.0017, \sigma^2_P = 0.0050). For homosexual sex, the 10-year grouping yields a slightly larger cohort variance (\sigma^2_C = 0.0071) but period dominance remains clear (\sigma^2_P = 0.0675, ICC_P = 0.049). The results are not sensitive to cohort bandwidth.

Binary Logit HAPC

Dichotomizing responses into permissive versus restrictive and estimating logistic HAPC models amplifies the variance components (as expected given the logistic scale) but preserves the cross-domain ordering. Premarital sex shows \sigma^2_C = 0.1363 versus \sigma^2_P = 0.0249; homosexual sex shows \sigma^2_C = 0.0022 versus \sigma^2_P = 0.2826; extramarital sex shows the smallest total variance. The period ICC for homosexual sex (0.079) is the largest of any specification, confirming the dominance of period effects.

Excluding 2021–2024

Restricting the sample to 1973–2018 (pre-web-mode waves) does not alter the central pattern. Premarital sex retains cohort dominance (\sigma^2_C = 0.0142 > \sigma^2_P = 0.0077), homosexual sex retains period dominance (\sigma^2_P = 0.0497 > \sigma^2_C = 0.0052), and extramarital sex remains stable (\sigma^2_C = 0.0035, \sigma^2_P = 0.0044). The divergent APC signatures are not artifacts of the GSS mode transition.

Discussion

This study set out to determine whether American sexual attitudes followed a single liberalizing trajectory or whether distinct moral domains exhibited different temporal dynamics. The answer is unambiguous: they did not move in unison. Across half a century of GSS data (1973–2024, N = 69{,}517), our HAPC-CCREM decomposition reveals three qualitatively different age-period-cohort signatures – one cohort-dominant, one period-dominant, and one essentially flat – corresponding to premarital sex, homosexuality, and extramarital sex, respectively. We organize the discussion around what these three signatures mean for theory, what the heterogeneity patterns reveal about who is driving change, and what limitations bound the conclusions we can draw.

Three Revolutions, Not One

The variance decomposition in Table [tab:hapc_main] makes the divergence concrete. For premarital sex attitudes, cohort variance far exceeds period variance (\sigma^2_C = 0.050 vs. \sigma^2_P = 0.006; \text{ICC}_C = 4.4\%, \text{ICC}_P = 0.5\%). Each successive birth cohort entered adult life with more permissive premarital sex norms than its predecessor, and that imprint persisted as cohorts aged. This pattern replicates and extends Harding and Jencks (2003), who concluded from GSS data through 2002 that cohort replacement was the primary engine of premarital sex liberalization. Our analysis confirms their finding with 22 additional years of data and a formal HAPC variance partition. Elias et al. (2013) challenged the cohort-only interpretation by identifying within-cohort period effects for premarital sex using GSS data through 2008; our estimates do show a non-zero \sigma^2_P, but it is an order of magnitude smaller than \sigma^2_C, indicating that cohort replacement remains the dominant process even as some period-level softening occurs. The mechanism is consistent with impressionable-years socialization: each generation was raised in a progressively more permissive cultural milieu, and the norms internalized during adolescence proved durable (Davis 2004; Jeannet and Dražanová 2023). Declining religiosity across cohorts – itself a generational replacement process (Molteni and Biolcati 2023) – further loosened the constraint that religious attendance places on premarital sex permissiveness.

Homosexuality acceptance tells a different story. Here, period variance dominates (\sigma^2_P = 0.073 vs. \sigma^2_C = 0.004; \text{ICC}_P = 5.3\%, \text{ICC}_C = 0.3\%). Attitudes shifted across all cohorts simultaneously, consistent with society-wide forces operating independently of generational turnover. The period signature aligns with a cascade of institutional and legal milestones: the declassification of homosexuality as a mental disorder, the rise of LGBT visibility in media (Witeck 2014), state-level same-sex marriage legislation, and the 2015 Obergefell v. Hodges ruling. Ofosu et al. (2019) demonstrated that same-sex marriage legalization was associated with reduced implicit and explicit anti-gay bias, providing direct evidence that legal change operates as a period-level attitude shifter. Andersen and Fetner (2008) documented substantial within-cohort change in homosexuality tolerance in both Canada and the United States between 1981 and 2000, a finding our HAPC results confirm and extend through 2024. The contact hypothesis offers a complementary mechanism: as more LGBT individuals came out publicly, the proportion of Americans reporting personal acquaintance with a gay or lesbian person rose, and Paluck et al. (2018) confirmed in a meta-analysis that intergroup contact causally reduces prejudice (see also Hans et al. 2012; Hegarty 2020). Together, legal recognition, media representation, and interpersonal contact created period-level shocks that moved attitudes across the age spectrum.

Extramarital sex stands apart from both patterns. Its variance components are near zero on both dimensions (\sigma^2_C = 0.001, \sigma^2_P = 0.005; \text{ICC}_C = 0.3\%, \text{ICC}_P = 0.9\%), confirming that condemnation of marital infidelity is a cross-generational moral consensus that has resisted the forces driving liberalization in the other two domains. The descriptive trends (Table [tab:desc]) reinforce this: the XMARSEX mean barely moved from 1.47 in the 1970s to 1.30 in the 2000s (on a 1–4 scale), fluctuating within a narrow band of strong disapproval. Fitouchi et al. (2022) proposed that puritanical sexual morality rests on a cognitive architecture linking sexual restraint to cooperative trustworthiness. Extramarital sex, unlike premarital sex or homosexuality, involves a direct violation of a partner’s trust and consent – a harm that triggers moral condemnation rooted in fairness intuitions rather than purity concerns alone. This harm-based moral logic appears to transcend generational and period boundaries.

The divergence we document is not unique to the United States. Gao and Wang (2025), using Chinese General Social Survey data (2010–2021), found that premarital sex and homosexuality attitudes liberalized through cohort and period effects while extramarital sex attitudes remained stable. Liu and Yang (2024) reported a similar pattern of divergent sexual attitude trajectories in China. That two societies with profoundly different cultural, religious, and political histories exhibit the same domain-specific divergence suggests the underlying moral logic – liberalization extends to consensual behavior but halts at behavior involving non-consensual third-party harm – may reflect a general feature of how humans moralize sexual conduct.

The Consent-Autonomy Boundary

We propose a unifying framework: the sexual revolution extended the logic of individual autonomy to consensual adult sexual behavior but did not extend it to behavior that inflicts non-consensual harm on third parties. This consent-autonomy boundary parsimoniously explains the three divergent trajectories.

Premarital sex involves two consenting adults who are not violating any third party’s trust. As autonomy norms strengthened across successive cohorts – propelled by postmaterialist value change (Inglehart and Appel 1989), rising education, and secularization – each new generation was socialized into greater acceptance, producing the observed cohort-dominant pattern. Homosexuality, likewise involving consenting adults, was reframed over the study period from a moral failing to an identity deserving legal protection. Because this reframing operated through institutional channels (courts, legislatures, media) that reach all age groups simultaneously, the attitude shift was period-dominant. Extramarital sex, by contrast, involves a betrayal of a partner who has not consented to the breach of exclusivity. Andersson (2022) demonstrated in qualitative interviews that even practitioners of consensual non-monogamy draw a bright moral line at infidelity, condemning it on grounds of honesty and consent violation. The condemnation of extramarital sex, then, is not a residual puritanical taboo but an expression of the same autonomy ethic that drove liberalization elsewhere: individuals have the right not to be deceived by their partners.

The heterogeneity results in Table [tab:heterogeneity] enrich this framework. Women show the most extreme cohort dominance for premarital sex (P/C = 0.08, meaning period variance is less than one-tenth of cohort variance) and the most extreme period dominance for homosexuality (P/C = 213). The premarital sex pattern suggests that women’s attitudes converged with men’s across cohorts as the sexual double standard eroded generationally (England and Bearak 2014; Stick and Fetner 2020). The homosexuality pattern indicates that women were especially responsive to the period-level forces – legal milestones, rights-based framing, contact – that drove acceptance across all cohorts. These gender-specific patterns align with evidence that women’s moral reasoning is more responsive to rights-based and care-based frameworks (Blau and Kahn 2017), which are precisely the frameworks that the marriage-equality movement mobilized.

Political ideology amplifies the period effect for homosexuality: liberals show P/C = 92 while conservatives show near-zero cohort variance (\sigma^2_C < 0.001) and a more modest period effect (\sigma^2_P = 0.045). This suggests that the society-wide period shift in homosexuality acceptance operated primarily through politically receptive populations. Baldassarri and Park (2020) documented a parallel process: partisan sorting on moral issues has intensified since the 1970s, with Democrats adopting progressive sexual views earlier and Republicans following at a slower pace. Hodson and Puffer (2025) showed that generalized prejudice toward marginalized groups – including gay people – became increasingly correlated with political conservatism between 2004 and 2020, reflecting growing ideological alignment of anti-gay bias. Similarly, Asongu (2026) demonstrated how sexual conservatism can function as a primary political identity, overriding other considerations. Our results extend these findings by showing that the APC engine itself differs by political orientation – liberals experienced a faster period-driven shift, while conservatives showed less variance on both dimensions.

The 2020s may test the stability of the consent-autonomy boundary. XMARSEX means rose from 1.30 in the 2000s to 1.54 in the 2020s (Table [tab:desc]), the largest decade-over-decade shift in the series. Growing discourse around consensual non-monogamy (CNM) has begun to reframe some forms of extra-dyadic sexual activity as consensual rather than transgressive (Gupta et al. 2023). Masterson et al. (2025) found that 23% of US adults reported decreased importance of monogamy following the COVID-19 pandemic, and 11% expressed greater interest in CNM relationships. If the consent-autonomy boundary is indeed the operative moral principle, then reframing extramarital sex as consensual (when it occurs within an agreed-upon CNM arrangement) could erode the long-standing condemnation. Whether the 2020s uptick in XMARSEX permissiveness reflects a genuine normative shift or a survey-mode artifact (the GSS transitioned to web administration in 2021) remains an open question that future research must adjudicate.

Broader Implications and Limitations

The finding that different sexual attitude domains have different APC engines carries direct policy implications. If premarital sex norms change primarily through generational socialization, then interventions targeting young people – comprehensive sex education, media representation, campus programming – are the most effective levers. If homosexuality acceptance changes through period-level institutional action, then legal reforms, public campaigns, and judicial rulings can accelerate attitude change across all age groups simultaneously. And if extramarital sex condemnation is anchored in a harm-consent logic that resists both cohort replacement and period shocks, then it is unlikely to be dislodged by the same forces that drove liberalization elsewhere – unless the framing of the behavior itself changes, as the CNM movement seeks to do.

Religious attendance is the strongest predictor of conservative sexual attitudes across all three domains (Table [tab:hapc_main]), and the heterogeneity analysis reveals that low-attendance respondents show amplified cohort and period effects (Table [tab:heterogeneity]: \sigma^2_C = 0.125 for low-attendance PREMARSX vs. \sigma^2_C = 0.011 for high-attendance). This pattern is consistent with Molteni and Biolcati (2023), who showed that religious decline in Europe proceeds via generational replacement, and with Reimer and Watts (2025), who documented a weakening link between religiosity and moral restrictiveness in five Western countries. As secularization continues through cohort replacement, it removes one of the strongest anchors of conservative sexual attitudes, potentially accelerating liberalization in all three domains – though the extramarital sex domain may prove resistant for reasons independent of religion.

Several limitations bound the inferences we can draw. First, the APC identification problem remains fundamental. Bell and Jones (2014) and Bell and Jones (2017) demonstrated through simulation that the HAPC model can misattribute variance between periods and cohorts under certain data-generating processes. Our multi-method strategy – triangulating HAPC-CCREM, the Intrinsic Estimator (Table [tab:robustness]), alternative cohort groupings, and binary logit specifications – provides convergent evidence for the core finding of domain-specific divergence, but we cannot claim to have fully resolved the identification constraint. The APC-IE results show elevated cohort variance for HOMOSEX (\sigma^2_C = 0.115) relative to HAPC (\sigma^2_C = 0.004), reflecting the sensitivity that E. Fosse and Winship (2019) documented. What is robust across all specifications is the relative ordering: PREMARSX is always more cohort-driven than HOMOSEX, and XMARSEX always shows near-zero variance on both dimensions.

Second, the GSS shifted from in-person interviewing to a web-based push-to-web design beginning in 2021. Although our robustness check excluding 2021–2024 data shows that the core patterns hold (Table [tab:robustness]: the divergence persists in pre-2021 data), the XMARSEX uptick in the 2020s should be interpreted cautiously. Social desirability pressures differ between face-to-face and self-administered web surveys (Phoo et al. 2022), and web respondents may report more permissive extramarital sex attitudes simply because of reduced interviewer presence.

Third, the four-point response scale (1 = always wrong to 4 = not wrong at all) creates ceiling and floor effects. XMARSEX responses are heavily concentrated at “always wrong” (the 2020s mean is still only 1.54 on a 1–4 scale), leaving limited room for variance to emerge on either the period or cohort dimension. The near-zero variance components for XMARSEX may partly reflect measurement compression rather than true attitudinal homogeneity, though the consistency of this pattern across five decades and multiple specifications suggests it captures a genuine phenomenon.

Fourth, the GSS is a repeated cross-section, not a panel. We observe aggregate cohort and period patterns but cannot track individual-level attitude trajectories. Ekstam (2022, 2023) addressed this gap using panel data and found that individual attitudes toward homosexuality are remarkably stable, with aggregate liberalization driven by cohort composition – a finding consistent with our HAPC decomposition. Extending this panel approach to premarital and extramarital sex attitudes would strengthen causal inference about the mechanisms underlying domain-specific change.

Fifth, social desirability bias is not constant over time. As liberal sexual attitudes became more socially accepted, respondents may have become more willing to express permissive views, inflating the apparent magnitude of period and cohort effects (Delfgaauw and Boring 2024; Heide-Jørgensen 2023). The observed trends thus reflect some combination of genuine attitude change and declining self-censorship, a confound inherent to all survey-based attitude research.

Conclusion

Over five decades of GSS data reveal that American sexual attitudes underwent not one revolution but three distinct processes: premarital sex liberalized through cohort replacement (\sigma^2_C = 0.050, \text{ICC}_C = 4.4\%), homosexuality acceptance surged through society-wide period effects (\sigma^2_P = 0.073, \text{ICC}_P = 5.3\%), and extramarital sex condemnation persisted as a cross-generational consensus (both ICCs < 1\%).

These findings carry three implications. For scholarship, they demonstrate that “the sexual revolution” is a misleading singular noun. Different sexual attitude domains are governed by different moral logics – autonomy-based reasoning for consensual behavior, harm-based reasoning for trust violations – and these logics produce fundamentally different temporal dynamics. Treating sexual liberalization as a monolithic trend obscures the distinct APC engines that drive change in each domain and risks misidentifying the social forces responsible.

For theory, the consent-autonomy boundary framework offers an integrative explanation for why some sexual taboos fall while others endure. The boundary predicts that attitudes will liberalize when a behavior can be reframed as involving only consenting adults, and will remain stable when a behavior is perceived as inflicting non-consensual harm. The cross-national parallel with Chinese data (Gao and Wang 2025; Liu and Yang 2024) suggests this framework may generalize beyond the American context.

For policy, the APC engine matters. Cohort-driven change, as in premarital sex attitudes, is slow – it requires a full generational turnover – but it is durable, because it reflects deep socialization that individuals carry throughout their lives. Period-driven change, as in homosexuality acceptance, can be rapid, because institutional and legal actions reach all age groups simultaneously. But period-driven change is also potentially reversible: legal protections can be rolled back, and the Ofosu et al. (2019) finding that states which never passed same-sex marriage legislation showed increased anti-gay bias after federal legalization is a cautionary reminder that period effects can move in both directions.

As consensual non-monogamy discourse grows and relationship norms continue to evolve, future research should monitor whether the 2020s mark the beginning of the last sexual taboo’s erosion or whether the uptick in extramarital sex permissiveness reflects only a survey-mode artifact. Our findings complement research using similar GSS-based APC designs in adjacent domains: Price et al. (2015) applied the intrinsic estimator to pornography attitudes across 40 GSS years and found cohort and age effects distinct from period trends, while Behrman (2024) documented stable ideal family size preferences alongside shifting reproductive orientations, and Herbenick et al. (2021) documented declining sexual frequency between 2009 and 2018, suggesting behavioral and attitudinal trends do not always move in tandem. The answer will test whether the consent-autonomy boundary is a fixed feature of moral cognition or a historically contingent line that, like the boundaries around premarital sex and homosexuality before it, can be redrawn.
