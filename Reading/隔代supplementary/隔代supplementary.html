Intergenerational Transmission of Caregiver Maltreatment and Joint Developmental Trajectories of Internalizing and Externalizing Behavior: Prospective Links to Young Adult Psychopathology

[Blinded for Review]

Overview

This supplement provides additional analytic detail and sensitivity analyses that support the findings reported in the main text. Five supplementary items (S1–S5) are organized as follows:

Latent Growth Model Parameter Estimates

Domain-Specific GBTM Model Comparison and Dual Trajectory Combinations

Negative Binomial vs. Poisson Comparison

Binary Victimization (Any vs. None) Results

Sex-Stratified Poisson Regression Results

Items S1 and S2 document the model-building steps that informed the analytic decisions described in the main text. Items S3–S5 present sensitivity analyses that test the robustness of the main findings to alternative model specifications, predictor operationalizations, and sample stratifications.

S1: Latent Growth Model Parameter Estimates

Prior to fitting the dual group-based trajectory model (GBTM), we estimated unconditional and conditional parallel-process latent growth models (LGM) in lavaan using FIML estimation. The purpose of this step was twofold: (a) to determine whether linear or quadratic polynomial specifications best captured the developmental course of internalizing and externalizing raw scores across ages 4–16, and (b) to test whether caregiver victimization count predicted growth factor intercepts and slopes.

Panel A of the table below compares model fit for linear and quadratic specifications. For both domains, the quadratic model provided substantially better fit (internalizing: \Delta\text{BIC} = 150, \Delta\text{CFI} = .067; externalizing: \Delta\text{BIC} = 38, \Delta\text{CFI} = .017). Quadratic specifications were therefore retained for all subsequent trajectory analyses.

Panel B reports growth factor estimates from the unconditional quadratic models. Internalizing scores showed a significant positive linear slope (B = 0.57, p < .001) tempered by a significant negative quadratic term (B = -0.034, p < .001), indicating an inverted-U pattern that rose through middle childhood before leveling off. Externalizing scores showed no significant linear or quadratic trends at the mean level, consistent with the relatively flat average trajectory observed in this sample, but substantial between-person variance in both intercepts and slopes.

Panel C reports the conditional model, in which growth factors were regressed on caregiver victimization count and child sex. Victimization predicted higher intercepts for both internalizing (B = 0.34, p < .001) and externalizing (B = 0.73, p < .001) but did not predict the externalizing slope (p = .851). The internalizing slope was marginally associated with victimization (B = 0.018, p = .026). Panel D reports cross-domain covariances, which confirmed strong positive associations between internalizing and externalizing intercepts (B = 23.34, p < .001) and between slopes (B = 0.34, p < .001), motivating the dual-trajectory approach used in the main analysis.

CFI = comparative fit index; TLI = Tucker–Lewis index; RMSEA = root mean square error of approximation; SRMR = standardized root mean square residual; BIC not shown (INT: 44,726 vs. 44,576; EXT: 49,464 vs. 49,426). Quadratic models selected for both domains based on lower BIC, higher CFI/TLI, and lower RMSEA/SRMR.

Note. N = 1{,}197. Time coded as age 4 = 0, age 16 = 12 (in 2-year units). Conditional model controls for caregiver victimization count (range 0–8) and child sex.

S2: Domain-Specific GBTM Model Comparison and Dual Trajectory Combinations

Following the LGM results, separate GBTMs were estimated for internalizing and externalizing CBCL raw scores using Stata’s traj command with censored normal (CNORM) distributions and quadratic polynomial time functions. For each domain, models with 1 through 5 groups were compared on three criteria recommended by Nagin (2005): Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC; lower is better), average posterior probability of correct classification (AvePP > .70), and minimum group size (\geq 5% of the sample).

For both internalizing and externalizing domains, BIC decreased monotonically from the 1-group through the 5-group solution (Panels A and B). The elbow in BIC improvement occurred at k = 3 for internalizing (\Delta\text{BIC}_{3 \text{ vs } 2} = 271; \Delta\text{BIC}_{4 \text{ vs } 3} = 107) and at k = 3 for externalizing (\Delta\text{BIC}_{3 \text{ vs } 2} = 347; \Delta\text{BIC}_{4 \text{ vs } 3} = 94). The 3-group solution was selected for both domains because it provided the best balance of parsimony, interpretability, and group size. All groups in the 3-group solutions exceeded the 5% minimum size threshold, and AvePP values were high (> .85).

Panel C reports the BIC for the selected 3 \times 3 dual-trajectory model estimated in Stata’s traj command. The dual model BIC (-45{,}673) represented a substantial improvement (\Delta\text{BIC} = 2{,}460) over the sum of the separate domain-specific BICs (-22{,}305 + {-}25{,}828 = -48{,}133), supporting the joint specification. The resulting domain-specific group assignments were cross-tabulated to form the five joint trajectory categories described in the main text (see Table [tab:joint_groups]).

Min Group refers to the smallest group across both domains (internalizing High-Chronic: n = 73, 6.1%). The 3 \times 3 solution yielded three internalizing groups (Low-Stable, 55.8%; Moderate-Rising, 38.1%; High-Chronic, 6.1%) and three externalizing groups (Low-Declining, 50.8%; Moderate-Stable, 37.6%; High-Chronic, 11.6%).

S3: Negative Binomial vs. Poisson Comparison

The main analyses used Poisson regression with robust (sandwich) standard errors, which provides valid inference even when the Poisson equidispersion assumption is violated. To evaluate the degree of overdispersion and its impact on point estimates, we re-estimated all outcome models using negative binomial (NB) regression. The estimated overdispersion parameter \hat\alpha ranged from 0.97 for MDD (near-equidispersion) to 10.77 for PTSD (substantial overdispersion).

IRR point estimates were virtually identical across Poisson and NB specifications for MDD, GAD, and panic, confirming that the robust Poisson approach adequately handles mild-to-moderate overdispersion. For PTSD (\hat\alpha = 10.77) and substance use (\hat\alpha = 8.42), NB estimates were modestly larger. Across all five outcomes and all four non-reference joint groups, the direction and magnitude of effects were highly consistent between the two estimators, with NB occasionally yielding slightly wider confidence intervals for the most overdispersed outcomes.

S4: Binary Victimization (Any vs. None) Results

The main analyses operationalized caregiver victimization as a cumulative count (range 0–8). To assess sensitivity to predictor specification, we re-estimated the multinomial and KHB mediation models using a binary indicator (any victimization vs. none; 54.6% endorsed at least one type).

Panel A shows that the binary predictor yielded significant associations with three of the four non-reference joint groups: Elevated Both (\text{RRR} = 2.30, p < .001), High Unilateral (\text{RRR} = 3.34, p < .001), and Comorbid High (\text{RRR} = 2.25, p = .008). The Moderate group did not reach significance (\text{RRR} = 1.35, p = .131). These results confirm that the binary predictor differentiates among trajectory groups and that any-versus-none victimization exposure is associated with membership in the higher-risk joint categories.

Panel B reports KHB linear decomposition results. All five indirect effects were significant (p < .001–.016), with magnitudes ranging from 0.040 (substance use) to 0.284 (MDD). The pattern parallels the count-predictor results and confirms that joint trajectory group membership mediates the link between binary victimization and age-18 psychiatric symptoms across all outcome domains.

Panel B: KHB linear decomposition; Reduced = total effect, Full = direct effect, Indirect = Reduced - Full; mediators: four joint-group indicators (Moderate, Elevated Both, High Unilateral, Comorbid High); concomitants: child sex, race/ethnicity. S5: Sex-Stratified Poisson Regression Results

To examine whether the associations between trajectory group membership and age-18 psychiatric outcomes differed by sex, we re-estimated the Poisson outcome models separately for boys (n = 577) and girls (n = 620). The five joint groups were distributed as follows: Low-Low (boys = 264, girls = 310), Moderate (boys = 70, girls = 57), Elevated Both (boys = 160, girls = 178), High Unilateral (boys = 61, girls = 43), and Comorbid High (boys = 22, girls = 32).

Among boys, the Elevated Both group showed significant associations with MDD, GAD, and panic; the High Unilateral group showed significant associations with MDD, GAD, PTSD, panic, and substance use; and the Comorbid High group showed significant associations with MDD, GAD, panic, and substance use. The Moderate group showed significant associations with substance use only. Among girls, the Elevated Both group showed significant associations with MDD, PTSD, panic, and substance use; the Moderate group with panic only; and the Comorbid High group with MDD and panic. The High Unilateral group did not reach significance on any outcome among girls, likely reflecting reduced power in the smaller subgroup (n = 43). The broader pattern for boys, particularly the significant substance use associations across multiple groups, is consistent with prior evidence that externalizing-pathway risk carries greater prognostic weight for males in the transition to adulthood.
