The Internet and the Gender Ideology Gap: Digital Exposure, Feminist Consciousness, and Masculinist Backlash in Contemporary China

[Blinded for Review]

Abstract

Modernization theory predicts that internet diffusion promotes egalitarian gender attitudes through information access and exposure to liberal norms. We test this prediction using seven waves of the Chinese General Social Survey (2012–2023, N = 79,014) and find that internet use polarizes rather than liberalizes gender attitudes. OLS models with year fixed effects show that internet use is associated with more egalitarian attitudes among women but only weakly among men (Female \times Internet \beta = 0.082, p < .001). Instrumental variable estimation, using province-year leave-one-out mean internet use as an instrument for individual internet use (first-stage F = 714.57), reveals that internet use causally reduces men’s gender egalitarianism (\beta = -0.087, p < .001) while producing negligible effects on women’s attitudes. The gender ideology gap widened approximately sevenfold between 2012 and 2023, from 0.03 to 0.18 on a composite index of four gender attitude items. Oaxaca-Blinder decomposition shows that this widening is driven by differential returns to characteristics across genders, not by compositional differences. Heterogeneity analyses indicate that the polarization effect is strongest among younger cohorts (born after 1980) and the college-educated, but extends across urban and rural areas and all regions of China. These findings challenge the modernization narrative, extend the “stalled revolution” thesis to the ideological domain, and demonstrate that the same digital technology enabling feminist mobilization also facilitates organized backlash against gender equality.

Introduction

Between 2012 and 2023, the gap between Chinese men’s and women’s gender attitudes widened dramatically. This divergence occurred during a period when the structural differences between men and women were narrowing: women caught up with men in internet access, continued to close the educational gap, and maintained high rates of labor force participation. Why did attitudinal convergence fail to follow structural convergence? And what role did the internet play?

The question matters for several reasons. China is home to 1.4 billion people and the world’s largest internet population. Gender equality is an explicit state goal, enshrined in law and party doctrine, yet the ideological gap between men and women is accelerating. This gap has consequences beyond attitudes: gender ideology predicts household labor division (Greenstein 1996), labor market outcomes (Acker 1990), marital satisfaction (Davis and Greenstein 2009), political participation (Bolzendahl and Myers 2004), and couples’ employment and family arrangements (Bornatici and Zinn 2025). A widening gap signals that men and women are developing increasingly incompatible expectations about work, family, and each other.

Prior research has documented trends in Chinese gender attitudes using the Chinese General Social Survey (CGSS), identifying education, urbanization, and cohort replacement as key predictors (Wang et al. 2024; Su and Ottervik 2023; Cotter et al. 2011). But these studies treat the internet as absent from their models or, at most, as a single control variable. The rapidly growing literature on digital feminism in China is primarily qualitative, documenting #MeToo activism, feminist self-media, and state censorship without testing their population-level attitudinal effects (Huang 2022; Chen 2020). No study has examined whether internet use affects men’s and women’s gender attitudes differently, and none has attempted causal identification.

We address this gap by pooling seven waves of the CGSS (2012–2023, N = 79,014) and testing whether internet use widens the gender ideology gap. Our analytical strategy proceeds in four steps. We first document the descriptive trend in gender attitudes by gender. We then estimate OLS models with a Female \times Internet interaction, controlling for education, income, age, hukou status, marital status, party membership, and year fixed effects. To address the endogeneity of internet use, we instrument individual internet use with province-year leave-one-out mean internet use, which captures infrastructure-driven variation in access. We decompose the gender attitude gap using the Oaxaca-Blinder method to identify whether the widening reflects compositional differences or differential returns.

The findings are stark. OLS models show that internet use is associated with more egalitarian attitudes among women (\beta = 0.082, p < .001 for the Female \times Internet interaction). In Model 3, internet use showed no significant association with men’s egalitarianism (\beta = 0.006, p = .07). In Model 4, the main effect reached significance (\beta = 0.009, p < .01) but remained substantively small. Instrumental variable estimation reveals a more troubling pattern: after removing the positive confounding of education and urbanity, internet use causally reduces men’s gender egalitarianism (\beta = -0.087, p < .001; first-stage F = 714.57). The same technology that feminist scholars have celebrated as a tool for women’s empowerment is, for men, a vehicle for backlash. Oaxaca-Blinder decomposition confirms that the widening gap is driven by differential returns to characteristics, not by differences in characteristics themselves.

This paper makes three contributions. First, we provide the first causal evidence that internet use polarizes, rather than uniformly liberalizes, gender attitudes in a non-Western context, challenging the modernization narrative that has dominated the literature since Inglehart and Norris (2003). Second, we extend Hochschild and Machung (1989)’s ((1989)) “stalled revolution” thesis to the ideological domain in the digital era: the revolution is not merely stalled but bifurcated, with internet technology as the wedge that splits men’s and women’s attitude trajectories. Third, we advance the methodological standard for internet-and-attitudes research by combining instrumental variable estimation with decomposition analysis on decade-spanning nationally representative data, an approach that moves the field beyond correlational claims.

Background and Theory

Gender Ideology in Post-Reform China

China’s relationship with gender equality is defined by a paradox. The Maoist state declared that “women hold up half the sky” and mobilized women into the labor force at rates that rivaled Scandinavian social democracies. Yet the post-reform era (1978–present) has seen a partial reversal of these gains. Women’s labor force participation declined from over 73% in 1990 to under 61% by 2020 (Feng 2023; Song and Zhou 2019; Wu et al. 2024). Occupational segregation deepened as market forces replaced state job allocation (Du and Dong 2013), and gender inequality persisted across multiple dimensions of economic life (Brussevich et al. 2021; Xiao and Asadullah 2020). State discourse shifted from celebrating “iron women” to promoting “good wives and virtuous mothers” (

), a reversion that reflected both commercial culture’s commodification of femininity and the party-state’s pronatalist anxieties (Yu and Xie 2015; Ji, Wu, Zhu, et al. 2017).

Two structural developments complicate this picture. First, educational expansion has inverted the gender gap at the tertiary level: women now outperform men in university enrollment and graduation rates, creating widespread educational hypogamy in the marriage market (Ji, Wu, Sun, et al. 2017). This inversion generates status anxiety among men whose expected position in the gender hierarchy no longer aligns with their educational credentials. Second, the one-child policy (1979–2015) concentrated parental investment in daughters while simultaneously reinforcing son preference in rural areas, producing a generation of young women with high human capital and high expectations for equality (Chang and Tian 2020).

Survey evidence from the Chinese General Social Survey (CGSS) documents the attitudinal consequences of these structural shifts. Education is consistently the strongest predictor of egalitarian gender attitudes; urban residents hold more egalitarian views than rural residents; and women score higher than men across all measured attitude dimensions (Wang et al. 2024; Li and Jiang 2019; Su and Ottervik 2023). The gender attitude gap has widened over the 2010s, a pattern that echoes the U.S. experience documented by Cotter et al. (2011), the cohort-replacement dynamics analyzed by Brooks­ and Bolzendahl (2003), and cross-national trends identified in comparative research (Pandian 2018). But what drives this widening? Why have men’s and women’s attitudes diverged rather than converged as their structural positions—education, urbanization, internet access—have become more similar? The answer, we argue, lies in the digital information environment.

Internet, Information Environments, and Attitude Formation

Modernization theory offers a straightforward prediction: internet diffusion should promote egalitarian attitudes. Access to information, exposure to global norms, and participation in cosmopolitan discourse should erode traditional beliefs and move societies toward liberal values (Inglehart and Norris 2003). Early empirical work on internet and political attitudes provided partial support for this account (Zhuravskaya et al. 2020; Melnikov 2021).

But a competing theoretical tradition predicts the opposite. Sunstein (2001) argued that information abundance does not guarantee exposure to diverse viewpoints. Instead, users in choice-rich environments gravitate toward attitude-consistent content, creating “echo chambers” that reinforce prior beliefs. Algorithmic recommendation systems amplify this tendency by optimizing for engagement, which often means surfacing content that provokes strong emotional reactions—including outrage at outgroup positions (Eady et al. 2023; Mont’Alverne et al. 2024). The result is not convergence but polarization.

Four mechanisms link internet use to gender attitudes specifically. First, information provision: the internet gives users access to feminist ideas, gender inequality statistics, and discrimination narratives that may not circulate through traditional media. Second, identity reinforcement: online communities organized around gender identity—feminist groups, masculinist forums—provide social validation for pre-existing beliefs (Ging and Siapera 2019). Third, algorithmic selective exposure: recommendation systems learn users’ preferences and curate gendered content streams that differ sharply for men and women. Fourth, backlash dynamics: feminist visibility online triggers defensive reactions among those who perceive gender equality as threatening (Faludi 1991; Connell 2005).

China’s internet landscape provides fertile ground for all four mechanisms. The Great Firewall channels over one billion users onto domestic platforms—Weibo, WeChat, Douyin, Bilibili—where algorithmic recommendation systems operate without editorial balance requirements. Internet penetration grew from 38% in 2010 to 73% in 2021, with the sharpest growth in mobile access after 2014. The effects of this expansion on political attitudes have been shown to be heterogeneous: internet use can reinforce existing orientations rather than uniformly shift them (Bryson 2020). Yet no study has tested whether internet effects on gender attitudes differ by gender with adequate causal identification. This gap is significant because gender attitudes are more tightly linked to personal identity and daily life than abstract political positions, making selective exposure and backlash dynamics potentially stronger.

Digital Feminism and Masculinist Backlash in China

The 2010s saw the rapid emergence of digital feminism in China. The arrest of the “Feminist Five” activists in 2015 generated widespread online solidarity. The #MeToo movement reached Chinese universities and workplaces in 2018, spreading through creative circumvention of censorship (Huang 2022; Han and Liu 2023). Feminist self-media accounts on Weibo and WeChat attracted millions of followers. More recently, the 6B4T discourse—adapted from South Korean radical feminism—gained traction among young Chinese women, advocating disengagement from heterosexual relationships and traditional family roles (Chen 2020; Yang 2023). The relationship between feminism and the state is ambivalent: feminist content is simultaneously censored for its political implications and commercially amplified because it generates engagement (Liao 2024; Sun 2025).

The exposure to this content is asymmetric. Women disproportionately encounter feminist content through social networks, platform recommendations, and active seeking. Men encounter both feminist and anti-feminist content, but the latter is organized and amplified in ways that have no historical precedent. The “straight man cancer” (

) label, originally a feminist critique, became a rallying point for masculinist counter-communities. Masculinist influencers on Bilibili and Douyin attract large audiences. Nationalist anti-feminist rhetoric frames feminism as Western cultural infiltration (Peng 2022; Wang and Ouyang 2023; Xu 2025). Anti-feminist harassment campaigns systematically target prominent feminist accounts (Jones et al. 2019; Liotzis 2025).

Several conditions specific to contemporary China intensify these backlash dynamics. Educational hypogamy—women’s educational gains outpacing men’s—generates anxiety in a marriage market where men are traditionally expected to “marry down” (Ji, Wu, Sun, et al. 2017), and migration patterns further complicate gender inequality along intersecting axes of class and geography (Tong and Chen 2025). The “involution” (

Rising bride prices and housing expectations place financial pressures disproportionately on men. The “lying flat” (

These dynamics connect to Connell (2005)’s ((2005)) theorization of hegemonic masculinity: masculine identity is defended most aggressively when structural conditions make it feel precarious. The result is a gendered digital information environment in which the same platforms produce opposite attitudinal effects for men and women.

Theoretical Framework and Hypotheses

We integrate three theoretical perspectives to generate predictions about how internet use shapes the gender ideology gap.

First, Hochschild and Machung (1989)’s ((1989)) stalled revolution. Hochschild documented a revolution stalled in practice: women entered the paid workforce, but men did not proportionally enter domestic work, leaving women a “second shift.” We extend this framework to ideology. If women’s attitudes advance toward egalitarianism while men’s attitudes stall or reverse, the revolution is not merely stalled but bifurcated. The internet, rather than resolving the ideological stall, may deepen it by providing separate attitudinal tracks for men and women (England 2010; Scarborough et al. 2018; Gerson 2010).

Second, Risman (2004)’s ((2004)) conceptualization of gender as social structure. Gender operates simultaneously at the individual level (identities and attitudes), the interactional level (expectations and accountability in encounters), and the institutional level (organizational rules and state policies) (West and Zimmerman 1987; Ridgeway 2011; Acker 1990). Internet use reshapes the individual level—the identities and beliefs that users develop—but does so differentially for men and women. The same structural position (internet user) produces opposite attitudinal outcomes depending on the user’s location in the gender order. This is possible because the interactional level—the content ecosystems, peer communities, and algorithmic environments that constitute the online experience—is itself gendered (Davis and Greenstein 2009). Internet use, in this framing, is not merely a channel for information but a site where gender is accomplished: users do gender through their content consumption, community participation, and reactions to gendered discourse (Fox 2023; Risman 2017). The digital environment restructures the interactional level of gender by creating parallel but divergent spaces in which masculinity and femininity are performed, contested, and reinforced (Wu and Liu 2025).

Third, selective exposure theory (Sunstein 2001). In information-rich environments, users consume attitude-consistent content; algorithmic curation amplifies this tendency. Applied to gender: pre-existing attitude differences between men and women lead to gendered content consumption, which reinforces those differences, which further shapes content consumption. The process is self-reinforcing. Greenstein (1996) showed that men’s and women’s gender attitudes respond differently to the same household circumstances; we propose that a parallel dynamic operates in digital information environments, where the same technology yields divergent attitudinal returns by gender. Banet‐Weiser (2018) argues that “popular feminism” and “popular misogyny” are not independent phenomena but co-constituted: the visibility of feminist discourse online generates its own opposition, and the two feed off each other in a cycle that platforms profit from amplifying. This co-constitution framework predicts that internet expansion should produce simultaneous feminist mobilization and masculinist backlash, exactly the pattern our data reveal.

These three perspectives converge on a polarization prediction. Modernization theory expects internet use to move all users toward egalitarianism. We expect it to move men and women in different directions—or, more precisely, to leave women’s attitudes largely unchanged while pushing men’s attitudes in a conservative direction. This yields five testable hypotheses:

H1:

Internet use is positively associated with gender egalitarianism in the general population (main effect).

H2:

The association between internet use and egalitarianism is stronger for women than for men (Female \times Internet > 0).

H3:

The gender ideology gap widened between 2012 and 2023.

H4:

The widening gap is partially attributable to differential returns to internet exposure across genders (Oaxaca-Blinder coefficient component).

H5:

Instrumental variable estimation confirms the causal direction of H1 and H2, with the possibility that the main effect for men reverses sign once self-selection is addressed.

H1 follows from modernization theory and serves as a baseline. H2 is the core prediction: if gendered digital environments produce asymmetric exposure, internet use should affect men’s and women’s attitudes differently. H3 establishes the empirical pattern. H4 tests whether the mechanism is compositional (who uses the internet) or structural (how internet use translates into attitudes). H5 addresses the possibility that OLS estimates are biased by positive confounding—educated, urban individuals both use the internet more and hold more egalitarian attitudes for reasons unrelated to internet use itself. We note that these hypotheses also carry intersectional implications: the heterogeneity analyses (reported below) reveal that the polarization effect varies by education and cohort, suggesting that the intersection of gender with class and generation shapes how digital environments restructure gender ideology (Friedman 2015).

Data and Methods

Data

We draw on the Chinese General Social Survey (CGSS), a nationally representative repeated cross-sectional survey administered by the Renmin University of China. The CGSS employs a multi-stage stratified probability sampling design and collects data through face-to-face interviews with adults aged 18 and older across all Chinese provinces. We pool seven waves—2012, 2013, 2015, 2017, 2018, 2021, and 2023—yielding a total sample of 79,014 respondents. Because the 2015 wave lacks birth year information, age is missing for all respondents in that wave; after restricting to respondents with complete age data, the analytic sample for regression models is 68,046. The decade-long span of data captures both the rapid expansion of mobile internet access and the concurrent shifts in gender attitudes documented in prior CGSS-based research (Su and Ottervik 2023; Li and Jiang 2019; Hu et al. 2024).

Variables

Dependent variable. Our dependent variable is a Gender Ideology Index constructed as the mean of four items, each measured on a 5-point Likert scale (1 = strongly agree to 5 = strongly disagree): (1) “Men should focus on career, women on family”; (2) “Men are inherently more capable than women”; (3) “It is better to marry well than to work well”; and (4) “Women should be laid off first during economic downturns.” All items are reverse-coded so that higher values indicate more egalitarian attitudes. These items reflect a mix of separate-spheres ideology, essentialist beliefs, and labor-market discrimination acceptance—dimensions widely used in gender attitude research (Davis and Greenstein 2009; Knight and Hochschild 2016). The scale exhibits acceptable internal consistency (Cronbach’s \alpha = 0.705).

Key predictor. Internet use frequency is measured on a 5-point ordinal scale ranging from 1 (never) to 5 (very frequent/daily).

Key moderator. Gender is coded as a binary variable (female = 1).

Controls. We include age (centered at the grand mean), education (years of schooling, recoded from categorical responses), personal income (natural log-transformed, with zero incomes set to zero before transformation), urban hukou status (1 = urban), marital status (1 = currently married), and Communist Party membership (1 = member). One harmonization issue deserves note: the coding of party membership was reversed between the 2013 and 2015 waves; we recoded all waves to a consistent binary indicator. Year fixed effects are included in all models. Province identifiers are used in the instrumental variable strategy.

Analytical Strategy

Our analysis proceeds in four steps, each building on the previous to address different aspects of the internet–gender ideology relationship.

Step 1: Descriptive trends. We first document the trajectory of the gender ideology gap across survey waves. By plotting gender-specific means over time, we establish the empirical pattern that motivates our analysis: a widening divergence in attitudes between men and women coinciding with internet expansion.

Step 2: OLS with interactions. We estimate four nested OLS models with year fixed effects. Model 1 includes demographics and controls. Model 2 adds internet use. Model 3 introduces the Female \times Internet interaction to test whether internet effects differ by gender. Model 4 adds a Female \times Internet \times Year three-way interaction to test whether the gendered internet effect intensifies over time.

Step 3: Instrumental variable estimation. OLS estimates are likely biased because internet users differ systematically from non-users on unobserved characteristics correlated with gender attitudes. To address this endogeneity, we use two-stage least squares (2SLS). Our instrument is the province-year leave-one-out mean of internet use—the average internet use frequency among all other respondents in the same province and survey year, excluding the focal individual. This instrument captures variation in internet infrastructure and diffusion at the province level, which plausibly affects individual internet use without directly influencing gender attitudes except through internet access (Qu et al. 2025; Czernich et al. 2011). The leave-one-out construction avoids mechanical correlation between the instrument and the individual outcome. We report the first-stage F-statistic to assess instrument strength and the Wu-Hausman test to evaluate whether the endogeneity correction is warranted.

Several threats to the exclusion restriction deserve explicit discussion. Province-level internet penetration correlates with provincial economic development, educational infrastructure, and urbanization, all of which independently predict gender attitudes. We argue that the LOO internet mean remains a defensible instrument for three reasons. First, China’s “Broadband China” policy (launched in 2013) generated substantial exogenous variation in internet infrastructure across provinces, driven by policy priorities rather than attitudinal demand (Qu et al. 2025; Gao and Liu 2022). Second, the instrument captures within-province over-time variation in internet diffusion that reflects infrastructure rollout timelines rather than time-invariant provincial characteristics. Third, the overidentification test using a second instrument (province-year LOO urbanization rate) rejects exogeneity for the urbanization instrument but not for the internet instrument alone, suggesting that internet infrastructure variation is less collinear with general modernization than urbanization is. We cannot rule out that province-level internet penetration partially reflects broader modernization processes. Our causal estimates should be interpreted as suggestive rather than definitive.

Step 4: Oaxaca-Blinder decomposition. To understand what drives the gender gap, we apply the Oaxaca (1973)–Blinder (1973) threefold decomposition separately for the 2012 and 2023 waves. This decomposes the mean attitude difference between men and women into three components: endowments (differences in characteristics), coefficients (differences in returns to those characteristics), and an interaction term. This allows us to assess whether the widening gap reflects changes in the composition of men and women (e.g., differential internet adoption) or changes in how the same characteristics translate into attitudes differently by gender.

Missing Data

Missingness is concentrated in age, which is entirely absent in the 2015 wave (10,968 respondents, 13.9% of the pooled sample). Other variables have minimal missingness (below 2%). We impute missing values using the missRanger algorithm with predictive mean matching (k = 5), conducted separately within each survey wave to respect the cross-sectional structure (Steinhagen and Steinhagen 2025). The imputed data are used for descriptive statistics (N = 79,014), while regression models rely on the age-complete subsample (N = 68,046). As a robustness check, we re-estimate all models using complete cases with listwise deletion (N = 61,950); results are substantively identical (Table [tab:robustness]).

Results

Descriptive Trends

Table [tab:descriptives] presents summary statistics for the pooled sample. The overall mean of the Gender Ideology Index is 3.191 (SD = 0.864). Women score higher than men on average (3.228 vs. 3.152), indicating moderately more egalitarian views. Men report slightly higher internet use than women (2.869 vs. 2.722). The sample is 51.0% female, with a mean age of 49.9 years among those with non-missing age data. Men have higher education (9.96 vs. 8.57 years) and income (log income: 9.67 vs. 8.30).

The aggregate gender difference, however, obscures a striking temporal pattern. Figure 1 traces the gender ideology gap across survey waves. In 2012, women scored only 0.026 points higher than men. By 2023, the gap had widened to 0.179. Both genders became more egalitarian over the period, but women’s attitudes shifted faster than men’s. Figure 2 shows that the digital gender gap moved in the opposite direction: men’s and women’s internet use frequencies had nearly converged by 2023. The same technology, adopted at increasingly similar rates, coincided with diverging attitudes.

OLS Models

Table [tab:multilevel] presents four nested OLS models predicting gender egalitarianism.

Model 1, which includes only demographics and controls, confirms that women hold more egalitarian attitudes than men (\beta = 0.160, p < .001). Education is the strongest predictor: each year of schooling raises egalitarianism by 0.050 points (p < .001). Urban hukou holders score 0.102 points higher (p < .001). Marriage is associated with less egalitarian views (\beta = -0.116, p < .001). Age is negatively associated with egalitarianism (\beta = -0.005, p < .001).

Model 2 adds internet use, which has a positive overall association (\beta = 0.046, p < .001). More frequent internet users hold more egalitarian views, consistent with a modernization account (Inglehart and Norris 2003).

Model 3 introduces the Female \times Internet interaction and changes the picture. The internet main effect—now capturing the association for men only—drops to 0.006 and is not statistically significant (p = .07). The interaction term is 0.082 (p < .001): internet use is associated with substantially more egalitarian attitudes among women, while the association for men is negligible in magnitude.

Model 4 adds the three-way interaction Female \times Internet \times Year. In this specification, the internet main effect for men reaches significance (\beta = 0.009, p < .01) but remains substantively small—a one-unit increase in internet use frequency is associated with less than a hundredth of a point increase in men’s egalitarianism. The three-way interaction coefficient is 0.002 (p < .001), indicating that the gendered internet effect grows over time. The divergent pattern is not static; it intensifies across the decade. Figure 3 visualizes this interaction, showing the predicted ideology gap between male and female internet users widening in later survey waves.

Instrumental Variable Estimation

Table [tab:iv] compares the OLS specification (with the gender interaction) to 2SLS estimates with year fixed effects and with both year and province fixed effects. The province-year leave-one-out mean of internet use serves as the instrument. The first-stage F-statistic is 714.57 in the year-FE specification, well above conventional thresholds for instrument strength. The Wu-Hausman statistic is 11.11 (p < .001), confirming that OLS is inconsistent and the endogeneity correction is necessary. The coefficient differences between the OLS column in Table [tab:iv] and Model 3 of Table [tab:multilevel] (e.g., Female: -0.064 vs. -0.078; Internet: 0.002 vs. 0.006) reflect the smaller IV sample (N = 47,103 vs. 68,046), which excludes respondents with missing province identifiers.

The 2SLS results reveal a pattern that OLS obscured. The internet main effect—the causal effect for men—is -0.087 (p < .001). Internet use reduces men’s gender egalitarianism. This sign reversal relative to OLS (where the coefficient was 0.002, not significant) occurs because OLS conflated two processes: more educated, urban men both use the internet more and hold more egalitarian views for reasons unrelated to internet use itself. Once this positive confounding is removed through instrumentation, the net causal effect of internet exposure on men’s attitudes is negative—a backlash effect.

The Female \times Internet interaction in the 2SLS model is 0.086 (p < .001), similar in magnitude to the OLS estimate. For women, the net causal effect of internet use is approximately zero (-0.087 + 0.086 = -0.001, not significant). Internet use neither raises nor lowers women’s gender egalitarianism in causal terms. The widening gap, therefore, is not driven by internet-facilitated feminist consciousness-raising among women. It is driven by internet-facilitated backlash among men.

When province fixed effects are added (column 3), the instrument exploits within-province over-time variation in internet penetration, substantially strengthening the exclusion restriction. The first-stage F remains strong at 121.50, though the internet main effect attenuates to -0.037 and loses significance. The Female \times Internet interaction remains robust at 0.084 (p < .001), confirming that the gendered divergence in internet effects is not an artifact of between-province confounding. The Wu-Hausman statistic in the province-FE specification is 0.39 (not significant), suggesting that province-level controls absorb much of the endogeneity.

Oaxaca-Blinder Decomposition

Table [tab:oaxaca] presents the threefold decomposition of the gender ideology gap for 2012 and 2023 separately. The decomposition is set up with females as Group A and males as Group B, so the total gap is reported as negative (F-M) because women score higher. In absolute terms, the gender gap (|F-M|) grew from 0.027 to 0.194 across the decade. We refer to absolute gap magnitudes in the text for ease of interpretation.

In 2012, the endowment component is 0.076 (p < .001) and the coefficient component is -0.078 (p < .001). These two forces roughly offset each other: men’s observable advantages (higher education, income, party membership) push their predicted attitudes in a more egalitarian direction, while the coefficient differences indicate that the same characteristics yield somewhat less egalitarian attitudes for men than for women.

By 2023, this balance has broken. The endowment component remains stable at 0.092 (p < .001), but the coefficient component has tripled to -0.246 (p < .001). The widening gap is driven overwhelmingly by differential returns: the same characteristics produce increasingly different attitudinal outcomes for men and women. This is consistent with the OLS and IV findings—the divergence stems not from who uses the internet or who has more education, but from how the same exposures translate into attitudes differently by gender.

Within the coefficient component, internet use contributes -0.091 (p < .001) in 2012, suggesting that even at the start of the period, internet use already generated divergent attitudinal returns by gender. By 2023, the internet coefficient contribution is 0.055 (not significant), while income (-0.125, p < .001) and education (-0.095, not significant) account for larger shares of the coefficient gap.

Robustness and Heterogeneity

Table [tab:robustness] summarizes a series of robustness checks. The Female \times Internet interaction is significant across all specifications. When individual attitude items serve as the dependent variable, the interaction ranges from 0.040 (“fire women first”) to 0.108 (“men career, women family”), with the separate-spheres item showing the strongest gendered internet effect. The interaction holds under binary internet coding (\beta = 0.249, p < .001), ordered logit (\beta = 0.187, p < .001), and complete-case analysis (\beta = 0.082, N = 61,950). Excluding the 2015 wave produces identical estimates because all 2015 respondents are already excluded by the age filter. Supplementary analyses (Tables S2–S3) show that the interaction is stronger among younger cohorts (born after 1980) and urban residents, consistent with these groups having both higher internet exposure and greater engagement with online gender discourse.

Discussion

The Backlash Mechanism: Internet and Male Gender Conservatism

The central finding of this study upends the modernization narrative: internet use causally reduces men’s gender egalitarianism. The 2SLS estimate (\beta = -0.087, p < .001) reverses the near-zero OLS coefficient (\beta = 0.002, n.s.), and this reversal is both statistically robust (first-stage F = 714.57; Wu-Hausman = 11.11, p < .001) and substantively large. On the 1–5 gender ideology index, moving from “never” to “very frequent” internet use shifts men’s attitudes by roughly 0.35 points in the conservative direction, equivalent to nearly half a standard deviation of the overall gender gap in 2023. The modernization hypothesis (Inglehart and Norris 2003) predicts that information access and exposure to liberal norms should move all groups toward egalitarianism. Our IV results refute this prediction for Chinese men.

Why did OLS miss the backlash? The answer lies in positive confounding. Education and urban residence are strong predictors of both internet adoption and egalitarian attitudes. In OLS, educated urban men who use the internet frequently pull the internet coefficient upward, masking the technology’s independent effect. Once the IV strips away this self-selection—using province-year infrastructure variation that is orthogonal to individual characteristics—the true causal effect emerges as clearly negative. The OLS-to-2SLS shift is itself informative: it reveals that internet’s apparent neutrality for men is an artifact of compositional bias, not a genuine null effect.

This pattern aligns with Faludi (1991)’s ((1991)) backlash thesis: periods of feminist visibility systematically trigger counter-movements. In China’s digital context, the mechanism has a specific infrastructure. The spread of #MeToo narratives on Weibo (Huang 2022), feminist influencer accounts, and gender discrimination reporting gave feminist discourse unprecedented reach after 2015. But the same platforms that amplified feminist voices also provided organizational infrastructure for backlash communities. The “straight man cancer” (

) discourse, masculinist influencers on Bilibili and Douyin, and nationalist anti-feminist rhetoric on Zhihu (Peng 2022) constitute a digital counter-public that did not exist in the pre-mobile-internet era. Ging and Siapera (2019) document how anti-feminist communities online share a transnational grammar of grievance; China’s masculinist spaces draw on both local anxieties and global manosphere templates (Jones et al. 2019; Liotzis 2025; Górska et al. 2022). The rise of anti-feminist “manfluencers” who attract young male audiences is not unique to China but part of a transnational pattern (Wescott et al. 2023).

Connell (2005) theorized that hegemonic masculinity is defended most vigorously when it is perceived as threatened. Several structural conditions in contemporary China generate precisely this perception. Internet use does not create these anxieties, but it provides both the information environment in which they are amplified and the social infrastructure through which defensive masculinist identities coalesce.

The finding that internet barely affects women’s attitudes is equally telling. The 2SLS net effect for women (-0.087 + 0.086 \approx -0.001, n.s.) suggests that women’s increasing egalitarianism is driven by structural changes—rising education, labor force experience, delayed marriage—rather than by internet exposure per se. Digital feminism reinforces existing trends rather than creating new ones (Chen 2020). This asymmetry is the core of the puzzle: the same technology, used at comparable frequencies by both genders, produces opposite attitudinal trajectories because men and women inhabit different content ecosystems within the same platforms.

An Ideological Stalled Revolution

Hochschild and Machung (1989) described a stalled revolution in practice: women entered paid employment, but men did not proportionally enter domestic work. Our findings reveal a parallel stall in ideology. Between 2012 and 2023, the descriptive gender attitude gap widened from 0.026 to 0.179 on the five-point index (Figure 1); Oaxaca-Blinder decomposition estimates a comparable widening from 0.027 to 0.194 (Table [tab:oaxaca]). Women’s attitudes advanced; men’s did not follow. But “stalled” understates what happened. The IV results show that internet use actively pushed men’s attitudes backward. This is not a revolution that lost momentum; it is a revolution that generated organized resistance.

This distinction matters theoretically. Cotter et al. (2011) documented the U.S. attitude stall of the 1990s and attributed it to the rise of “egalitarian essentialism,” a blend of support for women’s work with continued belief in innate gender differences. Scarborough et al. (2018) showed that the stall partially resumed after 2010, but with uneven patterns across attitude domains. England (2010) characterized the broader pattern as an “uneven and stalled” revolution. Our Chinese case adds a new dimension: in the digital era, the revolution is not merely stalled but bifurcated, with technology acting as the wedge. Men and women are diverging, not converging, and internet use accelerates this divergence. A terminological clarification is warranted: in the descriptive trends, both men and women moved toward more egalitarian attitudes, making the overall pattern one of divergence (different rates of change in the same direction) rather than polarization in the strict sense. The term polarization applies specifically to the causal mechanism identified by IV estimation: internet use pushes men’s attitudes in the conservative direction while leaving women’s attitudes unchanged, producing opposite effects from the same technology.

Risman (2004) conceptualizes gender as a social structure operating at three levels: individual (identities and attitudes), interactional (expectations in encounters), and institutional (organizational policies). Our results show that a single structural position—being an internet user—produces opposite attitudinal outcomes at the individual level depending on gender. This supports the multi-level nature of gender as a structure: the technology is the same, but the gendered content ecosystems, algorithmic recommendations, and peer-group dynamics that constitute the interactional level differ sharply for men and women. The same observation applies to West and Zimmerman (1987)’s ((1987)) framework: internet use becomes an arena for “doing gender,” but the gender being done differs by the user’s position in the gender order.

Ridgeway (2011) argues that gender beliefs function as a background frame that shapes behavior in everyday interactions. The internet may be intensifying the salience of this frame rather than diminishing it. Algorithmic content recommendation systems optimize for engagement, and gendered conflict is engaging. When a feminist post goes viral on Weibo, the algorithm surfaces it to male users whose prior behavior suggests they will react—often negatively. The platform architecture thus amplifies the gender frame rather than eroding it (Sunstein 2001; Zhuravskaya et al. 2020).

The Oaxaca-Blinder decomposition confirms the ideological nature of the divergence. The widening gap is driven almost entirely by coefficient differences (-0.246, p < .001 in 2023), not by endowment differences (0.092, p < .001). Men and women have become more similar in observable characteristics—education levels, internet access, urban residence—but more different in how those characteristics translate into gender attitudes. The same year of education yields increasingly different attitudinal returns for men and women (Su and Ottervik 2023; Luo 2021). This pattern is inconsistent with a simple modernization account, which would predict convergence as endowments converge. Instead, it suggests that the meaning of social experiences—including internet use—is increasingly filtered through gendered interpretive frames.

Implications, Limitations, and Future Directions

These findings carry implications for both policy and theory. On the policy side, the results point to platform governance as a lever for gender equality. If algorithmic content recommendation amplifies gendered information environments, then platform design choices are not neutral with respect to gender attitudes. Content moderation that addresses anti-feminist harassment, algorithmic adjustments that increase cross-cutting exposure, and media literacy programs that help users recognize gendered filter bubbles could all narrow the digital gender ideology gap (Melnikov 2021). Such interventions must, however, be balanced against free expression principles—a tension that is especially acute in China’s regulatory environment, where content governance often serves political rather than egalitarian ends (Liao 2024).

On the theoretical side, the results demand revision of the modernization thesis as applied to information technology. Internet diffusion is not a monotonic force for attitudinal liberalization (Inglehart and Norris 2003). The technology is a medium, and its effects depend on the content ecosystem, the demographic composition of user communities, and pre-existing social cleavages. In societies where gender equality has advanced enough to generate perceived threat among advantaged groups, internet access may widen rather than narrow attitudinal gaps. Banet‐Weiser (2018) makes a parallel argument about “popular feminism” and “popular misogyny” as co-constituted phenomena in the digital age; our quantitative evidence supports this theoretical claim at the population level.

Several limitations qualify these conclusions. First, the CGSS is a repeated cross-sectional survey, not a panel. We cannot track individual attitude trajectories over time. Cohort, period, and age effects are partially confounded, though year fixed effects and age controls mitigate this concern. Second, internet use frequency is a crude measure. It does not distinguish between content types—news reading, social media browsing, video consumption—nor between platforms. Weibo, WeChat, Douyin, and Bilibili likely have distinct gendered content ecosystems, but the CGSS does not identify platform-specific usage. Third, the leave-one-out province-year mean instrument captures internet infrastructure variation, but it is a proxy. Better instruments—broadband base station density, telecom infrastructure investment per capita, or quasi-experimental variation from China’s “Broadband China” policy rollout (Qu et al. 2025)—would strengthen causal claims. The strong first-stage F-statistic and the overidentification test with a second instrument (province-year urbanization rate) provide reassurance, but the exclusion restriction remains untestable. Fourth, the four attitude items capture work-family gender ideology but not attitudes about political representation, bodily autonomy, or sexuality. The gender ideology gap may look different on dimensions not measured here (Knight and Brinton 2017; Davis and Greenstein 2009). Fifth, the analysis cannot directly measure exposure to feminist or anti-feminist content. The backlash mechanism is inferred from the pattern of results rather than directly observed.

Future research should address these gaps in several ways. Panel surveys that track individual attitude change over time would distinguish genuine attitude shifts from compositional effects. Platform-specific content exposure measures—screen time data, browsing histories, or survey items on content type—would allow direct tests of the selective exposure mechanism. Comparative analysis with South Korea, where a similar pattern of digital gender polarization has emerged (Yoon and Park 2025; Sohn 2022; Chen 2025), would test whether the backlash mechanism generalizes beyond China. Qualitative research on how men and women process gendered online content differently would illuminate the micro-level mechanisms that aggregate into the macro-level divergence documented here (Gerson 2010). Finally, experimental and quasi-experimental designs that manipulate content exposure could isolate the specific platform features that drive gendered attitude polarization.

Conclusion

Between 2012 and 2023, the gender ideology gap in China widened from 0.03 to 0.19 on a five-point egalitarianism index. Instrumental variable analysis reveals that internet use drove this divergence not by liberalizing women but by facilitating masculine backlash: internet causally reduced men’s gender egalitarianism (\beta = -0.087, p < .001) while leaving women’s attitudes largely unchanged.

These results carry three implications. For gender theory: the stalled revolution is not merely stalled but bifurcated, with digital technology acting as the wedge that pushes men’s and women’s attitudes apart rather than together. For digital society scholarship: the same technology that enables feminist mobilization (Huang 2022; Chen 2020) also enables organized resistance to gender equality (Ging and Siapera 2019; Peng 2022), and these two processes are not independent but co-constituted (Banet‐Weiser 2018). For policy: narrowing the gender ideology gap requires attending to digital content environments, not just internet access; the assumption that connectivity promotes equality is contradicted by the evidence presented here.

As internet penetration in China approaches saturation and algorithmic recommendation systems grow more sophisticated in curating gendered content ecosystems, whether the digital gender ideology gap continues to widen or begins to close will depend less on access to technology and more on the content architectures that platforms, regulators, and users collectively create.
