Selection on Trends or Social Causation? Living Arrangements and Health Among Older Adults Using Fixed Effects with Individual Slopes

Sample Selection

Figure 1 presents the sample selection process from the full RAND HRS to the analytic samples used in the main analyses. Starting from 42,405 unique respondents in the RAND HRS Longitudinal File (1992–2020, Version 2), we applied sequential inclusion criteria. Merging with the Gateway Harmonized HRS to obtain living arrangement variables retained all respondents. Restricting to Waves 5 through 15 (2000–2020) and requiring age 50 or older at each observation reduced the sample to 36,167 respondents contributing 213,504 person-wave observations. Requiring a minimum of three non-missing living arrangement observations per person, necessary for FE-IS estimation of individual-specific slopes, yielded the primary analytic sample of 28,770 persons and 191,822 person-waves. Because the CES-D was not administered to proxy respondents, the depression analysis used a reduced sample of 27,084 persons (177,772 person-waves).

Sample Attrition Analysis

Panel attrition is a concern in longitudinal studies of older adults, as respondents who drop out of the HRS tend to be older, sicker, and more functionally impaired than those who remain. To assess whether attrition biases our estimates, we compared baseline characteristics of respondents who completed six or more waves (“completers”) with those who completed fewer than six waves (“attriters”). Table [tab:s1] presents these comparisons. Attriters were older at baseline, had higher CES-D scores, worse self-rated health, and more ADL limitations. They were also more likely to live alone or in institutional care. These patterns are consistent with health-related attrition, which our FE-IS specification partially addresses by modeling individual health trajectories. We also report inverse probability weighted estimates in Section 5 as a direct correction for attrition bias.

Fixed Effects vs. Fixed Effects with Individual Slopes

The main text employs two panel data estimators: standard fixed effects (FE) and fixed effects with individual slopes (FE-IS). This section explains the distinction between these approaches and why it matters for estimating the health effects of living arrangements.

The standard FE model eliminates all time-invariant individual heterogeneity by demeaning the data within individuals. This removes confounding from stable characteristics such as personality, genetic health endowment, childhood socioeconomic status, and cultural values. The identifying assumption is that, after removing individual means, changes in living arrangements are uncorrelated with changes in the error term. This assumption is violated when individuals differ not only in their baseline health levels but in their rates of health change. An older adult on a steeper health decline trajectory is simultaneously more likely to transition into coresidence with adult children (as declining health prompts family support) and more likely to report worse health outcomes at any given time point. Standard FE cannot separate the effect of the living arrangement transition from the pre-existing trajectory that prompted it (Rüttenauer and Ludwig 2023).

The FE-IS estimator, developed by Wodtke (2018), extends standard FE by including an individual-specific linear slope on age. This additional term absorbs each person’s idiosyncratic health trajectory, so that the treatment effect is identified from deviations from each person’s own trend rather than from deviations from their mean. The key advantage is that FE-IS controls for trajectory-driven selection: if health decline causes both living arrangement changes and worse outcomes, FE-IS removes this confounding pathway.

The practical consequence is visible in our results. Table [tab:s2] summarizes the differences between the two estimators across nine dimensions. The most striking empirical contrast appears for self-rated health: the standard FE model estimates that spousal coresidence improves SRH by +0.121 (p < .001), but the FE-IS model reverses this to -0.031 (p < .05). This sign flip indicates that the apparent SRH benefit of spousal coresidence in FE models is driven by healthier individuals selecting into (or remaining in) spousal households, not by a causal protective effect of the arrangement itself. The depression results, by contrast, survive the FE-IS correction, supporting a genuine social causation interpretation.

FE-IS requires a minimum of three observations per person (one more than standard FE) to estimate the individual slope. The model assumes that individual health trajectories are linear in age; non-linear health shocks (e.g., stroke, hip fracture) are not fully absorbed. We use age centered at 65 as the slope variable, consistent with the gerontological focus on aging-related health change.

Note. Y_{it} = health outcome for individual i at time t; \alpha_i = individual fixed effect; \beta_i = individual-specific slope on age; X_{it} = time-varying covariates including living arrangement; \gamma = vector of coefficients; \varepsilon_{it} = idiosyncratic error. FE-IS was developed by Wodtke (2018) and implemented in R by Rüttenauer and Ludwig (2023).

Heterogeneity in Living Arrangement–Health Associations

The main text reports that the depression-reducing effect of coresidence varies significantly by gender, race/ethnicity, and age group, with all interaction tests yielding p < .001. This section presents the full stratified FE-IS estimates underlying those findings.

Depressive Symptoms (CES-D)

Table [tab:s3] reports FE-IS estimates for CES-D depressive symptoms stratified by gender, race/ethnicity, and age group. Several patterns stand out. Men benefited from coresidence three to four times more than women: the spousal coresidence coefficient was -0.548 for men versus -0.403 for women, and the children-only coefficient was -0.313 for men versus -0.085 for women. Among racial and ethnic groups, non-Hispanic White older adults showed the largest spousal coresidence effect (-0.494), while non-Hispanic Black (-0.237) and Hispanic (-0.373) older adults showed more moderate effects. The protective effect of coresidence intensified with age: the spousal coresidence coefficient increased from -0.354 among adults aged 50–64 to -0.576 among those aged 80 and older, consistent with greater vulnerability to social isolation at advanced ages.

Note. All models are FE-IS with full controls and individual-specific slopes on age (centered at 65). Reference category: living alone. Interaction p-values from Wald tests in standard FE models with living_arr \times subgroup interaction terms. Robust standard errors in parentheses.

p < .05, ** p < .01, *** p < .001.

Self-Rated Health and ADL Limitations

Table [tab:s4] extends the heterogeneity analysis to self-rated health and ADL limitations. Gender interactions were not statistically significant for either outcome (p = .528 for SRH, p = .248 for ADL), suggesting that the gendered pattern observed for depression does not extend to physical health domains. Race/ethnicity interactions were significant for both SRH (p = .018) and ADL (p < .001), while age group interactions were significant for SRH (p < .001) and strongly significant for ADL (p < .001). The ADL results for institutionalization showed a pronounced age gradient: the coefficient increased from 1.253 among adults aged 50–64 to 1.859 among those aged 80 and older, reflecting the accelerating functional decline that accompanies institutional care at advanced ages.

Robustness Analyses

We conducted five sets of robustness checks to assess the sensitivity of our main FE-IS findings. All robustness models use standard fixed effects with clustered standard errors, providing a consistent comparison framework across specifications. The core pattern—coresidence reduces depression but not self-rated health or functional limitations after accounting for individual trajectories—was robust across all specifications.

Binary Living Arrangement Coding

To assess whether our results depend on the six-category classification, we collapsed living arrangements into a binary indicator of coresidence with adult children (categories 3 and 4) versus all other arrangements. Table [tab:s5] shows that coresiding with children was associated with lower CES-D scores (p < .05), consistent with the main results. The binary specification necessarily obscures the heterogeneity across coresidence types documented in Table 3, but confirms that the direction of the coresidence-depression association holds under a simpler operationalization.

Note. FE-IS models with full controls and individual-specific slopes on age (centered at 65). Reference category: living alone. Robust standard errors in parentheses. * p < .05, ** p < .01, *** p < .001.

Lagged Living Arrangement

To test whether results are sensitive to the timing of exposure measurement, we replaced contemporaneous living arrangement with the arrangement reported at the prior wave (a two-year lag). Table [tab:s6] presents these estimates. The lagged specification produced coefficients similar in direction and magnitude to the main results, though some estimates were attenuated. Spousal coresidence at t-1 remained significantly associated with lower CES-D scores at t, supporting the interpretation that the depression-reducing effect of coresidence operates over sustained exposure rather than reflecting only contemporaneous conditions.

Note. FE-IS models with full controls and individual-specific slopes on age (centered at 65). Reference category: living alone. Robust standard errors in parentheses. * p < .05, ** p < .01, *** p < .001.

Excluding the COVID-19 Wave (2020)

Wave 15 of the HRS (fielded in 2020) coincided with the COVID-19 pandemic, which disrupted both living arrangements and health for older Americans. Interview protocols shifted from in-person to telephone and web modes. To assess whether the pandemic wave drives our results, we re-estimated the main models excluding all Wave 15 observations. Table [tab:s7] shows that all main findings were substantively unchanged: coresidence reduced depression, the SRH and ADL coefficients remained small or null, and the institutionalization-ADL association remained large and significant. The pandemic wave does not account for the patterns reported in the main text.

Note. FE-IS models with full controls and individual-specific slopes on age (centered at 65). Reference category: living alone. Robust standard errors in parentheses. * p < .05, ** p < .01, *** p < .001.

Complete Cases vs. Multiple Imputation

Our main analyses used data imputed via the missRanger algorithm with predictive mean matching (k = 5). To test whether the imputation procedure affects the results, we compared FE estimates from the imputed sample with those from the complete-case sample (listwise deletion of observations with any missing covariate). Table [tab:s8] shows that the two approaches produced similar coefficients for all three outcomes. The complete-case sample was smaller (26,281 vs. 28,770 persons for SRH/ADL) but yielded estimates within the confidence intervals of the imputed-data estimates. The imputation did not introduce bias into the main findings.

Note. FE-IS models with full controls and individual-specific slopes on age (centered at 65). Reference category: living alone. Robust standard errors in parentheses. * p < .05, ** p < .01, *** p < .001.

Inverse Probability Weighting for Attrition

To directly address the attrition patterns documented in Section 2, we constructed inverse probability weights based on the predicted probability of remaining in the sample at each wave, conditional on prior-wave characteristics (age, gender, race/ethnicity, living arrangement, CES-D, SRH, ADL, chronic conditions, and hospitalization). We then re-estimated the main FE models with these weights applied. Table [tab:s9] shows that the IPW-adjusted estimates were similar to the unweighted estimates for all three outcomes, indicating that attrition-related selection does not substantially bias the main findings.
