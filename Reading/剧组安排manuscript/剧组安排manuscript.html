Living Arrangements and Health Among Older US Adults: Evidence from Fixed Effects with Individual Slopes Models

[Blinded for Review]

Abstract

Background and Objectives: Living arrangements are associated with health among older adults, but observed associations may reflect health selection — the process by which declining health drives transitions into co-residence or institutional care — rather than causal effects of household composition.

Research Design and Methods: We applied Fixed Effects with Individual Slopes (FE-IS) models to 11 waves of the Health and Retirement Study (2000–2020; N = 27,024 individuals; 177,588 person-wave observations). FE-IS removes individual-specific age-health trajectories that confound standard fixed effects (FE) estimates. Outcomes included ADL limitations (0–6), IADL limitations (0–5), CES-D depressive symptoms (0–8), and chronic conditions (0–8). Living arrangements were classified as living alone (reference), spouse only, children/others, spouse plus others, and institutionalized. Heterogeneity analyses stratified by gender, age group, and race/ethnicity.

Results: Under standard FE, non-institutional co-residence was associated with worse physical health but fewer depressive symptoms relative to living alone. FE-IS eliminated all physical health associations — ADL, IADL, and chronic condition effects became statistically non-significant — exposing them as health selection artifacts. Depression effects persisted: spousal co-residence reduced CES-D by 0.140 points (p < .001), with minimal attenuation from the FE estimate. Institutionalization effects were attenuated 58–65% for physical health and disappeared entirely for depression. Men benefited from all co-residence forms for depression (spouse: b = -0.175; children: b = -0.167; spouse plus others: b = -0.187; all p < .01), while women benefited only from spousal co-residence (b = -0.141, p < .001). The spousal protection effect weakened with age, from b = -0.208 (p < .001) at ages 50–64 to non-significance at 75+. All findings were robust to inverse probability weighting, complete-case analysis, alternative living arrangement coding, and slope specification changes.

Discussion and Implications: Co-residence genuinely reduces depression through social-psychological mechanisms, but associations with physical health are driven by health selection. Interventions targeting depression among older adults should prioritize men living alone and younger-old adults transitioning out of spousal households.

Introduction

Does living with a spouse protect against depression in old age, or do healthier people simply remain in spousal households longer? This question captures a challenge that pervades the literature on living arrangements and health: the difficulty of separating genuine causal effects from health selection, the process by which declining health drives changes in household composition rather than the reverse.

The question matters for both science and policy. In the United States, more than 14 million adults aged 65 and older live alone, a figure projected to rise as the population ages and divorce rates remain elevated (Lamidi 2022). Public health agencies and aging-services organizations increasingly view household-level interventions — shared housing programs, co-residence support, alternatives to institutional placement — as tools for promoting healthy aging. Yet the evidence base for these interventions rests on observational associations that may reflect the health conditions preceding a move rather than the consequences of the living arrangement itself (Hughes and Waite 2002; Zimmer 2005).

Two decades of longitudinal research have established that living arrangements are associated with health outcomes among older adults, including functional limitations, depressive symptoms, and chronic disease burden (Hughes and Waite 2002; Lee and Lim 2023). Standard fixed effects (FE) models have been the primary tool for addressing confounding, removing stable individual characteristics that jointly predict both living arrangements and health. Yet standard FE cannot remove dynamic health selection: the process by which individuals whose health is declining at a faster rate are precisely those who transition into co-residential or institutional care. This form of confounding biases FE estimates in a predictable direction, inflating apparent effects of living arrangements on physical health.

We address this gap by applying Fixed Effects with Individual Slopes (FE-IS) models (Brüderl and Ludwig 2015; Rüttenauer and Ludwig 2019) to 20 years of nationally representative panel data from the Health and Retirement Study (HRS; 2000–2020). FE-IS extends standard FE by allowing each individual to follow their own linear and quadratic age-health trajectory, absorbing the person-specific health slopes that standard FE leaves in the error term. By comparing FE and FE-IS estimates, we directly quantify the magnitude of health selection bias for each living arrangement–health association.

Our results reveal a sharp divergence across health domains. For physical health outcomes — ADL limitations, IADL limitations, and chronic conditions — all associations between non-institutional co-residence and health that appear significant under standard FE become statistically indistinguishable from zero under FE-IS. These associations are entirely health selection artifacts. For depression, by contrast, co-residence effects persist after removing individual trajectories: spousal co-residence reduces CES-D scores by 0.140 points (p < .001), an effect that is barely attenuated from the standard FE estimate of 0.148. The apparent harmful effect of institutionalization on depression disappears completely under FE-IS, exposed as pure health selection. These effects vary by gender — men benefit from all forms of co-residence, while women benefit only from spousal co-residence — and by age, with the protective effect weakening after age 75.

This study makes three contributions. First, we provide the most rigorous causal test to date of living arrangement effects on health among older adults, demonstrating that FE-IS substantially revises conclusions drawn from standard FE. Second, we resolve an apparent paradox in the literature: the finding that co-residence is simultaneously associated with worse physical health and better mental health. Under FE-IS, the physical health associations vanish, revealing a domain-specific pattern in which co-residence genuinely affects depression through social-psychological mechanisms while leaving physical health trajectories unchanged. Third, we document gendered and age-graded heterogeneity in co-residence effects, showing that the protective function of household composition for mental health is concentrated among men and younger-old adults — a finding with direct implications for targeting prevention efforts.

Background and Theoretical Framework

Living Arrangements and Health in Later Life

The household is a primary site of social interaction and resource exchange for older adults. Who one lives with shapes daily routines, access to assistance, emotional companionship, and exposure to health-promoting or health-damaging behaviors. In the United States, the proportion of adults aged 65 and older living alone has grown steadily over recent decades, driven by increases in life expectancy, divorce, and the preference for independent living (Lamidi 2022; Waite and Karraker 2022). At the same time, multigenerational households, spousal co-residence, and institutional care remain common configurations, each embedding older adults in distinct social environments with different implications for well-being.

This study distinguishes five living arrangements — living alone, living with a spouse only, living with children or other non-spouse members, living with a spouse and others, and residing in an institution — that represent qualitatively different configurations of social resources (Hughes and Waite 2002; Waite and Karraker 2022). Prior research has documented associations between household composition and a range of health outcomes, including functional limitations, chronic disease burden, and depressive symptoms (Hughes and Waite 2002; Cabrero-Castro et al. 2025; Lee and Lim 2023). Living alone has been linked to elevated depression risk through social isolation (Lee and Kim 2024; Pomeroy et al. 2023), while co-residence with a spouse has been associated with better physical and mental health across multiple populations (Takagi et al. 2024; Liu 2024). Institutional residence is consistently associated with the worst health profiles, though this pattern likely reflects the health conditions that precipitate admission rather than harmful effects of institutional environments themselves (Gaugler et al. 2007).

A persistent challenge in this literature is that observed associations between living arrangements and health may reflect health selection rather than causal effects of household composition. Older adults whose health is declining are more likely to move in with family members or enter institutional care (Zimmer 2005; Verspeek et al. 2024). Disentangling the direction of causality requires methods that go beyond standard longitudinal designs.

Social Convoy Model and Stress-Buffering

The social convoy model, introduced by Kahn and Antonucci (1980) and elaborated in subsequent work (Antonucci et al. 2019; Sherman et al. 2015), provides a theoretical framework for understanding how household composition affects health. The model conceptualizes each individual as moving through life surrounded by a dynamic, layered network of social ties. The innermost ring of this convoy consists of the most intimate relationships — typically a spouse, close family members, or long-term companions — who provide the most sustained forms of support. Household co-residents occupy this innermost position and are uniquely situated to deliver daily assistance with functional tasks, monitor health status, and offer emotional companionship (Fuller et al. 2020).

The convoy framework distinguishes two functions of social support that are relevant to the living arrangement–health link. First, instrumental support encompasses tangible aid: help with activities of daily living, medication management, meal preparation, and transportation to medical appointments. Second, emotional support encompasses companionship, affection, shared daily routines, and a sense of belonging that regulates negative affect (Sherman et al. 2015; Fuller et al. 2020). These two functions may operate through distinct causal pathways. Instrumental support addresses the practical consequences of health decline but does not necessarily alter the underlying physiological trajectory; a co-resident who assists with bathing does not slow joint degeneration. Emotional support, by contrast, operates through psychosocial mechanisms — reducing loneliness, providing a sense of purpose, buffering against depressive responses to stressful events — that directly influence mental health (Antonucci et al. 2019).

The stress-buffering hypothesis, articulated by Cohen and Wills (1985), offers a complementary mechanism. This framework distinguishes a main-effects model, in which social support improves well-being regardless of stress exposure, from a buffering model, in which support specifically protects individuals from the adverse consequences of stressful events. Cohen and Wills (1985) concluded that functional social support — the availability of instrumental aid and emotional reassurance — is the active ingredient in buffering, not the mere structural presence of social ties. Applied to living arrangements, the buffering model predicts that co-residents are most health-protective when stressful events occur: a health shock, a bereavement, or the accumulation of age-related losses. House et al. (1988) established that social isolation constitutes a major risk factor for mortality across prospective studies, comparable in magnitude to smoking, providing foundational evidence that the quantity and quality of social relationships causally affect health. Together, these theoretical perspectives predict that co-residence should protect against depression through emotional support and stress-buffering, while the relationship between co-residence and physical health may be more ambiguous — confounded by the very health decline that triggers changes in household composition.

Health Selection and the Endogeneity Problem

The health selection hypothesis posits that health decline causes living arrangement transitions rather than the reverse. Older adults experiencing functional deterioration are more likely to move in with adult children or a spouse; those with severe disability or cognitive impairment are more likely to enter institutional care (Zimmer 2005; Gaugler et al. 2007). Gaugler et al. (2007), in a meta-analysis of predictors of nursing home admission, identified three or more ADL dependencies, cognitive impairment, and prior nursing home use as the strongest predictors of institutional placement. Zimmer (2005), analyzing longitudinal data on Chinese oldest-old, documented that families respond altruistically to health deterioration by increasing co-residence — confirming that selection into living arrangements is health-driven. Verspeek et al. (2024) showed that even expectations of future nursing home relocation are longitudinally linked with functional decline, suggesting both anticipatory and reactive pathways connecting health trajectories and living arrangement transitions.

Standard fixed effects (FE) panel models address one source of confounding by removing all time-invariant individual characteristics — genetic endowment, childhood conditions, stable personality traits — that might jointly predict both living arrangements and health. Yet standard FE relies on a parallel-trends assumption: conditional on covariates, all individuals are assumed to follow the same baseline health trajectory regardless of whether they change living arrangements (Bell and Jones 2014). This assumption is violated when health selection is dynamic — that is, when individuals whose health is deteriorating at a faster rate are precisely those who transition into co-residential or institutional care.

The Fixed Effects with Individual Slopes (FE-IS) estimator, developed by Brüderl and Ludwig (2015) and formalized by Rüttenauer and Ludwig (2019), addresses this limitation. FE-IS augments standard FE by allowing each individual to have their own linear and quadratic age trajectory, thereby absorbing person-specific health slopes that may correlate with living arrangement transitions. Rüttenauer and Ludwig (2019) demonstrated through both analytical derivation and Monte Carlo simulation that standard FE returns biased estimates when selection into treatment is based on individual growth of the outcome — the exact scenario that characterizes health-driven living arrangement changes. The difference between FE and FE-IS estimates quantifies the magnitude of health selection bias: if associations between living arrangements and physical health are driven by health selection, FE-IS coefficients should be substantially attenuated relative to standard FE; if associations reflect genuine causal effects, FE-IS coefficients should remain stable.

Heterogeneity: Gender, Age, and Race/Ethnicity

The protective function of co-residence is unlikely to operate uniformly across the older adult population. Three dimensions of heterogeneity are theoretically motivated.

Gender. Men and women differ in the structure and function of their social convoys (Manalel et al. 2022). Men tend to rely more heavily on household members — and on spouses in particular — as their primary source of emotional support, whereas women cultivate broader, more diversified networks that extend beyond the household (Berkman and Syme 1979; Antonucci et al. 2019). Takagi et al. (2024) documented gender differences in the association between living arrangement type and well-being, with spousal and non-spousal co-residence producing different effects for men and women. If men depend more on household-based emotional support, then co-residence should yield larger depression reductions for men than for women.

Age. The convoy model predicts that convoy composition shifts across the life course as individuals experience widowhood, the departure of adult children, and the contraction of friendship networks (Sherman et al. 2015; Antonucci et al. 2019). At younger-old ages (50–64), household members represent a larger share of the total support network; at advanced ages (75+), the cumulative burden of biological decline and social losses may overwhelm the protective capacity of any single relationship. The protective effect of co-residence on depression may therefore weaken with age.

Race and ethnicity. Living arrangement patterns and their health implications vary across racial and ethnic groups. Liu (2024) showed that for White older adults, living alone without nearby family carries the strongest depression penalty, while for Black and Hispanic older adults, extended family and community networks partially compensate for the absence of household co-residents. These patterns suggest that the mental health returns to co-residence may operate across racial lines, even as the availability of substitute support networks differs by group.

Hypotheses

Drawing on the theoretical framework above, we derive four testable hypotheses:

H1 (Depression protection):

Co-residential living arrangements reduce depressive symptoms relative to living alone, net of individual-specific health trajectories (FE-IS specification). The social convoy model and stress-buffering theory predict that emotional support from household co-residents operates through psychosocial mechanisms that directly reduce depression.

H2 (Physical health selection):

Observed associations between co-residential living arrangements and physical health outcomes (ADL limitations, IADL limitations, chronic conditions) in standard FE models are substantially attenuated — toward zero — in FE-IS models. This pattern would indicate that the apparent physical health “effects” of living arrangements are driven by health selection rather than by a genuine causal pathway.

H3 (Gender moderation):

The protective effect of co-residence on depression is stronger for men than for women. Men’s greater reliance on household-based emotional support predicts that they benefit more from co-residence of all forms, while women’s broader extra-household networks attenuate their dependence on household composition for mental health.

H4 (Age attenuation):

The protective effect of spousal co-residence on depression weakens with age. As individuals age and the convoy contracts, the marginal contribution of household-level support diminishes relative to the cumulative burden of health decline and social loss.

Each hypothesis maps directly to a specific statistical test: H1 and H2 are tested by comparing FE and FE-IS main-effect estimates across depression and physical health outcomes; H3 is tested by gender-stratified FE-IS models; and H4 is tested by age-group-stratified FE-IS models.

Data and Methods

Data

We use data from the Health and Retirement Study (HRS), a biennial, nationally representative longitudinal survey of US adults aged 50 and older (Sonnega et al. 2014). The HRS has followed respondents since 1992, collecting detailed information on health, economic resources, household composition, and demographic characteristics. We draw on waves 5 through 15, covering the period from 2000 to 2020 (11 waves). The analytic sample includes 27,024 individuals contributing 177,588 person-wave observations. We restrict the sample to respondents aged 50 or older at each observation, exclude proxy interviews to ensure consistent self-reported health measurement, and require at least three valid waves per person to support estimation of individual-specific age trajectories.

Measures

Outcome Variables

We examine four health outcomes that capture distinct dimensions of physical and mental well-being.

Activities of daily living (ADL) difficulties is a count (0–6) of self-reported difficulty with bathing, dressing, eating, toileting, transferring in and out of bed, and continence. Instrumental activities of daily living (IADL) difficulties is a count (0–5) of difficulty using the telephone, managing money, taking medications, shopping for groceries, and preparing meals. Depressive symptoms is measured by the 8-item modified Center for Epidemiologic Studies Depression (CES-D) scale (range 0–8), which asks respondents whether they experienced each of eight depressive symptoms much of the time during the past week. Chronic conditions is a count (0–8) of doctor-diagnosed conditions: hypertension, diabetes, cancer, lung disease, heart disease, stroke, psychiatric problems, and arthritis.

Key Predictor: Living Arrangement

The key independent variable is a five-category measure of living arrangement: (1) living alone (reference category), (2) living with spouse only, (3) living with children or other non-spouse household members, (4) living with spouse plus others, and (5) institutionalized (nursing home or long-term care facility). These categories are constructed from HRS household composition and nursing home status variables. This coding scheme distinguishes the distinct social environments associated with each arrangement — spousal companionship, intergenerational co-residence, multigenerational households, and institutional care — which may affect health through different mechanisms (Hughes and Waite 2002; Waite and Karraker 2022).

Covariates

All models include the following time-varying covariates: age (centered at 65 years) and age squared to capture nonlinear aging effects; marital status; log-transformed household wealth (bottom-coded at $1 to handle zero and negative values); government health insurance coverage; household size; current smoking status; physical activity (any regular exercise); and body mass index (BMI, winsorized at 10 and 70 to reduce the influence of extreme values). Wave fixed effects are included as explicit predictors in the FE-IS specification to absorb period-level shocks common to all respondents.

Analytical Strategy

Standard Fixed Effects

Our baseline specification is a two-way fixed effects (FE) model with individual and wave fixed effects, estimated using the fixest package in R. For individual i observed at wave t, the standard FE model is:

\begin{equation} Y_{it} = \alpha_i + \lambda_t + \beta_1 \text{LivArr}_{it} + \boldsymbol{\gamma} \mathbf{X}_{it} + \epsilon_{it} \end{equation}

where \alpha_i is a person fixed effect, \lambda_t is a wave fixed effect, \text{LivArr}_{it} is a vector of living arrangement indicators (with living alone as the reference), and \mathbf{X}_{it} includes age, age^2, and all time-varying covariates. Standard errors are clustered at the person level.

The standard FE estimator removes all time-invariant confounders (e.g., genetic endowment, childhood conditions, stable personality traits) but relies on a parallel-trends assumption: conditional on covariates, individuals who change living arrangements would have followed the same health trajectory as those who did not (Bell and Jones 2014). This assumption fails when living arrangement transitions are triggered by individual-specific health decline — precisely the health selection mechanism of interest.

Fixed Effects with Individual Slopes (FE-IS)

To address health selection, we estimate Fixed Effects with Individual Slopes (FE-IS) models (Brüderl and Ludwig 2015; Rüttenauer and Ludwig 2019). The FE-IS model augments standard FE by allowing each individual to have their own linear and quadratic age trajectory:

\begin{equation} Y_{it} = \alpha_i + \beta_1 \text{LivArr}_{it} + \boldsymbol{\gamma} \mathbf{X}_{it} + \left(\delta_{0i} + \delta_{1i} \text{Age}_{it} + \delta_{2i} \text{Age}_{it}^2\right) + \epsilon_{it} \label{eq:feis} \end{equation}

where \delta_{0i}, \delta_{1i}, and \delta_{2i} are person-specific intercept, linear slope, and quadratic slope parameters. Because these individual trajectories are estimated from within-person variation, the model removes not only time-invariant unobservables (as standard FE does) but also person-specific health trajectories that may correlate with living arrangement transitions. Age terms are absorbed into the individual slopes and are therefore not included as separate predictors in the FE-IS specification. The model is implemented using the feisr package in R.

The central logic is as follows: if living arrangement effects on physical health are driven by health selection — that is, individuals whose health deteriorates faster are more likely to transition into co-residential care — then the FE-IS estimates, which partial out these individual trajectories, should be substantially attenuated relative to standard FE. The difference between FE and FE-IS estimates quantifies the magnitude of health selection bias. We use Hausman-type tests to formally compare the two specifications, where rejection of the null indicates that individual-specific trends are related to living arrangement transitions and that FE-IS is preferred.

Heterogeneity Analyses

We estimate stratified FE-IS models to examine whether living arrangement effects on health vary across population subgroups: by gender (female, male), age group (50–64, 65–74, 75+), race/ethnicity (White, Black, Other), and urban versus rural residence.

Sensitivity Analyses

We conduct five robustness checks: (1) inverse probability weighting (IPW) for panel attrition, (2) complete-case analysis excluding imputed observations, (3) exclusion of individuals institutionalized at baseline, (4) an alternative three-category living arrangement coding (alone, with others, institutionalized), and (5) a linear-only individual slope specification (omitting the quadratic age term). Full results are reported in Supplementary Table S2.

Missing Data

Missing values on time-varying covariates are handled via missRanger, a random-forest-based imputation method with predictive mean matching (k = 5). Before imputation, the panel data are pivoted to wide format (one row per person, with wave-specific columns) to preserve within-person correlation across waves. This approach avoids treating repeated observations from the same individual as independent during imputation.

Results

Descriptive Statistics

Table 1 presents sample characteristics by living arrangement. The five groups differ markedly in demographic composition and health status. Institutionalized respondents are the oldest on average (76.7 years), report the highest levels of ADL difficulty (1.53), depressive symptoms (2.32), and chronic conditions (3.17), and have the lowest rates of physical activity (43.5%). Respondents living alone are the second oldest (70.7 years), predominantly female (70.0%), and report elevated depressive symptoms (1.80) despite relatively modest physical health burdens (ADL = 0.39). Those living with spouse only are the healthiest on most indicators: lowest depressive symptoms (1.13), highest wealth (ln = 11.98), and highest educational attainment (13.1 years). Respondents living with children or other non-spouse members are predominantly female (77.1%), report the highest depressive symptoms among non-institutionalized groups (1.94), and have the lowest wealth (ln = 9.07). Spouse-plus-others households are the youngest on average (63.0 years) and show intermediate values on most health measures.

Main Results: FE versus FE-IS

Table 2 reports the central comparison of standard FE and FE-IS estimates for all four health outcomes. The pattern of attenuation from FE to FE-IS reveals a sharp distinction between physical health and depression.

Physical Health: Selection Artifacts

For ADL difficulties, the standard FE model shows that all non-institutional living arrangements are associated with significantly worse physical function relative to living alone: spouse only (b = 0.048, p < .001), children/others (b = 0.050, p < .01), and spouse plus others (b = 0.049, p < .001). These coefficients suggest that co-residence is linked to greater functional limitation. The FE-IS estimates, which account for person-specific health trajectories, reduce all three coefficients to non-significance: spouse only (b = 0.016, p > .05), children/others (b = 0.018, p > .05), and spouse plus others (b = 0.014, p > .05). The same pattern holds for IADL difficulties, where FE estimates of 0.059 (spouse only, p < .001), 0.075 (children/others, p < .001), and 0.061 (spouse plus others, p < .001) all fall to near zero and non-significance under FE-IS (0.008, 0.010, and 0.001, respectively). For chronic conditions, the FE estimates are already small and mostly non-significant; FE-IS estimates confirm no association (all |b| < 0.01).

These results indicate that the apparent associations between co-residential living arrangements and worse physical health are driven entirely by health selection. Individuals with steeper health decline trajectories are more likely to transition into co-residential arrangements, and standard FE conflates these pre-existing trajectories with causal effects of living arrangements.

Depression: Genuine Effects

The pattern for depressive symptoms is strikingly different. Standard FE shows that all co-residential arrangements are associated with fewer depressive symptoms relative to living alone: spouse only (b = -0.148, p < .001), children/others (b = -0.073, p < .05), and spouse plus others (b = -0.113, p < .001). Under FE-IS, the spousal co-residence effect barely attenuates (b = -0.140, p < .001; 5% reduction), the children/others effect remains significant (b = -0.068, p < .05; 7% reduction), and the spouse-plus-others effect shows moderate attenuation but retains significance (b = -0.077, p < .01; 32% reduction). These persistent effects, net of individual-specific health trajectories, support a causal interpretation: co-residential living arrangements reduce depressive symptoms through mechanisms that operate independently of pre-existing health decline.

Institutionalization: Massive Selection Bias

The institutionalization coefficients reveal the largest selection bias. For ADL difficulties, the FE estimate of 0.525 (p < .001) drops to 0.181 (p < .001) under FE-IS — a 65% attenuation. IADL difficulties show a parallel pattern: the FE estimate of 0.388 (p < .001) falls to 0.164 (p < .001), a 58% reduction. The chronic conditions effect is attenuated by 65% (FE: 0.140, p < .001; FE-IS: 0.049, p > .05), becoming non-significant. Most remarkably, the depression effect disappears entirely: the FE estimate of 0.073 (p < .05) becomes -0.009 (p > .05) under FE-IS. The residual ADL and IADL effects likely reflect the acute health events (e.g., hip fractures, strokes) that precipitate institutional placement and cannot be captured by smooth age trajectories, rather than a harmful effect of institutional care itself.

Heterogeneity

Table 3 reports stratified FE-IS models for depression and ADL difficulties across population subgroups.

Gender

The gender-stratified results reveal a qualitative difference in how men and women benefit from co-residential arrangements. For depressive symptoms, men show significant reductions under all forms of co-residence: spouse only (b = -0.175, p < .001), children/others (b = -0.167, p < .01), and spouse plus others (b = -0.187, p < .001). Women, by contrast, benefit only from spousal co-residence (b = -0.141, p < .001); the effects of living with children/others (b = -0.032, p = .326) and spouse plus others (b = -0.026, p = .430) are small and non-significant. For ADL difficulties, neither men nor women show significant co-residence effects under FE-IS, confirming that physical health associations are selection artifacts in both groups. This gender disparity in depression effects aligns with research showing that men rely more heavily on household members for emotional support, while women draw on broader extra-household networks (Takagi et al. 2024).

Age

The protective effect of spousal co-residence on depression follows a monotonic age gradient. Among adults aged 50–64, living with a spouse is associated with a significant reduction in CES-D scores (b = -0.208, p < .001). This effect narrows to -0.188 (p < .01) among those aged 65–74 and becomes non-significant (b = -0.082, p = .114) among those 75 and older. The youngest age group also shows significant depression reductions for children/others (b = -0.153, p < .01) and spouse plus others (b = -0.144, p < .01), suggesting that co-residence of any form carries stronger mental health benefits earlier in the aging process.

Race and Ethnicity

The spousal co-residence effect on depression is significant and similar in magnitude for White (b = -0.131, p < .001) and Black (b = -0.135, p < .01) respondents. The estimate for respondents of other racial/ethnic backgrounds is comparable in direction but not statistically significant (b = -0.103, p = .249), likely reflecting limited statistical power given smaller sample sizes in this group. These results suggest that the mental health benefits of spousal co-residence operate across racial and ethnic lines, consistent with findings that living arrangement effects on depression are broadly shared, even as the prevalence of specific arrangements varies across groups (Liu 2024).

Sensitivity Analyses

Results are robust across all five alternative specifications (Supplementary Table [tab:sensitivity]). The spousal co-residence effect on depression remains significant under complete-case analysis (b = -0.216, p < .001), inverse probability weighting for attrition (b = -0.112, p < .001), exclusion of baseline institutionalized respondents (b = -0.139, p < .001), three-category living arrangement coding (b = -0.113, p < .001), and a linear-only slope specification (b = -0.154, p < .001). Physical health effects remain non-significant across all specifications. The institutionalization effect on ADL difficulties is consistent across robustness checks, ranging from 0.182 to 0.282. These results confirm that the core finding — genuine depression effects but spurious physical health associations — does not depend on specific modeling choices regarding imputation, sample definition, or functional form.

Discussion

This study applied Fixed Effects with Individual Slopes (FE-IS) models to 20 years of Health and Retirement Study data (2000–2020; N = 27,024; 177,588 person-wave observations) to distinguish genuine causal effects of living arrangements on health from health selection bias. Three principal findings emerged. First, all associations between non-institutional co-residence and physical health (ADL limitations, IADL limitations, chronic conditions) disappeared once individual-specific health trajectories were absorbed, exposing these associations as artifacts of health selection. Second, co-residence effects on depression persisted in the FE-IS specification, with spousal co-residence producing the largest reduction (b = -0.140, p < .001). Third, these protective depression effects were strongly gendered and age-graded: men benefited from all forms of co-residence, whereas women benefited only from spousal co-residence; and the protective effect attenuated from ages 50–64 to 75 and older.

Disentangling Selection from Causation

The most consequential finding is that every non-institutional living arrangement coefficient for ADL limitations, IADL limitations, and chronic conditions became statistically nonsignificant when FE-IS models replaced standard fixed effects. This pattern held for spousal co-residence, co-residence with children or others, and co-residence with spouse and others. The standard FE models had estimated that spousal co-residence was associated with fewer chronic conditions and that co-residence with children was associated with greater ADL and IADL limitations relative to living alone. Under FE-IS, none of these coefficients remained distinguishable from zero.

What the standard FE estimator captured was not a causal effect of household composition but rather the correlation between individual health trajectories and the propensity to change living arrangements. Older adults whose physical health is declining faster are more likely to move in with family members or to enter institutional care (Gaugler et al. 2007; Verspeek et al. 2024). Standard FE models, which difference out time-invariant heterogeneity, cannot remove this source of confounding because the confound is dynamic: it is the slope of health change, not its level, that drives both the treatment (living arrangement transition) and the outcome (Rüttenauer and Ludwig 2019; Brüderl and Ludwig 2015). FE-IS models address this by estimating individual-specific linear and quadratic age slopes, absorbing person-level health trajectories that would otherwise bias the coefficients.

The institutionalization results provide a particularly vivid illustration of this selection mechanism. Standard FE estimated that institutionalization was associated with substantially worse physical health: b = 0.525 (p < .001) for ADL limitations, b = 0.388 (p < .001) for IADL limitations, and b = 0.140 (p < .001) for chronic conditions. FE-IS attenuated these coefficients by 58–65%. For chronic conditions, the association became nonsignificant entirely (b = 0.049, n.s.). These attenuation magnitudes are consistent with evidence that three or more ADL dependencies, cognitive impairment, and prior institutional use rank among the strongest predictors of nursing home admission (Gaugler et al. 2007), and that expectations of future institutional relocation are themselves longitudinally linked with functional decline (Verspeek et al. 2024). In other words, the standard FE model was attributing to institutionalization what was, in large part, the continuation of pre-existing health decline that precipitated the institutional transition.

These findings carry direct implications for the broader literature on living arrangements and health. Studies that reported significant associations between household composition and physical health using cross-sectional designs or standard FE models — including foundational work such as Hughes and Waite (2002) — likely overestimated causal effects by conflating them with health selection. This does not invalidate those studies as descriptions of population-level associations, but it does caution against interpreting such associations as evidence that changing household composition would alter physical health trajectories. The FE-IS framework provides a more rigorous test: it asks whether, net of the individual’s own health trajectory, a change in living arrangement produces additional health effects. For physical health, the answer is no.

Depression as a Genuine Causal Pathway

The depression results tell a qualitatively different story. Spousal co-residence (b = -0.140, p < .001), co-residence with children or others (b = -0.068, p < .05), and co-residence with spouse and others (b = -0.077, p < .01) all remained significant predictors of lower depressive symptoms in the FE-IS specification. These coefficients survived the same stringent test that eliminated every physical health association, indicating that the depression-reducing effects of co-residence are not driven by health selection.

This divergence between physical and mental health aligns with theoretical predictions from the social convoy model (Kahn and Antonucci 1980; Antonucci et al. 2019). The convoy framework posits that household members occupy the innermost ring of an individual’s social network, providing both instrumental support (assistance with daily tasks) and emotional support (companionship, affection, shared routines) (Fuller et al. 2020; Sherman et al. 2015). Our results suggest that these two support functions operate through distinct pathways with different causal statuses. Instrumental support — help with ADL tasks, medication management, meal preparation — addresses symptoms of physical decline but does not alter the underlying physiological trajectory. A co-resident who helps with bathing does not slow joint degeneration or cardiovascular disease; the recipient’s physical health trajectory continues on its course. Emotional support, by contrast, operates through psychosocial mechanisms — reducing loneliness, regulating negative affect, providing a sense of purpose and belonging — that directly influence depressive symptomatology (Cohen and Wills 1985; House et al. 1988).

The stress-buffering model offers a complementary interpretation (Cohen and Wills 1985). Co-residents may buffer against depressive episodes triggered by health shocks, bereavement, or accumulating social losses. When a stressful event occurs, the presence of household members provides both tangible aid (navigating the healthcare system, managing household disruptions) and emotional reassurance that attenuates the depressive response. This buffering function is consistent with evidence that living arrangement transitions — from cohabitation to living alone due to widowhood or children’s departure — significantly increase depression risk (Zhang et al. 2025), and that co-residence with children mediates the relationship between widowhood and loneliness (Jiao et al. 2024).

The institutionalization result for depression deserves special attention. Standard FE estimated a modest positive association between institutionalization and depressive symptoms (b = 0.073, p < .05), consistent with popular narratives about the depressogenic nature of institutional environments. Under FE-IS, this coefficient was fully attenuated to near-zero (b = -0.009, n.s.) — a 100% reduction. The apparent depression effect of institutionalization was entirely an artifact of health selection: older adults who enter nursing homes are on steeper health decline trajectories that themselves produce greater depression (Li and Qin 2026; Gaugler et al. 2007). Once individual trajectories are absorbed, the institutional environment per se does not increase depression above what the person’s health trajectory would predict. This finding challenges the assumption that institutional settings are inherently harmful for mental health and redirects attention to pre-admission health trajectories as the primary driver of elevated depression in institutional populations.

Gender, Age, and Race: Heterogeneity in the Depression Pathway

The gender pattern in depression effects was stark and theoretically informative. Men benefited from all forms of co-residence: spousal (b = -0.175, p < .001), children or others (b = -0.167, p < .01), and spouse with others (b = -0.187, p < .001). Women, by contrast, benefited only from spousal co-residence (b = -0.141, p < .001); co-residence with children or others (b = -0.032, n.s.) and with spouse and others (b = -0.026, n.s.) produced no significant depression reduction.

This pattern is consistent with longstanding evidence that men rely more heavily on household members — and on spouses in particular — as their primary source of emotional support, whereas women cultivate broader, more diversified social networks beyond the household (Berkman and Syme 1979; Antonucci et al. 2019). For women, non-spousal co-residence may not provide net emotional benefit because it can entail role strain, loss of autonomy, or the assumption of caregiving responsibilities that offset any companionship gains. Liu (2024) showed that distant social networks (extended family, friends, neighborhood ties) moderate the relationship between living arrangements and depressive symptoms in older adults, a pattern more consistent with women’s broader network structure than men’s household-centered support. Takagi et al. (2024) similarly reported gender differences in living arrangement-health associations, with spousal and child co-residence producing differential effects for men and women. The key distinction is not that women are less affected by social context, but that the relevant social context for women’s mental health extends beyond the household in ways that it does not for men.

The age gradient in spousal co-residence effects was monotonically declining: b = -0.208 (p < .001) for ages 50–64, b = -0.188 (p < .01) for ages 65–74, and b = -0.082 (n.s.) for ages 75 and older. One interpretation draws on the convoy model’s prediction that social network structure contracts with age (Antonucci et al. 2019; Sherman et al. 2015): as individuals age and experience multiple losses (of friends, of mobility, of cognitive capacity), the marginal contribution of any single relationship — including the spousal relationship — may diminish relative to the cumulative burden of health decline. At advanced ages, biological and cognitive deterioration may overwhelm the protective capacity of emotional support, rendering household composition less consequential for mental health. Alternatively, the surviving population at 75+ is selected on resilience: individuals who have maintained stable mental health into advanced old age may be less sensitive to living arrangement influences because they possess other protective resources.

The race/ethnicity results showed similar protective magnitudes of spousal co-residence for White (b = -0.131, p < .001) and Black (b = -0.135, p < .01) older adults. This convergence suggests that the emotional support mechanism of spousal co-residence operates across racial lines, even as structural factors (wealth, neighborhood composition, healthcare access) shape which living arrangements are feasible (Liu 2024). The non-significant finding for the “Other” racial category likely reflects insufficient statistical power due to small sample size rather than a true null effect.

Limitations

Several limitations warrant consideration. First, the HRS does not capture within-household relationship quality. A co-resident spouse or child may provide warm companionship or may be a source of conflict; our models treat all co-residence within a category as equivalent. Studies that measure relationship satisfaction alongside living arrangement type would provide a more complete picture (Chen et al. 2025; Srivastava et al. 2021). Second, the HRS does not distinguish voluntary from involuntary co-residence. An older adult who chooses to live with an adult child may experience different mental health effects than one who is compelled to do so by financial or health constraints. Third, institutionalization is heterogeneous: skilled nursing facilities, assisted living communities, and continuing care retirement communities differ in their social environments, yet our measure groups them together. Fourth, the FE-IS model assumes that individual health trajectories follow linear or quadratic functions of age. If trajectories are characterized by sudden discontinuities or higher-order nonlinearities, the model may not fully absorb selection bias. Fifth, the HRS is specific to the US context. In societies with stronger filial piety norms or different institutional care systems, the associations between living arrangements and health may differ substantially. Sixth, self-reported health measures, while validated (Idler and Benyamini 1997), are subject to reporting heterogeneity across demographic groups. Seventh, despite the FE-IS specification, we cannot rule out all time-varying confounding: major life events (job loss, divorce of an adult child, neighborhood disruption) that coincide with living arrangement transitions and independently affect health are not captured.

Conclusion

Using FE-IS models applied to two decades of nationally representative panel data, this study demonstrates that the widely reported associations between household composition and physical health among older adults are artifacts of health selection rather than causal effects, while co-residence genuinely reduces depressive symptoms through social-psychological mechanisms.

This finding carries three implications. Methodologically, FE-IS models reveal that a substantial portion of the living arrangement–health literature is confounded by individual health trajectories that standard fixed effects cannot remove. The 58–65% attenuation of institutionalization effects and the complete elimination of non-institutional physical health effects illustrate how dramatically health selection can inflate estimated associations. Future studies of household composition and health should, at minimum, consider whether individual-specific trends explain their findings.

Substantively, the persistence of depression effects in the FE-IS specification provides credible evidence that co-residence operates as a genuine causal determinant of mental health. The social convoy model’s prediction — that household members provide emotional support that protects against depression — is validated, while its prediction about instrumental support and physical health is revealed to be confounded by selection. This domain-specific pattern refines our theoretical understanding: the convoy’s protective function is real but limited to the psychosocial domain.

For policy, these results identify specific populations for targeted intervention. Men living alone are a priority group: they lack the household-based emotional support that appears causally protective against depression and, unlike women, do not compensate through extra-household networks. Adults aged 50–64 show the largest spousal co-residence effects, suggesting that household-level interventions for depression may be most effective before age 75. Policies aimed at reducing depression among older adults — community mental health programs, social prescribing, housing placement decisions — should attend to household composition as a modifiable determinant, while recognizing that household composition is unlikely to alter physical health trajectories.

Future research should incorporate measures of within-household relationship quality, distinguish voluntary from involuntary co-residence, and extend the FE-IS approach to non-US populations where cultural norms about intergenerational co-residence differ. Studies linking living arrangement transitions to biomarker data could further clarify whether co-residence affects physiological stress pathways or operates exclusively through self-reported psychological outcomes.
