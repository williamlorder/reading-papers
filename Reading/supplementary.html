Historical Religious Institutionalization and Population Health:
A Cross-National Panel Analysis

Global Distribution of Key Variables

Correlation Matrix

Table [tab:correlations] presents the pairwise Pearson correlation matrix for all variables in the estimation sample. Religious freedom is strongly positively correlated with electoral democracy (r = 0.72), freedom of expression (r = 0.78), and log GDP per capita (r = 0.45). These correlations motivate the mediation analysis in the main text, since they suggest that religious freedom is embedded in a broader cluster of political and economic liberalization. Life expectancy at birth is positively correlated with all institutional and economic variables, with the strongest association observed for log GDP per capita (r = 0.62) and electoral democracy (r = 0.47).

Causal Mediation Analysis

Table [tab:mediation] presents the full causal mediation results for six candidate pathways, following Imai et al. (2010). The table reports the average causal mediation effect (ACME), average direct effect (ADE), total effect, and proportion mediated for each candidate mediator. Quasi-Bayesian 95% confidence intervals are based on 1,000 simulations. Country and year fixed effects are absorbed via within-transformation prior to estimation.

Heterogeneity Analysis

Table [tab:heterogeneity] reports the religious freedom coefficient from separate TWFE regressions within subgroups defined by World Bank income classification, world region, and historical religious majority. Stratified estimation permits the coefficient to vary freely across contexts, at the cost of reduced statistical power within each subgroup.

Robustness Checks

Table [tab:robustness] reports six alternative specifications designed to probe the sensitivity of the main finding. Specification R1 replaces life expectancy with the natural logarithm of the infant mortality rate; the coefficient on religious freedom is -0.042 (SE = 0.019, p = 0.028), confirming the main finding with an alternative early-life health outcome. R2 uses healthy life expectancy (HALE); the coefficient is -0.133 (SE = 0.081, p < 0.10). R3 replaces the two-way fixed effects with country-only fixed effects, permitting the time-invariant historical baseline of religious freedom (1945–1965 mean) to enter as a predictor; the result confirms that it is current, not inherited, religious freedom that drives within-country health dynamics. R4 introduces a five-year lag to address reverse causality: the lagged coefficient is +0.485 (SE = 0.240, p = 0.044), confirming that religious freedom changes temporally precede health improvements. R5 adds World Religion Project composition controls (Christian, Muslim, and non-religious population shares); the religious freedom coefficient rises to +0.911 (SE = 0.580). R6 uses the suicide rate as an alternative outcome; the positive coefficient (+0.543, SE = 0.257, p = 0.035) is consistent with the well-documented pattern that politically open societies report higher suicide rates, partly due to more complete vital registration (Durkheim 1897).
