Instrumental Variable Identification Strategy

This appendix develops the full identification strategy for the instrumental variable (IV) estimates reported in the main manuscript. We formalize the three assumptions required for valid 2SLS inference, present the theoretical and empirical case for each instrument, and report overidentification and diagnostic test results.

Identification Assumptions

Two-stage least squares estimation requires three assumptions for the instruments to produce consistent estimates. Figure 1 illustrates the general logic of instrumental variable identification: an instrument Z affects the outcome Y only through its effect on the endogenous variable X, bypassing the confounding path created by unobserved variables U. Figure 2 then applies this logic to our specific identification strategy. We state each assumption formally below.

Assumption 1: Relevance.

The instruments Z must predict the endogenous regressor X (individual internet use frequency): \begin{equation} \text{Cov}(Z, X) \neq 0 \end{equation} This is directly testable through first-stage F-statistics. Using 1984 provincial telephone density interacted with survey year (Tel84) as the sole instrument yields a first-stage F of 177.63. Using the province-year NBS internet penetration rate (Inet) alone yields F = 599.91. In the overidentified specification with both instruments, the minimum eigenvalue statistic is 360.27. All three values far exceed the Stock and Yogo (2005) critical value of 16.38 for 10% maximal IV size with two endogenous regressors and two instruments, ruling out weak-instrument bias. The instruments are not marginal predictors; they are among the strongest supply-side predictors available for internet use in the Chinese context.

The strength of the first stage reflects a well-documented empirical regularity: internet infrastructure in China follows path-dependent patterns rooted in pre-digital telecommunications networks (Czernich et al. 2011). Provinces with denser historical telephone networks built broadband infrastructure faster, and provinces targeted by the Broadband China policy expanded penetration more rapidly. Both processes generate strong, policy-driven variation in individual internet access that is captured by the first-stage regression.

Assumption 2: Exclusion restriction.

The instruments must affect the outcome (gender ideology) only through their effect on the endogenous regressor: \begin{equation} \text{Cov}(Z, \varepsilon) = 0 \end{equation} where \varepsilon is the structural error in the second-stage equation. This assumption cannot be directly tested in a just-identified model, where the number of instruments equals the number of endogenous regressors. In the overidentified specification with both Tel84 and Inet as instruments, the Sargan J-test provides a joint test of instrument validity. The test yields p = 0.111, failing to reject the null that both instruments satisfy the exclusion restriction at conventional significance levels. While the Sargan test has limited power against certain alternatives (particularly when both instruments are invalid in the same direction), the failure to reject is consistent with, though not proof of, instrument validity.

The substantive case for the exclusion restriction rests on distinct arguments for each instrument, developed in Sections 1.2 and 1.3. The common thread is that both instruments capture supply-side variation in internet infrastructure that is determined by policy decisions and historical contingency rather than by individual demand for gender-related content. All IV models control for province-year GDP per capita, urbanization rate, and female labor force participation rate to absorb confounding channels through which provincial infrastructure might affect attitudes independently of internet use.

Assumption 3: Monotonicity.

For the LATE interpretation, each instrument must shift internet use weakly in the same direction for all individuals: \begin{equation} X_i(Z = z') \geq X_i(Z = z) \quad \text{for all } i \text{ whenever } z' > z \end{equation} No individual who would use the internet in a province with low infrastructure density would cease using it in a province with higher density. This assumption is inherently plausible for supply-side instruments. Greater provincial telephone density in 1984 facilitates subsequent broadband buildout; higher provincial internet penetration reflects greater infrastructure availability. Both instruments expand the opportunity set for internet access without constraining it. Some individuals are unaffected (always-takers who use the internet regardless of infrastructure, or never-takers who do not), but the assumption requires only that no individual’s internet use is reduced by higher instrument values. Because neither instrument operates through individual-level demand factors, preference heterogeneity, or price incentives that might induce some individuals to substitute away from internet use, the monotonicity condition is difficult to violate in this setting.

Instrument 1: 1984 Provincial Telephone Density

The first instrument is the number of fixed telephone lines per 100 persons in each province in 1984, interacted with a linear survey-year trend to create Bartik-style variation across both provinces and time. This instrument exploits path dependence in telecommunications infrastructure: provinces with denser pre-digital telephone networks later developed broadband and mobile internet infrastructure more rapidly.

Historical context.

China’s telecommunications system in 1984 was a product of the planned economy. Infrastructure allocation followed administrative hierarchy, military priorities, and industrial policy rather than consumer demand. Provincial capitals and heavy-industry centers received denser telephone networks; agricultural and frontier provinces received minimal investment. These decisions were made under a fundamentally different economic system, by planners whose objectives (industrial coordination, military communication, administrative control) bore no relation to gender attitudes or cultural values. The resulting cross-provincial variation in telephone density was large: in 1984, the most connected provinces had roughly ten times the telephone density of the least connected.

Path dependence mechanism.

When China modernized its telecommunications in the 1990s and 2000s, new infrastructure followed existing routes. Fiber optic cables were laid along copper-wire telephone trunk lines because the rights of way, conduit infrastructure, and switching station locations were already established. Central offices built for voice switching were upgraded to handle data. The Broadband China strategy (2013) and subsequent 4G/5G rollouts reinforced this pattern: network upgrades proceeded fastest where existing infrastructure was densest (Qu et al. 2025). The correlation between pre-digital telephone density and contemporary broadband quality is well documented in the comparative telecommunications literature (Czernich et al. 2011).

Bartik interaction.

Because 1984 telephone density is time-invariant, it cannot serve as an instrument in models with province fixed effects without additional structure. We interact it with the survey year (\text{TelDensity}_{1984,p} \times \text{Year}_t) to generate within-province variation over time. The identifying assumption is that provinces with denser historical infrastructure experienced faster internet adoption growth during 2012–2023. The year trend captures the national trajectory of internet expansion; the cross-sectional telephone density captures which provinces were positioned to expand more quickly.

Exclusion argument.

For 1984 telephone density to violate the exclusion restriction, it would need to affect 2012–2023 gender attitudes through a channel other than internet infrastructure, conditional on province fixed effects and individual-level controls. Province fixed effects absorb all time-invariant provincial characteristics, including the direct effect of historical infrastructure levels. The Bartik interaction identifies effects from differential growth rates, not levels. A violation would require that the rate of change in gender attitudes correlates with 1984 telephone density through some non-internet channel. Given that the 1984 allocation reflected planned-economy priorities three to four decades before the survey period, such a channel is difficult to construct. The logic follows Czernich et al. (2011), who used historical telephone infrastructure as an instrument for broadband in OECD countries, and Campante et al. (2018), who applied similar instruments in cross-national studies of internet and political attitudes.

Instrument 2: NBS Internet Penetration Rate

The second instrument is the provincial internet penetration rate (internet users as a share of provincial population) published in National Bureau of Statistics yearbooks. Unlike the telephone density instrument, this variable varies across both provinces and years, providing time-varying cross-sectional variation without requiring a Bartik interaction.

Source and coverage.

The NBS penetration rate is compiled from China Internet Network Information Center (CNNIC) surveys and provincial statistical yearbooks. It captures the aggregate share of each province’s population that uses the internet, measured at the province-year level. Data are available from 2000 through 2021. Because 2023 falls outside this range, all IV specifications using this instrument exclude the 2023 CGSS wave, reducing the sample to approximately 34,000 respondents. The telephone density instrument, which is available for all waves, provides a check on whether the exclusion of 2023 affects the results.

Supply-side variation.

Provincial internet penetration is driven by government infrastructure investment, regulatory decisions, and the timing of broadband and mobile network deployment. The Broadband China strategy designated specific provinces as pilot zones for accelerated infrastructure rollout. The 4G expansion (2014–2016) and 5G deployment (2019 onward) followed patterns shaped by provincial government priorities, population density, and prior network capacity. These supply-side policy choices generate variation in internet availability that is independent of individual demand for internet access and of individual gender attitudes.

Exclusion argument.

The exclusion restriction requires that provincial penetration affects individual gender attitudes only through its effect on individual internet use. The key concern is that penetration could proxy for provincial modernization: wealthier, more urbanized provinces have both higher penetration and potentially different gender attitude trajectories. We address this in three ways. Province fixed effects absorb all time-invariant provincial differences. Year fixed effects absorb national trends. Province-year controls for GDP per capita, urbanization rate, and female labor force participation absorb time-varying provincial modernization. The residual variation in penetration reflects infrastructure capacity and policy timing, not the content preferences or attitudinal predispositions of individual users.

Overidentification and Diagnostics

Using both instruments simultaneously allows us to test the overidentifying restriction and compare the estimates produced by each instrument. Four sets of diagnostics inform our assessment.

Sargan J-test.

In the overidentified specification with both Tel84 and Inet, the Sargan J-statistic yields p = 0.111, failing to reject the null of joint instrument validity at the 5% level. The test evaluates whether the two instruments produce statistically distinguishable estimates of the treatment effect; failure to reject supports the hypothesis that both instruments satisfy the exclusion restriction. The p-value of 0.111 provides moderate but not overwhelming support. The Sargan test is known to have limited power in large samples and cannot detect violations where both instruments are invalid in the same direction. The substantive exclusion arguments in Sections 1.2 and 1.3 remain the primary basis for credibility.

Wu-Hausman test.

All three IV specifications yield Wu-Hausman statistics with p < .001, rejecting the null that OLS is consistent. This confirms that internet use is endogenous in the gender ideology equation: unobserved factors simultaneously predict internet use and gender attitudes, biasing OLS estimates. The direction of the bias is informative. IV estimates of the Female \times Internet interaction are uniformly larger than OLS (0.121–0.452 vs. 0.081), consistent with attenuation from classical measurement error in the ordinal internet use frequency variable. If positive selection bias (egalitarian individuals choosing to use the internet more) were dominant, OLS would overestimate the effect; the opposite pattern points to measurement error as the primary source of OLS inconsistency. The IV estimates, which isolate exogenous variation in internet access driven by infrastructure supply, recover the effect net of measurement noise.

LATE interpretation.

The two instruments yield different point estimates for the Female \times Internet interaction: Tel84 produces 0.452 and Inet produces 0.121. Under the LATE framework (Imbens and Angrist 1994), this difference does not signal invalidity but rather reflects distinct complier populations. The Tel84 instrument identifies the effect among individuals whose internet use is determined by legacy telecom infrastructure, a group concentrated in less-developed provinces where infrastructure constraints remain binding. The Inet instrument identifies the effect among individuals responding to recent policy-driven broadband expansion, a broader and more heterogeneous population.

The larger Tel84 estimate is consistent with heterogeneous treatment effects: in provinces where internet access is most constrained by infrastructure, the marginal unit of internet exposure represents a sharper departure from pre-existing information environments. The gendered attitudinal effect of internet use may be strongest precisely where digital access is newest and most transformative. The Inet estimate, which captures a wider range of compliers including those in provinces with already-high penetration, reflects a smaller marginal effect in a population closer to saturation. The Sargan test’s failure to reject (p = 0.111) confirms that these two estimates are not statistically incompatible despite their difference in magnitude.

Robustness Analyses

This section presents robustness checks for the Female \times Internet interaction reported in the main manuscript. All models include controls for age, education, log income, urban hukou, marital status, party membership, and year fixed effects, with standard errors clustered by province.

Alternative Dependent Variable Specifications

The main manuscript uses a composite gender ideology index averaging four attitude items. To assess whether the gendered internet effect depends on this aggregation, we re-estimate the Female \times Internet interaction using each item as a separate dependent variable. The interaction is statistically significant for all four items (p < .001 in each case) but varies in magnitude. The “men should focus on career, women on family” item (separate-spheres beliefs) shows the largest effect (\hat{\beta} = 0.108, SE = 0.006), followed by “men are inherently more capable than women” (gender essentialism; \hat{\beta} = 0.092, SE = 0.006) and “it is better to marry well than to work well” (marriage pragmatism; \hat{\beta} = 0.082, SE = 0.006). The “women should be laid off first” item (labor market fairness) shows the smallest interaction (\hat{\beta} = 0.044, SE = 0.004). The gradient across items is consistent with the selective exposure mechanism proposed in the main text: internet-mediated feminist discourse engages most directly with separate-spheres ideology and least directly with abstract labor market fairness norms.

Alternative Predictor and Model Specifications

Two specifications test the sensitivity of the interaction to how internet use and the outcome are operationalized. Recoding internet use as a binary variable (any use vs. never) yields a Female \times Internet interaction of 0.251 (p < .001), confirming that the gendered effect is not an artifact of the ordinal coding. An ordered logit model treating the rounded ideology index as an ordinal outcome produces a log-odds interaction of 0.187 (p < .001), indicating that the result is robust to relaxing the linearity assumption imposed by OLS.

Alternative Sample Specifications

The main analyses use multiple imputation via missRanger with predictive mean matching (k = 5). To verify that imputation does not drive the results, we re-estimate the main model using complete cases with listwise deletion. The listwise sample retains 72,160 of 79,014 respondents (91.3%). The Female \times Internet interaction is 0.082 (p < .001, SE = 0.004), substantively identical to the imputed-data estimate of 0.081. The consistency between imputed and listwise results indicates that the gendered internet effect is not sensitive to missing data treatment.
