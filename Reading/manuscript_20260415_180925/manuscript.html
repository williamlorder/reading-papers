From Social Problem to Clinical Risk: Computational Textual Evidence of How ACE Discourse Reshapes the Child Welfare Knowledge Field

Introduction

A child welfare social worker in an underfunded urban office opens a referral. The family lives in public housing, the neighborhood has no pediatric clinic within walking distance, and the mother, herself a survivor of foster care, works two minimum-wage jobs. The intake form asks the worker to count the child’s adverse childhood experiences. The score tells the worker how “at risk” the child is for future depression, substance use, and chronic disease. It says nothing about the landlord who refuses to fix the mold, the school district that eliminated its counseling staff, or the child protective services system that removed the mother’s own siblings two decades earlier. The worker is left with a number and a referral to a therapist.

This vignette captures a tension at the center of contemporary child welfare: the ACE framework, developed by Felitti et al. (1998) in a Kaiser Permanente health maintenance organization, has become the dominant lens through which practitioners, policymakers, and researchers understand childhood adversity. Across its two waves, the study enrolled 17,337 predominantly white, middle-class adults and defined ACEs through categories of household dysfunction, abuse, and neglect (seven in the original report, later expanded to ten), and a dose-response gradient linked cumulative ACE scores to adult morbidity and mortality. That gradient became one of the most cited findings in public health, fueling an exponential growth in research that now exceeds 17,000 publications. The framework’s appeal is straightforward: it provides a standardized, quantifiable measure of childhood adversity that translates easily into clinical screening protocols and policy targets.

But translation comes at a cost. In social work, the study of child maltreatment has historically emphasized legal accountability, family systems, community context, and the structural conditions (poverty, racism, housing instability) that produce harm (Pelton 2014; Roberts 2002). The ACE framework recasts these complex social relationships as calculable risk exposures, individual attributes that predict future pathology. Critics within social work and public health have questioned whether this reconceptualization amounts to what Conrad (2007) terms the “medicalization of social problems”: the absorption of inherently social phenomena into biomedical categories that locate the problem, and the solution, in the individual body rather than the social structure (Finkelhor 2017; Karatekin and Hill 2018). Bernard et al. (2020) has argued that racism itself should be recognized as an adverse childhood experience, challenging the framework’s original silence on structural determinants. Cronholm et al. (2015) demonstrated in Philadelphia that community-level adversities (neighborhood violence, discrimination, poverty) predicted poor adult health outcomes independently of conventional ACEs; Wade et al. (2015) examined these patterns in a racially diverse urban sample, documenting the co-occurrence of household and community-level adversities while noting the continued primacy of family-level exposures.

Despite the scale of the accumulated literature, no study has systematically mapped the full trajectory of this epistemic transformation using computational methods capable of detecting subtle shifts in the field’s conceptual architecture. Existing reviews have relied on systematic review and meta-analysis (Hughes et al. 2017; Petruccelli et al. 2019) or standard bibliometric trend analysis (Struck et al. 2020) that cannot capture how core concepts change meaning as they circulate across disciplinary communities. Are we witnessing a genuine integration of structural analysis into ACE research, or is the field absorbing structural language without restructuring its clinical foundations? Are social workers and child welfare practitioners being given tools for systemic change, or for more efficient screening of future patients?

This study deploys three complementary computational methods across 17,758 publications to answer these questions. Bibliometric analysis maps the field’s institutional infrastructure: who publishes, where, and in what disciplinary contexts. Word2Vec word embedding models (Mikolov et al. 2013) trained on period-specific corpora measure shifts in the semantic distance between ACE terminology and vocabularies of structural determinants, clinical psychiatry, and social justice. BERTopic dynamic topic modeling (Grootendorst 2022) identifies and tracks 48 distinct research sub-domains across the field’s history, revealing which conversations are growing, which are stalling, and which remain marginal.

This review makes three contributions. First, it provides the most comprehensive computational mapping of ACE discourse to date, documenting a pattern of selective absorption: the differential incorporation of structural perspectives based on their compatibility with clinical logic, such that poverty and life course concepts gain semantic traction while racism and discrimination lose ground. Second, it introduces distributional semantic analysis to the study of child welfare knowledge production, providing quantitative evidence that the field’s stated commitment to structural determinants has outpaced its actual conceptual integration. Third, it traces the rapid expansion of trauma-informed care discourse alongside the marginalization of racial justice vocabulary, raising pointed questions for social work about whether the profession’s most visible practice innovation addresses the structural violence that produces childhood adversity in the first place.

Background

The ACE Framework: From Clinical Instrument to Contested Paradigm

The original ACE Study (Felitti et al. 1998) demonstrated a graded relationship between cumulative ACE exposure and leading causes of adult morbidity and mortality. Respondents reporting four or more ACEs showed elevated risks for heart disease, cancer, chronic lung disease, depression, and suicide attempts. The dose-response gradient, the study’s most influential empirical contribution, provided compelling evidence that childhood adversity operates as a cumulative stressor with lasting biological and behavioral consequences. Meta-analytic evidence from Hughes et al. (2017), synthesizing 37 studies across multiple countries, confirmed significant associations between ACEs and a broad array of health-risk behaviors and conditions, with pooled odds ratios for the highest exposure categories (\geq4 ACEs) reaching more than 30 for outcomes such as suicide attempts. Population-based surveillance data showed that approximately 61% of U.S. adults reported at least one ACE, and nearly 16% reported four or more (Merrick et al. 2019).

The framework’s rapid diffusion was accompanied by growing recognition of its conceptual limitations. Finkelhor et al. (2015) argued that the original questionnaire omitted common forms of childhood adversity, including peer victimization, exposure to community violence, and socioeconomic hardship, while Finkelhor (2017) raised broader cautions about screening practices. The Expanded ACE framework developed by Cronholm et al. (2015) in Philadelphia incorporated community-level adversities and demonstrated that these structural exposures significantly predicted poor adult health outcomes independently of conventional ACEs. Bernard et al. (2020) explicitly argued that racism should be recognized as an adverse childhood experience, Metzler et al. (2016) argued that ACEs are linked to broader socioeconomic disadvantage and that prevention efforts must address root causes beyond individual households. International studies documented similar prevalence patterns across diverse cultural contexts, including national household surveys in England (Bellis et al. 2014) and investigations in low- and middle-income settings (Soares et al. 2015).

This expansion marked a turning point. The field moved from conceptualizing childhood adversity as primarily household-level dysfunction toward recognizing it as embedded within broader systems of social stratification. But the nature of that recognition remains an open empirical question. Social work scholars have long distinguished between rhetorical acknowledgment of structural conditions and their substantive operationalization in research design and practice (Featherstone et al. 2018; Dominelli 2002). The gap between saying “poverty matters” and designing studies that treat poverty as a causal force rather than a covariate is precisely what this computational analysis is designed to detect.

Child Welfare at the Crossroads: Clinical Logic versus Anti-Oppressive Practice

Within child welfare, the tension between clinical and structural approaches has deep roots. Traditional child maltreatment scholarship emphasized the legal, relational, and community dimensions of harm to children: what constitutes abuse, who bears responsibility, and what systems of accountability exist to protect children and support families (Pelton 2014). The anti-oppressive practice (AOP) tradition in social work, articulated by Dominelli (2002) and extended by Featherstone et al. (2018), insists that child welfare cannot be separated from the structural conditions (poverty, racism, colonialism, gender inequality) that produce vulnerability. In this tradition, the social worker’s task is not merely to identify and treat individual trauma but to challenge the systems that generate it.

The ACE framework exists in tension with this tradition. By reducing complex social relationships to a ten-item checklist and framing the resulting score as a predictor of future pathology, the framework risks what Garrett (2017) calls “welfare words”: the adoption of seemingly progressive language that actually narrows the field of vision. A child who has experienced four ACEs is repositioned from a member of a community that needs resources to a patient who needs treatment. Roberts (2002) documented how child welfare systems already disproportionately surveil and separate Black families, and Feely and Bosk (2021) have shown that structural risk factors, particularly poverty and economic hardship, drive racial disproportionality in child protective services far more than differences in parenting behavior. The addition of ACE screening to this apparatus raises questions about whether the framework functions as a tool for understanding adversity or as a mechanism for extending clinical authority over marginalized populations.

The medicalization thesis (Conrad 2007) provides the theoretical anchor for this critique. Conrad (2007) argued that biomedical frameworks tend to absorb social critique by translating structural claims into clinical language, producing an appearance of integration without substantive epistemological change. Applied to ACEs, the medicalization thesis predicts that structural language (“social determinants,” “health equity,” “structural racism”) will enter the discourse, but the underlying research designs, measurement instruments, and causal models will remain individual-level. The computational analysis presented here tests this prediction directly.

The ACE concept’s capacity to travel across disciplinary boundaries suggests that it operates as what Star and Griesemer (1989) termed a “boundary object”: a construct sufficiently plastic to be adapted by different communities of practice yet robust enough to maintain a shared identity across them. Clinicians use ACEs to screen for trauma exposure; epidemiologists use the same construct to model dose-response gradients; sociologists invoke it to critique structural inequalities; and policymakers deploy it to justify prevention programs. This interpretive flexibility has fueled the framework’s diffusion but has also generated conceptual tensions, as different disciplinary communities project distinct meanings onto the same ostensible construct. The question is whether some meanings have more institutional power than others, and whether the clinical interpretation has become hegemonic in ways that constrain alternatives.

Computational Approaches to Knowledge Field Analysis

The scale of the ACE literature (nearly 18,000 publications accumulated over three decades) exceeds the capacity of conventional review methods. Computational approaches to studying scientific knowledge fields have gained ground in sociology and the health sciences, where large textual corpora are increasingly treated as artifacts of collective meaning-making amenable to systematic empirical investigation (DiMaggio et al. 2013; Evans and Aceves 2016; Nelson 2017).

Bibliometric mapping enables quantitative characterization of a field’s infrastructure: publication trajectories, collaboration networks, and disciplinary diffusion (Aria and Cuccurullo 2017; Donthu et al. 2021; Župič and Čater 2014). Word embedding models, exemplified by Word2Vec (Mikolov et al. 2013), represent words as dense vectors in continuous semantic space, capturing the associative structure of meaning within a corpus. Social scientists have shown that such embeddings encode meaningful cultural content, from gender stereotypes (Caliskan et al. 2017) and racial bias (Garg et al. 2018) to shifting class associations (Kozlowski et al. 2019), making them effective instruments for tracking how conceptual boundaries move over time. BERTopic (Grootendorst 2022) combines transformer-based document embeddings with UMAP dimensionality reduction and HDBSCAN density-based clustering to generate coherent, interpretable topics. Where earlier approaches such as Latent Dirichlet Allocation (Blei et al. 2003) and structural topic models (Roberts et al. 2014) relied on bag-of-words representations, BERTopic leverages contextual embeddings to produce more semantically coherent topic structures.

No prior study of the ACE literature has combined these computational approaches to move beyond metadata-level description toward deep semantic analysis of the field’s conceptual evolution and its implications for child welfare practice. The present study addresses this gap.

Data and Methods

Data and Corpus Preparation

We retrieved all publications indexed in the Web of Science Core Collection (WoS; SCI-Expanded, SSCI, A&HCI, ESCI) with topic-level relevance to adverse childhood experiences. The search query targeted ACE-specific terminology: TS=("adverse childhood experience*" OR "early adversit*" OR ("ACE*" AND ("child*" OR "adversit*"))). The search was conducted in February 2026 and covered 1998–2026. An important scope clarification: this query captures ACE-labelled literature specifically, not the entire field of childhood adversity research. Substantial bodies of work on child maltreatment, family violence, community violence, toxic stress, and historical trauma exist under separate terminological traditions and are not captured by ACE-centric search terms. Our findings therefore describe epistemic patterns within the discourse that explicitly adopts the ACE framework, and the clinical turn we document is a property of this ACE-labelled literature rather than a claim about all research on childhood adversity. This scope is analytically appropriate: it is precisely the ACE label’s capacity to reorganize how childhood adversity is discussed, measured, and intervened upon that our analysis is designed to evaluate. We selected WoS rather than Scopus or PubMed because WoS provides the most comprehensive structured metadata (including institutional affiliations with country information, author keywords, and Keywords Plus) needed for multi-level bibliometric analysis.

The initial retrieval yielded 20,579 bibliographic records exported in BibTeX format across 21 files. Each record contained structured metadata including title, abstract, author names, institutional affiliations, journal name, publication year, document type, author keywords, Keywords Plus (WoS-assigned), and DOI.

The raw retrieval was refined through a systematic cleaning pipeline. We first excluded non-research document types that lack original empirical or theoretical contributions, principally meeting abstracts (n = 1,978), which contained no full abstract text, along with corrections, retractions, news items, book reviews, and reprints (collectively n = 98). Editorial materials and letters presented a judgment call: we retained those containing substantive abstracts of at least 100 characters (127 of 547), reasoning that editorials in this field often advance theoretical arguments worthy of inclusion in discourse analysis. We then removed entries lacking valid abstracts (n = 304), as the natural language processing pipeline requires sufficient text for meaningful semantic analysis. Deduplication identified 21 redundant entries through normalized DOI and title matching. The final cleaned corpus comprised 17,758 publications, representing an 86.3% retention rate from the raw retrieval. All entries contain valid abstracts (mean length = 1,607 characters), 98.7% have DOIs, and 81.6% include author keywords.

Bibliometric Analysis

Descriptive bibliometric analysis examined publication volume trends (1998–2025), geographical distribution based on author institutional affiliations (parsed from the WoS affiliation field), journal concentration, author productivity, research area classification, and keyword frequency. Country information was extracted by parsing detailed affiliation strings, mapping institution addresses to countries using last-element parsing with standardized country name normalization (e.g., “England” \rightarrow “United Kingdom,” U.S. state abbreviations \rightarrow “USA”).

Analytical Strategy

Our analytical strategy employed two complementary computational text analysis methods to move beyond descriptive bibliometrics into the semantic and discursive structure of the ACE literature.

To capture semantic shifts in how the field’s core concepts relate to broader vocabularies, we trained period-specific Word2Vec skip-gram models (Mikolov et al. 2013) on the 17,444 documents that met the minimum text-length threshold (the same subset used for topic modeling; 314 short-text documents excluded). These were distributed across five temporal windows: 1998–2005 (n = 237), 2006–2010 (n = 593), 2011–2015 (n = 1,873), 2016–2019 (n = 3,562), and 2020–2025 (n = 11,179). The analysis corpus consisted of concatenated title, author keywords, and abstract text for each publication. Text preprocessing involved lowercasing and cleaning of URLs and LaTeX artifacts, followed by joining 41 multi-word domain concepts into single tokens (e.g., “adverse childhood experiences” \rightarrow adverse_childhood_experience; “social determinants” \rightarrow social_determinant) to preserve semantic integrity. Tokenization and lemmatization were performed, followed by domain-specific stopword removal and synonym consolidation to reduce surface-level lexical variation. For each period, we computed the mean cosine similarity between a set of ten ACE seed terms (including adverse_childhood_experience, maltreatment, trauma, childhood_adversity) and curated groups of target terms representing two broad domains: (a) structural determinants, encompassing poverty/inequality, discrimination/racism, housing/environment, education, healthcare access, criminal justice, intergenerational transmission, and life course perspective; and (b) clinical/individual factors, encompassing mental health, physical health, neuroscience, and substance use. Following Kozlowski et al. (2019) and Garg et al. (2018), who demonstrated that period-specific word embeddings capture meaningful shifts in cultural associations, we compared era-averaged similarity scores between the early period (1998–2015, comprising the first three windows) and the late period (2016–2025, comprising the final two), with each period weighted equally regardless of corpus size to avoid over-representing the most recent and largest window. Vocabulary sizes ranged from 2,847 tokens in the earliest window to 18,432 in the most recent.

To identify and track thematic structures, we applied BERTopic (Grootendorst 2022), which combines transformer-based document embeddings with UMAP dimensionality reduction and HDBSCAN density-based clustering to generate coherent, interpretable topics without requiring a pre-specified number of topics. Documents were embedded using a pre-trained sentence transformer, reduced to a low-dimensional manifold, and clustered with a minimum cluster size of 50. Topic representations were refined through KeyBERT-inspired re-ranking of candidate terms. The 48 topics identified after automatic reduction from an initial 65 clusters were organized into nine macro-themes through expert classification: the first author assigned each topic to a macro-theme based on top-20 keywords and representative documents, and a second researcher independently classified a random sample of 15 topics (\kappa = 0.91). Dynamic topic modeling traced frequency trajectories across 20 temporal bins, with evolution and global tuning enabled to smooth short-term fluctuations while preserving trends.

Each analytical layer captures a distinct dimension of epistemic change: bibliometrics establishes the institutional infrastructure of the field; Word2Vec penetrates beneath this surface to capture deep associative structure; and topic modeling reveals the manifest discourse structure. All analysis was conducted in R (v4.4.3) and Python (v3.11), using Gensim for Word2Vec models, spaCy for NLP preprocessing, and the BERTopic library with sentence-transformers for topic modeling.

Results

The Reinforced Clinical Core

The ACE literature has grown exponentially, expanding from 4 publications in 1998 to 2,415 in 2025 (Figure 1A). The cumulative count surpassed 17,000 by early 2026, with the majority of growth occurring after 2016 (Figure 1B). The post-2020 acceleration, with annual output nearly doubling from 1,355 in 2020 to 2,415 in 2025, likely reflects both policy-driven institutional demand and heightened societal awareness of childhood adversity during and after the COVID-19 pandemic.

Author affiliations spanned 149 countries, but the field remains heavily concentrated in the Global North. The United States contributed 9,183 publications (51.7%), followed by the United Kingdom (n = 1,874; 10.6%), Canada (n = 1,346; 7.6%), China (n = 1,148; 6.5%), Germany (n = 1,014; 5.7%), and Australia (n = 854; 4.8%) (Figure 2A). The top three English-speaking nations account for nearly 70% of the entire corpus, concentrating definitional authority over what counts as childhood adversity, how it should be measured, and what counts as evidence. Publications appeared across 2,608 unique journals, with the top-contributing outlets being Child Abuse & Neglect (n = 597), Journal of Affective Disorders (n = 315), Psychoneuroendocrinology (n = 242), PLOS ONE (n = 232), and Frontiers in Psychiatry (n = 222) (Figure 2B). That four of the five top journals are psychiatric or psychobiological outlets, with only Child Abuse & Neglect anchored in child welfare, is itself a telling indicator of the field’s center of gravity.

This clinical dominance is confirmed by the topic structure. The BERTopic model, applied to the 17,444 documents that produced valid embeddings (314 documents were excluded due to insufficient text length after preprocessing), identified 48 distinct topics organized into nine macro-themes (Figure 3). The largest topic, Mental Health & Depressive Symptoms (Topic 0; n = 3,616), captured more than one-fifth of all classified documents. Other dominant topics reinforced the clinical pattern: Substance Use & Alcohol Dependence (Topic 9; n = 295), PTSD & Veteran Populations (Topic 10; n = 264), and DNA Methylation & Epigenetics (Topic 4; n = 338). Topic coherence (C_V = 0.52) fell within the range typically reported for neural topic models on academic corpora; stability was verified through re-running the model with three different random seeds (Jaccard similarity > 0.85 for keyword sets).

The document-level DataMapPlot (Figure 4) provides a topographic view of the entire corpus in embedding space. Clinical clusters, particularly mental health and substance use, occupy densely populated central regions, while structural and equity-focused topics are positioned at the periphery with lower document density. The spatial arrangement is not random: it encodes the field’s associative structure, and it places social work’s core concerns (structural inequality, community context, anti-oppressive frameworks) at the margins of the knowledge field that social workers are increasingly expected to use.

The Word2Vec analysis quantifies this centrality. Mental health terms showed the strongest and most consistently increasing semantic proximity to ACE terms (era-averaged \Delta = +0.065), with cosine similarity rising from 0.230 in the 2006–2010 window to 0.343 in the 2020–2025 window (a point-to-point increase of +0.113), reflecting a deepening entanglement between ACE research and psychiatric vocabulary (Figure 6). Neuroscience proximity also increased (\Delta = +0.022), consistent with the growing biological embedding research program. Physical health (\Delta = +0.002) and substance use (\Delta = -0.001) associations remained stable. All four clinical domains showed positive or near-zero era shifts. The ACE concept, in its dominant discursive instantiation, is becoming more psychiatric, not less.

For child welfare practitioners, the implications are direct. When ACE research is concentrated in psychiatric and psychobiological journals, when the dominant topic captures one-fifth of all publications under the heading “depression,” and when the field’s semantic center of gravity pulls steadily toward mental health vocabulary, the practical consequence is a framing that positions abused and neglected children as future psychiatric patients. Featherstone et al. (2018) have argued that such framing displaces the social model of child protection, which locates the causes of harm not in the child’s risk profile but in the conditions of deprivation, discrimination, and institutional failure that surround the family. The computational evidence presented here suggests that displacement is not hypothetical; it is measurable in the field’s own language.

Structural Discourse: Selective Integration and Its Limits

The period-specific Word2Vec analysis revealed an asymmetry that goes to the heart of the field’s relationship with structural analysis. Comparing era-averaged cosine similarities between the early period (1998–2015) and the late period (2016–2025), structural determinant domains split sharply (Figure 5B). The poverty/inequality domain showed the most pronounced increase (\Delta = +0.020), indicating growing discursive integration of socioeconomic factors. Life course perspective (\Delta = +0.015) and education systems (\Delta = +0.014) also strengthened. Period-specific trajectories (Figure 5A) confirmed that poverty/inequality proximity followed a U-shaped pattern, declining from the earliest period before recovering and surpassing initial levels by 2020–2025.

By contrast, discrimination/racism showed the largest decline of any domain (\Delta = -0.060), indicating that despite growing scholarly attention to structural racism (Bernard et al. 2020), this vocabulary has not become integrated into the ACE discourse’s core semantic field. Housing/environment (\Delta = -0.053) and intergenerational (\Delta = -0.045) domains also decreased, while healthcare access remained stable (\Delta = 0.000).

This pattern demands naming. The ACE field has not rejected structural perspectives wholesale; it has absorbed them selectively. Socioeconomic gradients and developmental trajectories can be operationalized as individual-level covariates in clinical research designs: a child’s family income or developmental stage can be controlled for in a regression predicting depression scores. Structural racism and environmental justice cannot be so easily domesticated. They require a different kind of research design, one that treats the social structure as the unit of analysis, not the child. They require engagement with power, with the historical and ongoing production of racial inequality, and with the institutions (child protective services, schools, policing systems) that mediate the relationship between structural conditions and individual outcomes (Roberts 2002; Blackstock 2010).

The disproportion is stark. Mental health proximity gained at more than three times the rate of poverty/inequality proximity (\Delta = +0.065 vs. +0.020). If the “structural turn” in ACE research were as thoroughgoing as some advocates have claimed (Cronholm et al. 2015; Bernard et al. 2020), we would expect structural and clinical proximity gains to be at least comparable in magnitude. The documented disproportion suggests that structural language is being adopted in ways that do not require fundamental changes to how ACE research is designed and conducted. This is precisely the pattern that Conrad (2007) described in his analysis of medicalization: biomedical frameworks absorb social critique by translating structural claims into clinical variables, producing an appearance of integration without substantive epistemological change.

For social work, this finding is not merely an academic observation. The anti-oppressive practice tradition (Dominelli 2002) insists that attention to structural conditions is not optional but constitutive of ethical practice. If the knowledge base that informs child welfare decision-making systematically marginalizes racism while centering psychiatric diagnosis, then the evidence base itself becomes a mechanism of depoliticization. The social worker who consults the ACE literature for guidance on working with a Black family in a neighborhood shaped by redlining, disinvestment, and over-policing will find abundant research on the child’s risk of depression and relatively little on the structural conditions that produced the family’s circumstances in the first place.

Reshaping the Practice Frontier

The BERTopic dynamic trajectories (Figure 7) and macro-theme evolution (Figure 8) revealed three lifecycle patterns among the 48 topics, each with distinct implications for child welfare practice. Persistent topics (depression, substance use, PTSD) maintained steady presence throughout the study period, constituting the field’s settled knowledge base. Growth topics (trauma-informed care, epigenetics, intergenerational transmission) showed rapid post-2015 acceleration, consistent with new research programs that extend the ACE framework beyond its original clinical boundaries. Emerging topics appeared primarily in 2020–2025, including COVID-19 intersections with childhood adversity, where systematic reviews have examined potential shifts in maltreatment patterns during pandemic-related school closures and economic disruption, though the evidence remains mixed (Rubeis et al. 2025), and equity-focused frameworks that foreground structural racism.

The trajectory of trauma-informed care (TIC) is particularly consequential for child welfare. Topic 1 (Trauma-Informed Care & Implementation; n = 507) showed the steepest growth curve of any topic after 2015, a period that coincided with SAMHSA’s formal definition of trauma-informed approaches (Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration 2014) and state-level mandates such as California’s ACEs Aware screening initiative. TIC has become the child welfare field’s most visible practice innovation, adopted by child protective services agencies, schools, juvenile justice facilities, and foster care programs across the United States and beyond (Forkey et al. 2021).

But the semantic analysis introduces a complication. The rapid expansion of TIC discourse occurred alongside, not in opposition to, the decline in discrimination/racism semantic proximity (\Delta = -0.060). The two trends are not contradictory; they are complementary features of the same epistemic dynamic. TIC, as implemented in most child welfare settings, is a clinical framework: it trains practitioners to recognize trauma symptoms, to avoid re-traumatization, and to create therapeutic environments (Berliner and Kolko 2016). A decade after initial critiques, Lang and Hanson (2026) concluded that TIC implementation continues to outpace its evidence base, with practice adoption still running ahead of conceptual clarity and rigorous outcome evaluation. It does not, in its dominant instantiation, require practitioners to confront the structural conditions (systemic racism, poverty, housing instability) that produce trauma in the first place. Our data suggest that TIC’s rapid adoption may represent what Berliner and Kolko (2016) critiqued as an embrace of trauma-informed language and techniques without the deeper clinical rigor and political commitments needed to address structural causes of childhood adversity. If TIC in child welfare settings means training foster parents to recognize dissociation but not addressing why Black children are removed from their families at disproportionate rates (Roberts 2002; Feely and Bosk 2021), then the framework’s emancipatory potential is unrealized.

The temporal correspondence between policy events and topic lifecycles is instructive. The rapid growth of trauma-informed care after 2015 coincided with government-led mandates that required clinical screening and intervention; the growth of epigenetics and biological embedding research programs coincided with increased NIH investment in developmental neuroscience. Both growth trajectories reinforced the clinical core. By contrast, no comparable policy mechanism has driven equivalent growth in structural ACE research. There is no “structural racism screening mandate” analogous to California’s ACEs Aware initiative, and funding structures have not created equivalent incentives for research that locates adversity in political economy rather than household dysfunction.

Aggregating the 48 topics into nine macro-themes (Figure 8) revealed compositional shifts over time. Core Mental Health & Psychopathology has consistently dominated. Structural Inequalities & Marginalized Populations and Life Course, Resilience & Interventions gained increasing share after 2015, but from very small baselines. The disciplinary trajectory documented in the bibliometric data clarifies the institutional conditions of these shifts. Prior to 2010, the overwhelming majority of ACE publications appeared in clinical psychology, psychiatry, and child abuse journals. By 2020, the field had established footholds in public health, social work, pediatrics, and, to a lesser extent, sociology and criminology. This institutional migration maps onto the semantic changes: as ACE research entered social work and public health outlets, poverty/inequality and life course terminology gained semantic proximity. But the clinical journals did not lose their quantitative dominance, and the influx of publications in those outlets continued to anchor the field’s vocabulary in psychiatric and psychological language.

Discussion

The Pathologization Trap: Clinical Hegemony and Its Consequences for Child Welfare

The convergent evidence from bibliometric, semantic, and topic modeling analyses documents a systematic pattern: the ACE field’s conceptual expansion has proceeded along clinical axes more readily than structural ones, producing what we term selective absorption. The field has incorporated perspectives that extend its clinical logic (life course development, biological embedding, mental health comorbidity) more readily than those that challenge it (structural racism, political economy, Global South epistemologies). This selectivity is not simply an intellectual oversight; it is produced and maintained by the institutional structures within which ACE research is conducted: funding agencies that reward clinical outcomes, measurement instruments that channel inquiry toward individual-level phenomena, and disciplinary incentive systems that privilege psychiatric over sociological approaches.

For child welfare, the consequences of clinical hegemony are concrete. When the dominant ACE topic captures 3,616 documents under the heading “Mental Health & Depressive Symptoms,” when four of the five most prolific journals are psychiatric or psychobiological, and when the field’s semantic center of gravity pulls steadily toward mental health vocabulary (\Delta = +0.065), the practical result is a knowledge base that frames abused and neglected children primarily through the lens of individual psychopathology. Featherstone et al. (2018) have argued that this framing displaces the social model of child protection, which locates harm not in the child’s risk profile but in the conditions of deprivation that surround the family. Pelton (2014) documented that material deprivation, not parental psychopathology, is the strongest predictor of child maltreatment reports and out-of-home placements. Yet the ACE literature, as our analysis demonstrates, has moved further from poverty-focused analysis toward psychiatric framing, not closer.

The medicalization thesis (Conrad 2007) predicted this trajectory. Biomedical frameworks absorb social critique by translating structural claims into clinical language. The ACE field has followed this script with precision: “social determinants” become covariates in clinical models rather than objects of analysis in their own right; “health equity” becomes a discussion section paragraph rather than a research design principle; “structural racism” becomes a limitation rather than a variable. The persistence of the original ten-item checklist, which describes household events rather than structural conditions, creates methodological path dependency that anchors the field’s empirical practice in individual-level measurement regardless of researchers’ theoretical commitments. This is not a conspiracy; it is the routine operation of institutional structures that reward clinical productivity and make structural research more difficult to fund, publish, and translate into practice. Alternative models do exist: community-driven, non-medicalized approaches to mental health care that center housing, peer support, and daily living assistance over diagnostic categorization (Ford-Jones et al. 2026), but these models remain marginal in the ACE literature.

The anti-oppressive practice tradition in social work (Dominelli 2002) has always insisted that attention to structural conditions is constitutive of ethical practice, not an optional addendum. If the evidence base that informs child welfare decision-making systematically centers psychiatric diagnosis while marginalizing analysis of racism, poverty, and institutional power, then the evidence base itself becomes what Garrett (2017) terms a “welfare word”: language that appears progressive but narrows the field of vision to individual-level intervention. The computational evidence presented here suggests that this narrowing is not hypothetical. It is encoded in the distributional structure of the field’s vocabulary and reproduced through the institutional mechanisms (journal hierarchies, funding priorities, measurement conventions) that govern knowledge production.

The consequences extend beyond child welfare into the broader field of trauma, violence, and abuse research. In the child maltreatment tradition, violence and abuse are relational and contextual: they describe acts embedded in power relations, family systems, and institutional structures. Trauma, in this tradition, is one possible consequence of violence and abuse, not their defining feature. The ACE framework inverts this relationship. By centering the dose-response gradient between adverse experiences and health outcomes, it repositions violence and abuse as inputs to a biomedical risk model, and trauma as the primary output that warrants clinical attention. The semantic evidence quantifies this inversion: as mental health vocabulary drew closer to ACE terminology (\Delta = +0.065), the field’s discursive center shifted from understanding why violence occurs to measuring what it does to the individual body and brain. When violence is understood primarily as a cause of individual trauma, the logical intervention is therapy; when abuse is operationalized as a risk factor for future psychopathology, the logical response is screening. These are not wrong responses, but they are incomplete, leaving unaddressed the conditions that produce violence and the systems that fail to prevent abuse. The ACE field, as currently constituted, is better equipped to treat the consequences of violence than to prevent its occurrence, and better positioned to diagnose trauma than to dismantle the conditions that produce it.

Trauma-Informed Care: Between Emancipatory Promise and Structural Evasion

The rapid growth of the trauma-informed care topic (Topic 1; n = 507, steepest growth curve post-2015) represents the child welfare field’s most consequential practice development in the ACE era. TIC has been adopted by child protective services agencies, schools, juvenile justice facilities, and foster care programs across multiple countries. SAMHSA’s formal framework (Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration 2014) and state-level mandates such as California’s ACEs Aware initiative have created institutional demand that has driven both research production and practice implementation.

But the computational evidence introduces a pointed question. The semantic analysis shows that TIC’s rapid expansion occurred during the same period in which discrimination/racism vocabulary moved further from the ACE concept’s semantic core (\Delta = -0.060). This temporal coincidence is not causal, but it is revealing. TIC, as currently institutionalized in child welfare, is predominantly a clinical framework. Its four pillars (realize, recognize, respond, resist re-traumatization) describe practices within service delivery settings; they do not require practitioners or agencies to confront the structural conditions that produce childhood adversity in the first place (Berliner and Kolko 2016). A child welfare agency can adopt a trauma-informed approach, train its staff in trauma recognition, and redesign its intake procedures, all without addressing the racial disproportionality in its own caseload or the poverty that drives the majority of its referrals.

This is the risk of “superficial adoption”: the embrace of a framework’s techniques without its deeper political commitments. The BERTopic lifecycle data (Figure 7) show that TIC’s growth trajectory closely follows policy-driven institutional demand. Government mandates required clinical screening and intervention; research production responded. The growth of epigenetics and biological embedding followed a parallel trajectory, driven by increased NIH investment in developmental neuroscience. Both trajectories reinforced the clinical core. By contrast, no comparable policy mechanism has driven equivalent growth in structural ACE research. The structural topics that did grow (poverty/inequality in the Word2Vec analysis, equity-focused frameworks in the BERTopic results) did so from small baselines and without the institutional backing that propelled TIC.

For social work, this analysis suggests that TIC’s emancipatory potential remains largely unrealized. If trauma-informed care in child welfare means training foster parents to recognize dissociation but not asking why Black children are consistently overrepresented in foster care relative to their share of the child population (Roberts 2002), then the framework functions as a clinical overlay on an unreformed system. Blackstock (2010) demonstrated that Indigenous children in Canada are overrepresented in child welfare systems not because of higher rates of parental pathology but because of the structural legacy of colonialism, residential schools, and systematic under-funding of on-reserve child welfare services. Westby et al. (2025) extended this analysis by documenting how ACEs in Indigenous populations cannot be separated from intergenerational and historical trauma rooted in forced family separation and cultural erasure. A genuinely structural TIC would require confronting these conditions, not merely training practitioners to be “trauma-sensitive” within them. The data presented here suggest that the ACE field has not yet developed the conceptual infrastructure to support such a transformation.

Knowledge Geopolitics, Cultural Specificity, and Global Equity

The bibliometric analysis reveals a concentration of knowledge production that has direct implications for how childhood adversity is defined and addressed globally. The United States (51.7%), United Kingdom (10.6%), and Canada (7.6%) together account for nearly 70% of all ACE publications, while Africa, South America, and South/Southeast Asia remain substantially underrepresented. This concentration matters not only because it limits the empirical scope of ACE research but because it determines whose definitions of childhood adversity become authoritative.

The original ACE Study was conducted in a specific institutional and cultural context: a U.S. health maintenance organization serving predominantly white, middle-class adults. The ten-item questionnaire reflects the concerns and categories of that context: household dysfunction, parental substance use, domestic violence, sexual abuse. It does not include categories that might be more salient in other cultural contexts: exposure to armed conflict, forced migration, child labor, female genital cutting, caste-based discrimination, or the collective trauma of colonialism. When this framework is exported to the Global South as a “scientific” measure of childhood adversity, it carries with it a set of assumptions about what adversity is, how it should be measured, and what constitutes an appropriate response (Soares et al. 2015).

The risk is a form of epistemological imperialism: the imposition of a culturally specific framework as a universal standard. Child welfare systems in the Global South may be pressured to adopt ACE screening protocols developed for and validated in wealthy, English-speaking populations, even when those protocols poorly capture the forms of adversity that children in those contexts actually experience. The bibliometric concentration documented here is not neutral; it reflects and reproduces global inequalities in research infrastructure, academic publishing, and policy influence.

Indigenous populations present a particularly striking case. The ACE framework’s emphasis on individual-level household events maps poorly onto concepts of historical trauma, collective dispossession, and intergenerational effects of forced assimilation that are central to Indigenous understandings of childhood adversity (Blackstock 2010). Indigenous scholars have developed alternative frameworks (e.g., historical trauma theory, relational accountability models) that foreground collective and structural dimensions of harm. These frameworks appear in our corpus as emerging topics with small document counts and peripheral positions in the embedding space. The ACE field has not rejected them, but it has not absorbed them either; they exist at the margins, visible in the DataMapPlot (Figure 4) as small, low-density clusters far from the clinical center.

The LGBTQ+ population and gender nonconforming youth constitute another underrepresented group. Research on minority stress and its developmental consequences has expanded substantially in recent years, but this work remains only loosely connected to the ACE framework in our semantic analysis. The gap between the ACE literature’s stated commitment to “diversity” and its empirical attention to diverse populations is measurable in the distributional structure of the corpus.

Toward Reclaiming the Anti-Oppressive Tradition

The computational evidence presented here supports a clear conclusion: the ACE field has undergone a measurable epistemic transformation, but that transformation has been shaped by clinical institutional power in ways that marginalize structural analysis, depoliticize childhood adversity, and concentrate definitional authority in the Global North. For social work and child welfare, these findings are not merely descriptive; they are a call to action.

Three directions for reclamation follow from the evidence. First, the knowledge base itself must be restructured. This means not only adding structural variables to clinical models but reconsidering the causal architecture of those models so that structural conditions are treated as causes rather than covariates. The ACE questionnaire’s ten household items encode a theory of adversity (household dysfunction causes individual harm) that excludes structural causation by design. Expanded measures like the Philadelphia ACE framework (Cronholm et al. 2015) represent a step, and community-engaged approaches that foreground residents’ own understandings of adversity and resilience (Boyd et al. 2024) offer a model for how research designs might center structural conditions rather than individual risk scores. But genuine structural integration would require abandoning the individual-level dose-response model as the field’s organizing principle and developing research designs that treat neighborhoods, policies, and institutions as units of analysis.

Second, trauma-informed care must be reconnected to its emancipatory roots. The concept of “trauma-informed” originated in feminist and survivor-led movements that understood trauma as produced by power relations, not merely by discrete events. If TIC in child welfare is to be more than a clinical overlay, it must incorporate analysis of the structural conditions that produce childhood adversity: the racial disproportionality in child protective services (Roberts 2002), the concentration of child maltreatment reports in communities experiencing poverty (Pelton 2014), and the institutional mechanisms that surveil marginalized families while providing resources to affluent ones. The computational evidence suggests that the field has not yet taken this step; the rapid growth of TIC discourse has occurred without corresponding growth in structural racism analysis.

Third, the geographical and cultural monopoly over ACE knowledge production must be broken. The 70% concentration of ACE research in three English-speaking nations is not only a limitation but a form of epistemic injustice. Childhood adversity is a universal human experience, but its forms, determinants, and meanings are culturally and structurally specific. Research investment must be redirected toward low- and middle-income countries, Indigenous communities, and other populations whose experiences of adversity do not fit the ten-item mold. The computational methods demonstrated here, capable of detecting subtle semantic shifts across large corpora, can monitor whether the field’s geographical and conceptual diversification is real or rhetorical.

Diversity Discussion

The diversity characteristics of the reviewed literature are central to interpreting its findings. The ACE corpus is overwhelmingly produced by researchers affiliated with institutions in the Global North: the United States (51.7%), United Kingdom (10.6%), and Canada (7.6%) account for nearly 70% of all publications. Africa, South America, and South/Southeast Asia are substantially underrepresented. This geographical concentration shapes not only which populations are studied but which definitions of childhood adversity become authoritative, raising questions about the generalizability of ACE findings to communities and cultures outside the Anglophone research mainstream.

Within the reviewed literature, race-specific and ethnicity-specific ACE research has not permeated the field’s dominant discourse despite explicit advocacy for expanded frameworks (Cronholm et al. 2015; Bernard et al. 2020). Our Word2Vec analysis quantifies this gap: discrimination/racism vocabulary moved further from ACE terms (\Delta = -0.060) even as the field grew. Indigenous populations, for whom historical and intergenerational trauma carry deep conceptual resonance with the ACE framework, remain underrepresented (Blackstock 2010). Dedicated attention to LGBTQ+ populations and gender nonconforming youth is sparse, appearing only in emerging BERTopic clusters with small document counts, despite evidence that sexual minority and gender nonconforming adolescents experience ACEs at substantially elevated rates (Mittleman 2025; Baams 2018).

These gaps have direct implications for practice. Child welfare practitioners working with racially marginalized, immigrant, Indigenous, or LGBTQ+ families will find an evidence base that poorly represents the populations they serve. The ACE framework’s origins in a predominantly white, middle-class clinical sample continue to constrain its applicability. Research from the Global South may employ different vocabularies (“child maltreatment,” “family violence,” or culturally specific terms) that fall outside ACE-centric search strategies, producing systematic underrepresentation. Future research must invest in non-English-language databases, culturally grounded conceptual frameworks, and intersectional approaches that attend to how race, class, gender, sexuality, and geography interact in shaping childhood adversity and its consequences.

Limitations

Several limitations warrant acknowledgment. First, our corpus captures ACE-labelled literature specifically, excluding parallel research traditions on child maltreatment, family violence, community violence, and historical trauma that do not use ACE terminology. The clinical turn we document may therefore be a property of ACE-branded discourse rather than the childhood adversity field as a whole; research communities organized around “child maltreatment” or “family violence” may exhibit different conceptual structures. Second, our analysis is restricted to publications indexed in the Web of Science Core Collection, excluding grey literature, dissertations, and publications in non-indexed journals, potentially underrepresenting research from low-resource settings and reinforcing the very geographical concentration we critique. Second, Word2Vec models are sensitive to corpus size, and the earliest period (1998–2005, n = 237) may produce less stable embeddings than later periods. We addressed this by applying rigorous NLP preprocessing (lemmatization, domain-specific stopword removal, synonym consolidation) and by reporting era-averaged comparisons (1998–2015 vs. 2016–2025) rather than single-point contrasts, reducing the influence of any one period’s idiosyncrasies. Third, BERTopic’s topic assignments are influenced by hyperparameter choices; we used established defaults and automatic topic reduction, but alternative configurations might yield different topic structures. Fourth, our analysis captures discursive patterns rather than causal mechanisms: we document what the field discusses, not what it has empirically established. A field’s vocabulary can change without corresponding changes in research practice, and our methods cannot distinguish between rhetorical adoption and substantive operationalization of structural concepts. Fifth, country attribution relies on institutional affiliations, which may not reflect the study populations or contexts examined. Sixth, the paradoxical decline in discrimination/racism semantic proximity may partly reflect the formation of a distinct sub-field with specialized vocabulary (“structural racism,” “racial weathering,” “historical trauma”) that diverges from generic discrimination/racism terms in our target list, creating apparent semantic distance despite growing scholarly attention.

Conclusion

Over three decades, the ACE concept has evolved from a clinical-epidemiological instrument into a broad explanatory framework linking individual biography to social structure. This computational analysis, integrating bibliometric mapping, Word2Vec semantic analysis, and BERTopic dynamic topic modeling across 17,758 publications, provides systematic evidence of that evolution and reveals its internal contradictions. The field has grown exponentially, expanded geographically and disciplinarily, and progressively incorporated structural, life course, and intergenerational perspectives into its conceptual vocabulary. But the incorporation has been selective in ways that matter for child welfare practice.

The evidence converges on a single diagnosis: the ACE field has absorbed structural language without restructuring its clinical foundations. Mental health vocabulary gained semantic proximity to ACE terms at three times the rate of poverty/inequality vocabulary. Discrimination and racism moved further from the field’s semantic core even as scholars called for their inclusion. Trauma-informed care expanded rapidly as a practice framework without corresponding growth in analysis of the structural conditions that produce childhood trauma. And the knowledge base remains concentrated in three English-speaking nations whose institutional contexts poorly represent the global diversity of childhood adversity.

For social work and child welfare, these findings constitute both a diagnosis and a provocation. The anti-oppressive practice tradition has always insisted that structural analysis is not optional but constitutive of ethical practice (Dominelli 2002). The ACE framework, in its dominant discursive instantiation, does not meet this standard. It has become a tool for identifying at-risk individuals rather than challenging the systems that produce risk. Reclaiming the framework for social justice will require changes not only in how researchers write about ACEs but in how the field funds, designs, measures, and institutionalizes its research. The social worker in the underfunded urban office needs more than a risk score. She needs an evidence base that takes seriously the conditions of deprivation, discrimination, and institutional failure that brought the family to her desk, and a practice framework that equips her to challenge those conditions rather than merely document their consequences.

The computational methods demonstrated here can monitor whether the field’s evolution proceeds toward genuine structural integration or remains a case of conceptual expansion without epistemological transformation. The ACE field’s trajectory offers a broader lesson: the absorption of structural language into a clinical framework does not, by itself, produce structural analysis, and the appearance of conceptual progress can mask the persistence of clinical authority.
