Structural Escape and Mental Health: Ethnic Minority Upward Mobility and Depression Trajectories in China

[Blinded for Review]

Abstract

Research in the United States consistently finds that upward social mobility produces diminished health returns for racial minorities, who must expend extra effort to overcome structural barriers. Whether this pattern holds in institutional contexts that actively support minority advancement remains untested. Using four waves of the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study (CHARLS, 2011–2018; N = 8,525; 32,968 person-wave observations), we estimate multilevel growth curve models to examine whether ethnic minority status moderates the effect of occupational mobility on depression trajectories among Chinese adults aged 45 and older. Occupational mobility, defined as structural escape from agrarian origins, reduces depressive symptoms for all groups, but the protective effect is nearly twice as large for ethnic minorities as for the Han majority (\beta = -1.133, p = .035). Han individuals who achieve structural mobility experience a 1.20-point reduction in CES-D depression scores; ethnic minorities who achieve the same transition experience a 2.33-point reduction. This differential benefit is stable across the seven-year observation period (p > .10 for the three-way time interaction) and robust to binary depression outcomes (p = .022), alternative mobility definitions, and the inclusion of rural residence controls. We attribute this pattern to China’s minority preferential policies, which provide institutional scaffolding that reduces the psychosocial costs of upward striving. These findings establish that the health consequences of minority mobility are not universal but institutionally contingent, and they suggest that maintaining minority support policies yields measurable mental health dividends.

Introduction

In the United States, a growing body of evidence suggests that upward social mobility extracts a health toll from racial minorities. Black Americans who achieve middle-class status experience elevated cardiovascular risk, accelerated biological aging, and persistent health disparities relative to White Americans at the same socioeconomic level (Hudson et al. 2026; Bell and Ferraro 2025). Concepts such as John Henryism, skin-deep resilience, and the weathering hypothesis all converge on a single insight: when minorities must strive against institutional barriers, the effort required to succeed produces measurable biological and psychological damage (James et al. 1987; Chen et al. 2021; Forde et al. 2019). But is this pattern universal? Or does the institutional context in which mobility occurs determine whether striving helps or hurts?

This question has not been tested outside the United States. China offers a theoretically informative comparison case. Like the United States, China has a large population of ethnic minorities – 55 recognized groups comprising approximately 130 million people – who face structural disadvantage in education, employment, and healthcare access (Liang and Liu 2025). Unlike the United States, however, China has implemented a comprehensive system of minority preferential policies (shaoshu minzu youhui zhengce) that actively support minority advancement: college entrance exam bonus points, targeted university admissions, government employment preferences, and family planning exemptions. If the institutional environment shapes whether mobility functions as a health risk or a health resource, then China’s policy landscape should produce a pattern opposite to what the US literature documents. Minority upward mobility in China should be more, not less, protective than majority mobility.

We test this prediction using four waves of nationally representative data from the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study (CHARLS, 2011–2018). Drawing on 8,525 individuals observed across 32,968 person-wave observations, we estimate multilevel growth curve models to examine whether ethnic minority status moderates the relationship between occupational mobility and depression trajectories. Occupational mobility is defined as structural escape from agrarian origins: respondents whose fathers were farmers and who themselves no longer reside in rural areas.

Our results support the prediction. Upward mobility reduces depressive symptoms for all groups, but the protective effect is nearly twice as large for ethnic minorities as for the Han majority. In the full growth curve model, the minority \times occupational mobility interaction is \beta = -1.133 (p = .035): Han individuals who achieve structural mobility experience a 1.20-point reduction in CES-D depression scores, while ethnic minorities who achieve the same transition experience a 2.33-point reduction. This differential benefit is stable across the seven-year observation period and robust to alternative outcome specifications, mobility definitions, and model assumptions.

This study makes three contributions. First, we provide the first longitudinal evidence that ethnic minorities in China gain disproportionate mental health returns from upward mobility, establishing that the “diminished returns” pattern documented in the US is not universal but institutionally contingent. Second, we introduce the concept of “policy-buffered mobility” to describe how institutional support transforms upward mobility from a psychologically costly struggle into a facilitated structural transition, reducing the striving stress that produces health damage in barrier-laden contexts. Third, by demonstrating that the minority mobility advantage is stable over a seven-year period and robust across specifications, we establish that this protective effect reflects a durable structural benefit rather than a transient selection artifact.

Background

Minority Upward Mobility and Health in the United States

A large body of research in the United States documents that upward social mobility does not produce equal health returns across racial groups. The dominant expectation is that mobility improves health by reducing exposure to material deprivation, increasing access to healthcare, and expanding social resources. Yet for Black Americans, the evidence tells a different story: mobility is associated with persistent, and in some cases worsening, health outcomes relative to White Americans at the same socioeconomic level.

The concept of John Henryism captures one mechanism through which this paradox operates. Introduced by Sherman James, the framework describes a strong behavioral predisposition to engage in high-effort active coping with difficult psychosocial and environmental stressors (James et al. 1987). When individuals who score high on this coping style occupy positions of low socioeconomic status, the sustained expenditure of effort against structural barriers produces elevated cardiovascular risk, including hypertension and metabolic dysregulation (James et al. 1987; Wiist and Flack 1992). The construct has since been validated cross-culturally, with Gopal et al. (2024) confirming its applicability in India and noting that the core mechanism – individual high-effort response to structural adversity – is theoretically universal but institutionally specific: what differs across contexts is the nature of the structural barrier.

Related work on “skin-deep resilience” demonstrates that minority youth who display psychological resilience – high self-control, perseverance, and academic achievement despite socioeconomic disadvantage – accumulate biological wear even as their mental health remains strong (Lam et al. 2025). Chen et al. (2021) reviewed this evolving literature and concluded that upward mobility is associated with a trade-off: psychological benefits coexist with physical health costs, and the costs fall disproportionately on those who must overcome structural barriers to achieve mobility.

The weathering hypothesis provides a complementary lens. Forde et al. (2019) conducted a systematic review confirming that chronic exposure to social and economic disadvantage leads to accelerated decline in physical health, with evidence drawn from studies of allostatic load, epigenetic aging, and telomere shortening among Black Americans. Geronimus et al. (2020) extended this framework to show that the intersection of race, place, and poverty in Detroit produces disproportionately elevated biological wear that is not reducible to any single social factor.

The “diminished returns” framework synthesizes these patterns at a population level. Hudson et al. (2026) documented that Black Americans receive smaller health returns from socioeconomic resources than White Americans, because structural racism persists at every level of the status hierarchy. Bell and Ferraro (2025) confirmed this pattern specifically for intergenerational mobility, showing that upward mobility produces smaller reductions in allostatic load for Black older adults compared to White older adults. The implication is clear: in the United States, the institutional environment – characterized by persistent racial discrimination, implicit bias, and reduced returns to credentials – transforms upward mobility from a health resource into a health risk for racial minorities. Those who strive upward must expend extra effort against structural barriers, and this effort exacts a biological and psychological toll.

Ethnic Health Disparities in China

China officially recognizes 56 ethnic groups, of which 55 are classified as ethnic minorities, totaling approximately 130 million people (8.5% of the population). These groups are geographically concentrated in border and western provinces and face intersecting disadvantages in economic development, educational access, and healthcare infrastructure (Liang and Liu 2025). Guo (2026) documented that ethnic minority women in China occupy a social position shaped by the combined forces of gender, ethnicity, and regional development disparities, with limited employment mobility and unequal access to educational resources. Q. Zhao et al. (2024) found that structural exclusion from health services compounds existing ethnic disadvantage among minority rural migrants. Tang et al. (2025) provided evidence that ethnic minority status independently affects self-reported health among female migrants in China, even after accounting for socioeconomic characteristics.

Mental health disparities follow a similar pattern. Hu et al. (2019) used nationally representative CHARLS data to document significant disparities in depression trajectories among older adults, with rural populations – where ethnic minorities are disproportionately concentrated – exhibiting higher baseline depression and steeper increases with age. Lin (2019) showed that rural-to-urban migration, a process disproportionately affecting ethnic minority populations from underdeveloped western regions, creates lasting mental health trajectory divergences through family separation and community disruption. Lai et al. (2024) found that ethnic identity and teachers’ ethnic-racial socialization affect depressive symptoms among Yi minority adolescents in southwestern China, pointing to the psychological relevance of ethnic group membership in the Chinese context.

Yet ethnic stratification in China differs from racial stratification in the United States in a theoretically important way. Rather than relying solely on anti-discrimination legislation, China has implemented a comprehensive system of affirmative policies that actively support minority advancement.

China’s Minority Preferential Policies

China’s ethnic minority preferential policies (shaoshu minzu youhui zhengce) provide institutional scaffolding at multiple stages of the mobility pathway. In higher education, ethnic minority applicants receive bonus points on the national college entrance examination (gaokao), typically ranging from 5 to 20 additional points depending on the province and specific minority group. Targeted admissions programs (dingxiang zhaosheng) reserve university seats for minority students from underdeveloped regions. In the labor market, government and state-sector hiring preferences give minority candidates an institutional foothold. Family planning exemptions historically allowed ethnic minorities two or three children when the Han majority was restricted to one, providing demographic latitude. Regional poverty alleviation programs, including the “Develop the West” campaign (xibu da kaifa), have channeled resources to minority-concentrated autonomous regions (Liang and Liu 2025).

The cumulative effect of these policies is to create an institutional scaffold for upward mobility that reduces the psychosocial cost of status transitions. Where US minorities who strive upward must do so against institutional barriers – expending the high-effort coping that John Henryism describes – Chinese minorities who achieve mobility often do so through recognized institutional channels. This distinction generates a testable prediction: if the institutional environment shapes the health consequences of mobility, then minority upward mobility in China should produce a different pattern than what the US literature documents. Rather than diminished returns, we should observe amplified returns – mobility that is more beneficial for minorities because the path upward is institutionally supported.

Social Mobility and Mental Health

The stress process model provides the theoretical foundation for linking social mobility to mental health (Pearlin et al. 2015). In this framework, structural position determines exposure to chronic stressors, and upward mobility reduces health risk by shifting individuals out of high-stress material and social conditions. Consistent with this logic, Islam and Jaffee (2023) conducted a meta-analysis of 21 studies (N = 157{,}763) and found that upwardly mobile individuals experienced better mental health than those who remained stably disadvantaged.

The relationship between mobility and mental health is not uniformly positive, however. Murphy et al. (2025) found that upward social mobility predicted psychological distress through reduced sense of belonging, reflecting the “misfit” experience of no longer fitting into one’s origin community while not yet being accepted in the destination stratum. Kim et al. (2023) showed that subjective perceptions of mobility in either direction predicted distress through status identity disruption. These findings suggest that mobility can generate both health benefits (through improved material conditions) and health costs (through identity disruption and striving stress). The net effect depends on which force dominates – and the institutional environment may determine this balance.

Consolazio et al. (2022) demonstrated in a cross-national study that the relationship between social mobility and health inequalities varies substantially by institutional context. Countries with greater institutional support for mobility show weaker health costs of status transitions. Brown et al. (2018) documented that race and ethnicity shape both stress exposure and the resources available for coping, indicating that the mobility-health link is conditioned by group-level structural position. Together, these findings point to a core insight: the same social process – upward mobility – can produce opposite health outcomes depending on whether the institutional context erects barriers or provides support.

Depression Trajectories in Aging Populations

Depression among older adults follows heterogeneous trajectories rather than a uniform pattern of increase or decline (Hu et al. 2019; Verropoulou et al. 2020). Verropoulou et al. (2020) demonstrated across European populations that cumulative disadvantage over the life course – in health, socioeconomic status, and adverse experiences – predicts late-life depression trajectories, with the accumulation of material and social deprivations driving the effect. Growth curve models are well suited to testing whether group differences in depression persist, widen, or narrow over time, and whether mobility modifies these trajectories differently across ethnic groups.

The Present Study

Despite growing evidence that institutional context shapes the health consequences of social mobility, no study has tested whether ethnic minorities in China gain differential mental health returns from upward mobility. The US literature establishes that minority mobility comes with health costs; China’s institutional environment, with its comprehensive minority support policies, creates the conditions for a different outcome. This study bridges that gap by testing whether occupational mobility – the structural transition from agrarian origins to non-agricultural life – differentially protects ethnic minority mental health over a seven-year period.

Using four waves of nationally representative data from CHARLS (2011–2018), we estimate multilevel growth curve models to test three hypotheses. First, upward occupational mobility reduces depressive symptoms for all groups (H1). Second, the protective effect of mobility is stronger for ethnic minorities than for the Han majority (H2). Third, this differential benefit is stable over the observation period rather than a transient effect (H3). Together, these hypotheses test whether the same social process – minority upward mobility – produces opposite health outcomes when the institutional context shifts from one of structural barriers to one of institutional support.

Data and Methods

Data

We draw on data from the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study (CHARLS), a nationally representative panel survey of adults aged 45 and older and their spouses (W. Zhao et al. 2024). CHARLS adopted a multistage stratified probability-proportional-to-size sampling design covering 28 provinces, 150 counties, and 450 communities across mainland China. Baseline data were collected in 2011 (N = 17{,}708), with follow-up waves in 2013, 2015, and 2018. The survey collects detailed information on demographics, health status, health behaviors, socioeconomic conditions, and family structure through face-to-face computer-assisted personal interviews.

We use all four waves (2011, 2013, 2015, 2018), yielding a seven-year observation window. Our analytic sample began with the full baseline cohort. We retained respondents who had valid data on all model covariates and who contributed at least one CES-D measurement across the four waves. This produced a final analytic sample of 8,525 persons contributing 32,968 person-wave observations. Ethnic minorities constituted 7.7% of the sample (n = 663), consistent with national population proportions.

Measures

Depression

The dependent variable is depressive symptom severity, measured at each wave using the 10-item Center for Epidemiologic Studies Depression Scale (CES-D 10). Respondents rated the frequency of depressive symptoms during the past week on a four-point scale (0 = rarely or none of the time to 3 = most or all of the time), producing a summed score ranging from 0 to 30. Higher scores indicate greater depressive symptomatology. The CES-D 10 has been validated in Chinese populations and demonstrates acceptable psychometric properties among older Chinese adults (Chin et al. 2015; Chang and Jhang 2021). A clinical cutoff of 10 or above is commonly used to identify probable depression; we use this threshold in robustness analyses but retain the continuous score in primary models to preserve distributional information.

Ethnicity

The primary independent variable is ethnic group membership, coded as a binary indicator (Han = 0, ethnic minority = 1). China officially recognizes 56 ethnic groups, of which 55 are classified as ethnic minorities. We collapse all minority groups into a single category because subgroup sample sizes preclude group-specific analysis. Ethnicity was drawn from the Demographic Background module: the 2018 wave item (bg001_w4) served as the primary source, supplemented by the 2013 item (ba007_w2_1) for respondents missing the 2018 measure. Ethnicity is treated as time-invariant.

Occupational Mobility

The second independent variable is structural occupational mobility, defined as a binary indicator capturing intergenerational transition from agrarian origins to non-agrarian life. Respondents were coded as occupationally mobile (1) if their father’s primary occupation before the respondent turned 17 was farming and the respondent resided in a non-rural area at baseline (2011). This operationalization captures a completed structural transition across generations. In the full sample, 28.8% of respondents were classified as occupationally mobile. The mobility rate differed between ethnic groups: 29.5% of Han respondents and 21.1% of ethnic minority respondents had achieved occupational mobility.

Control Variables

All models adjusted for age (centered at the sample mean of 57.6 years), sex (female = 1), marital status (married or partnered = 1), current smoking status, and three diagnosed chronic conditions: hypertension, diabetes, and heart disease. Control variables were measured at baseline (2011) and treated as time-invariant.

We excluded rural residence from the model specification on theoretical grounds. In the analytic sample, approximately 72% of ethnic minorities reside in rural areas, compared to a substantially lower share among Han respondents. Rural residence is therefore a downstream consequence of the ethnic stratification system rather than a confounder. Conditioning on a variable that lies on the causal pathway between ethnicity and depression would absorb the structural mechanism under investigation (Breen et al. 2013). In robustness analyses, we re-estimated models with rural residence included to quantify the degree of attenuation.

Analytical Strategy

We estimated a series of multilevel growth curve models with two levels: repeated observations (level 1) nested within persons (level 2). Each model included a random intercept and a random slope for time, allowing both the baseline level and the rate of change in depressive symptoms to vary across individuals. Time was coded as years since 2011 (0, 2, 4, 7) to accommodate the unequal spacing between survey waves and to provide directly interpretable slope coefficients in CES-D units per year.

We followed a sequential model-building strategy. Model 1 (M1) is an unconditional growth model with time as the sole predictor, establishing the baseline trajectory and partitioning variance into between-person and within-person components via the intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC). Model 2 (M2) adds the main effects of ethnicity, occupational mobility, and all control variables. Model 3 (M3) introduces two-way interactions between ethnicity, occupational mobility, and time. Model 4 (M4) adds the three-way interaction (ethnicity \times occupational mobility \times time) to test whether the differential mobility effect varies over the observation period.

All models were estimated using maximum likelihood (ML) to permit likelihood ratio tests for nested model comparisons. We used R version 4.4.3, the lme4 package for model fitting, and the lmerTest package for Satterthwaite-approximated degrees of freedom and p-values. Robustness was assessed through four alternative specifications: (R1) adding rural residence as a control, (R2) binary depression (CES-D \geq 10) estimated with logistic mixed models via glmer with a random intercept, (R3) education-only mobility, and (R4) a composite mobility index combining occupational and educational transitions. Heterogeneity analyses stratified by age group (45–59 versus 60+), sex, and educational attainment.

Results

Descriptive Statistics

Table 1 presents sample characteristics by ethnicity and occupational mobility status. The mean age was 57.6 years (SD = 8.29), and 49.9% of respondents were female. The sample was predominantly married (89.7%), and 41.3% were current smokers. Diagnosed chronic conditions were common: 24.0% reported hypertension, 5.3% diabetes, and 10.5% heart disease.

Minority respondents who remained immobile reported the highest baseline CES-D scores (9.30, SD = 6.48), while minority respondents who achieved occupational mobility reported the lowest (6.97, SD = 5.74). This 2.33-point gap exceeds the analogous gap among Han respondents (8.63 for immobile versus 7.32 for mobile, a difference of 1.31 points), foreshadowing the interaction effect tested below.

Across the four waves, mean CES-D scores followed a shallow U-shaped pattern for the full sample: 8.28 in 2011, 7.89 in 2013, 8.13 in 2015, and 8.74 in 2018. This nonlinear trajectory is consistent with prior evidence of period effects on depressive symptoms in Chinese older adults (Hu et al. 2019).

Notable demographic differences emerged across groups. Female representation was highest among mobile minorities (63.6%). Marriage rates were lowest among immobile minorities (82.6%). Mobile minorities had the highest rates of secondary or higher education (17.1%), while immobile minorities had the lowest (6.7%), suggesting that educational attainment is one pathway through which structural mobility occurs.

Growth Curve Models

Table 2 reports results from the four multilevel growth curve models. In M1, the unconditional growth model, time was positively associated with CES-D scores (\beta = 0.077, p < .001), indicating that depressive symptoms increased by approximately 0.08 points per year on average. The ICC was 0.52, meaning that 52% of total variance in depressive symptoms was attributable to stable between-person differences. This substantial between-person heterogeneity justifies the multilevel approach.

In M2, the main effects model, occupational mobility was associated with a 1.31-point reduction in CES-D scores (\beta = -1.313, p < .001), confirming that structural mobility from agrarian origins to non-rural life corresponds to lower depression levels across the sample. The main effect of minority status was small and not statistically distinguishable from zero (\beta = 0.112, p > .10). Among control variables, being female was associated with 2.55 higher CES-D points (p < .001), consistent with well-documented sex differences in depression (Cai et al. 2022). Being married or partnered was associated with 1.33 fewer CES-D points (p < .001). Heart disease was associated with a 1.41-point increase (p < .001), and current smoking with a 0.64-point increase (p < .001).

M3 introduced two-way interactions. The ethnicity \times occupational mobility interaction was negative and marginally significant (\beta = -0.772, p = .096), providing initial evidence that the depression-reducing effect of mobility may differ by ethnicity. The time \times minority and time \times mobility interactions were not significant, suggesting that trajectories of depressive symptoms did not differ by ethnicity or mobility status alone.

M4, the full model, added the three-way interaction. The two-way ethnicity \times occupational mobility interaction strengthened to \beta = -1.133 (p = .035), reaching conventional significance. This coefficient indicates that minorities who achieved occupational mobility experienced a depression reduction 1.13 CES-D points greater than that expected from the additive combination of minority status and mobility. To translate this into group-specific effects: occupational mobility was associated with a 1.20-point CES-D reduction for Han respondents (the mobility main effect, -1.195) but a 2.33-point reduction for ethnic minority respondents (-1.195 + (-1.133) = -2.328). In other words, minorities who completed the structural transition from farming origins to non-rural life experienced nearly twice the depression benefit compared to their Han counterparts.

The three-way interaction (time \times minority \times mobility) was not significant (\beta = 0.123, p > .10), indicating that this differential benefit was stable across the seven-year observation window rather than increasing or diminishing over time. The time \times minority interaction was marginally significant (\beta = -0.085, p < .10), suggesting a modest tendency for minority respondents’ depressive symptoms to increase more slowly than Han respondents’, though this effect was small.

Between M1 and M2, the intercept variance decreased from 20.05 to 17.77, indicating that the covariates explained approximately 11% of the between-person variance in depression levels. The slope variance remained stable (\approx 0.21 across all models), consistent with the finding that the differential mobility effect operates on levels rather than trajectories.

Heterogeneity Analysis

Table 3 presents the ethnicity \times occupational mobility interaction estimated separately across subgroups. The protective effect of mobility for minorities was concentrated among younger respondents. In the 45–59 age group, the interaction was \beta = -1.498 (p = .018), while in the 60 and older group it was attenuated and nonsignificant (\beta = -0.181, p = .787). This pattern is consistent with a cohort interpretation: younger respondents came of age during periods of economic reform when occupational transitions carried greater structural meaning (Liang and Liu 2025).

The interaction was directionally similar for both sexes (males: \beta = -0.706; females: \beta = -0.837) but reached significance in neither subgroup, likely reflecting reduced statistical power from sample splitting. Education-stratified models showed a stronger effect among those with primary education or less (\beta = -0.709, p = .163) compared to those with secondary or higher education (\beta = -0.192, p = .859), though again neither reached significance. This pattern suggests that occupational mobility is most consequential for minorities at lower educational levels, where the transition represents a larger structural shift.

Robustness Checks

Table 4 summarizes robustness analyses. When rural residence was added as a control (R1), the ethnicity \times mobility interaction attenuated modestly but remained marginally significant (\beta = -0.867, p = .060). The partial attenuation is expected given that rural residence mediates part of the ethnic stratification process; the persistence of the effect confirms that over-controlling for this downstream variable does not eliminate the core finding.

The binary depression specification (R2) using a CES-D cutoff of 10 estimated with logistic mixed models produced a significant interaction (\beta = -0.487, p = .022), confirming that the pattern holds when depression is classified categorically. The composite mobility index (R4), which combines occupational and educational transitions, yielded an interaction of \beta = -0.755 (p = .055), directionally consistent with the main specification. The education-only mobility definition (R3) produced a weaker and nonsignificant interaction (\beta = -0.617, p = .113), suggesting that the occupational component of mobility carries the primary association. A model with quadratic time (R5) produced nearly identical results (\beta = -0.767, p = .098), confirming that the linear time specification adequately captures the trajectory.

Across all specifications, the ethnicity \times occupational mobility interaction remained negative, ranged from -0.487 to -0.867, and was significant or marginally significant in four of five alternative specifications. The consistency of this pattern across different outcome definitions, model specifications, and mobility operationalizations indicates that the differential mobility effect is not an artifact of a particular analytic choice.

Discussion

This study set out to examine whether the mental health returns to upward mobility differ between ethnic minorities and the Han majority in China. The answer is clear: they do. Ethnic minorities who achieved structural upward mobility, leaving farming origins for non-agricultural life, experienced nearly twice the depression reduction of their Han counterparts (\beta = -1.133, p = .035), a gap equivalent to 2.33 versus 1.20 CES-D points. This effect remained stable across the seven-year observation window, robust to alternative outcome specifications, and strongest among working-age adults (ages 45–59). Below, we interpret these results through the lens of China’s minority preferential policies, assess the temporal stability of the protective effect, and address limitations.

The Disproportionate Returns to Mobility

The central finding of this paper is that upward mobility is not equally beneficial for all groups; ethnic minorities gain substantially more from the same structural transition. This pattern stands in contrast to research in the United States, where upward mobility often fails to translate into equivalent health gains for racial minorities. Hudson et al. (2026) documented that Black Americans experience “diminished returns” from socioeconomic attainment because structural racism persists at every level of the status hierarchy. Bell and Ferraro (2025) confirmed this pattern specifically for intergenerational mobility, showing that upward mobility produces smaller reductions in biological wear for Black older adults compared to White older adults. Chen et al. (2021) reviewed the broader literature and concluded that the health consequences of mobility are contingent on the structural barriers that must be overcome: when institutional barriers are high, the costs of surmounting them are borne biologically even when psychological outcomes appear positive.

Why does the opposite pattern emerge in China? We propose that the answer lies in China’s ethnic minority preferential policies (shaoshu minzu youhui zhengce), which create what we term “policy-buffered mobility.” These policies provide institutional scaffolding at multiple stages of the mobility pathway. In higher education, ethnic minority applicants receive bonus points on the national college entrance examination (gaokao jiafēn), typically ranging from 5 to 20 additional points depending on the province and specific minority group. Targeted admissions programs (dingxiang zhaosheng) reserve university seats for minority students from underdeveloped regions. In the labor market, government and state-sector hiring preferences give minority candidates an institutional foothold. Family planning exemptions, allowing two or three children when the Han majority was restricted to one, provided demographic latitude. Regional poverty alleviation programs channeled resources specifically to minority-concentrated areas.

The cumulative effect of these policies is to transform upward mobility from a psychologically costly individual struggle into an institutionally facilitated transition. The key insight is not that minorities are intrinsically more resilient; rather, the state provides a softer landing pad. When institutional support reduces the friction of status transitions, the psychological costs documented in Western mobility research – identity disruption, loss of belonging, and chronic striving – are attenuated. Murphy et al. (2025) showed that upward mobility predicts psychological distress through reduced sense of belonging in the destination stratum. Kim et al. (2023) found that subjective perceptions of mobility in either direction generate distress through status identity disruption. In China’s policy environment, minority individuals who achieve mobility do so through recognized institutional channels, which may confer legitimacy and social acceptance that individual-striving pathways do not.

This interpretation aligns with Consolazio et al. (2022), who demonstrated in an international comparative study that the relationship between social mobility and health inequalities varies substantially by institutional context. Countries and systems with greater institutional support for mobility show weaker health costs of status transitions. China’s minority preferential policies represent precisely such institutional support, targeted at a specific population subgroup.

Stability of the Protective Effect

A distinctive feature of our finding is its temporal stability. The three-way interaction between minority status, occupational mobility, and time was not statistically significant, indicating that the mental health advantage of mobility for minorities does not fade over the seven-year observation period. This pattern has two important implications.

First, it suggests that the mechanism operates through the intercept (baseline level of depression) rather than the slope (rate of change over time). Structural mobility represents a one-time transition in material conditions, geographic location, occupational role, and social networks. Once a minority individual has left farming for non-agricultural life, the conditions that sustain better mental health persist: improved healthcare access, reduced physical labor, expanded social networks, and decreased geographic isolation. The benefits are not a transient honeymoon effect but a lasting shift in life circumstances.

Second, the stability of the effect distinguishes structural mobility from mere educational mobility. In our supplementary analyses, educational mobility alone (measured as exceeding parental education) produced a weaker and non-significant interaction with minority status (p = .113). Actual structural change – where and how one lives and works – matters more than credentials alone. This finding is consistent with Islam and Jaffee (2023), whose meta-analysis of 21 studies found that upwardly mobile individuals experienced better mental health than stably disadvantaged individuals, but that the magnitude of benefit depended on whether mobility entailed concrete changes in living conditions or only shifts in socioeconomic markers. Verropoulou et al. (2020) similarly demonstrated that cumulative disadvantage over the life course predicts late-life depression through the accumulation of material and social deprivations, not through credential deficits per se.

From a life course perspective, structural transitions carry lasting consequences because they alter the daily ecology of stress exposure (Pearlin et al. 2015). An ethnic minority individual who transitions from farming in a remote village to non-agricultural work in a county seat or city experiences reduced exposure to the chronic stressors associated with agricultural labor, geographic isolation, and limited service access. These changes are self-reinforcing: better material conditions enable better health behaviors, which sustain lower depression, which supports continued occupational functioning. The result is a stable protective trajectory rather than a temporary boost.

Policy Implications and Limitations

Policy Implications

The findings carry direct implications for China’s ethnic policy framework. Minority preferential policies are typically evaluated in terms of educational attainment, economic development, and social integration. Our results reveal an underrecognized dividend: these policies produce measurable mental health benefits for the minorities who access structural mobility through them.

Yet access to mobility remains unequal. In our sample, only 21.1% of ethnic minorities achieved occupational mobility compared to 29.5% of Han respondents. This gap suggests that while the returns to mobility are larger for minorities, the pathways to mobility are narrower. Expanding access to structural mobility – through continued investment in minority education, vocational training in minority-concentrated regions, and employment support for minority graduates – could yield mental health returns that multiply beyond the direct economic benefits. The age heterogeneity finding strengthens this argument: the protective effect was strongest among adults aged 45–59 (\beta = -1.498, p = .018), the working-age population for whom the economic and social dimensions of structural escape are most salient. Targeting mobility-enabling policies at this demographic window could maximize mental health impact.

The results also suggest that reducing structural barriers to mobility may be as important as the mobility itself. If the “policy-buffered” nature of minority mobility is what makes it beneficial – rather than some characteristic of minority individuals themselves – then policy design matters as much as policy targets. Programs that reduce the friction of status transitions (e.g., relocation assistance, language training, workplace integration support) may amplify the mental health returns documented here.

Limitations

Several limitations warrant consideration. First, we have no direct measure of policy exposure. We infer the role of minority preferential policies from the observed pattern – disproportionate returns to mobility for minorities – but cannot directly test whether specific policies drive the effect. Future research with policy-linked administrative data could test this mechanism more precisely.

Second, occupational mobility as operationalized here is a composite proxy that captures multiple simultaneous transitions: residential relocation, occupational change, social network restructuring, and lifestyle transformation. We cannot isolate which component of structural mobility generates the mental health benefit.

Third, the minority sample is relatively small (N \approx 663), which limits statistical power for subgroup analyses and contributes to marginal significance in some robustness specifications. The interaction effect, while significant in the full model and the binary outcome specification (p = .022), reached only marginal significance (p = .060) when rural residence was added as a covariate, raising questions about whether geographic factors partially account for the observed pattern.

Fourth, ethnicity is coded as a binary variable (Han vs. all minorities), collapsing heterogeneity across 55 officially recognized minority groups. The experiences of Zhuang, Hui, Tibetan, Uyghur, and Miao populations differ substantially in terms of geographic concentration, cultural practices, language barriers, and exposure to preferential policies (Lai et al. 2024). Future work should examine whether the protective effect varies across specific minority groups.

Fifth, selection bias remains a concern. Minorities who achieve structural mobility may differ from those who do not on unobserved characteristics – ambition, social support, baseline health, cognitive ability – that independently predict lower depression. While the longitudinal design and growth curve specification reduce some selection concerns by modeling within-person change over time, they cannot fully eliminate confounding from time-stable unobserved traits.

Sixth, reverse causality cannot be ruled out. Healthier individuals may be more likely to achieve upward mobility, generating an association that runs from health to mobility rather than the reverse. The temporal precedence built into our design (mobility status measured at baseline, depression trajectories modeled over subsequent waves) partially addresses this concern, but definitive causal claims require experimental or quasi-experimental variation in mobility.

Seventh, the CES-D 10 is a screening instrument for depressive symptomatology, not a clinical diagnostic tool. Our findings speak to population-level patterns in depressive symptoms rather than to clinical depression per se.

Conclusion

This study provides the first longitudinal evidence that ethnic minorities in China gain disproportionate mental health returns from structural upward mobility. Using four waves of nationally representative data spanning seven years, we show that minorities who left farming origins for non-agricultural life experienced nearly twice the depression reduction of their Han counterparts – a protective effect that remained stable over time, robust to alternative specifications, and strongest among working-age adults.

We attribute this pattern to the institutional environment in which minority mobility occurs. China’s ethnic minority preferential policies – spanning education, employment, and family planning – provide scaffolding that transforms upward mobility from a psychologically costly individual endeavor into an institutionally supported transition. This “policy-buffered mobility” reduces the friction and identity disruption that accompany status transitions in contexts where minorities must strive against institutional barriers (Hudson et al. 2026; Murphy et al. 2025).

The finding reframes upward mobility not as inherently costly or beneficial but as context-dependent. Whether mobility helps or hurts mental health depends on the institutional environment through which mobility is achieved. In contexts with institutional support, mobility can function as a mental health resource; in contexts with institutional barriers, it can become a health risk (Islam and Jaffee 2023; Consolazio et al. 2022). This institutional contingency is the central theoretical contribution of the paper.

From a practical standpoint, maintaining and expanding minority support policies carries measurable health returns – a benefit that conventional policy evaluations, focused on educational and economic outcomes, have not captured. The gap between minority and Han mobility rates (21% vs. 30%) suggests substantial room for expanding access to structural mobility among minority populations, with potential mental health dividends that compound over time.

Future research should pursue three directions: directly measuring exposure to specific preferential policies to test the “policy-buffered” mechanism, disaggregating the binary ethnicity measure to examine variation across specific minority groups, and extending the analysis to physical health biomarkers to test whether the mental health benefits of minority mobility coexist with or come at the expense of physical health costs (Chen et al. 2021; Lam et al. 2025).
