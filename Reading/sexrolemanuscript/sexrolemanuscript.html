When Convergence Produces Divergence: Gendered Returns, Internet Use, and the Widening Gender Ideology Gap in China, 2012–2023

[Blinded for Review]

Abstract

Internet use widens the gender ideology gap in China. Using seven waves of the Chinese General Social Survey (2012–2023, N = 79,014), we show that the same digital access produces stronger egalitarian returns for women than for men. OLS models yield a Female \times Internet interaction of 0.081 (p < .001); instrumental variable estimation using province-level 1984 fixed telephone density and province-year internet penetration rates yields larger coefficients (\beta = 0.121–0.452; first-stage F = 177.6–599.9), and Wu-Hausman tests reject OLS consistency (p < .001), indicating that selection bias attenuates the OLS estimate. This gendered internet effect is strongest among younger cohorts (0.089 for post-1980 vs. 0.052 for pre-1980), producing a pattern in which digital natives are more egalitarian on average but more gender-polarized. Oaxaca-Blinder decomposition places this finding in broader context: the widening gap is driven not by differences in characteristics (endowment component = 0.092) but by divergent returns to the same characteristics (coefficient component = -0.246, p < .001), a pattern consistent with the gendered internet mechanism. Between 2012 and 2023, the gender attitude gap nearly septupled while the gaps in education, income, and internet access narrowed. Men and women are becoming structurally more similar but attitudinally more different, and internet use is one causally identified channel through which identical access produces divergent outcomes.

Introduction

Chinese men and women have never been more structurally similar, or more ideologically divided. Between 2012 and 2023, the gender gap in education continued to narrow, internet access converged to near-parity, and urbanization rates rose for both sexes. Yet over the same period, the gap between men’s and women’s gender attitudes nearly septupled (Figure 1). In absolute terms, this gap (0.179 points on a 5-point scale) is modest, corresponding to approximately 4.5 percent of the scale range. The substantive importance lies less in its absolute magnitude than in its trajectory: a divergence that was statistically indistinguishable from zero in 2012 had become one of the largest and most consistent attitudinal gender gaps in the CGSS by 2023. What explains this divergence, and why does structural convergence fail to produce attitudinal convergence (Ji, Wu, Sun, et al. 2017; Wu et al. 2024)?

The question has consequences beyond attitudes. Gender ideology predicts household labor division (Greenstein 1996), labor market outcomes (Acker 1990), marital satisfaction (Davis and Greenstein 2009), political participation (Bolzendahl and Myers 2004), and couples’ work-family arrangements (Bornatici and Zinn 2025). Gender attitudes also shape migration experiences and rural-urban inequality through intersecting axes of disadvantage (Tong and Chen 2025). In a country of 1.4 billion people with the world’s largest internet population, where gender equality is an explicit state goal, the widening gap signals that men and women are developing incompatible expectations about work, family, and each other.

Prior research has documented trends in Chinese gender attitudes using the Chinese General Social Survey, identifying education, urbanization, and cohort replacement as key predictors (Wang et al. 2024; Su and Ottervik 2023; Cotter et al. 2011; Luo 2021). But these studies examine what predicts the level of gender attitudes. They do not ask a more fundamental question: is the widening gap driven by men and women having different characteristics (an endowment gap), or by the same characteristics producing different attitudinal outcomes for men and women (a returns gap)? This distinction matters because it implies different mechanisms and different policy responses. The growing literature on digital feminism in China is primarily qualitative, documenting #MeToo activism and feminist self-media without testing population-level effects (Huang 2022; Chen 2020). No study has combined decomposition analysis with causal identification to distinguish these pathways.

We address this gap by pooling seven waves of the CGSS (2012–2023, N = 79,014). Our analytical strategy proceeds in five steps. We first document the descriptive trend: the gender ideology gap widened from 0.027 to 0.194 despite structural convergence. We then estimate OLS models with a Female \times Internet interaction to identify one mechanism through which attitudinal returns diverge by gender. We confirm this mechanism with two external instrumental variables: province-level 1984 fixed telephone density (Czernich et al. 2011) and province-year internet penetration rates from NBS yearbooks. We decompose the widening gap using Oaxaca-Blinder analysis, separating endowment and coefficient components to show that the returns-driven divergence extends well beyond internet use. We test for heterogeneity across birth cohorts and education levels.

Two findings stand out. First, internet use has gendered attitudinal returns: the Female \times Internet interaction is positive and significant across OLS (\beta = 0.081, p < .001) and two independent IV specifications (\beta = 0.121–0.452), with first-stage F statistics well above conventional thresholds (177.6–599.9). Wu-Hausman tests reject OLS consistency (p < .001), indicating that the IV estimates correct for attenuation bias. The same internet access yields stronger egalitarian returns for women than for men, and this differential is most pronounced among younger cohorts (0.089 for post-1980 vs. 0.052 for pre-1980), creating a paradox: digital natives are more egalitarian on average but more gender-polarized than their elders. Second, Oaxaca-Blinder decomposition reveals that this pattern extends beyond internet use: the widening gap is driven by divergent returns across the board, not divergent endowments. The coefficient component grew from -0.078 to -0.246 (p < .001) between 2012 and 2023, while the endowment component changed little (0.075 to 0.092). Men and women are becoming more alike in their characteristics but more different in how those characteristics translate into attitudes.

This paper makes three contributions. First, we provide the first IV-identified evidence that internet use has gendered attitudinal returns in the Chinese context, using two external instruments that capture distinct sources of variation in internet access. Wu-Hausman tests reject OLS consistency, indicating that naive estimates understate the gendered differential due to selection bias; the IV estimates are larger than OLS, consistent with attenuation bias from measurement error in internet use frequency. This identifies a specific, causally supported mechanism through which structural convergence produces attitudinal divergence. Second, we show that the gendered internet effect is strongest among younger cohorts, who display greater average egalitarianism alongside greater gender-internal divergence, challenging linear modernization narratives (Inglehart and Norris 2003). Third, we show through Oaxaca-Blinder decomposition that the gendered internet effect is part of a broader structural pattern: the widening gap reflects divergent returns to the same characteristics, not different characteristics, providing quantitative evidence for Risman (2004)’s ((2004)) theorization of gender as a social structure operating at the individual level.

Background and Theory

Gender Ideology in Post-Reform China

China’s post-reform trajectory presents a puzzle for theories of gender attitude change. The Maoist state mobilized women into the labor force at rates rivaling Scandinavian social democracies, yet market reforms since 1978 have reversed many of these gains. Women’s labor force participation declined from over 73% in 1990 to under 61% by 2020 (Feng 2023; Brussevich et al. 2021; Wu et al. 2024; Song and Zhou 2019). State discourse shifted from celebrating “iron women” to promoting “good wives and virtuous mothers” (a traditional ideal of feminine virtue), reflecting both commercial culture’s commodification of femininity and the party-state’s pronatalist turn (Yu and Xie 2015; Ji, Wu, Zhu, et al. 2017). Occupational segregation deepened as market forces replaced state job allocation (Du and Dong 2013; Shu and Bian 2003).

Two countervailing developments complicate this picture. Educational expansion has inverted the gender gap at the tertiary level: women now outperform men in university enrollment, creating widespread educational hypogamy in the marriage market (Ji, Wu, Sun, et al. 2017; Qian and Qian 2014). And the one-child policy (1979–2015) concentrated parental investment in daughters, producing a generation of young women with high human capital and high expectations for equality (Chang and Tian 2020; Zhang 2017). Parents’ own gender ideology shapes children’s attitudes through both modeling and explicit socialization (Halpern and Perry-Jenkins 2016). These structural shifts have not produced a simple convergence in attitudes. Survey evidence from the CGSS shows that while education and urbanization predict egalitarianism for both genders, the gender attitude gap has widened over the 2010s (Wang et al. 2024; Li and Jiang 2019; Su and Ottervik 2023). Gender role attitudes also predict income inequality between men and women, with traditional attitudes depressing women’s earnings (Qing 2020). Women’s attitudes have moved toward egalitarianism faster than men’s, echoing U.S. patterns documented by Cotter et al. (2011), Pepin and Cotter (2018), and Scarborough et al. (2019). Brooks­ and Bolzendahl (2004) identified cohort replacement, social-structural change, and ideological learning as drivers of gender attitude liberalization; the Chinese case tests whether these mechanisms operate symmetrically by gender.

Research on gender identity has long recognized that structural position and internalized beliefs are distinct constructs that do not always move in tandem (Wood and Eagly 2015). The critical question is not whether the gap exists but what drives its expansion. In principle, a widening gap could reflect compositional divergence: men and women acquiring different characteristics (education, income, internet access) that predict different attitudes. Or it could reflect divergent returns: the same characteristics producing increasingly different attitudinal outcomes for men and women. The distinction matters theoretically. Compositional divergence implies that equalizing access to resources would close the attitude gap. Divergent returns imply that the gap persists, and even widens, precisely as men and women come to share the same structural positions.

Internet, Information Environments, and Attitude Formation

Modernization theory predicts that internet diffusion should promote egalitarian attitudes. Access to information, exposure to global norms, and participation in cosmopolitan discourse erode traditional beliefs (Inglehart and Norris 2003). Early work on internet and political attitudes provided partial support: internet access is associated with more liberal views in cross-national comparisons (Campante et al. 2018; Zhuravskaya et al. 2020).

A competing tradition predicts polarization rather than convergence. Sunstein (2001) argued that information abundance does not guarantee exposure to diverse viewpoints. Users in choice-rich environments gravitate toward attitude-consistent content, and algorithmic recommendation systems amplify this tendency by optimizing for engagement (Eady et al. 2023; Mont’Alverne et al. 2024). The result is not uniform liberalization but selective reinforcement: the same technology can produce divergent outcomes depending on users’ prior orientations.

Four mechanisms link internet use to gender attitudes specifically. First, information provision: the internet gives users access to feminist ideas, gender inequality data, and discrimination narratives absent from traditional media. Second, identity reinforcement: online communities organized around gender identity provide social validation for pre-existing beliefs (Ging and Siapera 2019). Third, algorithmic selective exposure: recommendation systems learn user preferences and curate gendered content streams that differ systematically for men and women. Fourth, backlash dynamics: feminist visibility online triggers defensive reactions among those who perceive gender equality as threatening, a dynamic linked to anti-femininity norms that police gender boundaries (Faludi 1991; Connell 2005; Hoskin 2019).

China’s internet landscape provides fertile ground for all four mechanisms. The Great Firewall channels over one billion users onto domestic platforms (Weibo, WeChat, Douyin, Bilibili) where algorithmic recommendation operates without editorial balance requirements. Internet penetration grew from 38% in 2010 to 73% in 2021, with the sharpest growth in mobile access after 2014 under the “Broadband China” (

Prior work using propensity score matching on CGSS data has found that internet usage reduces gender division of labor bias (Gao and Liu 2022), but this approach cannot address unobserved confounding.

Digital Feminism and Gendered Counter-Mobilization in China

The 2010s saw the rapid emergence of digital feminism in China. The arrest of the “Feminist Five” activists in 2015 generated widespread online solidarity (Chen 2020). The #MeToo movement reached Chinese universities and workplaces in 2018, spreading through creative circumvention of censorship (Huang 2022; Han and Liu 2023). Feminist self-media accounts attracted millions of followers on Weibo and WeChat, and 6B4T discourse adapted from South Korean radical feminism gained traction among young women (Chen 2020; Yang 2023). Comparative analysis with South Korea, where the “Escape the Corset” movement emerged through similar digital channels, illuminates how platform-mediated feminism reshapes women’s consciousness (Chen 2025). The relationship between feminism and the state is ambivalent: feminist content is simultaneously censored for its political implications and commercially amplified because it generates engagement (Liao 2025). Since 2018, Chinese digital feminism has continued to evolve under intensifying state pressure. The 2022 Tangshan restaurant assault sparked massive online solidarity, with the hashtag generating hundreds of millions of views before censorship (Qin et al. 2024). The “full-time daughter” (

Meanwhile, the state’s designation of certain feminist positions as “extreme feminism” (

The Chinese state occupies an ambivalent position in this dynamic. On one hand, the party-state has censored feminist accounts, banned the term “feminism” from trending topics on Weibo, and promoted traditional family values through the three-child policy (2021) and state media campaigns celebrating domesticity (Han and Liu 2024). On the other hand, anti-feminist content circulates with less regulatory friction, and masculinist influencers face fewer content moderation interventions than feminist activists (Tan 2023). This asymmetric content governance may itself contribute to the gendered information environment: if feminist content is systematically suppressed while anti-feminist content is tolerated, the recommendation dynamics of platforms are tilted in ways that reinforce rather than moderate the gendered internet effect.

Exposure to this content is asymmetric by gender. Women disproportionately encounter feminist content through social networks, platform recommendations, and active seeking. Men encounter both feminist and anti-feminist content, but the latter is organized and amplified in historically unprecedented ways. The “straight man cancer” label (a feminist critique of toxic masculinity), originally a feminist critique, became a rallying point for masculinist counter-communities (Liu 2025). Nationalist anti-feminist rhetoric frames feminism as Western cultural infiltration, merging gender politics with state-endorsed patriotism (Altman Yuzhu Peng 2022; Wang and Ouyang 2023; Liotzis 2025). Critical discourse analysis of Chinese anti-feminist forums reveals how nationalist framings stigmatize feminism as a foreign threat (Xu 2025).

Several conditions specific to contemporary China intensify these dynamics. Educational hypogamy generates anxiety in a marriage market where men are traditionally expected to “marry down” (Ji, Wu, Sun, et al. 2017). The “involution” (

Rising bride prices and housing expectations place financial pressures disproportionately on men (Wei and Zhang 2011; Jiang et al. 2014). These conditions connect to Connell (2005)’s ((2005)) theorization of hegemonic masculinity: masculine identity is defended most aggressively when structural conditions make it feel precarious (Friedman 2015). The result is a gendered digital information environment where the same platforms facilitate opposite attitudinal trajectories for men and women.

China’s combination of formal state feminism, rapid internet penetration under authoritarian content governance, and a marriage market distorted by sex-ratio imbalance (Raymo et al. 2015) creates a uniquely informative case for testing whether internet use has gendered attitudinal effects. The Great Firewall channels all users onto domestic platforms whose recommendation algorithms operate without the content diversity requirements that constrain Western platforms, creating conditions where selective exposure dynamics may be amplified. Unlike open-internet societies where cross-platform migration dilutes algorithmic sorting, the Chinese digital ecosystem concentrates gendered content curation within a closed system, making any gendered return to internet use a conservative test of platform-mediated polarization (Pepin and Cotter 2018).

Theoretical Framework and Hypotheses

We integrate three theoretical perspectives to frame the widening gender ideology gap as a problem of divergent returns rather than divergent endowments.

First, Risman (2004)’s ((2004)) conceptualization of gender as social structure. Gender operates simultaneously at the individual level (identities), the interactional level (expectations in encounters), and the institutional level (organizational rules) (West and Zimmerman 1987; Ridgeway 2011; Acker 1990). A returns-driven gap is precisely what this framework predicts: the same structural position produces different attitudinal outcomes because gender mediates the translation of position into orientation. If the gap were driven by endowments alone, gender would function merely as a sorting mechanism. Divergent returns reveal gender operating as a structure that transforms how identical positions are experienced and interpreted (Davis and Greenstein 2009).

Second, Hochschild and Machung (1989)’s ((1989)) stalled revolution, extended from practice to consciousness. Hochschild documented a revolution stalled in the domestic sphere: women entered the paid workforce, but men did not proportionally take on domestic work (Sullivan et al. 2018; Greenstein 1996). We extend this framework to ideology. If women’s attitudes advance toward egalitarianism while men’s stall or regress, the revolution is not merely losing momentum; it is bifurcating. The same structural inputs (education, internet access, urbanization) that accelerate women’s ideological shift fail to produce equivalent change among men (England 2010; England et al. 2020).

Third, selective exposure theory (Sunstein 2001) provides the mechanism through which bifurcation operates in digital environments. Pre-existing attitude differences between men and women lead to gendered content consumption patterns, which reinforce those differences, which further shape consumption. The process is self-reinforcing and strongest among the heaviest users: younger, more educated, more urban cohorts who are also the most egalitarian on average (Wu and Liu 2025; A. Y. Peng 2022). The internet does not cause the gap from nothing; it amplifies a pre-existing divergence in how men and women process the same structural changes (Banet‐Weiser 2018; Gerson 2010).

This framework also speaks to the debate over whether gender can be “undone” (Deutsch 2007). If internet use enabled both men and women to move toward egalitarianism, it would constitute a site where gender is undone through exposure to counter-normative ideas. Our expectation is the opposite: the same technology reinforces rather than dissolves gender differences in attitude formation, making internet use a site where gender is done in West and Zimmerman (1987)’s ((1987)) sense. The asymmetry suggests that undoing gender may be structurally easier from subordinate positions, where the status quo provides material grievances, than from dominant ones, where it threatens existing advantages (Bridges and Pascoe 2014).

These three perspectives converge on a set of predictions that move from the macro pattern through a specific mechanism to its broader structural source.

If modernization has shifted both men’s and women’s structural positions toward convergence, we should observe a corresponding convergence in gender attitudes. Our first hypothesis tests this expectation against the evidence:

  • The gender ideology gap widened between 2012 and 2023.

If algorithmic curation produces gendered information diets (Sunstein 2001), and if women’s digital environments amplify feminist content while men’s amplify masculinist counter-narratives (Banet‐Weiser 2018), then the same internet access should produce stronger egalitarian returns for women than for men. The prediction is not simply that internet “matters more” for women in an atheoretical sense; it follows from the intersection of selective exposure with gendered content ecosystems that pre-sort informational inputs before any individual choice is made:

  • Internet use has a stronger positive association with gender egalitarianism for women than for men (Female \times Internet > 0), and this differential survives instrumental variable estimation using external instruments.

To understand the broader structural context of this gendered internet effect, we ask whether the widening gap as a whole reflects compositional differences or divergent returns. If internet use produces gendered returns, does this pattern extend to other characteristics as well?

  • The widening gap is driven by the coefficient component (divergent returns to the same characteristics) rather than the endowment component (different characteristics).

If algorithmic selective exposure drives the gendered internet effect, it should concentrate among the cohorts with the deepest digital engagement:

  • The gendered internet effect is strongest among younger cohorts, who are the heaviest digital users.

H4 also implies a distinguishing prediction. If the gendered internet effect operates through algorithmically sorted selective exposure, it should be larger among heavy users, who receive more curated content, than among light users. The interaction should accelerate rather than remain linear across usage intensity. By contrast, if the effect operates through general information provision alone, it should be similar across usage intensities, since even infrequent users gain access to the same informational landscape. We test this by examining whether the Female \times Internet interaction is stronger at higher levels of internet use frequency.

Data and Methods

Data

We draw on the Chinese General Social Survey (CGSS), a nationally representative repeated cross-sectional survey administered by the Renmin University of China. The CGSS employs a multi-stage stratified probability sampling design and collects data through face-to-face interviews with adults aged 18 and older across all Chinese provinces. We pool seven waves (2012, 2013, 2015, 2017, 2018, 2021, and 2023), yielding a total analytic sample of 79,014 respondents. For instrumental variable models, the sample is 79,006 when using the 1984 telephone density instrument (available for all waves), and 34,171 when using the NBS internet penetration rate (available only for 2017–2021, excluding the 2023 wave). The decade-long span captures both the rapid expansion of mobile internet access and the concurrent shifts in gender attitudes documented in prior CGSS-based research (Su and Ottervik 2023; Li and Jiang 2019). Social norms measured in the CGSS have been shown to predict gendered labor market outcomes, confirming the survey’s validity for capturing attitudinal constructs with behavioral consequences (Xiao and Asadullah 2020). Prior CGSS-based work using propensity score matching has found that internet usage reduces gender division of labor bias (Gao and Liu 2022), but this approach cannot address the gendered heterogeneity in internet effects that is our central concern.

Missingness across all variables is minimal (below 2% per variable). We impute missing values using the missRanger algorithm (Stekhoven and Bühlmann 2012) with predictive mean matching (k = 5), conducted separately within each survey wave to respect the cross-sectional structure. As a robustness check, we re-estimate the main interaction model using complete cases with listwise deletion (N = 72,160); results are substantively identical (Table [tab:robustness]).

Variables

Our dependent variable is a Gender Ideology Index constructed from four survey items that capture attitudes toward the gendered division of labor. Respondents indicated their agreement (1 = strongly agree to 5 = strongly disagree) with the following statements: “Men should focus on career, women on family”; “Men are inherently more capable than women”; “It is better to marry well than to work well”; and “Women should be laid off first during economic downturns.” These items tap into distinct dimensions of gender ideology: separate-spheres beliefs, gender essentialism, marriage pragmatism, and labor market fairness. We reverse-coded all items so that higher values indicate more egalitarian attitudes and averaged them into a composite index (Cronbach’s \alpha = 0.705), following standard practice in the gender attitudes literature (Davis and Greenstein 2009; Knight and Hochschild 2016).

Our key predictor is internet use frequency, measured on a 5-point ordinal scale ranging from 1 (never) to 5 (very frequent/daily). Gender, coded as a binary variable (female = 1), serves as the key moderator. The interaction between gender and internet use tests whether digital exposure carries different attitudinal returns for men and women.

We control for a set of sociodemographic characteristics that prior research has identified as predictors of gender attitudes. Age is centered at the grand mean. Education is measured in years of schooling. Personal income is natural log-transformed, with zero incomes set to zero before transformation. We include binary indicators for urban hukou (household registration system) status, current marital status, and Communist Party membership. The coding of party membership was reversed between the 2013 and 2015 CGSS waves; we recoded all waves to a consistent binary indicator. Year fixed effects are included in all models; province fixed effects are included in the IV specifications.

Analytical Strategy

Our analytical strategy proceeds through four stages, each building on the previous to move from establishing the empirical pattern through identifying a specific mechanism, confirming it causally, and then decomposing the broader sources of the gap.

We begin by documenting the trajectory of the gender ideology gap across survey waves, plotting gender-specific means to establish the empirical puzzle: a widening attitudinal divergence between men and women coinciding with structural convergence in education and internet access.

We then estimate four nested OLS models with year fixed effects to test whether internet use has gendered attitudinal returns. Model 1 includes demographics and controls. Model 2 adds internet use. Model 3 introduces the Female \times Internet interaction to test whether internet use is one channel through which attitudinal returns diverge by gender. Model 4 adds a Female \times Internet \times Year three-way interaction to test whether this gendered effect intensifies over time.

To address endogeneity concerns – OLS estimates of internet effects are subject to selection bias because internet users differ from non-users on unobserved characteristics (Zhuravskaya et al. 2020) – we employ two-stage least squares (2SLS) with two external instruments. The first is provincial fixed telephone density in 1984 (telephones per 100 persons) interacted with a linear year trend, following the Bartik-style approach used in studies of broadband diffusion (Czernich et al. 2011; Qu et al. 2025). Provinces with denser pre-digital telecommunications infrastructure later developed internet infrastructure more rapidly due to path dependence in network buildout. The second is the provincial internet penetration rate from NBS yearbooks, a supply-side measure capturing policy-driven variation in digital infrastructure (Melnikov 2021). Both instruments interact with Female to create instruments for the endogenous Internet \times Female term. We report first-stage F-statistics, Wu-Hausman tests, and Sargan J tests. All IV models include province and year fixed effects. A detailed discussion of the identification assumptions, including a formal assessment of each instrument’s exclusion restriction and a directed acyclic graph of the identification strategy, is provided in Section S1 of the Online Supplementary Materials.

Having identified internet use as one mechanism with gendered returns, we step back to ask a broader question through Oaxaca-Blinder decomposition: is the widening gap driven by men and women having different characteristics (an endowment gap), or by the same characteristics producing different attitudinal outcomes (a returns gap)? We apply the Oaxaca (1973)–Blinder (1973) threefold decomposition separately for the 2012 and 2023 waves. This decomposes the mean attitude difference between men and women into endowments (differences in characteristics), coefficients (differences in returns to those characteristics), and an interaction term. Comparing the two time points reveals whether the widening gap reflects compositional changes or shifts in how the same characteristics relate to attitudes differently by gender. Finally, we estimate the Female \times Internet interaction within subgroups defined by birth cohort, education, hukou status, and region to test whether the gendered internet effect varies across social positions.

All standard errors in OLS models are clustered by province to account for within-province correlation. All analyses were conducted in R (version 4.4.3) using the haven, lme4, ivreg, oaxaca, and missRanger packages. Instrumental variable diagnostics were computed using the ivreg package.

Results

Descriptive Trends

Table [tab:descriptives] presents summary statistics for the pooled sample. The overall mean of the Gender Ideology Index is 3.191 (SD = 0.864). Women score higher than men on average (3.227 vs. 3.152), indicating moderately more egalitarian views. Men report slightly higher internet use (2.869 vs. 2.721) and have more education (9.96 vs. 8.57 years) and income (log income: 9.67 vs. 8.30).

The aggregate gender difference masks a striking temporal pattern. Figure 1 traces the gender ideology gap across survey waves. Raw descriptive means show a gap of 0.026 in 2012 widening to 0.179 in 2023 (Figure 1); the Oaxaca decomposition, which conditions on covariates, yields slightly different estimates of 0.027 and 0.194 (Table [tab:oaxaca]). Both genders became more egalitarian over the period, but women’s attitudes shifted faster. Figure 1(B) shows the digital gender gap moved in the opposite direction: men’s and women’s internet use frequencies had nearly converged by 2023. Structural convergence in internet access, education, and urbanization coincided with attitudinal divergence – a pattern that cannot be explained by compositional differences alone (Scarborough et al. 2019; England et al. 2020).

Instrumental Variable Estimation

Table [tab:final_iv] presents instrumental variable estimates alongside the OLS benchmark. The core result, the Female \times Internet interaction, is robust across all four specifications. In OLS with province and year fixed effects, the interaction is 0.081 (p < .001). The 2SLS estimate using 1984 provincial telephone density is substantially larger at 0.452 (p < .001). Both specifications using the NBS internet penetration rate yield 0.121 (p < .001). The IV estimates exceed the OLS estimate, consistent with attenuation bias from measurement error in the internet use frequency variable. Across all columns, the conclusion holds: internet use carries a stronger egalitarian return for women than for men, and the IV estimates suggest that OLS understates this differential (Qu et al. 2025; Melnikov 2021).

The internet main effect, capturing the association for men, is small and non-significant in OLS (0.005, ns) but positive and significant in IV specifications: 0.204 (p < .01) with the telephone instrument, 0.169 (p < .001) with the penetration rate instrument, and 0.169 (p < .001) when both instruments enter jointly. The IV estimates suggest that internet use has a positive effect on egalitarianism for men as well, but the gendered differential remains: the Female \times Internet interaction is positive and significant across all specifications, indicating that women’s egalitarian returns to internet use consistently exceed men’s.

Diagnostic tests confirm instrument strength. The first-stage F-statistic for the telephone density instrument is 177.63, well above conventional thresholds for weak instruments (Czernich et al. 2011). The penetration rate instrument yields F = 599.91, and when both instruments enter jointly, the minimum F is 360.27. All specifications pass the weak-instrument threshold by a wide margin, removing concerns about finite-sample bias in the 2SLS estimates.

Wu-Hausman tests reject the null of OLS consistency across all three IV specifications (p < .001 in each case). This indicates that OLS estimates are biased, and the IV corrections are substantively necessary. The direction of the bias is consistent with attenuation: measurement error in the ordinal internet use variable biases OLS coefficients toward zero, and the IV estimates, which isolate exogenous variation in internet access, recover larger effects. The Sargan J-test for overidentifying restrictions in the two-instrument model yields p = 0.111, failing to reject the null of joint instrument validity. This supports the exclusion restriction for both instruments. The interaction coefficient differs in magnitude across instruments (0.121 for the penetration rate, 0.452 for the telephone density), consistent with heterogeneous local average treatment effects across complier populations: the telephone density instrument identifies a larger effect among compliers whose internet access depends on legacy infrastructure. Taken together, the IV results demonstrate that the gendered internet differential is not only robust to endogeneity concerns but is larger than OLS suggests: selection bias and measurement error attenuate the naive estimates, and the true causal effect of internet use on gender attitude polarization is stronger than the correlational evidence indicates.

Oaxaca-Blinder Decomposition

The OLS and IV results above established that internet use has gendered attitudinal returns. We now ask whether this pattern extends beyond internet use: is the widening gender ideology gap as a whole driven by differences in characteristics or by differences in returns? Table [tab:oaxaca] presents the threefold decomposition for 2012 and 2023. The decomposition reveals that the gendered internet effect is part of a broader structural pattern: the gap widened not because men and women acquired different characteristics, but because the same characteristics began producing increasingly different attitudinal outcomes.

In 2012, the total gap was small (0.027 points). The endowment component was 0.075 (p < .001): men’s advantages in education, income, and party membership pushed their predicted attitudes in a more egalitarian direction, partly offsetting the gap. The coefficient component was -0.078 (p < .001): the same characteristics yielded somewhat less egalitarian attitudes for men than for women. These two forces roughly balanced, keeping the observed gap near zero.

By 2023, this balance had broken. The endowment component remained stable at 0.092 (p < .001) – compositional differences between men and women had changed little. But the coefficient component tripled to -0.246 (p < .001). The gap widened because the returns to the same characteristics diverged sharply by gender.

The variable-level contributions illuminate which returns shifted most. Income shows the largest coefficient contribution in 2023 (-0.125, p < .001), meaning that income gains translated into more egalitarian attitudes for women but not equivalently for men. Education’s endowment contribution stayed stable (0.051 \rightarrow 0.044, both p < .001), while its coefficient contribution remained large but imprecise (-0.126 \rightarrow -0.095, ns in 2023). Internet use tells a revealing story consistent with the IV-confirmed gendered effect reported above: its endowment contribution shrank from 0.008 to 0.002 as the digital access gap closed, while its coefficient contribution shifted from -0.092 (p < .001) in 2012 to 0.055 (ns) in 2023. The closing of the access gap did not close the attitude gap; the returns to internet use, along with returns to income and education, diverged by gender in ways that compositional convergence could not offset (Cotter et al. 2011).

Heterogeneity Analysis

Table [tab:heterogeneity] reveals a counterintuitive pattern. Among respondents born after 1980, the Female \times Internet interaction is 0.089 (p < .001); among older cohorts, it is 0.052 (p < .001). The gendered internet effect is nearly twice as large for digital natives. College-educated respondents show a similar pattern (0.072 vs. 0.047 for those with nine or fewer years of schooling). The groups with the highest average egalitarianism – young, educated, urban – are the same groups where internet use most polarizes attitudes by gender (Wu and Liu 2025). Figure 3 shows that this divergence is not uniform across attitude dimensions: the largest gender gap appears on the “men career, women family” item, while the “fire women first” item shows the least divergence.

This creates a cohort-level divergence: younger cohorts are more egalitarian on average, yet the gender gap within these cohorts is wider than among their elders. Structural modernization produces more egalitarian attitudes overall, but the gendered processing of digital environments ensures that these gains accrue disproportionately to women. Regional variation, by contrast, is minimal. The Female \times Internet interaction ranges only from 0.078 to 0.091 across East, Central, and West China (Table [tab:heterogeneity], Panel D). The gendered internet effect is a nationwide phenomenon, not a regional one (Luo 2021; Knight and Brinton 2017).

Robustness

The Female \times Internet interaction is robust to alternative specifications (Table S1 in the Online Supplementary Materials). When each of the four attitude items is used as a separate dependent variable, the interaction ranges from 0.044 to 0.108 (all p < .001), with the “men career, women family” item showing the largest gendered internet effect (0.108) and the “layoff women first” item the smallest (0.044). Recoding internet use as a binary variable (any use vs. never) yields a coefficient of 0.251 (p < .001). An ordered logit specification produces a log-odds interaction of 0.187 (p < .001). Complete-case analysis with listwise deletion (N = 72,160) returns a coefficient of 0.082 (p < .001), substantively identical to the imputed-data estimate of 0.081. Across all specifications, the direction and significance of the gendered internet effect are consistent. Section S2 of the Online Supplementary Materials reports these results in full.

Discussion

The Gendered Internet Effect in Theoretical Context

The central finding of this study is that internet use carries gendered attitudinal returns: the same digital access produces stronger egalitarian shifts for women than for men. This gendered differential is consistent across OLS and instrumental variable specifications using two independent sources of exogenous variation, and it is largest among the youngest, most digitally immersed cohorts. The IV estimates are larger than OLS (0.121–0.452 vs. 0.081), and Wu-Hausman tests reject OLS consistency (p < .001), indicating that naive estimates understate the true gendered differential due to attenuation bias from measurement error in internet use frequency. The IV results do not merely confirm the OLS findings; they correct for downward bias, revealing a stronger causal effect than correlational analysis alone would suggest. The Sargan J-test fails to reject in the combined specification (p = .111), providing moderate support for joint instrument validity.

Why would the same technology produce asymmetric attitudinal returns? Sunstein (2001) proposed that information-rich environments do not guarantee exposure to diverse viewpoints. Users gravitate toward attitude-consistent content, and algorithmic recommendation systems amplify this tendency through engagement optimization. In the gender domain, this selective exposure operates on a pre-existing asymmetry: men and women enter digital spaces with different prior orientations shaped by lived experience, socialization, and material interests (Eady et al. 2023; Mont’Alverne et al. 2024). Women encounter feminist commentary, workplace discrimination narratives, and #MeToo discussions that validate egalitarian commitments (Huang 2022; Chen 2020). Men encounter a content ecosystem where masculinist creators frame feminism as threatening and repackage traditional gender norms as counter-cultural resistance (Wang and Ouyang 2023; Ging and Siapera 2019). The same bandwidth delivers different ideological raw material, and the difference compounds with use.

England (2010) described the gender revolution as “uneven” because the persistent devaluation of feminized activities created asymmetric incentives: women had strong material reasons to enter male-dominated domains, but men had little reason to move in the opposite direction. Our findings extend this asymmetric incentive framework from labor markets to information environments. Women encounter both the material incentive (workplace discrimination, pay gaps, domestic labor burdens) and digital content that validates egalitarian commitments. Men lack both. The asymmetry is not only in who benefits from structural change but in what the information environment offers as interpretive resources for processing that change. Faludi (1991) documented how gains in women’s status triggered organized cultural resistance; the digital ecosystem has industrialized this dynamic, making it continuous rather than episodic and personalized rather than broadcast (Liotzis 2025). The finding here, however, demands precision. Our data show that the gendered interaction is robust, not that men become more traditional. The differential reflects asymmetric returns, not male backlash per se. What the internet appears to do is open a channel for women’s egalitarianism while failing to open an equivalent channel for men. We hypothesize that gendered content ecosystems drive this differential internet effect, but the CGSS does not provide data on content consumption, platform use, or exposure to specific types of gender-related content. The 5-point internet frequency measure captures how often respondents use the internet, not what they encounter online. Establishing the content exposure mechanism directly requires platform-level data – screen time logs, content type surveys, or algorithmic audit studies – that are not available in nationally representative surveys. Our analysis establishes that the gendered interaction exists and survives causal identification, but the specific content pathway remains an inference from theory rather than a direct observation.

Banet‐Weiser (2018) argued that popular feminism and popular misogyny are co-constituted: the same platform ecosystem that amplifies feminist voices simultaneously generates and circulates anti-feminist content. This co-constitution helps explain why the gendered internet effect is not simply about women gaining access to feminist ideas; it is about a platform ecology in which gendered content streams diverge by design. Engagement optimization rewards emotional intensity, and for gender-related content, intensity runs in opposite directions for men and women. Cross-national evidence is consistent with this interpretation. In South Korea, where a parallel digital feminist movement (the “Escape the Corset” campaign) emerged through similar platform dynamics, young men’s gender attitudes have moved in a conservative direction even as young women’s have liberalized (Yoon and Park 2025; Chen 2025). A key institutional difference, however, is that South Korea has parliamentary channels for anti-feminist politics: the People Power Party ran on an explicitly anti-feminist platform in the 2022 presidential election, translating online gender grievances into formal political competition. China’s single-party system blocks this channel, forcing gender contestation entirely onto digital platforms. The Chinese internet may therefore bear a heavier burden as the primary arena for gender attitude formation than in democracies where political parties also mediate gender politics. In the United States, the gender gap in political attitudes among Generation Z has widened despite structural convergence (Scarborough et al. 2019). The gendered internet effect appears to be a feature of platform-mediated societies, not a Chinese exception.

Why Internet Produces Gendered Returns: The Broader Pattern of Divergent Coefficients

The gendered internet effect documented above operates within a broader structural pattern. The Oaxaca-Blinder decomposition helps explain why internet use produces different returns for men and women: because all characteristics, not just internet access, are filtered through gendered meaning systems that transform identical inputs into divergent attitudinal outputs. The coefficient component, capturing divergent returns to the same characteristics, tripled over the study period, while the endowment component remained stable. Men and women have become more similar in education, income, and internet access. This structural convergence has not produced attitudinal convergence. The gap widened because the same characteristics yield increasingly different attitudinal outcomes depending on the gender of the person holding them.

This finding speaks directly to Risman (2004)’s ((2004)) conceptualization of gender as a social structure operating at three levels. At the individual level, identical structural positions are internalized differently depending on whether one occupies them as a man or as a woman; the decomposition result captures precisely this pattern. At the interactional level, digital platforms constitute a new arena of gendered interaction where users encounter content, participate in communities, and receive algorithmically curated information that reflects gendered interpretive frames (A. Y. Peng 2022). Internet use is not passive consumption but an activity through which gender is accomplished, in the sense that West and Zimmerman (1987) ((1987)) theorized: users select, interpret, and share content in ways that reproduce gendered orientations. At the institutional level, algorithmic architectures embed gender differentiation into the infrastructure of information delivery (Acker 1990). All three levels of Risman’s framework are implicated: the decomposition captures the individual-level outcome; the internet mechanism operates at the interactional level; and the platform infrastructure constitutes an emergent institutional force that channels gendered meaning-making.

Ridgeway (2011) argues that gender functions as a “primary frame” organizing cognition as a background process in social encounters. If this frame had weakened under modernization, we would expect the coefficient component to shrink as endowments converge: similar positions should produce similar outputs. The opposite occurred. The gender frame has not weakened with structural convergence; it has become more consequential in mediating how experiences are interpreted. Ridgeway’s framework explains this directly: because the gender frame operates as a background default rather than a deliberate choice, it can intensify its mediating role in contexts where conscious gender distinctions appear to be fading. The overall pattern contradicts modernization accounts that predict attitudinal convergence as structural conditions converge (Inglehart and Norris 2003).

The variable-level contributions illuminate which returns diverge most, and why. Income shows the largest coefficient contribution: the same earnings produce different attitudinal outcomes for men and women. For women, income gains represent independence and lived proof that traditional breadwinner arrangements are neither necessary nor inevitable; each increment reinforces the plausibility of egalitarian arrangements. For men, higher earnings confirm rather than challenge the breadwinner model (England et al. 2020). Male income gains are compatible with both egalitarian and traditional worldviews, producing no directional push toward egalitarianism. Education follows a similar logic. Women in universities encounter feminist curricula, peer networks where inequality is discussed, and an environment where their own academic success contradicts essentialist assumptions. Men in the same institutions encounter a context where women increasingly outperform them, which may generate status anxiety rather than egalitarian conversion (DiPrete and Buchmann 2013). The same classroom produces different ideological raw material depending on the gender of the student.

Scarborough et al. (2019) identified an “egalitarian-essentialist” category in American gender attitudes, consisting of individuals who affirm women’s public participation while maintaining beliefs in innate gender difference. Our decomposition results suggest a mechanism through which this ambivalent category might emerge: when the same structural position (high education, high income, frequent internet use) produces different attitudinal returns by gender, the aggregate pattern can appear as a stable intermediate category that is, on closer inspection, a zone of gendered divergence. What looks like a coherent attitude profile in aggregate may reflect the averaging of women moving toward egalitarianism and men holding at or retreating to essentialism.

Internet use is not the largest contributor to the Oaxaca coefficient gap; income and education returns account for more. This is worth stating plainly: the returns-driven divergence extends well beyond digital access. Internet is one mechanism among several, and its analytical significance lies not in its relative magnitude within the decomposition but in being the only factor whose gendered effect is causally identified through instrumental variable estimation. Wu-Hausman tests reject OLS consistency, indicating that the true causal effect is larger than naive estimates suggest. The decomposition tells us where the gap lives; the IV analysis tells us that internet use is one causally identified contributor to the returns that drive it, and that selection bias attenuates rather than inflates its estimated effect.

Cohort Heterogeneity and Generational Divergence

The heterogeneity analysis reveals a counterintuitive pattern that complicates linear narratives of generational progress. The gendered internet effect is nearly twice as large for respondents born after 1980 as for older cohorts. College-educated respondents show a parallel gradient. The groups with the highest average egalitarianism are the same groups where internet use most polarizes attitudes by gender. We describe this as a digital generation paradox: younger cohorts are more egalitarian on average but more gender-polarized in how they process the same digital environments. This pattern has a generational-structural logic that goes beyond the observation that “young people are more polarized.” In Mannheim’s sociology of generations, political consciousness is formed during the “impressionable years” of late adolescence and early adulthood (Mannheim 1952; Krosnick and Alwin 1989); the worldview crystallized during this period shapes dispositions for a lifetime. For post-1990 cohorts in China, these formative years coincided with the maturation of algorithmically curated platforms. The formation process itself is gendered: young women’s political consciousness takes shape within feminist-inflected information streams, while young men’s forms within masculinist counter-narratives, and both streams are structurally embedded in how this generation acquires its worldview. The paradox, then, is not an empirical curiosity but a structural feature of generational formation in platform-mediated societies, where the mechanism of consciousness formation (algorithmic sorting) is itself gendered.

Scarborough et al. (2019) documented that younger Americans replaced explicit traditionalists not with committed egalitarians but with “egalitarian-essentialists” who affirm women’s public roles while maintaining beliefs in innate gender difference. Their latent class analysis revealed category replacement rather than uniform liberalization. Our finding adds a gendered dimension to this transition. Among young Chinese, the ambivalence that Scarborough and colleagues identified may not be a stable intermediate category but a zone of active gendered divergence. Young women appear to resolve the tension between traditional socialization and expanding opportunities by moving toward egalitarianism; young men appear to resolve it by selectively appropriating traditional frames that protect existing advantages (Cotter et al. 2011). The same generational shift that produces female egalitarians also produces male defensiveness, and what looks like category replacement in aggregate may, when disaggregated by gender, represent a gendering of category transitions.

The platform ecology of the Chinese internet sharpens this dynamic. Xiaohongshu, with a user base approximately 70 percent female, functions as a space where feminist discourse circulates through lifestyle content, workplace advice, and relationship commentary (Yang 2023; Wu and Liu 2025). Bilibili, with a male-skewing demographic, hosts masculinist content creators who frame feminism as anti-male, link gender equality to national weakness, and repackage traditional norms as counter-cultural resistance (Altman Yuzhu Peng 2022; Xu 2025). Douyin’s recommendation algorithm optimizes for engagement, and for gender-related content, engagement correlates with emotional intensity: outrage, indignation, solidarity (Zhuravskaya et al. 2020). Among the heaviest users, young people, this algorithmic sorting is most intensive, which helps explain why the gendered internet effect concentrates in precisely the cohorts where structural positions are most similar.

Hochschild and Machung (1989)’s ((1989)) “stalled revolution” described a gap between women’s entry into the workforce and men’s failure to redistribute domestic labor (Sullivan et al. 2018; Greenstein 1996). Our findings point to a parallel bifurcation in consciousness. Among younger cohorts, the revolution in gender ideology has not stalled uniformly; it has split. Women’s consciousness has advanced while men’s has lagged, and digital platforms provide the infrastructure through which this split is maintained and deepened (Connell 2005; Friedman 2015). The stalled revolution is not only about who does the housework; it is about who believes the housework should be shared, and digital environments are widening that belief gap among the very generation expected to close it.

Cross-national evidence reinforces this interpretation. South Korea’s gender polarization among youth follows a parallel trajectory, with digital feminism (the “Escape the Corset” movement) and masculinist counter-mobilization operating through the same platform dynamics (Yoon and Park 2025; Sohn 2022; Chen 2025). South Korea’s gender attitude polarization among youth shares structural similarities with the Chinese case: both countries feature high internet penetration, algorithmically driven domestic platforms (KakaoTalk and Naver in Korea; Weibo and Douyin in China), intense educational competition, and marriage market anxieties linked to housing costs and demographic pressures (Yoon and Park 2025; Sohn 2022). The parallel suggests that this generational divergence may emerge wherever platform-mediated information consumption intersects with structural preconditions for gender anxiety. The pattern may be a feature of platform-mediated societies more broadly, arising wherever algorithmic content curation interacts with pre-existing gender asymmetries in incentives and identity. If so, the finding carries implications well beyond the Chinese context.

Limitations and Future Directions

Several limitations qualify these conclusions. First, the CGSS is a repeated cross-section, not a panel. We observe between-person differences across waves but cannot track within-person attitude change; cohort, period, and age effects remain partially confounded despite year and province fixed effects. Second, the CGSS measures internet use on a 5-point frequency scale (never to daily) without distinguishing between platforms, content types, or active versus passive consumption. This is a meaningful limitation because the mechanism we propose – gendered content ecosystems – operates at the platform and content level, not at the level of aggregate frequency. The selective exposure mechanism we propose depends on content-specific processes that the CGSS cannot capture. Third, the four gender attitude items measure a narrow dimension of gender ideology centered on work-family arrangements (Davis and Greenstein 2009; Knight and Brinton 2017). Attitudes toward political representation, bodily autonomy, or sexual norms may show different patterns. Fourth, while all first-stage F-statistics comfortably exceed conventional thresholds (178–600), the telephone density instrument produces a substantially larger interaction coefficient (0.452) than the internet penetration rate instrument (0.121), which may reflect heterogeneous local average treatment effects across complier populations rather than instrument invalidity. Fifth, our heterogeneity analysis examines education, cohort, and urban/rural status separately, but does not model their intersection. Future research should examine whether the gendered internet effect concentrates at specific intersections – for instance, among rural young men with limited education, or among urban educated young women – to test whether the digital generation paradox is a universal phenomenon or one concentrated at particular social locations. Sixth, the Oaxaca-Blinder decomposition decomposes correlational gaps, not causal ones. The coefficient differences reflect all gender-specific processes simultaneously: socialization, social networks, media consumption, workplace cultures, and family dynamics. The decomposition tells us that the gap lives in returns rather than endowments; it cannot isolate individual causal pathways. The IV analysis confirms that internet use is one causal contributor, but the remaining coefficient gap is not causally identified.

Future research should pursue three directions. Panel data that tracks within-person attitude trajectories would distinguish true attitude change from compositional shifts across survey waves. Platform-specific content exposure measures, including screen time logs and content type surveys, would allow direct testing of the selective exposure mechanism that our aggregate internet measure can only approximate. Comparative analysis with South Korea (Yoon and Park 2025) and other platform-intensive societies would test whether the bifurcation thesis generalizes beyond the Chinese context. Qualitative work on how men and women process identical online gender content differently would illuminate the interpretive processes that generate divergent returns (Faludi 1991).

Conclusion

Internet use widens the gender ideology gap in China. The same digital access produces stronger egalitarian returns for women than for men, and instrumental variable estimation using two independent instruments reveals that OLS understates this differential: Wu-Hausman tests reject OLS consistency (p < .001), and the IV coefficients (0.121–0.452) exceed the OLS estimate (0.081), consistent with attenuation bias from measurement error. Between 2012 and 2023, the gender gap in attitudes nearly septupled while the gaps in education, income, and internet access narrowed. The Oaxaca-Blinder decomposition places this gendered internet effect in broader context: the widening gap is driven by divergent returns to the same characteristics, not by different characteristics. The returns-driven divergence extends beyond digital access to include income, education, and other characteristics whose meaning is gendered, but internet use is the only factor whose gendered effect is causally identified.

Three implications follow. First, for the study of digital society: internet access does not uniformly liberalize gender attitudes. It amplifies pre-existing divergence through gendered content ecosystems, and this amplification is strongest among the youngest and best-educated cohorts (Sunstein 2001; Banet‐Weiser 2018). The generational divergence documented here, in which structural convergence coincides with attitudinal polarization, challenges accounts that treat internet diffusion as a modernizing force and suggests that the relationship between digital access and attitudes is conditional on the gendered meaning systems through which content is produced, curated, and consumed.

Second, for gender theory: the returns-driven divergence revealed by the Oaxaca-Blinder decomposition provides the structural context for the gendered internet effect. Gender operates as a meaning-making structure that transforms what identical positions produce (Risman 2004; Ridgeway 2011). West and Zimmerman (1987)’s ((1987)) insight that gender is continuously accomplished through interaction helps explain why convergence in endowments has not produced convergence in attitudes: the “doing” of gender transforms how positions are inhabited, and digital platforms have become a primary site where this transformation occurs.

Third, for policy: narrowing the gap requires attending not only to who has access to resources but to how those resources are interpreted. Platform governance and algorithmic design shape the content ecosystems through which gendered attitudes are formed and reinforced. If gendered digital environments are producing attitudinal divergence wherever platform-mediated information consumption is intensive, as emerging evidence from South Korea, the United States, and Europe suggests (Yoon and Park 2025; Scarborough et al. 2019), the implications extend well beyond any single national context. This generational divergence may prove to be a defining feature of platform-mediated societies, one that demands attention to the content architectures through which the next generation forms its beliefs about gender.
