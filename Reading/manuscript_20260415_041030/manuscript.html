Age, Period, and Cohort Effects on Poor Mental Health Days Among US Adults, 1993–2024

[Blinded for Review]

Abstract

Objectives. To decompose secular increases in poor mental health days among US adults into age, period, and cohort effects, and to examine whether these effects differ by sex and race/ethnicity.

Methods. We analyzed 31 years of Behavioral Risk Factor Surveillance System data (1993–2024; United States; N = 10{,}406{,}149) using hierarchical age-period-cohort cross-classified random effects models. We fit separate models for males and females and for four racial/ethnic groups (White non-Hispanic, Black non-Hispanic, Hispanic, Other).

Results. Cohort effects explained 185 times more variance than period effects (\sigma^2_{\text{cohort}} = 3.43 vs. \sigma^2_{\text{period}} = 0.02). Poor mental health days increased monotonically across birth cohorts, spanning 6.5 days from the Silent Generation to Generation Z. Women showed stronger cohort effects than men (\sigma^2 = 4.39 vs. 2.74). White non-Hispanic Americans exhibited the steepest cohort gradient (\sigma^2 = 5.52); Hispanic Americans showed near-zero cohort effects (\sigma^2 = 0.20).

Conclusions. The US mental health crisis is primarily generational, not a response to recent period-level disruptions.

Public Health Implications. Structural interventions targeting formative-year conditions – childhood poverty, housing, education – may be more effective than period-focused responses. The racial heterogeneity in cohort effects demands targeted, culturally informed strategies.

Introduction

US adults in 2024 reported 70% more poor mental health days than those surveyed in 1993 – 4.9 versus 2.9 days per month on the Behavioral Risk Factor Surveillance System (BRFSS). The source of this deterioration remains contested. Popular accounts point to social media, political polarization, and the COVID-19 pandemic, all period-level forces that, in principle, should affect everyone exposed to them at a given moment in time. An alternative explanation locates the crisis in generational experience: successive cohorts come of age under progressively worse structural conditions and carry that burden forward across the life course. Distinguishing these accounts has direct consequences for intervention. If the crisis is period-driven, it is potentially reversible once the contextual stressor recedes. If it is cohort-driven, it reflects accumulated structural damage that requires long-term investment to address.

The BRFSS mentally unhealthy days item, available since 1993, provides the longest continuous surveillance series of self-reported mental health in the US adult population (Iachan et al. 2016; Pierannunzi et al. 2013). During the pandemic, 40.9% of US adults reported adverse mental or behavioral health conditions (Czeisler et al. 2020), and elevated distress persisted into the third year of COVID-19 with racial and income disparities (Kim et al. 2023). In parallel, Case and Deaton (2015) documented rising midlife mortality among White non-Hispanic Americans from suicides, drug poisoning, and liver disease – deaths of despair linked to cumulative economic distress.

Age-period-cohort (APC) analysis offers a formal framework for separating these temporal dimensions. Age effects capture biological and developmental processes; period effects represent shared historical forces; cohort effects reflect cumulative exposures during formative years that become embedded in health trajectories (Bell and Jones 2020; Yang and Land 2013). The hierarchical APC cross-classified random effects model (HAPC-CCREM), developed for repeated cross-sectional designs, treats period and cohort as crossed random intercepts and focuses interpretation on variance decomposition and nonlinear curvature rather than linear trends.

Whether APC patterns differ by sex and race/ethnicity is a question with clear theoretical grounding. Women report higher rates of internalizing disorders than men. Racial disparities in mental health persist after socioeconomic adjustment, with discrimination operating as an independent risk (Williams et al. 1997). The weathering hypothesis proposes that cumulative adversity accelerates health decline among Black Americans (Geronimus 1992), and fundamental cause theory predicts that groups with fewer flexible resources face disproportionate health consequences when new threats emerge (Phelan et al. 2010). These frameworks predict that if cohort effects drive the mental health crisis, their magnitude should vary across demographic groups.

Only one prior study applied APC methods to US adult mental health using BRFSS. Xu et al. (2024) decomposed age, period, and cohort effects on depression from 2013 through 2022, covering 3.1 million respondents with a binary indicator over 10 years. That short window missed the 1993–2007 baseline needed to identify older cohort trajectories, and the binary outcome discarded severity information. No study has used the full 30-year MENTHLTH series for APC decomposition, and none has stratified APC effects by sex and race/ethnicity at national scale.

We addressed these gaps using 31 years of BRFSS data (N = 10.4 million), fitting HAPC-CCREM models to the overall sample and to subgroups defined by sex and race/ethnicity. Cohort effects explained 185 times more variance than period effects, with a monotonic 6.5-day increase in poor mental health days from the Silent Generation to Generation Z. Women and White non-Hispanic Americans showed the steepest cohort gradients; Hispanic Americans were nearly immune to cohort effects. This study contributes to the literature in three ways. First, we provide the first 30-year APC decomposition of a continuous mental health outcome in the US adult population. Second, we identify generational drift – not period-level disruption – as the primary mechanism underlying the mental health crisis. Third, we document racial divergence in cohort trajectories that connects the deaths-of-despair thesis with the Hispanic health paradox within a unified analytical framework.

Methods

Data Source

Data came from the Behavioral Risk Factor Surveillance System (BRFSS), an annual state-based telephone survey administered by the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) in collaboration with state health departments. BRFSS is the largest continuously conducted health survey in the world, covering all 50 states and the District of Columbia, and uses random-digit-dialed probability sampling to produce nationally representative estimates of the noninstitutionalized US adult population aged 18 years and older (Iachan et al. 2016; Pierannunzi et al. 2013). We used data from 1993 through 2024, the full period during which the mental health days item has been available. We excluded the 2002 survey year because questionnaire restructuring that year produced approximately 60% missingness on the mental health outcome, yielding 31 survey years for analysis. After restricting to respondents with complete data on all study variables, the analytic sample comprised N = 10{,}406{,}149 adults. Complete-case exclusions accounted for less than 1% of all respondents.

Measures

Outcome. The primary outcome was self-reported number of poor mental health days in the past 30 days (MENTHLTH): “Now thinking about your mental health, which includes stress, depression, and problems with emotions, for how many days during the past 30 days was your mental health not good?” Responses ranged from 0 to 30. As a secondary outcome, we defined frequent mental distress (FMD) as reporting 14 or more poor mental health days, consistent with the CDC standard threshold for clinically relevant burden.

Age-Period-Cohort Dimensions. Age was measured using the BRFSS five-year age group variable (_AGEG5YR), producing 14 categories from 18–24 through 80 and older. Period was defined as survey year (1993–2024, excluding 2002). Birth cohort was computed as period minus the midpoint of the respondent’s age group, then classified into 17 five-year cohort groups spanning those born in 1924 or earlier through those born in 2000–2004.

Covariates. Fixed-effect covariates included sex (male, female), race/ethnicity (White non-Hispanic, Black non-Hispanic, Hispanic, Other), and education (six levels ranging from no formal schooling through college graduate). Race/ethnicity was harmonized across three coding eras in the BRFSS to maintain consistent four-category classification over the 31-year study period.

Survey Weights. We applied the recommended BRFSS survey weights: _FINALWT for survey years 1993–2010 and _LLCPWT for 2011–2024, reflecting the transition from post-stratification to iterative proportional fitting (raking) that accompanied the addition of cell phone sampling. All weights were scaled to a mean of 1 within each survey year to prevent years with larger samples from dominating the pooled estimates.

Statistical Analysis

We estimated hierarchical age-period-cohort cross-classified random effects models (HAPC-CCREM), following the framework developed by Yang and Land (2013). In this specification, individual-level covariates (age group, sex, race/ethnicity, and education) entered as fixed effects, while period and birth cohort entered as crossed random intercepts. The HAPC-CCREM treats each survey year and each birth cohort as draws from separate normal distributions, allowing simultaneous estimation of period-specific and cohort-specific deviations from the population mean after adjusting for age and compositional changes.

To reduce computational burden and stabilize estimation with over 10 million observations, we aggregated individual responses into cells defined by the full cross-classification of age group, sex, race/ethnicity, education, period, and cohort. This produced 17,391 cells. The outcome for each cell was the weighted mean of poor mental health days, and each cell received a precision weight equal to the square root of its sample size. Cell-level aggregation is standard practice in APC analyses of large-scale repeated cross-sectional surveys (Yang and Land 2013).

We fit six HAPC models: one full-sample model and five stratified models (two by sex, four by race/ethnicity). Stratified models allowed cohort and period variance components to differ across demographic groups, testing whether the APC structure of mental health varies by sex and race/ethnicity.

Sensitivity Analyses. We conducted three sets of sensitivity analyses. First, we fit the APC-I (age-period-cohort-interaction) model (Luo and Hodges 2019), which reconceptualizes cohort effects as deviations from the average age profile within each period, bypassing the linear identification constraint through a different parameterization. Second, we split the sample at the 2011 weight-methodology break to test whether the transition from post-stratification to raking weights influenced the pattern of results; we fit separate HAPC models for 1993–2010 and 2011–2024. Third, we restricted the analysis to the 1993–2012 subsample where continuous age (rather than grouped age) was available, to verify that five-year age grouping did not distort the estimated age profile.

Identification. The exact linear dependency among age, period, and cohort (C = P - A) means that linear trends cannot be uniquely attributed to any single temporal dimension (Bell and Jones 2020; Fosse and Winship 2019). We interpret results with this constraint in mind, focusing on nonlinear curvature in age, period, and cohort profiles and on variance decomposition rather than point estimates of linear slopes. The HAPC random-effects specification partially addresses the identification problem by treating period and cohort effects as variance components rather than fixed coefficients, though this approach rests on distributional assumptions that have been debated (Bell and Jones 2014).

All models were fit in R (version 4.x) using the lme4 package. Variance components, best linear unbiased predictions (BLUPs), and fixed-effect coefficients were extracted for reporting.

Results

Descriptive Trends

Mean poor mental health days increased across the three decades of observation (Table 1). During 1993–2000, respondents reported an average of 2.98 days per month (SE = 0.007), with 8.8% meeting the FMD threshold. By 2001–2010, the mean had risen to 3.42 days (SE = 0.004) and FMD prevalence reached 10.3%. The most recent period, 2011–2024, showed a further increase to 4.19 days (SE = 0.003) with 13.1% reporting FMD. This 41% increase in mean days and 49% relative increase in FMD prevalence over three decades occurred against a backdrop of substantial compositional change in the BRFSS sample, including shifts in age distribution, racial/ethnic composition, and educational attainment.

Age-Period-Cohort Decomposition

The full-sample HAPC model revealed that birth cohort membership explained the vast majority of temporal variation in poor mental health days. The estimated variance component for cohort random effects was \hat{\sigma}^2_{\text{cohort}} = 3.4335, compared with \hat{\sigma}^2_{\text{period}} = 0.0185 for period effects, a ratio of approximately 185:1. This finding indicates that knowing when a person was born provides far more information about their expected mental health burden than knowing when they were surveyed.

Age effects followed an inverted-U pattern, with poor mental health days increasing from young adulthood through middle age, peaking in the 55–59 age group, declining through early old age, and then spiking again among those aged 85 and older. Period effects were near zero across all 31 survey years, with only a slight uptick during 2021–2024 that did not substantively alter the overall pattern. Even the COVID-19 pandemic years did not produce a detectable period deviation in the HAPC framework once cohort composition was accounted for.

Cohort effects showed a monotonic increase across successive birth cohorts (Figure 1). The BLUP for the earliest cohort (born 1924 or earlier) was -2.5 days relative to the grand mean, while the BLUP for the most recent cohort (born 2000–2004) was +4.05 days. This 6.5-day gap means that, at any given age and in any given survey year, members of the youngest cohort reported approximately 6.5 more poor mental health days per month than members of the oldest cohort. The cohort gradient was approximately linear on the log scale, with each five-year birth cohort reporting roughly 0.4 more poor mental health days than the preceding one.

Sex-Stratified Analysis

Sex-stratified HAPC models confirmed that cohort effects dominated period effects for both women and men, though the magnitude differed (Figure 2). The cohort variance component for women (\hat{\sigma}^2 = 4.39) was 60% larger than for men (\hat{\sigma}^2 = 2.74). For cohorts born before 1980, the male and female BLUP trajectories were roughly parallel, with women reporting higher levels at each point. Beginning with the 1980 birth cohort, the trajectories diverged: women’s cohort BLUPs accelerated while men’s continued at a steadier rate. By the 2000–2004 birth cohort, the female BLUP reached +4.75 days compared with +3.5 days for males, a 1.25-day gap that was not present among earlier cohorts. Period effects remained near zero for both sexes.

Race/Ethnicity-Stratified Analysis

The race/ethnicity-stratified models produced the most striking divergence in cohort patterns (Figure 3). White non-Hispanic respondents showed the steepest cohort gradient, with a variance component of \hat{\sigma}^2 = 5.52, the largest of any subgroup. Each successive White non-Hispanic birth cohort reported a larger increment in poor mental health days than the corresponding increment for any other racial/ethnic group. Black non-Hispanic respondents showed an intermediate pattern (\hat{\sigma}^2 = 3.67), with a clear cohort gradient that was less steep than the White non-Hispanic trajectory. Respondents classified as Other race/ethnicity also displayed a moderate cohort gradient (\hat{\sigma}^2 = 3.14).

Hispanic respondents presented a qualitatively different pattern. Their cohort variance component was \hat{\sigma}^2 = 0.20, approximately 28 times smaller than the White non-Hispanic estimate. The cohort BLUP trajectory for Hispanic respondents was nearly flat, with minimal differences across birth cohorts born from the 1930s through the 2000s. This near-immunity to cohort effects stands in sharp contrast to the steep generational deterioration observed among White non-Hispanic Americans and represents a cohort-level manifestation of what has been termed the Hispanic health paradox in the mortality literature. Period effects remained negligible across all four racial/ethnic groups.

Sensitivity Analyses

All three sensitivity analyses supported the main findings. When the sample was split at the 2011 weight-methodology break, HAPC models fit separately to the 1993–2010 and 2011–2024 subsamples both showed cohort dominance over period effects, indicating that the transition from post-stratification to raking weights did not drive the observed pattern. The APC-I model (Luo and Hodges 2019) produced results consistent with the HAPC specification; model fit improved by \DeltaAIC = 341.6 relative to an age-period-only model, confirming that cohort-related variation captured meaningful structure beyond what age and period alone could explain. The continuous-age subset analysis (1993–2012) yielded cohort and period effect estimates in the same direction and of comparable magnitude as the main models, confirming that five-year age grouping did not introduce systematic bias into the decomposition.

Discussion

The central finding of this study is that birth cohort membership, not historical period, drives the deterioration of mental health among US adults. Cohort effects explained 185 times more variance than period effects (\sigma^2_{\text{cohort}} = 3.43 vs. \sigma^2_{\text{period}} = 0.02), with a monotonic increase in poor mental health days from the Silent Generation (BLUP = -2.5 days for those born before 1925) to Generation Z (BLUP = +4.05 days for the 2000–04 cohort), spanning a 6.5-day range. Period effects were negligible even during the COVID-19 pandemic, the 2008 Great Recession, and other widely discussed historical disruptions. These results reframe the US mental health crisis: it is not a reaction to recent contextual shocks but a generational drift rooted in the accumulated conditions under which successive cohorts came of age.

The Mental Health Crisis as Generational Drift

The near-zero period variance challenges prevailing narratives that attribute rising psychological distress to social media proliferation, political polarization, or pandemic-related disruption (Czeisler et al. 2020). If these period-level forces were the principal drivers, the HAPC decomposition would have revealed meaningful period random effects; instead, the data point to a slow, cohort-by-cohort accumulation of risk.

Xu et al. (2024) reported both cohort and period effects in a 10-year BRFSS analysis of depression (2013–2022), with COVID-19 emerging as a detectable period shock. Our 30-year window, which captures pre-Great Recession baseline cohorts born as early as the 1920s, provides a far longer observational lever. The 10-year design in Xu et al. conflated the steep cohort gradient among younger adults with a short-term period spike, an interpretation our extended time series can adjudicate more cleanly. In the United Kingdom, Thomson and Katikireddi (2018) identified cohort-specific mental health deterioration among the post-1979 “jilted generation” following the Great Recession, a pattern consistent with our observation that each successive US birth cohort enters adulthood with a higher burden of poor mental health days. Otten et al. (2023) applied HAPC to German population data and found significant cohort effects for women but not men, a sex-stratified pattern that aligns with our finding of stronger female cohort variance (\sigma^2 = 4.39) compared with male cohort variance (\sigma^2 = 2.74), with post-1980 divergence reaching 1.25 days by the 2000–04 cohort.

Life course epidemiology offers a coherent theoretical account of these cohort effects (Kuh et al. 2003; Ben-Shlomo et al. 2016). Cohort membership captures cumulative exposures during formative developmental periods – childhood economic conditions, family stability, educational environments, and availability of community social capital. Successive US cohorts entered adulthood facing compounding structural disadvantages: stagnant real wages, rising housing costs, growing student debt burdens, and declining rates of civic participation and institutional trust. These exposures are not experienced as discrete period events but as the ambient conditions of a generation’s formative years, and they become embedded in health trajectories that persist across the life course.

Racial Divergence: Deaths of Despair Meets the Hispanic Paradox

Race-stratified analyses revealed sharply divergent cohort trajectories. White non-Hispanic Americans exhibited the steepest cohort gradient (\sigma^2 = 5.52), extending the “deaths of despair” framework from mortality to mental health at a population scale. Case and Deaton (2015) documented a reversal in all-cause mortality among midlife White non-Hispanic Americans driven by suicides, drug poisoning, and liver disease – outcomes tied to cumulative economic distress. Our data suggest that the same generational erosion of economic security and social integration that produces excess mortality among White Americans also generates a steep, cohort-linked rise in poor mental health days among this group.

Hispanic Americans displayed near-immunity to cohort effects (\sigma^2 = 0.20), a finding that extends the Hispanic health paradox – typically discussed in the context of mortality (Williams et al. 1997) – to the domain of cohort-level mental health trajectories. Strong family networks, immigrant selection, and cultural norms of collective coping may buffer successive Hispanic cohorts against the structural deterioration that afflicts other groups. This cohort-level resilience held even as cross-sectional racial disparities in mental health care access persisted (Olfson et al. 2023).

Black non-Hispanic Americans occupied an intermediate position (\sigma^2 = 3.67). The weathering hypothesis (Geronimus 1992) predicts that cumulative adversity produces accelerated health decline among Black Americans, and Simons et al. (2020) documented biological markers of this process. Yet the Black non-Hispanic cohort gradient was less steep than that of White non-Hispanic Americans, possibly because Black communities have long borne high baseline mental health burdens – leaving less room for cohort-driven deterioration – and have developed durable coping structures in response to persistent adversity.

The finding that White non-Hispanic Americans show the steepest decline complicates straightforward applications of fundamental cause theory (Phelan et al. 2010; Clouston and Link 2021). If flexible resources consistently protect the socioeconomically advantaged, the group with the greatest historical advantages should show the least deterioration. Instead, the mental health crisis operates through mechanisms – erosion of economic expectations, decline of social capital, loss of perceived status – that disproportionately affect the group whose previous advantages are receding. The SES-health gradient persists (Phelan et al. 2004), but the cohort-level story is one of relative decline rather than absolute disadvantage.

Limitations

Several limitations warrant consideration. First, the BRFSS is a repeated cross-sectional survey; we cannot observe within-individual aging, and cohort effects are inferred from between-cohort comparisons at comparable ages. Second, MENTHLTH is a self-reported measure, and growing mental health awareness across successive cohorts may inflate the apparent trend. Jp Jos Leeuwen and Leopold (2022) found that rising diagnosed depression in Germany coexisted with stable or improving general mental health, suggesting that changing reporting norms can decouple self-report from underlying prevalence. Third, cell-level aggregation, while standard in APC research, sacrifices individual-level variation and precludes multilevel mediation analysis. Fourth, the fundamental APC identification constraint means that linear components of age, period, and cohort cannot be uniquely separated (Bell and Jones 2020; Fosse and Winship 2019); our interpretation focuses on nonlinear curvature effects, which are identifiable under all modeling approaches. Fifth, the 2011 transition from post-stratification to raking weights introduced a design discontinuity, though sensitivity analyses excluding years around this break confirmed the robustness of results. Sixth, race and ethnicity coding changed across the three eras of BRFSS data collection (pre-2001, 2001–2012, post-2013), and harmonization may introduce measurement error. Seventh, the BRFSS excludes institutionalized populations, who carry disproportionate mental health burdens.

Public Health Implications

If the mental health crisis is generational rather than contextual, several implications follow for practice, policy, and research.

Practice. Clinical approaches calibrated to acute period stressors – crisis hotlines scaled during pandemics, disaster mental health teams deployed after mass events – address real needs but do not reach the structural roots of cohort-driven deterioration. Mental health service planning should account for the differential burden across birth cohorts: younger cohorts carry a systematically higher baseline of poor mental health days and will require proportionally greater resources as they age into midlife and beyond. The inverted-U age curve, peaking at ages 55–59, implies that the largest absolute burden will materialize as Millennial and Generation Z cohorts reach middle adulthood. Racial stratification of cohort trajectories further indicates that service models effective for White non-Hispanic populations – the group with the steepest rise – will not translate directly to Hispanic communities, where cohort effects are minimal and access barriers remain the dominant concern (Olfson et al. 2023).

Policy. Structural interventions targeting conditions during formative years are more likely to bend the cohort curve than period-focused regulations. Childhood poverty reduction, affordable housing programs, equitable educational access, and student debt relief address the cumulative exposures that life course epidemiology identifies as the substrates of cohort effects (Kuh et al. 2003; Ben-Shlomo et al. 2016). These investments operate on a generational time scale and will not produce immediate changes in population mental health statistics – a political liability that public health advocates must address directly. The ACA Medicaid expansion increased mental health treatment admissions (Ortega 2022) and improved coverage and access for low-income adults (Miller and Wherry 2017), demonstrating that policy levers can reach populations at highest risk. The racial heterogeneity in cohort effects demands targeted strategies: understanding what protects Hispanic communities – family cohesion, community networks, immigrant cultural frameworks – could inform universal prevention models. Conversely, the steepness of the White non-Hispanic cohort gradient suggests that policy responses directed solely at racial minorities will miss the largest source of growing population burden.

Research. Future BRFSS analyses should maintain the MENTHLTH item for ongoing cohort monitoring; replacing it with screener instruments would break the 30-year time series. Mechanism-based APC studies that link cohort effects to measurable generational exposures – childhood economic conditions, neighborhood lead levels, community social capital indices – are needed to move beyond description toward causal understanding (Fosse and Winship 2019). The finding that COVID-19 produced minimal period effects in a cohort-dominated model warrants replication with post-2024 data as additional pandemic follow-up years accumulate. The sex-stratified divergence after the 1980 birth cohort invites investigation into whether changes in labor force participation, educational attainment patterns, or social media exposure during adolescence differentially shaped the mental health trajectories of women and men born in recent decades.
