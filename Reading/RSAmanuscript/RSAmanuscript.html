The Discordant Self: A Longitudinal Response Surface Analysis of Objective-Subjective Health Congruence and Well-being in Older Americans

[Author Names Blinded for Review]

Abstract

A persistent puzzle in medical sociology is why people with comparable physical limitations often differ so profoundly in their subjective well-being. Most prior research either treats self-rated health (SRH) and objective functional status as independent predictors or conflates them through difference-score methods that obscure the geometry of alignment. Drawing on the Health and Retirement Study (HRS) Waves 9–14 (2008–2018; N = 6{,}708 individuals; 40,248 person-waves), we apply response surface analysis (RSA) to examine how the joint configuration of objective health (standardized ADL+IADL functional limitation count) and SRH shapes life satisfaction. Three findings emerge. Congruence between objective and subjective health is positively associated with life satisfaction (a_1 = 0.373, p < .001): when older Americans’ self-assessments align with their functional status, their well-being is higher. When misalignment exists, however, the direction matters: optimistic self-appraisers—those who perceive themselves as healthier than their functional limitations suggest—report significantly higher satisfaction than pessimistic self-appraisers who underestimate their health (a_3 = -0.143, p < .001). Bootstrap-based longitudinal tests across six biennial waves confirm that both effects are remarkably stable over 10 years of aging; only the curvature along the congruence line shows modest temporal change. These findings reframe health discordance as a substantively meaningful psychosocial phenomenon rather than measurement noise, support theoretical accounts linking self-enhancing health cognition to well-being, and carry implications for how medical sociology conceptualizes the subjective-objective divide in aging populations.

Keywords: self-rated health; functional limitations; health congruence; response surface analysis; life satisfaction; Health and Retirement Study; aging

Introduction

Among the most durable findings in the sociology of health is that how people feel about their health often diverges from what their bodies do. Clinicians observe patients who rate their health as excellent despite accumulating limitations; survey researchers encounter respondents who report feeling sick yet demonstrate surprising functional capacity. This divergence between subjective health appraisal and objective functional status has been catalogued since the earliest longitudinal studies of aging (Maddox and Douglass 1973; Idler and Benyamini 1997). Yet its systematic consequences for subjective well-being—how different forms and directions of health discordance shape the experience of a good life—remain theoretically underdeveloped and empirically unresolved.

The stakes of this puzzle extend well beyond measurement. Self-rated health (SRH) is among the most powerful predictors of mortality, hospitalization, and functional decline in the gerontological literature (Mossey and Shapiro 1982; Idler and Benyamini 1997; Benyamini 2011). Its predictive power has, remarkably, increased over recent decades (Schnittker and Baćak 2014), suggesting that self-assessment encodes information about health trajectories beyond what standard clinical measures capture (Jylhä 2009). Meanwhile, life satisfaction—the cognitive evaluation of one’s life as a whole—is itself consequential for health and longevity (Steptoe et al. 2015). Understanding how health self-perceptions and functional capacities jointly configure the landscape of well-being in late life is therefore not merely descriptive but theoretically and practically significant.

Prior research has addressed this question in two limited ways. The first tradition examines SRH and objective health as independent predictors of well-being, finding both to be positively associated with life satisfaction (Okun et al. 1984; Menec 2003). This approach cannot capture whether congruence between the two measures matters above and beyond their individual effects. The second tradition uses difference scores (SRH minus objective health, or vice versa) to operationalize misalignment, a strategy that imposes strong and typically unverified assumptions about the symmetry and linearity of discordance effects (Edwards and Parry 1993). Both approaches leave unanswered a more fundamental question: when objective and subjective health diverge, does it matter in which direction?

Theoretical arguments would predict that it should. From positive psychology, research on self-enhancing cognition suggests that slightly inflated self-assessments—relative to objective reality—are adaptive rather than pathological (Taylor and Brown 1988). Applied to health, this would predict that optimistic health self-appraisal (perceiving oneself as healthier than objective limitations imply) sustains the psychological resources—sense of control, mastery, coherence—that underpin subjective well-being (Pearlin et al. 1981). Conversely, pessimistic self-appraisal (perceiving worse health than objective status warrants) may deplete these resources, amplifying the adverse effects of functional decline. Yet this directional asymmetry has never been formally tested in a large, longitudinal, population-based cohort using methods appropriate to congruence analysis.

We address this gap by applying response surface analysis (RSA) to six biennial waves of the Health and Retirement Study (HRS), a nationally representative panel of older Americans. RSA, developed by Edwards and Parry (1993) and formalized for use in the social sciences by Nestler et al. (2019), models the full second-order polynomial surface relating two predictors to an outcome without the restrictive assumptions of difference scores. This allows us to simultaneously estimate (a) how life satisfaction changes as objective and subjective health move together along the line of congruence, (b) whether that relationship is linear or curved, and (c) whether incongruence effects differ depending on which health indicator is dominant. We further employ bootstrap-based longitudinal tests across all six waves to assess whether these surface patterns are stable, increasing, or diminishing as respondents age.

Our contribution is threefold. Methodologically, we introduce RSA to the study of objective-subjective health misalignment, demonstrating its advantages over conventional difference-score approaches. Theoretically, we provide the first systematic longitudinal evidence that the direction of health discordance matters for well-being in ways consistent with positive self-enhancement theory. Empirically, we establish that these patterns are structurally stable across a decade of aging in a nationally representative cohort, suggesting that health discordance shapes well-being trajectories rather than merely reflecting measurement error.

Background

The Measurement Gap: When Objective and Subjective Health Diverge

The gap between objective and subjective health has been a defining methodological and substantive concern in health sociology since Maddox and Douglass’s (1973) foundational demonstration that physicians’ and patients’ health ratings frequently diverge. Contemporary research operationalizes this gap in diverse ways: between physician-assessed conditions and SRH, between performance-based physical tests and functional self-reports, and—as in the present study—between observed functional limitations (Activities of Daily Living [ADL] and Instrumental Activities of Daily Living [IADL]) and global health self-appraisal (Katz and Akpom 1976; Lawton and Brody 1969).

A substantial literature now establishes that SRH is a valid global health indicator, not merely an artifact of individual psychology. Jylhä (2009) conceptualizes SRH as a multidimensional “health competence” assessment that integrates biomedical information, symptom experience, functional capacity, social comparisons, and health values. Its robust prospective prediction of mortality—across 27 community studies (Idler and Benyamini 1997) and extending to biomarker-level mechanisms (Benyamini 2011)—confirms that SRH captures health-relevant information that complements, rather than duplicates, objective measures. Schnittker and Baćak (2014) show, using linked survey-clinical data, that SRH’s predictive validity has strengthened over time, plausibly because physiological heterogeneity at any given level of disease burden has increased as older populations survive to later ages.

Yet this very validity raises the question: when SRH and objective health diverge, which is “right,” and what are the consequences? In surveys with repeated measures, misalignment is far from rare. Among the older Americans in our analytic sample, only 37% show congruence between standardized functional status and SRH; nearly half (47%) rate their health worse than their functional status alone would predict (pessimistic misalignment), while 16% rate their health better than functional status predicts (optimistic misalignment). Such patterns are not idiosyncratic but reflect systematic social processes: SRH integrates social comparisons with age-peers (Mechanic and Hansell 1987), cultural norms about stoicism and complaint (Bailis et al. 2003), socioeconomic positioning that shapes both resources and reference groups (Prus 2011), and racial and ethnic health cultures (Farmer and Ferraro 2005). Objective functional limitations, by contrast, are comparatively insensitive to these psychosocial modifiers, capturing only whether a person can perform discrete tasks.

Crucially, these two health dimensions have independent consequences for outcomes. Herd et al. (2007) demonstrate that the pathways through which SRH and functional limitations shape health are distinct, while Crimmins et al. (1996) show that the gap between active and total life expectancy depends on how both subjective and functional health evolve over the life course. Against this backdrop, treating the two health dimensions as interchangeable or collapsing them into a single difference score ignores theoretically meaningful heterogeneity in their joint configuration.

From a sociological standpoint, health discordance is best understood not as individual cognitive error but as a socially structured phenomenon. The degree to which SRH tracks objective functional status depends on access to health information (which is distributed along educational and socioeconomic gradients), cultural frameworks for interpreting bodily experience (which vary by gender and race/ethnicity), and social comparison referents (which are shaped by neighborhood, occupational, and peer contexts). Mirowsky and Ross (2008) argue that SRH’s measurement properties themselves reflect the social distribution of health knowledge: among more educated respondents, SRH more tightly integrates biomedical information and thus more closely tracks clinical measures. The amount and direction of health discordance thus function as social indicators, mapping the unequal access to health knowledge and the variable cultural salience of physical limitation across stratification systems.

Health Discordance, Psychological Resources, and Well-Being

Whether health discordance matters for well-being, and in which direction, is theoretically contested. Three distinct accounts generate competing predictions.

The congruence hypothesis holds that alignment between objective and subjective health is intrinsically beneficial, independent of the level of health. Drawing on cognitive consistency theory, this view predicts that when self-assessments and objective reality are aligned, individuals experience less cognitive dissonance, can engage in more accurate health-related decision-making, and maintain a coherent self-schema (Bailis et al. 2003). Congruence also facilitates the acceptance of functional decline that clinical psychology associates with adaptive aging (Hertzog and Nesselroade 2003). Under this hypothesis, we expect positive effects of health alignment on well-being regardless of whether both health indicators are high or low.

The optimism advantage hypothesis draws on Taylor and Brown (1988)’s influential theory of positive illusions, which proposes that self-enhancing perceptions—mildly inflated assessments of one’s own attributes—are normative, adaptive, and associated with psychological well-being in healthy populations. Applied to health, this argument predicts that older adults who perceive themselves as healthier than their functional limitations suggest (optimistic misalignment) will experience higher well-being than those whose self-assessments accurately track or underestimate their limitations. The mechanism is resource preservation: optimistic health perceptions sustain the sense of control, perceived efficacy, and future orientation that Pearlin’s stress process model identifies as central buffering resources against the health stressors of aging (Pearlin et al. 1981; Pearlin 1999). Consistent with this, Benyamini (2011) reviews evidence that positive health expectations—independent of objective status—predict better health behaviors and lower mortality, plausibly through their effects on motivation and persistence.

The pessimism penalty hypothesis is the mirror image: those who underestimate their health relative to their functional capacity (pessimistic misalignment) may experience disproportionately poor well-being because they fail to draw on the psychological resources their functional status could support. Mechanic and Hansell (1987) shows that health self-concept is a key mediator of the relationship between symptoms and functional outcomes; a systematically negative health self-concept may constitute a chronic stressor that independently depletes well-being (Pearlin et al. 1981). Importantly, pessimistic misalignment is also structurally patterned: older African Americans, women, and those in lower socioeconomic positions are more likely to report worse health than their functional status predicts (Farmer and Ferraro 2005; Mirowsky and Ross 2008), raising questions about whether discordance-related well-being differences may reinforce broader health inequalities.

These three theoretical accounts generate distinct and empirically distinguishable predictions at the level of the RSA surface parameters. Under the congruence hypothesis alone, we expect a positive a_1 (life satisfaction rises as both health measures move together) and an a_3 near zero (alignment matters, but direction does not). The optimism advantage hypothesis additionally predicts a negative a_3: for any given degree of misalignment, the surface is higher in the Y > X (optimistic) region than in the X > Y (pessimistic) region. The pessimism penalty hypothesis generates the same directional prediction for a_3 but attributes the asymmetry to the amplified costs of negative self-assessment rather than to the positive value of optimism. These accounts thus agree on the sign of a_3 while differing in their emphasis on gain versus loss. Curvature parameters a_2 and a_4 provide additional information: a negative a_2 (inverted-U along the LOC) would indicate diminishing returns to health congruence at extreme levels, while a non-zero a_4 would indicate asymmetric curvature in the misalignment region. Our analysis tests all five parameters simultaneously.

Critically, neither the congruence hypothesis nor the directional asymmetry hypotheses can be adequately tested with the methods conventionally employed in this literature. Treating SRH and functional health as independent predictors in regression models cannot estimate the surface curvature that distinguishes a congruence effect from additive effects. Difference-score approaches impose symmetry by construction: the “cost” of being one standard deviation above the congruence line is assumed to equal the cost of being one standard deviation below it. RSA removes these restrictions, estimating the response surface freely from polynomial terms and then deriving interpretable summary parameters.

Longitudinal Perspectives on Health Discordance

A final gap in the literature concerns temporal dynamics. Cross-sectional studies identify associations between health discordance and well-being at a single time point but cannot distinguish stable structural patterns from transitory states. Longitudinal evidence is essential for two reasons.

First, the experience of aging is itself a process of recalibrating health self-perceptions to evolving functional realities. Dannefer (2003)’s cumulative advantage/disadvantage framework predicts that health-related psychological resources compound or erode over time, and health discordance may be a mechanism through which this compounding occurs. If optimistic health illusions are adaptive resources, we would expect their effects to persist or strengthen over time. If they are instead a fragile response to early functional challenge that later becomes untenable, we would expect the optimism advantage to diminish as functional decline deepens.

Second, many observed associations between health measures and well-being may reflect between-person differences in health trajectories rather than within-person congruence dynamics. An individual who was always healthier by both objective and subjective standards will show a different congruence pattern than one whose functional limitations accelerated before her self-perceptions adjusted. The HRS’s panel design—with six biennial waves spanning 2008 to 2018—enables us to track whether the surface parameters describing health-well-being relationships remain structurally constant across these aging trajectories, providing a direct longitudinal test of temporal stability.

Data and Methods

Data and Sample

Data come from the Health and Retirement Study (HRS) (Health and Retirement Study 2020), a nationally representative longitudinal panel survey of Americans aged 50 and older administered by the University of Michigan with support from the National Institute on Aging. We utilize Waves 9 through 14 (2008–2018), which correspond to biennial survey rounds conducted approximately every two years.

Our analytic sample consists of HRS respondents who (1) were aged 60 or older at Wave 9 (2008), (2) had no more than two missing observations across waves on each of the three focal variables (life satisfaction, ADL/IADL count, and SRH), and (3) were present in the HRS from Wave 9 onward. This procedure yielded a balanced panel of 6,708 individuals providing 40,248 person-wave observations across six waves. Among the 60+ population present in Wave 9, we excluded those with more than two missing waves on any key variable (see Supplementary Materials for full attrition details).

Missing Data and Sample Attrition

Mortality is a primary mechanism of attrition in aging panels. Our inclusion criterion of \leq 2 missing waves on key variables is designed to maximize the retention of participants while acknowledging that some attrition is non-random. For the 6,708 individuals in our sample, by design each contributes observations across all six waves; however, because the data include imputed values for missing biennial observations (see below), the effective structure is a balanced panel. Variables measured at the person level (age, race, education) were drawn from the wave-9 observation or, where missing, from the earliest available wave.

Missing values on time-varying covariates (marital status, employment, insurance, wealth) were imputed using the missRanger algorithm (Mayer 2023), a random forest-based multiple imputation procedure that handles multivariate missingness patterns with predictive mean matching. This procedure was implemented prior to analysis on the analysis-ready dataset; the bootstrap confidence intervals reported in longitudinal analyses therefore account for the residual uncertainty introduced by imputation.

Measures

Life Satisfaction (Outcome). Life satisfaction was measured using the single-item global life satisfaction question (“All things considered, how satisfied are you with your life these days?”; 1 = not at all satisfied, 5 = completely satisfied). This item was standardized (M \approx 0, SD \approx 1) across the full person-wave sample and serves as the outcome variable (Z) in all RSA models. Single-item life satisfaction measures have demonstrated convergent validity with multi-item scales in nationally representative samples (Steptoe et al. 2015) and are standard in gerontological panel research.

Objective Health (Predictor X). Objective functional health was operationalized as the count of limitations on Activities of Daily Living (ADL) and Instrumental Activities of Daily Living (IADL), which together form a well-validated composite measure of functional status in older adults (Katz and Akpom 1976; Lawton and Brody 1969). The ADL scale (range 0–6) assessed limitations in bathing, dressing, eating, transferring, walking, and toileting; the IADL scale (range 0–5) assessed limitations in using maps, managing medication, handling finances, making telephone calls, and grocery shopping. We summed ADL and IADL items (range 0–11), reverse-coded so that higher values indicate better functional health, and standardized across the full sample (M \approx 0, SD \approx 1) to yield the predictor variable X.

Self-Rated Health (Predictor Y). Self-rated health was measured using the standard five-point global SRH question (“Would you say your health is excellent, very good, good, fair, or poor?”; 1 = poor, 5 = excellent), standardized across the full sample (M \approx 0, SD \approx 1) to yield predictor Y.

Control Variables. Following prior research on health and well-being in aging populations (Herd et al. 2007; House et al. 1994), we control for age (continuous, in years at wave), biennial survey wave (linear trend), subjective social status (Cantril Self-Anchoring Ladder, 1–10; Singh-Manoux et al. (2005)), log-transformed non-housing wealth (with negative wealth set to 0 before transformation), marital status (Married/Partnered as reference; Divorced or Separated; Widowed; Never Married), race/ethnicity (Non-Hispanic White as reference; Non-Hispanic Black; Hispanic; Other), U.S. Census region (South as reference; Northeast; Midwest; West), current labor force participation (Yes/No), and health insurance coverage (Yes/No).

Analytic Strategy: Response Surface Analysis

RSA estimates the full second-order polynomial surface relating two predictors X and Y to outcome Z: \begin{equation} Z = b_0 + b_1 X + b_2 Y + b_3 X^2 + b_4 XY + b_5 Y^2 + \mathbf{C}\boldsymbol{\gamma} + \varepsilon \label{eq:poly} \end{equation} where \mathbf{C} is a matrix of control variables and \boldsymbol{\gamma} the corresponding coefficient vector. From the five polynomial coefficients b_1–b_5, RSA derives five theoretically interpretable surface parameters: \begin{align} a_1 &= b_1 + b_2 \label{eq:a1} \\ a_2 &= b_3 + b_4 + b_5 \label{eq:a2} \\ a_3 &= b_1 - b_2 \label{eq:a3} \\ a_4 &= b_3 - b_4 + b_5 \label{eq:a4} \\ a_5 &= b_3 - b_5 \label{eq:a5} \end{align}

Parameters a_1 and a_2 describe the slope and curvature of the response surface along the line of congruence (LOC; X = Y), where both predictors take equal values. A positive a_1 indicates that life satisfaction increases as both health indicators are congruently higher; a negative a_2 indicates an inverted-U curvature along the congruence line. Parameters a_3 and a_4 describe the slope and curvature along the line of incongruence (LOIC; X = -Y), the diagonal where the two predictors are maximally opposed. A negative a_3 indicates that the surface is higher on the side where Y > X—that is, where self-rated health exceeds objective functional health—signaling an optimism advantage in the direction of misalignment. Parameter a_5 describes the rotation of the first principal axis of the surface relative to the LOC (Edwards and Parry 1993; Nestler et al. 2019).

RSA was implemented using the RSA R package (Schönbrodt 2016), which estimates polynomial models using lavaan structural equation modeling and derives surface parameters using the delta method (Nestler et al. 2019). All 95% confidence intervals reported for the main pooled model draw on a 5,000-replication parametric bootstrap anchored to the SEM-estimated parameter distributions (pre-computed and stored in the analysis workspace).

An important caveat concerns the pooled model’s standard errors. Because the pooled dataset stacks 40,248 person-wave observations from 6,708 individuals, the lavaan SEM-derived standard errors treat each observation as independent and do not account for within-person correlation across waves. In a fully hierarchical framework, this would require person-cluster robust or multilevel SEs. We address this limitation in two ways. First, the wave-specific longitudinal analyses (Section 4.3) are estimated separately for each wave and rely on person-cluster bootstrap CIs (sampling respondents with replacement), which explicitly account for the hierarchical data structure. Second, the robustness of the pooled estimates to within-person correlation is indirectly supported by the high consistency of surface parameters across all six individual-wave models. Readers should interpret the pooled model’s CIs as reflecting cross-sectional precision at the person-wave level; the wave-specific person-cluster bootstrap estimates provide our primary basis for inference about effect magnitudes and temporal stability.

Wave-by-Wave RSA and Temporal Stability Tests. To assess temporal stability of surface patterns, we estimated RSA models separately for each of the six waves (9–14), maintaining identical control variable specifications. Each wave-specific model is a standard cross-sectional RSA using between-person variation at that wave; this design does not estimate within-person trajectories but rather tests whether the surface geometry governing the health-wellbeing relationship is structurally invariant across aging cohorts observed at different time points. This yielded six sets of surface parameters (a_1–a_5) with their standard errors and 95% confidence intervals. We then used person-level cluster bootstrapping (5,000 replications; sampling respondents with replacement) to formally test whether each parameter differed significantly between any two waves, computing all 15 pairwise wave comparisons for each of the five RSA parameters, yielding 75 bootstrap difference tests. This approach accounts for the hierarchical structure of the data (repeated observations nested within persons) and provides robust inference about parameter stability that is appropriately adjusted for within-person correlation.

Results

Descriptive Statistics

Table 1 presents baseline characteristics of the analytic sample at Wave 9 (2008). Respondents had a mean age of 69.8 years (SD = 6.4; range 60–93) and were predominantly female (61.0%), Non-Hispanic White (76.4%), and married (64.5%). Mean educational attainment was 12.75 years (SD = 3.07), and a third (33.4%) were currently working. The sample’s health profile reflected the expected functional heterogeneity of a community-dwelling older adult population: standardized objective health (X) and SRH (Y) were both modestly above the overall-sample mean at Wave 9, as Wave 9 respondents are relatively younger and healthier on average than later-wave survivors.

The distribution of health congruence at baseline warrants particular attention. Only 37.1% of respondents fell within the congruent zone (within 0.5 SD of the X = Y diagonal), while 47.4% exhibited pessimistic misalignment—rating their health more poorly than their functional status predicted—and 15.5% showed optimistic misalignment. These proportions are consistent with broader patterns in aging research: functional health, measured as the capacity to perform specific tasks, tends to remain intact longer than global health self-assessments, which integrate symptomatic experience and social comparison (Jylhä 2009; Bailis et al. 2003).

Main Response Surface Analysis

Table 2 presents the full RSA results pooled across all six waves (N = 40{,}248 person-waves; n = 6{,}708 individuals). The polynomial model accounts for 15.8% of variance in life satisfaction overall, representing an 8.8 percentage-point increment above the baseline model containing only control variables (\Delta R^2 = 0.088, p < .001)—a substantively meaningful contribution given the known predictive saturation of standard health and demographic predictors.

Before interpreting the surface parameters, we compared five nested RSA models to verify the polynomial specification (full results in Supplementary Table S3). The full second-order polynomial model provided substantially better fit than both the additive model (\Delta\chi^2(3) = 133.2, p < .001) and the interaction-only model (\Delta\chi^2(2) = 69.9, p < .001), confirming that the quadratic terms (X^2, Y^2) capture genuine curvature in the health–well-being surface beyond linear and interaction effects. Crucially, the full model fit was not significantly worse than an unconstrained cubic model (\Delta\chi^2(4) = 6.3, p = .177; \text{CFI} = .999; \text{SRMR} = .000), supporting the adequacy of the second-order polynomial specification as the basis for interpretation.

Congruence effects (Panel A/B). The slope along the line of congruence was significantly positive (a_1 = 0.373, 95% CI [0.350, 0.396], p < .001), confirming the congruence hypothesis: older Americans whose subjective and objective health assessments move together in the positive direction report substantially higher life satisfaction. To appreciate the magnitude, a one-standard-deviation improvement in both health indicators simultaneously (congruent improvement) is associated with a 0.373 SD increase in life satisfaction—larger than the effect of subjective social status (b = 0.070) and larger than the life satisfaction deficit associated with marital dissolution (b = -0.275 for Divorced/Separated relative to Married). The curvature along the LOC was significantly negative (a_2 = -0.037, 95% CI [-0.050, -0.025], p < .001), indicating a mild inverted-U: the well-being benefits of congruent health improvement are largest at intermediate levels and show slight diminishing returns at the extremes of the health distribution. This pattern is visible in the three-dimensional response surface (Figure 1): the surface rises steeply from the low-health to the moderate-health region of the LOC, then flattens somewhat at very high health levels.

Incongruence effects and directional asymmetry. Critically, the slope along the line of incongruence was significantly negative (a_3 = -0.143, 95% CI [-0.171, -0.115], p < .001), providing strong support for the optimism advantage hypothesis. Moving along the LOIC from the region where X > Y (pessimistic misalignment) toward the region where Y > X (optimistic misalignment) is associated with a 0.143 SD increase in life satisfaction per standard deviation of displacement. This effect is not trivial: the optimism-vs-pessimism contrast in satisfaction is approximately equivalent to the well-being difference between married and non-married respondents. Curvature along the LOIC was negligible and statistically indistinguishable from zero (a_4 = 0.000, 95% CI [-0.019, 0.020], p = .971), indicating that the directional asymmetry is well-approximated as a linear function of the degree of misalignment: each additional unit of optimistic offset is associated with a proportional gain in satisfaction.

The contour plot (Figure 1, panel b) provides an intuitive visualization of these patterns. In the “pessimistic” corner (high X, low Y)—where older adults can perform daily tasks but rate their health poorly—life satisfaction is substantially lower than in the “optimistic” corner (low X, high Y), where functional limitations exist but self-perceptions remain relatively positive. The surface tilt is thus not symmetric around the diagonal: the plateau of high satisfaction extends further into the optimistic misalignment region than into the pessimistic region.

Among control variables, higher subjective social status (b = 0.070, p < .001) and older age (b = 0.012, p < .001) were positively associated with life satisfaction, consistent with prior research on social stratification and well-being in aging populations (House et al. 1994; Herd et al. 2007). Notably, controlling for health, all three racial/ethnic minority groups reported higher life satisfaction than Non-Hispanic White respondents: Non-Hispanic Black (b = 0.076, p < .001), Hispanic (b = 0.082, p < .001), and Other (b = 0.091, p = .004). This pattern is consistent with the well-documented “well-being paradox” whereby African American and Hispanic older adults report comparable or superior subjective well-being to White peers once objective health and socioeconomic circumstances are held constant (Herd et al. 2007; Link and Phelan 1995). All non-married statuses were associated with substantially lower satisfaction relative to Married/Partnered respondents: Divorced/Separated (b = -0.275, p < .001), Widowed (b = -0.224, p < .001), and Never Married (b = -0.161, p < .001). Current employment was also associated with lower satisfaction (b = -0.086, p < .001), likely reflecting selection of economically less secure respondents into continued labor force participation at older ages (Dannefer 2003).

Wave-by-Wave RSA: Temporal Stability Across Waves 9–14

Figure 2 displays the trajectory of each RSA surface parameter across Waves 9 through 14 (2008–2018), with 95% confidence intervals derived from the wave-specific models. Table 3 presents the full set of wave-specific parameter estimates.

The dominant story is one of striking stability. The congruence slope a_1 ranged from 0.347 (Wave 14) to 0.416 (Wave 11) across the six waves, with all estimates significantly positive (p < .001 in every wave). Bootstrap difference tests found no statistically significant change in a_1 between any pair of waves, confirming that the positive relationship between congruent health improvement and life satisfaction is structurally constant across aging trajectories from age \approx70 to age \approx80.

Similarly, the incongruence slope a_3 remained consistently negative across all waves (range: -0.171 to -0.085, all p \leq .047). Bootstrap tests found no significant pairwise changes, confirming that the optimism advantage in life satisfaction is equally present at Wave 9 (2008) and Wave 14 (2018). The first principal axis parameter a_5—which captures surface tilt—was significantly positive in five of six waves and showed no significant longitudinal change.

The one parameter showing systematic temporal change was the congruence curvature a_2. At Wave 9, the curvature was negligible and non-significant (a_2 = -0.010, p = .656), suggesting an approximately linear congruence effect. By Wave 14, the curvature had strengthened to a_2 = -0.054 (p < .001), and bootstrap tests confirmed that this change was statistically significant from Wave 11 to Wave 12 (\Delta a_2 = -0.055, 95% CI [-0.098, -0.011]) and from Wave 11 to Wave 14 (\Delta a_2 = -0.058, 95% CI [-0.100, -0.016]). This finding suggests that, as respondents age from their early seventies into their late seventies and eighties, the congruence-satisfaction relationship develops a more pronounced inverted-U curvature: at very high levels of objective-subjective congruence, the incremental satisfaction gain diminishes, a pattern potentially consistent with ceiling effects in well-being at extreme health levels (Hertzog and Nesselroade 2003).

Sensitivity Analyses

To assess robustness, we conducted several supplementary analyses reported in the Supplementary Materials. First, we re-estimated the pooled RSA model using complete-case data (excluding imputed observations), finding near-identical surface parameters (differences <0.005) with slightly wider confidence intervals, suggesting that imputation did not materially bias our estimates. Second, we estimated gender-stratified models; while the magnitude of a_3 was modestly larger among women (a_3 = -0.158) than men (a_3 = -0.127), the direction and significance of all surface parameters were consistent across both subgroups. Third, restricting the objective health measure to ADL limitations only (excluding IADL) yielded substantively identical surface parameters, confirming that our composite operationalization does not drive the results. Fourth, we estimated race-stratified models; Non-Hispanic White and Black respondents showed similar surface geometries, with the optimism advantage present in both groups, though modestly attenuated among Hispanic respondents. These sensitivity analyses support the generalizability of our findings across key subpopulations.

Discussion

We set out to resolve a persistent puzzle at the intersection of health measurement and aging research: when older Americans’ objective functional health and self-rated health diverge, does the direction of that divergence matter for their subjective well-being? The answer, grounded in six biennial waves of nationally representative longitudinal data and modeled using response surface analysis, is an unambiguous yes—and the pattern is remarkably stable across a decade of aging.

Congruence as a Structural Resource

The significant positive slope along the line of congruence (a_1 = 0.373) establishes that aligned health self-assessment is itself a resource for well-being, independent of the level of health involved. This finding extends the congruence literature beyond its origins in job-person fit (Edwards and Parry 1993; Edwards and Rothbard 1999) and parenting research to the domain of health self-perception in late life. When older adults hold self-conceptions of their health that match—or move in concert with—their observable functional capacities, they report higher life satisfaction than the independent effects of either health indicator would predict.

The mechanism linking health congruence to well-being likely operates through cognitive and self-regulatory processes. Aligned health perceptions reduce the need for ongoing renegotiation of one’s self-concept in the face of changing health evidence—what Bailis et al. (2003) describe as the “coherence cost” of discordant health information. Cognitively, when subjective and objective health tell the same story, older adults can engage in more effective health behavior planning, realistic goal adjustment, and accurate social comparison (Hertzog and Nesselroade 2003). Sociologically, health congruence may also reflect social-structural advantages: those with greater access to health information, medical care, and trusted informational networks are more likely to develop SRH that accurately tracks their functional status (Link and Phelan 1995; Herd et al. 2007). The social patterning of health congruence is thus itself an equity question, with implications for how structural disadvantage operates through health perception.

The Optimism Advantage and Its Limits

The directional asymmetry captured by a_3 = -0.143 provides the first RSA-based longitudinal evidence for a health-specific version of the positive illusions hypothesis (Taylor and Brown 1988). Older adults who perceive themselves as healthier than their functional limitations suggest are not merely miscalibrated—they experience a measurable advantage in life satisfaction relative to those who underestimate their health. This pattern is consistent with a substantial body of evidence linking self-enhancing health perceptions to better health behaviors, lower mortality risk, and superior psychological functioning (Benyamini 2011; Pearlin et al. 1981).

The key theoretical contribution here is situating this advantage within the RSA framework, which allows us to disentangle the optimism advantage from the underlying health level. Our results show that the asymmetry is a property of the surface—not simply a proxy for respondents who are healthier on average. Controlling for both objective health and SRH independently, the geometry of their alignment carries additional predictive information about well-being.

At the same time, the present findings cannot resolve whether optimistic health self-assessment causes better well-being, or whether shared unmeasured factors (dispositional positive affect, social support, or socioeconomic security) drive both optimistic self-assessment and higher satisfaction. Positive affect, in particular, is known to bias both health self-reports (Mechanic and Hansell 1987) and life satisfaction evaluations (Diener 1984), and its omission from our models is a limitation. Future research should consider explicit mediation models incorporating affect and self-efficacy measures to test the psychological mechanisms implied by the positive illusions account.

The flipside of the optimism advantage—the pessimism penalty—deserves equal sociological attention. Nearly half of older Americans in our sample (47.4%) rated their health worse than their functional status predicted, constituting the largest single group in our congruence distribution. This group, with its lower life satisfaction, is demographically skewed toward women, older respondents, and those with lower socioeconomic resources (Farmer and Ferraro 2005; Mirowsky and Ross 2008).

The social production of pessimistic health self-assessment operates through multiple structural pathways. First, exposure to discrimination—in healthcare settings, labor markets, and residential environments—shapes the informational infrastructure through which individuals interpret their health. Repeated encounters with inadequate care or dismissive providers may lead members of disadvantaged groups to adopt more negative health frameworks that systematically underestimate functional capacity relative to objective limitations (Link and Phelan 1995; Williams et al. 2001). Second, socioeconomic disadvantage reduces health literacy and access to specialist information that would allow more accurate calibration of health self-perceptions to functional reality. Third, cultural norms that valorize stoicism or stigmatize health complaints can produce systematic downward bias in SRH among groups socialized into these norms, particularly among older women in the current cohort (Mechanic and Hansell 1987).

The sociological implication is stark: pessimistic health misalignment is not merely a cognitive error to be corrected by better information but a structurally produced condition with measurable costs for subjective well-being. The RSA framework enables us to quantify these costs as the gap along the LOIC between the X > Y (pessimistic) and Y > X (optimistic) regions—a gap that, in our data, is equivalent in magnitude to the well-being difference between Widowed and Married older adults (a_3 = -0.143 vs. b_{\text{widowed}} = -0.224). Interventions targeting health self-concept—through health education, patient empowerment programs, or culturally tailored health literacy initiatives—may therefore yield well-being benefits that compound with, rather than merely overlap, benefits from improving functional health itself (Link and Phelan 1995).

Longitudinal Stability and the Life Course of Health Perception

Perhaps the most striking finding of our longitudinal analyses is how stable the surface geometry is across Waves 9 through 14. The congruence benefit and the optimism advantage are present at Wave 9 (when respondents averaged 69.8 years) and remain structurally unchanged at Wave 14 (average age approximately 80). This stability argues against accounts that treat health discordance as a transient adaptation to new health information—a sort of “recalibration lag” that would diminish as aging progresses. Instead, it suggests that the psychological and social processes that generate congruence effects and directional asymmetries are deeply embedded in the life course of health self-perception and are not dislodged by the accumulation of functional decline.

The one exception—the strengthening of LOC curvature (a_2) from Wave 11 onward—is theoretically interpretable as a ceiling effect: as older adults develop serious functional limitations, the incremental satisfaction gain from additional congruent health improvement diminishes at the high end of the health distribution. This is consistent with Dannefer (2003)’s cumulative disadvantage framework, which would predict that the well-being returns to health congruence are not linearly distributed but depend on where on the health gradient individuals are located. Heterogeneity in the curvature trajectory may be a fruitful avenue for future research, particularly examining whether curvature strengthens more rapidly among those experiencing accelerated functional decline.

Methodological Implications

This paper demonstrates that RSA offers substantial analytic advantages over the difference-score approaches that dominate the health discordance literature. Difference scores impose symmetry, reduce statistical power, and conflate main effects with congruence effects (Edwards and Parry 1993). By contrast, RSA separately identifies (a) whether congruence matters beyond additive effects (a_1, a_2), (b) whether misalignment is directionally asymmetric (a_3), and (c) whether these patterns are linear or curvilinear—all critical questions for health discordance research. The model comparison we report shows that the full polynomial model fits significantly better than more constrained alternatives, and that the specific pattern we find (rising ridge with negative LOIC slope) would have been invisible to difference-score approaches.

We acknowledge several limitations. First, our measure of objective health—ADL and IADL limitations—captures only the functional domain of health and does not index biological health status (biomarkers, physiological reserves) directly. Future work should examine whether RSA patterns replicate with clinical or biomarker-based health indicators. Second, the HRS’s biennial design means that our “longitudinal” analysis is actually six cross-sectional RSA surfaces compared across time; within-person change in surface geometry cannot be directly estimated without person-specific modeling. Third, differential mortality and non-random attrition may influence our estimates, particularly at later waves when the surviving sample is healthier than the broader cohort. Fourth, like all survey-based research, our measure of life satisfaction may be subject to momentary mood and order effects (Diener 1984). Our use of a standardized, single-item global measure minimizes some of these concerns, but multi-item scales would offer greater psychometric precision.

Conclusion

The subjective-objective divide in health measurement has long been recognized but rarely examined as a theoretically and empirically meaningful phenomenon in its own right. Using response surface analysis applied to six waves of nationally representative data on older Americans, we have demonstrated that health discordance is neither random noise nor a simple measurement artifact. The alignment of objective and subjective health is independently associated with higher life satisfaction, and when misalignment occurs, the direction matters: optimistic self-appraisers—those who perceive themselves as healthier than their functional status predicts—consistently report higher well-being than pessimistic self-appraisers. These patterns are structurally stable across a decade of aging.

These findings have implications for theory, measurement, and practice. Theoretically, they support a resource-based account of health perception in late life and suggest that the positive illusions literature requires serious engagement with the sociology of aging. Methodologically, they demonstrate that RSA is a powerful and underutilized tool for studying alignment and misalignment between health indicators. For practice and policy, the pessimism penalty identified here—disproportionately borne by women, minorities, and the socioeconomically disadvantaged—suggests that interventions targeting health self-concept and health literacy may have well-being benefits that extend beyond accurate health information. Whether that optimism is cultivated, earned, or socially distributed, it appears to protect the subjective experience of aging even as the body tells a more complicated story.
